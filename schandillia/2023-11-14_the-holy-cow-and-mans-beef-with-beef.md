# The Holy Cow and Man’s Beef with Beef

*Understanding the Dietary Negotiations around Beef among Early Aryans*

Few objects of reverence move India the way cow does. The animal is sacred to the Hindus to whom beef is the mother of all taboos. This reservation, however, is not shared by other religions, Islam in particular. Which has led to much communal carnage with no compromise anywhere on the horizon. While one side speaks of religious sentiments, the other speaks of freedom of food. While Christianity too doesn’t accord any sanctity to the cow, it’s almost always Muslims who find themselves at the receiving end of this fatal debate, given the long history of bitter, irreconcilable animosity between the two communities. Islam forbids pigs too, but it’s erroneous to draw any parallels between the two strictures for the simple reason that Muslims do not consider pigs sacred.

So the question is, was it always the case? If so, why? It seems rather strange that of all animals Hindus singled out cows and not even other bovines. We ask these questions only because the debate is as much on cow’s sanctity as on the antiquity of that sanctity. Repudiators argue that the tradition is a recent innovation and that Hindus had no qualms against beef consumption in Vedic days. The advocates, on the other hand, reject this argument and posit that the practice finds sanction in Rigveda itself. While technically speaking, a loss of antiquity should weaken the Hindu nationalist argument, it would do little to affect the debate itself. Faith once established, is hard, if not impossible, to shake with reason. This discussion doesn’t attempt to do that either. The only objective here is erudition. All it plans to do is lay bare objective realities with no purpose whatsoever.

So we are going to investigate two key questions:

  1. Was cow always holy and beef always taboo?

  2. Is Hinduism unique in its treatment of cows?




Since the religion goes back to the Vedas, we too shall start our journey with the Vedas. Starting with Rigveda, we’ll continue on to the other Vedas and even post-Vedic literature to learn what each of them says about cows and their utility as food.

## Rigveda

Before all else, let’s get one important caveat down, for it has a bearing on the way we look at bovine references in not only Rigveda but all Hindu texts. And that caveat is the very identity of the Holy Cow. While in the contemporary Indian context, the term beef covers both buffalos and cows, and both male and female, the sanctity of religion is only accorded to cows, that too preferably females. With that, we can rule out a few verses right away—6.17.11,1 5.29.7,2 and 8.12.8.3 All three speak of Indra, the apex deity of the Vedas, as voracious beef aficionado consuming 100, 300, and 1,000 bovines respectively. Often advanced as evidence against cow slaughter in the Vedic society, these can be dismissed right away as the bovine they refer to is not cow but _mahiṣa_ or buffalo.

_Maṇḍala_ 10 offers several verses of help to Vedic beef proponents, two of which 27.24 and 28.3,5 both speak of bovine sacrifices to Indra. And yet, both can be dismissed right off the bat on similar grounds as before, because the bovine in question is _vṛṣabh_ or bull, and not cow. While it’s the same animal and bulls are even held sacred as Shiva’s mount, it’s not on the same level as cows. The cow is not just a deity but a divine mother as well, a status not enjoyed by her male counterpart. In the same maṇḍala, in 10.86.14,6 Indra brags to his wife about his preference for fatty beef and speaks of devotees sacrificing fifteen-twenty bulls for him. Again, this too fails the gender criteria, for it says _ukṣa_ or ox, not cow.

And finally, we come to 10.85.13,7 the verse quoted the most in defense of Vedic sanction for beef. It describes a wedding ceremony, a procession to be precise, where the bride is none other than _Sūryā_ , the daughter of _Savitā_ or Sun, and the groom is _Sо̄ma_ , the Moon. This hymn is the first time the Moon is identified as Sо̄ma, otherwise a mystical herbal concoction used in Vedic rituals. The wedding procession starts in the asterism of _Aghā_ or _Maghā_ with a send-off by Savitar and arrives at the groom’s place in the two _Arjunī_ asterisms or _Phālgunīs_ — _Pūrva_ and _Uttara_. That’s quite some journey, for Maghā corresponds to _Māgha_ or November and Phālgunī to _Phālguna_ or February, almost a three-month span. Most importantly, the procession starts with a ceremonial slaughter of several cows. The reason this verse is so popular in pro-beef circles is that it leaves little to imagination or ambiguity. The animal is of the right species and the right gender. But there’s still a problem. Let’s take a look at the exact verse:

     _sūryāyā vahatuḥ prāgāt savitā yam avāsṛjat.
    aghāsu hanyante gāvo 'rjunyoḥ pary uhyate._
    “Sūryā’s wedding proceeded, when Savitar set it going.
    In the Aghā’s the cows are killed; in the two Arjunīs she is conveyed.” (trans. Jamison)

The whole idea of cows being slaughtered hinges on two words— _gāvo,_ and _hanyante_. Typically, gāvo is translated as two cows, the dual first-person nominative declension of _gauḥ_ , Vedic Sanskrit for cow. Similarly, hanyante is a present tense conjugation of the verb _han_ which means to kill. Put together, _hanyante gāvo_ would translate as “two cows are killed” making a compelling case for the celebratory consumption of beef. However, there’s an alternate translation too. Besides cow, gāvo also means sunlight. And besides kill, han also means to weaken. Taking these interpretations, the translation becomes “sunlight weakens” which, at first crack, might seem to make no sense but seen in the context of the wedding, makes perfect sense. That’s because it’s no ordinary event, it’s a celestial wedding. And the procession is no ordinary journey, but a celestial march that starts in winter and concludes in spring. And it’s during the departure, i.e. in Maghā, that gāvo or sunlight is weakened. To put it all together, the alternative interpretation seems to be that of a metaphor for changing seasons. The solar theme seems far more consistent with this reading than the cow theme. The whole “wedding” is likely an allegorical reference to the shift from one season to the next.

So, thus far, we have no unassailable evidence of cow slaughter in Rigveda. We have buffalos, we have oxen, we have bulls, but we have no cows. Now let’s look at some that seem to protect cows from slaughter instead. There are seven such verses scattered across five maṇḍalas _._ But before we get to those, let’s understand some background of humanity’s relationship with cattle. It’s important to note that Aryans were pastoral people and like all pastoral peoples, relied heavily on livestock. In fact, some of the earliest acts of violence among our Yamnaya forebears were cattle raids. Even on the Indian subcontinent, cattle raids by the aborigines and by rival tribes were a recurring theme. So much so that wars even in general came to be called cow-derived names like _goṣu_ , _gavyu_ , _gaveṣaṇa_ , and _gaviṣṭi_.8 If cattle were precious in the Steppe, they became indispensable in India, for there wasn’t an abundance of horses to fall back on. Nutrition could only come from cows. To this point, there’s complete agreement across the board. The disagreement, however, is on whether this nutrition included beef. To Hindu ideologues, it was confined to dairy, to others it included meat. What makes cows special is the dairy bit. But what about other bovines, such as buffalo?

Well, the Aryans only encountered buffalos, more specifically water buffalos, once in India as these animals do not do well in colder climes. In fact, the only water buffalo native to Europe was _Bubalus murrensis_ and it went extinct more than 10,000 years ago. In the Indian subcontinent, on the other hand, the animal has enjoyed a close relationship with humans going back to the earliest years of the Indus Civilization. To the Aryans, it was never sacred and rather came to be associated with evil as evident in the legend of _Mahiṣāsura_. To the Harappans, however, it was both important and sacred. They even had a deified version in the form of the famous “Buffalo King.” Buffalo sacrifices to the “Mother Goddess” were a common practice in Harappan societies. It’s this practice that the Aryans adopted and water buffalos became a prestige offering to Indra. Gradually over time, though, the tradition waned as they moved away from blood offerings. Indra enjoyed beef. But of water buffalos, not cows. Even as an offering to Indra, buffalos start fading out of sacerdotal prominence after the eighth maṇḍala as the spotlight increasingly shifts to cows.

With that background, we should be well-equipped to examine the seven verses that seem to protect cows. Chronologically, the first apparent proscription against cow slaughter comes from 7.56.17.9 The hymn is dedicated to the Maruts, a collection of 27-60 storm deities often described as accompanying Indra serving as his troops in celestial battles. The verse in question petitions the Maruts to protect society against weapons that kill men and cows. While this seems to be a strong enough argument against cow slaughter, complete with divine invocation, it still falls short of being conclusive. There’s no way to establish that protection is being sought because cows are holy and not mere precious possessions. As we’ve noted before, cows have always been the subject of fierce battles and raids across the prehistoric world, even in societies that did not expressly prohibit the consumption of its meat, least of all for religious reasons. It’s natural for a worshipper to seek divine protection for his assets without the assets themselves being “divine.”

Moving on, we arrive at 5.83.810 where Indra, in the form of Parjanya, is requested to inundate the world with ghee and to bring about an abundance of pure water for the “prized cows.” Again, often posited by beef denouncers as evidence of cow worship in Rigveda, the verse says nothing more than the animal being a prized possession—the most prized, even, but certainly not an object of reverence. An argument against slaughter, nevertheless, is seen in the usage of the term _aghnyā_ , which literally translates into one that cannot or should not or is not slain, for the animal. While the translation is unambiguous, is it really the absolute that we’ve been looking for? We’ll return to this question a little later. For now, let’s visit the first maṇḍala for three verses relevant to this conversation.

First is 1.1.4.11 It does not explicitly mention cows, or any animal for that matter, but it does make an important point in the opinion of at least some Hindu ideologues—the nonviolent nature of Vedic sacrifice.

     _agne yaṃ yajñam adhvaraṃ viśvataḥ paribhūr asi.
    sa id deveṣu gacchati._
    “O Agni, the sacrifice and rite that you surround on every side—
    It alone goes among the gods.” (trans. Jamison)

The expression _yajñam adhvaraṃ_ here translates into sacrificial rite. The root of significance here is _adhvara_ which translates into sacrifice but is often interpreted as nonviolent instead in Hindutva circles. This is noteworthy. If sacrifices were meant to be nonviolent, no animal could be killed, much less cows. But there are several problems with this position the most obvious one being the irrefutable presence of buffalos and bulls as sacrificial victims throughout Rigveda. Another, less obvious, problem is the presence of _yūpas_ or sacrificial stakes in both Vedic, an example being RV 1.162.6,12 and post-Vedic works such as Ramayana 14.21–27.13 A nonviolent sacrifice has no need for such contraptions. The “nonviolent” interpretation comes not from Rigveda itself but from a commentary thereof by Sāyaṇa called Rigveda Bhashya. And he lived only a little over 600 years ago, which makes it a rather modern reading far removed from the original text it attempts to interpret.

The other two verses are 164.2714 and 164.40.15 The hymn is a collective ode to an assortment of deities including wind, fire, and Sun. The two verses in question, both pertain to cows, the focus being on milk. They wish for milch cows to prosper in an abundance of grass and pure water so they could, in turn, offer an abundance of milk to humans. While this underscores the animal’s economic significance, it does little to establish its divinity. Having said that, the name used for it in both instances is, again, aghnyā or the unslayable. Without this word, these verses are of little importance to this discussion.

The only time cow is accorded any semblance of divinity is in the eighth maṇḍala. Two verses are of import here, 100.11 and 101.15. We’ll visit the latter first. This is the only verse that expressly proscribes cow slaughter.16 Here, the animal is identified as the mother of a class of Vedic deities called Rudras. Also as a sister of another class called Adityas, it’s referred to as Aditi. In no nebulous terms, this verse enjoins the observant to refrain from killing “the blameless cow.” The descriptor used for the animal here is _anāgām_ or innocent. The other verse, 100.11 is a tad more interesting. It refers to the cow as _Vāk_ , the goddess of speech, and recognizes it as the source of _mandreṣam ūrjaṃ_ for men.17 These two words hold the key. Some like Jamison translate the expression as “refreshment and nourishment,” whereas others like Horace Wilson go with “meat and drink.”18 The confusion stems from the fact that the root _ūrj_ translates into both vigor and food.19 Now, food can mean both solid or liquid, including milk, but the root _iṣ_ that forms mandreṣam, also translates into something similar—refreshment or drink.20 If there are two food-related words and one of them is already specific to liquids, the other also referring to the same thing seems highly unlikely. Which leaves us with the only form of solid food one could derive from cows—beef. Long story short, 8.100.11 seems to identify cow as the goddess of speech as well as a giver of milk and meat. Given the paradoxical nature of this verse, it’s best we left it out of the beef debate and not use it as evidence for either position.

Another prominent verse often advanced as one of the irrefutable proofs of the Holy Cow’s infallibility is 10.87.16.21 The rather macabre hymn is an ode to Agni as the “demon smiter” where the deity in question lists out all the sins he punishes and how. Among them is cow slaughter which is assigned “severe punishment.” A closer examination, however, reveals a slightly different picture.

     _yaḥ pauruṣeyeṇa kraviṣā samaṅkte yo aśvyena paśunā yātudhānaḥ.
    yo aghnyāyā bharati kṣīram agne teṣāṃ śīrṣāṇi harasāpi vṛśca._
    “The sorcerer who anoints himself with the bloody flesh of men,
    Who (does so) with horseflesh,
    who carries off the milk of the fertile cow—
    Chop off their heads with your blaze.” (trans. Jamison)

The object of this verse is _yātudhānaḥ_ , Vedic Sanskrit for sorcerer or demon. Turns out, what the verse actually condemns is the killing and consumption of men and horses, not cows. Of cows, only theft of its milk is prohibited. That’s how Wilson reads it too.22 At least this verse does not have any opinion on cow slaughter even though it uses the word aghnyā for the animal.

In maṇḍala 7, there’s 56.17 which calls upon the Maruts to keep the society safe against weapons that smite men and cows.23 While not an explicit ban on cow slaughter, the act is certainly frowned upon here. Again, the question is, could this imply the animal’s economic value rather than spiritual? It’s not easy to argue in favor of the latter especially given that the word used for cow here is _gohā_ which translates into not just cow but cattle in general.24

Beef denouncers also posit 6.28 to add further teeth to their position. The entire hymn is dedicated to revering the cow and its virtues. In a text that contains similar hymns singing praises to deities like Agni and Maruts, it’s pertinant that one is dedicated to the Holy Cow as well. This can be a compelling argument in favor of the animal’s mystical appeal and special status, even if not full-blown divinity. And it should naturally follow that killing such an animal for food wouldn’t be a very welcome idea in such a society. However, the same Rigveda also has not one but two _Aśvastutis_ or horse hymns—1.162 and 1.163.25 And yet, horses aren’t infallible even in the Vedas.

And finally, we arrive at the one verse that again pertains to cows and sacrifice. But before we start, a little background into the Aryan’s relationship with the bovine is in order. One must note that the Aryans were a pastoral people. In fact, some of the earliest acts of violence among our Yamnaya forebears were cattle raids. Even on the Indian subcontinent, cattle raids by the aborigines and by rival tribes were a recurring theme. If cattle were precious in the Steppe, they became indispensable in India, for there wasn’t an abundance of horses to fall back on. Nutrition could only come from cows. Was this nutrition in the form of beef? The focus of Rigveda seems to be on milk. Much of the text sees the animal as a divine source of milk, rather than a primary source of meat.

But while cows enjoy the sanctity of religion, other bovines don’t. Case in point, buffalo. These were often associated with evil, as is readily evident in the legend of _Mahiṣāsura_ , the buffalo demon. The Aryans, it’s said, only encountered buffalos, more specifically water buffalos, on the Indian subcontinent. Here, the Indus Civilization had placed a great deal of sanctity in the animal and even had a deified version in the form of the “Buffalo King.” Buffalo sacrifices to the “Mother Goddess” were also a common practice in Harappan societies. It’s this practice that the Aryans adopted and water buffalos became a prestige offering to Indra. That’s why some of the most voracious episodes of Indra’s bovine consumption—6.17.11, 5.29.7, and 8.12.8—involve buffalos, not cows. Over time, though, the tradition waned as Aryans movedaway from blood offerings in general and buffalos in particular.

We briefly touched upon 6.28, the cow hymn, before which depicts Indra as a divine cowherd and carries forward from 6.27.8 which speaks of cows given away as _dakṣiṇā_ or priestly gift by the pious.26 Worth noting here is 28.4 where cows donated as priestly gifts are said to be protected by Indra himself against the slaughterhouse.27 One way to interpret this would be that cows were held as precious enough to keep alive but not necessarily sacred enough to warrant a blanket stricture against slaughter unless owned by a priest. But that would only be an interpretation, albeit not an unreasonable one, for the text doesn’t explicitly say so. Buffalos start fading out of sacerdotal prominence after the eighth maṇḍala as the spotlight increasingly shifts to cows. With that background out of the way, let’s now return to the verse we dropped the pin on earlier.

Back in the eighth maṇḍala, lies 8.43.11, where the one doing the devouring is Agni instead of Indra. And besides oxen, this time it’s also barren cows.28 Quite a peculiar diet, one would note, and a rather unambiguous indicator of beef consumption. As expected, this verse also finds itself at the center of much controversy around its interpretation.

     _ukṣānnāya vaśānnāya somapṛṣṭhāya vedhase.
    stomair vidhemāgnaye._
    “To him whose food is oxen, whose food is barren cows,
    To the ritual adept with soma on his back.
    To Agni we would do honor with praises.” (trans. Jamison)

The crux of the debate this time is the word _vaśānnāya_ , which forms as a portmanteau of _vaśā_ and _anna_. While the latter refers to foodgrain, typically boiled rice, it’s the former that comes with two Rigvedic translations depending on how it’s spelled. If spelled vaśā, it means barren cow,29 although the meaning acquires a more generic form in later literature30; but if spelled _vaśa_ , it means marrow or lard.31 Obviously, there’s no way to tell which one’s meant because vaśānnāya can be broken both ways. However, a closer examination of Monier-Williams’ dictionary reveals that vaśa is actually a secondary spelling of _vasa_. In other words, Vedic Sanskrit for marrow is primarily spelled with an _/s/_ sound, without the retroflex. If that’s the case here, marrow is ruled out. While Jamison goes with barren cow, Wilson still sticks to marrow.32 The retroflex tilts the balance in favor of Jamison.

So far, we have exactly one verse that explicitly forbids the slaughter of cow, and exactly one that explicitly names it food. The former refers to cows in general, while the latter to barren. But none of the verses outside of one that calls it the goddess of Vāk, assigns any divinity to the animal. There’s no reference to the cow as _mātā_ or mother to humans. No ritual around cow worship has been detailed in any of the ten maṇḍalas. There’s an enormous difference between being prized as wealth and being revered as sacred. For the former, we have ample evidence; for the latter, not much.

While cow sacrifice isn’t common in Rigveda, abundant references to _gor tvaci_ or cowhide exists throughout the collection. Almost all references are in the context of _sо̄ma_ , the divine concoction of Vedic priests. The brew is described as a likely greenish pulp that was first placed on a stretch of cowhide, washed with water, and then crushed with stones to produce a reddish-brown liquid for further filtration.33 Some of the more explicit references include 1.28.9,34 9.65.25,35 9.66.29,36 9.101.11,37 9.101.16,38 10.94.9,39 and 10.116.4.40 As evident, almost all of these occur in later maṇḍalas indicating a possible but not certain shift in sentiments.

What’s notable, to reiterate, is that the verse manifestly speaking of cow as food specifically speaks of barren cows. This only serves to add weightage to the notion that cows were only valued as infallible so long as they were fertile and could be milked. Which brings us to aghnyā, a word of Vedic Sanskrit that warrants special attention.

## Aghnyā

As already noted, the literal translation is unslayable. Therefore, its use for cows should be a mighty unslayable indication of its status. And Rigveda is packed with it. But before we rush to draw conclusions, there’s a nuance that’s easy to miss but crucial to the discussion. Most mentions of the word are in reference to milch cows, or in the context of cows as a source of milk.

Among the first instances of aghnyā in use is 7.68.841 where the word is only used for fertile, milk-bearing cows. The same verse refers to barren cows as _starī_42 instead. If the same verse uses two separate words for two different cows where the only difference between the two is their capacity to bear milk, the capacity becomes naturally consequential. The very next verse again reinforces aghnyā as the cow that nourishes with her milk.43 Maṇḍala 10 has two similar illustrations in 60.1144 and 87.16 where aghnyā is applied to only milk-bearing cows and not others. The latter, we’ve already discussed where Agni is said to behead anyone who steals a cow’s milk. On similar lines, we’ve also noted before, 1.164.27 where the inviolable cow or aghnyā nourishes Aśvins with her milk. In 4.1.6, cows are referenced twice—first as a source of warm ghee for gods and the second as a gift animal to men.45 One of the words used is _dhenu_ , the other is aghanyā. 8.69.2 does the same and uses aghnyā as an appositive for dhenu or gift cows.46 9.1.9 invokes aghnyā as cows that give milk for the making of soma.47 In the same maṇḍala, 93.9 speaks of the aghnyā in reference to her _ūdhar_ or udder swollen with milk.

There are also verses where the aghnyā’s fertility is implied rather than explicitly stated. For instance, in 10.102.7, Indra helps a bull who is referred to as _patim aghnyānā_ or the inviolable cow’s husband.48 The implication is that by virtue of being coupled with a sexual partner, the cow in question is fertile and a potential source of milk. Similarly, 5.83.8 mentions aghnyā in conjunction with ghee, the implication being indirect but unambiguous. Another verse, 8.75.8 also applies the word to a cow in conjunction with her calf.49

Another observation pertinent to this discussion is that while there are several instances of aghnayā being used for fertile or milch cows, there’s not one instance in all of Rigveda where it’s been used for barren or non-milch cows. The distinction between productive and non-productive cows seems strong and deliberate, which affirms the position that only the economically viable was inviolable or aghnyā. A very strong indicator of this is 1.37.5 where the word is used in its masculine form _aghnya_ (without the macron) and is applied to bulls. Here, even bulls are inviolable. But we’ve already seen Indra happily consume those as food in 10.27.2 and 10.28.3. The usage of aghnya for specific bulls could be indicative of their economic value as draught animals in such contexts. Thus we see, aghnya and aghnyā are not blanket terms for all members of the species. Some bulls mustn’t be slayed, but others could. And in the same way, some cows mustn’t be slayed, while others could.

# Later Vedas and Brahmanas

Perhaps the most glaring illustration of animal slaughter as integral to the Indic tradition comes from the concept of _anustaraṇ_ , the ritual slaughter of animals at funerals. The sacrificed animal was subsequently believed to accompany the dead in the afterlife for nourishment and protection. This isn’t a uniquely Aryan practice either. A number of completely unrelated civilizations are recorded to have slaughtered various animals, sometimes even humans, to accompany the dead. Many Steppe burials have been found with slaughtered horse remains. Egyptians, similarly, buried camels and maidens to accompany and serve their pharaohs in the afterlife.

So there are at least two references to the practice of funerary slaughters in Late Vedic works, one being Śatapatha Brahmana, a commentary on the Śukla Yajurveda by sage Yajnavalkya. The work has been variously dated to between 800 and 500 BC. Here, 13.8.4.6 describes the sacrifice of an _anaḍuh_ or bull50 as part of funerary rituals. The animal’s tail is placed in the left hand of the deceased before the pyre is lit.51 The idea is that the animal would then convey the dead to the land of the “Fathers” and return to the mortal world in a metaphysical form. Now whether the ritual is merely symbolic or it’s actually carried out remains a subject of much contention. The text in question does not indicate the former. Prussian Indologist Friedrich Albrecht Weber draws a parallel between this practice and that of the Ancient Greek religion where the world of the dead is on the other side of the Styx River which the dead must cross. The river analogous to Styx in Vedic mythology is Vaitaraṇa. To cross the Styx, one must pay Charon, the ferryman. Similarly, to cross the Vaitaraṇa, one must donate a cow to a Brahmin. This is why cows are donated at Hindu funerals even today and the dead is said to cross the River holding on to a cow’s tail in the left hand. Which would make the whole sacrifice metaphysical rather than literal. However, the latter interpretation only comes from Garuda Purana, a text from a thousand years after the Vedas. Although the verse in question does not explicitly use the word anustaraṇ, Weber sees it as implicit in the description of the ritual itself.52

We shall return to Śatapatha Brahmana for one more verse but before we do, let’s touch upon some other texts of the time. A rather prominent reference to funerary slaughter is in a Vedanga Gṛhya Sūtra composition. There are several Gṛhya Sūtras but the one we’re concerned with is by sage Aśvalāyana. The last of the work’s four adhyāyas contains a verse that explicitly mentions anustaraṇ. In Herman Oldenberg’s translation, it’s 4.3.2053 but there seems to be some discrepancy in the exact verse number, for the original Sanskrit gives 4.3.19 in one version54 and 4.3.15 in another.55 Be that as it may, the section goes into extensive details on the rites before the lighting of the pyre. These rites include the extraction of various organs from the slaughtered animal, most notably the kidneys, and their placement on the corpse being thus disposed of. While anustaraṇ itself is a masculine noun, it’s not unlikely that the sacrificial animal was actually an _anustaraṇī_ or cow.56 That’s because the animal that accompanies the dead across the Vaitaraṇa, according to the thirteenth-century work _Caturvargacintāmaṇi_ , is a cow and not a bull.57

The concept of anustaraṇī is also alluded to in the Atharva Veda, the last of the four cardinal Hindu texts. The hymn in question speaks to a feminine character being sent away with her deceased _gopati_ or lord to grant him happiness and love in the afterlife. The female character’s identity hinges on how we translate the word gopati. The literal translation, “cow lord” would make the female character cow. But a less literal translation as “lord of the household” also exists which would make the character a woman, the widow of the deceased. American philologist and scholar of Sanskrit William Dwight Whitney prefers the cow interpretation presumably because it’s highly unlikely they practiced _sati_ 2,500 years ago. He, thus, translates gopati as a herdsman and sees the ritual in 18.3.4 as an anustaraṇī sacrifice.58

Now, if we stick to Atharva Veda a little longer, we also find a clear proscription against beef in 5.18, a hymn dedicated to the Brahmana’s cow. While this may at first glance seem to be a stricture against cow slaughter and consumption,59 the specificity of Brahmana’s cow is crucial. It’s only such cows that mustn’t be eaten, which leaves the option open for the rest, i.e. cows not owned by a Brahmana. Since the one being addressed is a king, it’s implicit that the latter is a habitual consumer of cow flesh. The same stricture is reinforced in 5.19.4 where the consumption of a Brahmana’s cow is said to smite the lights out of the king’s domain.60 The notion that cows not belonging to a Brahmana are free for consumption finds support in 7.5.5 where a sacrifice is described as involving dogs and cows.61

Coming back to the Brahmana corpus, we also find a reference of interest in Taittiriya Brahmana, a commentary on Krishna Yajurveda. The reference occurs in 3.9.8 where a cow is reported to be among the animals sacrificed by the priest or _adhvaryu_ at an _aśvamedha_ event.62 It even doubles down further by readily admitting meat from such a cow as food— _annam vai gauḥ_.63

Another Brahmana that seemingly endorses cow slaughter for consumption is Aitareya Brahmana where 1.15 hints at a peculiar rite around the reception of a distinguished guest, likely a royal, in reference to the worship of king _Soma_. When the deity arrives, it says, a small fire is lit and thrown into the larger firepit symbolizing the sacrifice of Agnī to the deity. This is because _Agnī_ is considered, in this context, the animal of Soma. To illustrate the ritual, the section refers to the practice of slaughtering an ox or a sterile cow to honor a royal guest.64 Among the later Brahmana compositions is Gopatha Brahmana which offers an insightful commentary on the Atharva Veda and elaborates on several rituals therein. In 1.3.18, it lays down the rules for distributing portions of beef among the priests after a ritualistic sacrifice.65 A total of thirty-six such portions are prescribed. The animal indicated is specifically gauḥ or cow.66 And now we should return to the promised verse in Śatapatha Brahmana.

In its third kāṇda, the Brahmana elaborates on the various rituals and stipulations pertaining to a special fire sacrifice called Agniṣṭoma. Animal sacrifice is also part of the fare. And yet again, in 1.2.21, the officiating priest is forbidden from consuming one usual suspect, the milch cow or dhenu. While the obvious implication is that other animals are available for consumption, it’s the stricture against cows that matter to this discussion. Clearly, some status is attached to this animal, which is further regarded as a giver of support to all lifeforms on Earth. The verse also warns of punitive rebirth to anyone partaking in cow beef. Before we leave the verse, however, a most peculiar twist comes in the last line where sage Yājñavalkya is quoted thus67:

     _taduhovāca yājñavalkyo 'śnāmyevāha maṃsalaṃ cedbhavatīti._
    “Nevertheless Yajnavalkya said:
    ‘I, for one, eat it, provided that it is tender.’” (trans. Eggeling)

The conflict within the verse is indicative of a conflict within society. Cow beef was probably going out of fashion with some influential men still refusing to relent. Remember, these were the early days of Aryan presence in the East. One can only speculate on the origins of this conflict. Yājñavalkya was an extremely prominent figure in that region, prominent enough to get quoted in a work of this stature. While his quote does not specify exactly what he’s insistent on consuming, the context makes it abundantly clear that it’s nothing but beef and not any beef but that of cows. By the way, Yājñavalkya did not stop there. He also elaborated his weakness for beef in his own work, Brihadaranyaka Upanishad. Here, in 6.4.18, a woman hoping for a boy child is advised to have rice with ghee and her husband, rice with beef. The animal this time, however, is ukṣa, or ox, and not cow.68

Even in the post-Vedic world, beef seems to have retained its spot in the sacerdotal diet. Āpastamba Dharmasūtra, the oldest of three such treatises, dates back to the fifth century BC, give or take a century. In it, 2.7.16.25 prescribes beef as part of the meal offering during the _śrādha_ , a ceremony performed for the gratification of _pitṛ_ or the departed, and suggests that it would satisfy the pitṛ for a whole year.69 Here, beef is explicitly cow’s flesh and not that of bull, ox, or buffalo, for the expression used is _gavyen māṃsa_.70 The very next verse, however, suggests a better alternative in _mahiṣen māṃsa_ or buffalo meat. Clearly, cow beef isn’t taboo yet.

Speaking of post-Vedic, there’s one most compelling argument against the widespread prevalence of cow slaughter that doesn’t get discussed as often as the Vedas and other scriptural texts. It’s Kautilya’s Arthashastra. The seminal treatise, while mostly on economics, is also one of the earliest law codes of the Indian subcontinent. It was meant to serve as a guiding principle for the nascent Mauryan regime. Among the codes is one titled “The Superintendent of Cows.” Naturally, cows are important enough to command an entire section in the Mauryan “constitution.” So much so, that one verse in the section prescribes nothing short of the death penalty for anyone who slaughters one or is an accessory to such a slaughter.71 An examination of the context, however, reveals that the discussion is of cows not owned by the killer. In which case, the killing would be akin to destroying someone’s most prized possession, likely a critical source of sustenance. The same verse mentions “cattle stolen by thieves” or “lost to the owner.72” The reason this makes sense instead of a blanket prohibition against all cows is Kautilya’s own inconsistency in the application of this stricture. If there’s a section on cows, there’s also one on slaughterhouses—“Authority of the Slaughterhouse.” Here, cows are also among the animals in question and Kautilya expressly bans calves and milch cows from slaughter and stipulates a fine of fifty silver coins or _paṇas_.73 While substantial, the penalty is vastly more lenient than death. And that makes sense only if the death penalty were reserved for very special cows, cows that were someone else’s property. In sum, fine for milch cows, even if own, but death for cows that are stolen. We once again, circle back to economics. Arthashastra is, after all, first and foremost about the economy.

Even as late as the second century AD, the prohibition against cow beef as food remains nebulous at best. The famous law code of Manu, known for its unforgiving sense of discipline, has an entire section—discourse 5—dedicated to food and makes all kinds of prohibitions.74 And yet it stops short of prohibiting beef. In the context of meat, all it forbids is flesh that isn’t “consecrated.” Interestingly, in the eighth discourse, verse 325 does penalize cow theft, the penalty being amputation of feet, it’s only cows that belong to a Brahmin and not all cows.75 Even more interestingly, 11.108 lists cow slaughter as a “minor offense” and stipulates a surprisingly mild punishment of shaving one’s hair, living in a cowshed, wearing the dead cow’s hide, and consuming only barley, for one month.76 Harsh, yes, but certainly preferable to death?

## Buddhist Literature

Śramaṇa systems like Buddhism and Jainism are noted for their aversion to violence of all kinds, including cruelty against animals. Much of Buddhist discourse is dedicated to prohibiting animal sacrifice. A possible inference of this is that such violence must have been otherwise commonplace in society at large. Unlike Vedic scripture which is in Sanskrit, Buddhist scripture is entirely Pali, an Indo-Aryan liturgical language derived from a partial Sanskritization of several eastern vernaculars. Magahi of Bihar is understood to be a major descendant. Part of this corpus is Majjhima Nikāya which was composed over several centuries and consists of 152 _suttas_ or discourses. Of our interest is the tenth, titled Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta, which has been dated to the final decades before the common era.

Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta is a discourse on mindfulness and forms the basis of the _vipassanā_ regimen to this day. Naturally, dietary strictures are an integral part of the conversation. The part that matters to us informs the _bhikkhus_ or monks that the human body is a collection of several primordial elements. To illustrate the point, an example is given of a dismembered cow lying before its butcher. The word used for cow is the Pali equivalent of Vedic gauḥ, which refers to cows in general and not just the barren ones.77

     _Seyyathāpi, bhikkhave, dakkho goghātako vā goghātakantevāsī vā gāviṃ vadhitvā catumahāpathe bilaso vibhajitvā nisinno assa;_
    “Just as if, monks, a skilful cow-butcher or his apprentice,
    after having slaughtered a cow and having divided it into portions,
    would sit down at the junction of four roads” (trans. Bhikkhus Nanamoli and Bodhi)

Another hint at cow slaughter as a societal practice is in Khuddaka Nikāya’s Sutta Nipāta, an early corpus of Buddhist discourse dating back to pre-Ashokan times. In the third chapter of this work is a sutta or aphorism titled _Salla Sutta_ (“The Dart”) wherein verse 7 makes a reference to bovine slaughter.78

     _pekkhataṃ yeva ñātīnaṃ passa lālapataṃ puthu
    ekameko va maccānaṃ go vajjho viya niyyati._
    “Mark! While relatives are looking on and lamenting greatly,
    one by one of the mortals is carried off,
    like an ox that is going to be killed.” (trans. Fausböll)

Once again, the word used for the animal is _go_ which specifically refers to a female cow, although some scholars translate it as bull or ox as well.79 Anyway, the fact that bovine slaughter is used as an example again and again in multiple Buddhist texts clearly seems to hint at something. While we’d still like to believe cow slaughter was at least frowned upon, if not outright prohibited, why is it that these illustrations always involve cows and not, say, goats, buffalos, or sheep? Could it indicate that cow slaughter was more common than we’d think? We can only speculate. A most bizarre interpretation comes from the Hindi translation by Bhikshu Dharmarakshita where rather than a victim, the cow is the perpetrator. He draws a parallel between death and _prāṇiyo kā vadh karne vāli gau_ or cow that kills men.80

The same text, in the preceding section titled _Brāhmaṇadhammika Sutta_ (“The Tradition of the Brahmins”), offers an even more detailed illustration in verses 25 thru 29.81 This is a prohibitionary aphorism that first describes the king ordering the slaughter of “hundreds of cows” on the Brahmin’s recommendation, then deplores the practice while highlighting the animal’s nonviolent nature. In verse 27, not only gods but even the _asuras_ and _rakshasas_ come together to condemn the brutality. These suttas hint at a growing disdain for animal cruelty in general and cow slaughter in particular in śramaṇa circles, toward the second half of the first millennium BC. Which also means that the practice was otherwise commonplace, or at least commonplace enough to warrant a concerted movement against it.

## Conclusion

Mankind’s relationship with cows is a complex but easily explicable one. As the primary and preferred source of dairy, cows have always enjoyed a status not enjoyed by other animals or even fellow bovines. A living cow is far more productive than a dead one. As draught animals, the same applies to bulls and oxen. The Egyptians deified the cow as Hesat who was said to suckle the pharaohs. Another cow deity, Hathor, is the mother to Horus and Ra. Ancient Sumerians worshipped the animal as Ninsun, the divine Lady Wild Cow and city goddess of Kullab. Similarly from Celtic Damona to Norse Auðumbla, cow reverence has been a constant theme across all ancient societies. And yet, consumption of cow beef has evidently remained non-taboo in almost all of them. This includes India where with the exception of those capable of producing milk, cows were quite edible even if not more so than other livestock.

As for milch cows and their draught counterparts, even societies that traditionally never worshipped cows as deities frowned upon their slaughter. The motivations have always been more economical than spiritual. A good example would be Han China which prohibited cow and bull slaughter purely on economic grounds. In India, the discomfort against the killing of cows only gained prominence with the emergence of śramaṇa traditions in the lower Gangetic plains. Until that point, economically unproductive cows could be and _were_ killed without much opposition. Sure, the cow was deified at times and held in high esteem, but so was the bull. And horse. And yet, ample evidence of both being slayed exists in both Vedic and post-Vedic texts.

Over time, the discomfort grew, probably under eastern influences, and by the time of the Guptas—who, by the way, also rose in the east—acquired a more religious character. At what point the taboo crystallized into a universal Hindu element beyond questioning, is hard to say, but it was certainly not earlier than the second century AD which is when Manusmriti is understood to have appeared. Having said that, _ahiṃsā_ or nonviolence has been a part of the Hindu ethos right since Vedic times which means, meat in general was frowned upon regardless of its source. Frowned upon but not exactly taboo, as we’ve already noted. It wouldn’t have been very different from today as in some ate meat, beef even, while others didn’t.

Before we end the discussion, here’s an interesting etymological cow trivia. Long before there were Aryans, there were Yamnaya. These people lived in the vast expanse of the Pontic Steppe and lived off cattle and horses. Cow’s milk was a major source of nutrition to them, besides of course, meat. Even in a society that primitive, there was a clear division of labor along gender lines not very different from today. While men went about thwarting cattle raids and herding livestock, women managed the household. Part of the latter’s tasks was milking the cows. In their language, the word for milking was the same as the word for producing, i.e. _dheugh_.82 Later, this word evolved into Sanskrit words _duhé_ (to milk) and _dugdhe_ (milk). Since the job entirely belonged to the girl of the household, she came to be called _dhug(h)əter_.83 It is from this that we get _duhitṛ_ in Sanskrit,84 _doxtar_ in Persian, and daughter in English. Long story short, daughters are, etymologically speaking, milkmaids.
