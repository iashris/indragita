Contrary to what the title seems to imply, this article isn’t about gods in general, for there are too many for one article or even one book. There are as many gods as there are civilizations and most of them share little overlap with their counterparts, other than being god, of course. This is about one specific subset of that unwieldy assortment—the Abrahamic gods. This is the story of the Christian God, the Jewish Yahweh, and the Islamic Allah, but more importantly, this is the story of monotheism itself. Humanity’s primordial notion of divinity has always tended toward polytheism, every civilization started off as cults of nature worshippers. A most natural question then is, how did such a system give way to monotheism? How did the myriad gods of elements succumb to the “One True God”? In other words, the story of God is the story of civilization’s journey from many gods to one, from city cults to organized theology, and from gods as proxies for the elements to gods as agents of conquest.

## Mesopotamia

More than five thousand years ago when civilization—one of several, actually—emerged in Sumer, there was only water, an infinite ocean called _Engur_ , and it was ruled by the primeval Ocean god. They called her _Nammu_ and built her temples all over the place. Her cult center was in Eridu, likely the first Sumerian city. Nammu birthed two kids _An_ (sky) and _Ki_ (Earth) who subsequently mated to conceive _Enlil_ , the wind god.1 Of the two, we’re only concerned with An, the god of the sky the king of gods—a literal ‘Sky Daddy.’ This is the Sumerian cult’s charter myth or origin story which has come to us from the famous Epic of Gilgamesh or, as he was called in Sumerian, Bilgamesh.2 Over time, the cult added several new members to its pantheon— _Nanna_ (Moon), _Utu_ (Sun), _Enki_ (water), _Inanna_ (sex), _Ninurta_ (war), and _Ereshkigal_ (Underworld), to name a few. By the Neo-Sumerian or Ur III period, it’s understood that there were no fewer than 3,600 different Sumerian deities.3

Around 4,300 years ago, a man called Sargon rose in Akkad a small city to the north, and quickly conquered Sumer and its neighbors. His is known as recorded history’s first empire and it spanned all of Mesopotamia, about ten thousand square miles. In another hundred years or so, however, it’d grow to almost ten times as big. With the Akkadians came a new language and An became _Anu_.4 Akkadian is one of the earliest Semitic tongues so far known. Now, either these new immigrants assimilated and adopted the existing Sumerian cult, or brought their own, but the pantheon changed. To be more realistic, it was probably a bit of both—a merger of cults not unlike what happened after the Roman conquest of Greece in the second century BC.

Despite his primeval status, though, Anu wasn’t worshipped exclusively. At least not in the Ur III period, that is twenty-first century BC. His role was mostly seen as passive.5 Think Brahma who is also rarely worshipped and has few temples despite his primeval status in the Hindu pantheon. By the time of Sargon’s empire, the pantheon had further burgeoned with a number of new gods, each with a distinct profile. Every city had its own guardian deity, as was the case during the Sumerians. Right next door outside the Empire’s northern fringes was a more or less autonomous city-state called Ugarit, a Mediterranean port near Latakia in modern Syria. The Ugaritic tongue shared the same Semitic family as Akkadian. Akkadian was East Semitic and Ugaritic, Northwest. Akkadian influence on Ugarit transcended the two entities’ separation of power. They almost shared a pantheon too, even if with different names, much like the Greeks and the Romans would later. In Ugarit, the sky deity was named _ʾEl_ and he was also primeval. The role on the Akkadian side, as we’ve already seen, was played by Anu. Here too, it seems, ʾEl was never worshipped. No temple to this deity has ever been found.6 This could be because, in a hundred years or so, Ugarit ended up in the Akkadian sphere of influence anyway, so a subsequent merger of pantheons cannot be ruled out. This overlap wasn’t limited to areas under Akkadian rule but also extended to other neighbors in the North.

These northern neighbors were the Hittites, a people very different from all others in the region. While the Akkadians and all others in the vicinity were Semitic, the Hittites were Indo-Europeans. They and the Mitanni were the only two of this stock. The latter inhabited southeast Anatolia and northern Syria and spoke Hurrian, a language isolate. The former, on the other hand, inhabited a small region in central Anatolia and spoke an Indo-European language, the only people to do so in the region. The Hittites too had a primeval sky daddy— _Elkunirša_. The _El-_ prefix is most likely indicative of its Ugaritic origins.7 Be that as it may, ʾEl has an interesting story that we learn of from a text called _Shachar and Shalim_ from tablet CTA 23 discovered in 1930.8

So the sky god ʾEl, goes the story, once went to the beach where he saw two women bathing and the sight turned him on. Driven by libido, ʾEl took both the women and offered them a strange choice—they could have him however they wanted, as a father or as a husband. The women elected to go with the latter. Anyway, once picked as husband, ʾEl proceeded to mate with them birthing two children, _Shachar_ or Dawn, and _Shalim_ or Dusk.9 One of the two wives was the goddess of fertility, _ʾAṯirat_ ,10 her Hittite counterpart being _Ašertu_ , later anglicized to _Asherah_. The etymological parallel between ʾAṯirat and Ašertu is just one of several indications of mutual Hittite-Ugaritic influences, which is why it isn’t unlikely for Elkunirša to be a Hittite adaptation of ʾEl.

Once part of the Akkadian realm, Ugarit’s ʾAṯirat evolved into _ʾElat_ as the wife of ʾEl. In other words, ʾElat became common to both pantheons. Meanwhile, these cults also came to be adopted by the nomadic tribes inhabiting the Arabian desert to the south. And by the Canaanites. In Southern Arabia—Yemen, actually—the most important deity those days was _ʿAmm_ , the Moon god.11 This was in sharp contrast to most other civilizations in both Old and New Worlds (the Mesoamericans, the Indo-Europeans, the Altaics, the Egyptians) where the Sun reigned supreme. ʿAmm incidentally derives from a proto-Arabic term for paternal uncle.12 Reminiscent of India’s very own “ _Chandamama_.” And here, Ugarit’s ʾElat entered the pantheon as ʿAmm’s consort. The name too underwent a slight corruption and ʾElat became _ʾal-Lāt_. In other words, ʾEl’s consort in Mesopotamia became ʿAmm’s consort in Arabia. With this, ʾEl and ʿAmm became analogous. Put a pin on ʾal-Lāt, we’ll return to her later.

## The Babylonian Exile

Meanwhile, the Canaanites, nestled between Ugarit and Northern Arabia, along the Mediterranean coast, also worshipped many gods and their supreme deity was a storm god13 likely imported from Edom in Transjordan— _Yhwꜣw_.14 Polytheism remained the norm in Canaan and the rest of Near East until the Babylonian exile about 2,600 years ago. This exile is a landmark episode in Jewish history and finds prominent mention in the Old Testament. By the early Iron Age, the Canaanite country had already splintered into several city-states, Judah being one of them. This is also where Jerusalem was. Back in Mesopotamia, the mighty Akkadian Empire had long since disappeared and been succeeded by a series of short-lived entities until the Babylonians came out on top a second time as the Neo-Babylonian Empire in the last quarter of the seventh century BC. Toward the end of the century, in quest of territory, this empire locked horns with the Egyptians in what’s known as the Battle of Carchemish. The man leading the Babylonian side—a coalition of Babylonians, Persians, Scythians, and Medes—was the well-known Nebuchadnezzar II. As we know, he prevailed. The same year, he also succeeded his father Nabopolassar to the Babylonian throne. The important outcome of this battle was Judah—and with it, Jerusalem—falling into Babylonian hands.

Only a few years down the line, however, the Judeans stopped paying tributes to their Babylonian suzerains in a bid to assert their independence. This led to Nebuchadnezzar’s second Siege of Jerusalem. This is when the infamous exile took place. The city was utterly destroyed, its residents forcefully removed to Babylon. The exile is among the most significant events in the Judeo-Christian religious history.

Babylon, being a Mesopotamian city, worshipped ʾEl which over time influenced the Canaanite cult of its new Judean imports. Eventually, the Canaanites must have incorporated ʾEl as a supreme deity on par with Yhwꜣw and slowly the two would’ve merged. The Exile had one more impact on the Canaanite cult—Polytheism gave way to monolatry. Not monotheism, mind you. Monolatry is just like polytheism where you have many gods, but unlike polytheism, you only worship one, the supreme deity. In the case of Canaanites, this supreme deity was a combination of ʾEl and Yhwꜣw at first, but later just Yhwꜣw as an embodiment of both.

The exile lasted about half a century and finally came to an end in 539 BC when Cyrus led his newly founded Achaemenid Empire to a conclusive victory against the Babylonians at the Battle of Opis and liberated its Judean exiles. But the sojourn lasted long enough to reconfigure their religion. The decline of ʾEl in Cannan could be rooted in fresh Canaanite nationalism triggered by the exile which made them do away with all remnants of their captors’ cult. But one remnant remained— _ʾilhm_. The Canaanite exiles also used _ʾel_ as a common noun for gods. In that sense, they referred to the pantheon as ʾilhm, plural of ʾel. This reference remained in place.

## Monotheism

About half a millennium after the liberation came another change, the shift to absolute monotheism. This was effected by the Maccabean Revolt during Greek occupation. Now it was just one God, others didn’t even exist, much less command reverence. And that one God was Yhwꜣw, often anglicized as _Yahweh_. Even Asherah was discarded; until this point, she’d been venerated as Yahweh’s consort. Now Yahweh was single.

We can still find Asherah in the Hebrew Bible (2 Kings 23:14) where King Solomon is said to have built temples to her, along with many other deities. Remember the plural they retained? As the Canaanite tongue evolved into Hebrew, ʾilhm became _ʾĔlōhīm_ or _Elohim_. Later, Elohim (when used as singular) and Yahweh came to be used interchangeably. In Hebrew, there’s a verb called _sará_ , “to wrestle.” In the Old Testament, in Genesis 32:26–28, there’s a story that involves this word. It’s about Jacob and mentions a slugfest between him and a man who is actually Yahweh in disguise. When Jacob refuses to relent and demands to be blessed, Yahweh demands to know his name. Then proceeds to tell him that he now had a new name. Since he struggled with God Himself, Jacob’s new name was a combination of sará and ʾel, i.e. _Israel_. That’s how the name was born in legends. ʾEl, as we can thus see, has traveled all the way from Ugaritic inscriptions to the Hebrew Bible. But the journey is far from over yet.

Now we travel to Arabia where polytheism is still the mainstay. If you recall, the supreme deity here is also ʾEl, which with time, evolved into a word we’re more familiar with today—Allah.15 And this brings us to the pin we dropped earlier— ʾal-Lāt, the consort. There’s a great deal of confusion around her true relationship with Allah. While her Ugaritic forebear ʾElat/ ʾAṯirat was ʾEl’s consort, in Arabia she was ʿAmm’s.16 That’s the Moon god if you’d recall. Initially, it’s ʿAmm that reigned the Arabian cult. But when ʾEl entered the scene as Allah, things got confusing. Some tribes started worshipping ʾal-Lāt as Allah’s consort while others saw her as his daughter. In the Meccan tradition, for instance, she was the daughter. And she became the goddess of war and peace. In this form, she also came to have sisters— _Manāt_ and _ʾal-ʾUzzā_.

Of these, Manāt was the eldest and gradually became the most significant of the three as the goddess of fate and time. Syria’s Palmyra and Arabia’s Taʾif emerged as ʾal-Lāt’s cult centers. Not far from Taʾif, Mecca became Manāt’s while ʾal-ʾUzzā got a distant Petra as its tutelary goddess. The trinity was anyway worshipped together all over the Peninsula, along with Allah. Somewhere between the towns of Mecca and Medina lay a major pilgrim center for Manāt worshippers back in the day; a statue of Manāt also stood here. It was called al-Mushallal. In nearby Mecca stood a large structure that housed hundreds of figures including those of the three sisters. This was called al-Kaʿba or Kaaba. The word literally means cube. Manāt worshippers visiting Mecca also visited al-Mushallal as part of the pilgrimage.

In Mecca, they would circumambulate around the Kaaba chanting Manāt’s name along with those of her sisters seeking their, especially her, blessings. Part of this chant was: “These are the exalted _gharāniq_ , whose intercession is hoped for.” Remember this chant, for we shall revisit it in a bit. Before the circumambulation, they’d shave their head as a part of the ritual for both Manāt and Hubal, another Mesopotamian import.17 Does any of this sound familiar? Then they would travel to al-Mushallal to offer prayers at Manāt’s other, more personal temple.

## The Devil’s Whisper

Fast forward to the seventh century and we arrive at Muhammad. His mission was to pull Arabia out of _jāhilīyah_ or “Age of Ignorance” and bring forth, well, Islam. Part of the mission was his unremitting campaign against all idolatry. The sisters’ days were now numbered. Being the biggest pre-Islamic religious center, Mecca became the centerpiece of Muhammad’s campaign. At the time, the city was in control of his own tribe, Banu Quraysh. Ideally, Mecca shouldn’t have been hard to crack. It proved otherwise.

Qurʾān’s _surah_ (chapter) 53 is an assortment of sixty-two _āyāt_ or verses collectively named an-Najm. Out of these sixty-two, five are of interest to us here, 19 through 23. These verses record one of Muhammad’s visions regarding the three sisters and his visit to Mecca.

Simply put, Muhammad is telling Meccans about the vanity of worshipping the three sisters and asking them to worship only the one supreme deity, i.e. Allah. Think of this vision or “revelation” as God’s advice to Muhammad on how to convert Mecca’s pagans. And Muhammad complied. He arrived at the Kaaba to carry out the vision and started articulating the verses verbatim. But things went wrong after 53:20 (“and the third one, Manāt as well?”). Because precisely at this point Satan played a trick on him to prevent the conversion—he whispered into Muhammad’s ear.

In Gabriel’s voice:

“These are the exalted gharāniq, whose intercession is hoped for.”

Remember that chant? The Meccans read this as Muhammad’s endorsement of pagan practices and went back to worshipping the three sisters. This blunder is famously known as the Satanic Verses. Of course, Muhammad recanted and corrected himself as soon as he realized the trickery, but the incident did cause considerable embarrassment and eventually got deleted from all official Islamic narratives. Do note that even today not all scholiasts agree with the story of the Satanic Verses for obvious reasons.

## Legacy

So, while ʾEl lives on as Elohim in Judaism and Allah in Islam, his consort ʾElat is all but lost to history now. As are her two sisters. Even ʿAmm, the original Arabian Moon god endures as the crescent moon. The latter, in fact, is not even a direct pagan import. The crescent Moon was not even a universal Islamic icon until the Ottomans. It’s the Sassanians who first adopted it as a political icon and placed it on their flag. Later, when the Rashidun established their Caliphate and wrested Persia from them, the crescent entered their flag, again, as a political symbol. The crescent was also a political emblem for Carthage in honor of the Phoenician Moon goddess Tanith. When Carthage fell to Rome and became part of Byzantium, the crescent became the symbol of Constantinople. And when Constantinople fell to the Ottomans in the fifteenth century, so did the crescent. Only then did it evolve into the religious symbol of Islam as known today. Tanith, by the way, still lives on in the name of Tunisia.

And that was the brief and extremely reductive story of the Abrahamic God. The journey from polytheism to monolatry to monotheism is a journey of civilizational contingencies and political machinations.
