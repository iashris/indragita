The teachings of Buddhism, founded on principles of compassion, non-violence, and the pursuit of enlightenment, have captivated millions for centuries. A central tenet of Buddhist doctrine, as articulated by the Buddha himself, is the concept of equality among all beings. In the Pali Canon, the Buddha emphasizes that social status, birth, or caste hold no bearing on a person’s spiritual worth. Instead, it is one’s actions or _karma_ , and adherence to the Eightfold Path that dictate one’s progress toward enlightenment. This message stood in stark contrast to the hierarchical and exclusionary caste system of ancient India, where social divisions were enforced with a measure of rigidity. The Buddha’s rejection of caste as a measure of human value offered a radical alternative to the dominant Brahmanical ideology, opening the path of spiritual liberation to all individuals, irrespective of birth.

However, the historical reality of Buddhist practices betrays a far more complex relationship with the caste system than the Buddha’s teachings might suggest. Over the centuries, as Buddhism spread throughout South Asia and beyond, it interacted with deeply entrenched social structures, including the caste system, in ways that were not always consistent with its founding ideals. In ancient India, early Buddhist communities may have been more inclusive, admitting individuals from all social backgrounds into the monastic order. Figures like Upāli, a low-caste barber who became a prominent monk, reflect the egalitarian aspirations of the Buddhist Sangha.

Yet, as Buddhism evolved and became institutionalized, particularly under powerful rulers like Aśoka during the Mauryan Empire, its communities often came into contact with the dominant social norms of the time. Over the centuries, particularly as Brahmanical influence resurged and Buddhism itself began to decline in India, the monastic and lay communities started to mirror the social hierarchies they had once resisted. Even in regions outside India, such as Sri Lanka and Southeast Asia, caste-based discrimination and exclusionary practices crept into Buddhist institutions. Monastic hierarchies and lay interactions began to reflect caste divisions, despite the doctrinal insistence on equality.

This paradox—the coexistence of Buddhist egalitarian ideals with the reality of caste-based discrimination—continued into the modern era. While the revival of Buddhism in India, particularly under Ambedkar, sought to reclaim the religion’s egalitarian roots, contemporary Buddhist communities in South Asia often still grapple with lingering caste consciousness. The tension between doctrine and practice, ideal and reality, remains a significant aspect of the historical and sociological study of Buddhism.

We will explore the historical evidence of caste discrimination within Buddhist practices, tracing how and why this paradox arose and persisted, and examining the ways in which Buddhism has been both a vehicle for social equality and a participant in caste-based exclusion. Through this lens, the complex and often contradictory relationship between Buddhism and caste will be critically analyzed, offering insights into how the religion navigated—and continues to navigate—the deeply ingrained social hierarchies of the Indian subcontinent.

## Brahmins in the East

In Buddhist philosophy, the Buddha’s rejection of caste-based hierarchies is one of the most profound aspects of his teachings, particularly in the context of sixth-century BC India, where caste determined every aspect of one’s life. The Buddha, originally born a prince of the Kshatriya caste, witnessed firsthand the stratified social order that bound individuals to specific roles and duties based on their birth. His teachings challenged the very foundation of this rigid system, advocating for a spiritual path that transcended societal norms and focused instead on individual actions and moral conduct as the true determinants of one’s worth. That’s pretty much the standard Buddhist framework that comes to us from tradition. In order to understand the Buddha’s own relationship with caste, however, it’s key to understand his society’s relationship with the concept. More importantly, its relationship with the Brahmins, the villains of every caste discourse.

Buddhism, like its other Sramanic cousins, originated and evolved in the east, primarily in and around Magadha. These were also some of the last territories to be occupied by the Aryans. In fact, they arrived here in two major waves, it’s the first that gave rise to the various Sramanic traditions. The second, which came centuries later, was mostly Brahmins. The first was too but they had over time evolved into various non-Brahminical sects. Think of it as a British migrating to America today vis à vis an American whose British ancestor had migrated more than three centuries ago. The Brahmins who came in this second wave were aliens to a land full of non-Brahminical sects, even though both natives and immigrants were of the same Aryan descent. This is where the concept of “Brahmin villages” or _brāhmaṇagāma_ emerged. These were villages that were, as the term implies, almost exclusively inhabited by Brahmins. In Buddhist literature, the suttas, these residents are referred to as _brāhmaṇagahapatikā_ or “Brahmin householder.”

The suttas list a total of twelve such villages, four in Magadha and eight in neighboring Kosala. The villages in Kosala are Icchānaṅgala,1 Sālā,2 Ekasālā,3 Opāsāda,4 Veḷudvāra,5 Venāgapura,6 and Nagaravinda.7 In Magadha we have Khāṇumata,8 Ambasaṇḍā,9 Pañcasālā,10 and Ekanāḷa.11 This is during the time of Siddhartha Gautama a clear implication of which is that the integration of these second wave Brahmins with the largely Sramanic society of the east had not fully realized at least as of sixth century BC. Many of these villages were grants from the royalties of Kosala and Magadha. At first crack, what this establishes is that Brahmins mostly lived in segregated colonies in the earliest days of Buddhism. What could be the reason?

The _Dīgha Nikāya_ narrates the story of a Brahmin named Ambaṭṭha who traveled to Kapilvastu, the capital of the Śākya clan, to which Siddhartha Gautama belonged. In this account, Ambaṭṭha complains to Siddhartha that the Śākyas, who were Kshatriyas, had insulted and ridiculed him during his visit.12 This happened when the Buddha was touring the Kosalan Brahmin village of Icchānaṅgala.13 The discourse is meant to drive home the lesson of equality and fairness and the immateriality of caste identity to the treatment accorded to an individual. What it also, perhaps unwittingly, indicates is the less than optimal of Brahmins in Kshatriya circles, at least in the east. This could also explain the ghettoization of Brahmins in Maghadha and Kosala since both polities were ruled by Kshatriya clans, just like the Śākyas. It must also be noted, however, that this does not in and of itself imply poor treatment across the board, for Brahmins continued to hold a position of esteem not only in the epics but also various Buddhist texts. Let’s just say, it was a complex relationship and since we’re talking a timeline of at least half a millennium, bound to be inconsistent.

The sharp disparity between the social standing of Brahmins in the west and in the east is largely attributable to the delayed establishment of Brahminism in the eastern regions. During Patañjali's time, areas east of the Ganga-Yamuna confluence were still considered predominantly non-Brahmin.14 This does not imply a complete absence of Brahmins in these regions, but rather that they were neither numerous nor politically influential.15

## The Buddha and Caste

Although not integral to this discourse, how the founder of Buddhism viewed caste is a reasonable topic to become familiar with before stepping into the thick of lay praxis. And in that context, it’s also key to establish what caste looked like in Buddha’s times. So, caste is, in essence, two things—hierarchy and endogamy. Of these, only hierarchy somewhat existed in Buddha’s times and endogamy was a much later evolution, a subject we won’t go into here, for it has already been covered at great length in an earlier discourse.

* * *

* * *

Now, the mainstream view holds that the Buddha was a rebel against caste distinctions. The truth, however, is that the Buddha recognized caste distinctions much like any Brahmin of his era. The only departure lay in his belief about the origin of one’s caste identity. While the Vedic tradition saw birth and lineage as the source of _varṇa_ , the Buddha viewed morality and personal actions as the true source. In several Buddhist texts, he refuted the Brahmanical claim of caste superiority on metaphysical grounds, asserting that one’s rebirth is determined by karma, not caste. He also suggested that purity of blood did not determine one’s social standing. He held Brahmins in high regards, just defined Brahmin a bit differently. We have touched upon this in an earlier article on the Brahmana–Sramana relations before the Common Era.

* * *

* * *

But we will explore the topic afresh anyway. The earlier article had a much broader scope and covered far more than just Buddhism. Here, we have the luxury of going deeper into the Buddha’s own views on caste. Buddha’s perspective on the caste system, particularly regarding his own clan, the Kshatriyas, can be illustrated through the story of Ambaṭṭha. We know from the Dīgha Nikāya that Ambaṭṭha was a Brahmin from Kosala who faced mockery and insults from the Śākyas during his visit to Kapilvastu. Distressed by this experience, he sought the Buddha's counsel, prompting the latter to deliver a comprehensive sermon on caste equality and the illogical nature of using caste identity as a basis for social hierarchy. However, a closer examination reveals a subtle bias in Buddha’s worldview that may not be immediately apparent.

When Ambaṭṭha first raises the issue, Buddha responds with the example of a quail, stating that even a bird is free to speak as it pleases when in its nest. The bird here is the Śākyas and the nest is Kapilvastu. This is no extrapolation, for the Buddha himself clarifies his connotations as such in this dialog.16 They do not deserve censure for such a trifle, said Buddha clearly diminishing Ambaṭṭha’s insult. Two takeaways here— _**a)**_ Kapilvastu belongs exclusively to the Śākyas, and _**b)**_ There’s nothing wrong in them insulting an outsider because they own Kapilvastu. It’s difficult to interpret this section any other way. But it doesn’t end there. Buddha continues with another reinforcement of Kshatriya superiority over Brahmin. To support his argument, he points to the fact that while a Brahmin family might accept a child born out of a Brahmin-Kshatriya union, a Kshatriya family would never.17 At least here, Buddha is not simply endorsing caste hierarchy but doing so on the basis of birth, which directly contradicts the broader principles of his teachings. In 1.27, he literally spells out that even a fallen Kshatriya is superior to a Brahmin.18

An argument could be made here that Buddha was merely trying to break Ambaṭṭha’s Brahmin pride using extreme illustrations and that he did not truly believe in such hierarchies and superiorities. After all, he does aver that the Kshatriya is superior to those who believe in birth-based caste identities. But we must also remember that he is saying all this as a justification for Ambaṭṭha’s ill-treatment at the hands of Kapilvastu’s Kshatriyas. Weren’t all men to be treated equal?

In _Majjhima Nikāya_ , a Brahmin named Esukārī told the Buddha about the occupational hierarchy and its service matrix as prescribed by the Brahmins. Essentially, he says that nobody can be served by someone superior to him. So, for instance, a merchant can serve a Brahmin, but a Brahmin can’t serve a merchant. He seeks Buddha’s views on this prescription. And the Buddha obliges. His take is that only services that are not in violation of ethics should be rendered.19 For example, even if ordered to consume meat by a Brahmin, a destitute mustn’t. In short, so long as the order or service did not contradict ethical values, he was okay with the hierarchical service matrix. Nowhere in this entire discourse does he categorically reject the notion of a member of a lower caste or class serving a member of a superior caste or class as stipulated by the Brahmins according to Esukārī. In fact, only a little further, the Buddha declares his view that a man’s caste identity directly draws on his lineage—that one born in a clan of Brahmins is a Brahmin, and so on.20 The easiest way to reconcile this uncomfortable discourse with the Buddha’s core tenet is to acknowledge that he’s preaching caste mobility and not caste annihilation. A Shudra can do pious deeds and become a Brahmin. But in the next life. Hence the usage of _reborn_.21

In another discourse between Pasenadi, the Kshatriya king of Kosala, and the Buddha, the latter reaffirms both caste distinctions and hierarchy in worldly matters.22 Pasenadi asks the Buddha three times whether there is any difference among the four castes, and it is only on the third occasion that the Buddha responds in the negative. This time, he explains that in the spiritual realm or the afterlife, caste becomes immaterial, and only actions and virtues matter.23 In the first instance, the question pertained to the mortal world, and the answer was affirmative.24

Another Buddhist work, the _Lalitavistara Sūtra_ , goes even further and avers that a Buddha must be born of noble blood. As a biographical account of Siddhartha Gautama, it provides valuable insights into the circumstances surrounding his incarnation. One section details the Buddha’s selection of Kapilavastu and its Śākya king, Śuddhodana, citing caste as the primary factor in this decision. Since the caste of the subjects and the merchants were deemed too low for the Buddha, he must be born in royal blood.25 This has to be one of the strongest indictments of Buddhism’s endorsement of caste hierarchies despite all its egalitarian tenets. Of course, one is free to dismiss it as a “Mahayana innovation,” but the fact that no Buddha has ever emerged from a caste other than Brahmin and Kshatriya comes not from a Mahayana work but from a very Theravada Dīgha Nikāya.26

Speaking of Mahayana, another interesting piece of work that’s rarely spoken of in popular discourses is the _Aṣṭasāhasrikā Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra_ which has been dated to the beginning of the second century BC, if not earlier. In chapter 25 of this text, Buddha is in a conversation with Subhūti, one of his ten principal disciples, on the training of a Bodhisattava. After elaborating on all the aspects of such training, he proceeds to reveal the rewards for completing it successfully. And part of the reward is, surprisingly, immunity from hells and rebirth in an undesirable community such as that of _Pretas_ , _Asuras_ , outcasts, and other low-class families.27 The usage of terms like outcasts and low-class in a negative light strongly indicates Buddha’s own reluctance to challenge the existing hierarchy despite his campaign against caste immobility and oppression.

So, this was the Buddha’s own personal relationship with castes. While he advocated reverence for renunciates and ascetics who were anyway considered outside the pale of caste in both Vedic and Sramanic traditions, he did not outright repudiate or condemn the prevailing social structure. If anything, the idea of karma—which is itself a Sramanic, likely Buddhist, concept—reinforces caste by tying one’s actions to their future identity. Do good deeds and you’ll be reborn as a Brahmin. Which means if you’re a Brahmin, you must have done good deeds in past life. Clearly, brahminhood is being presented as a reward, rather than a social evil. Karma, in this light, is a _determinant_ of the caste hierarchy rather than its annihilator. This is as crucial as it is subtle.

With that out of the way, we can now move on to his followers. Ultimately, the objective is to evaluate the position of caste in Buddhist practice. It’s very much possible that while Buddha, being a product of his time, stopped short of unambiguously challenging the institution, his later followers, products of _their_ time, did.

## The Slave and the Sangha

A system that rejects caste must also reject slavery or any form of bondage. A system that fails to reject slavery or bondage cannot genuinely reject caste. A fair proposition, most would agree. The Buddhist philosophy goes out of its way in making caste a function of deeds and morals, even though it falls short of rejecting birth unconditionally as we saw in the case of karma. It should then naturally follow that slavery or forced labor did not have a place in its worldview. But was that the case in the Buddhist society, or even the scripture? Let’s examine.

The Buddhist scripture is essentially organized as a _Tripiṭaka_ , or the “three baskets.” These are the _Suttas_ , the _Vinayas_ , and the _Abhidhamma_. Everything that we’ve examined thus far falls within the Sutta (Pali for _Sūtra_) category. Now we’ll take up the Vinayas. In at least two of the several Vinaya traditions, namely _Mūlasarvāstivādin_ and _Mahāvihāra_ , there’s a story from Buddha’s time. It recounts an interaction between King Bimbisara of Magadha and a senior monk named Pilindaka in the presence of the Buddha, who was in the Bamboo Grove.

The story goes that the king once encountered Pilindaka covered in mud toiling away at the _vihāra_ and offered him five hundred servants for help.28 Pilindika declined at first, then with Buddha’s approval, relented.29 Who are these servants? The source we’re referring to here is Gregory Schopen, Professor of Buddhist Studies at University of California, and he uses words like bondsman and slave. If accurate, this would be the most pronounced approval of a hierarchical institution in the Buddhist canon. Interestingly, Chinese redactions of the same text use the word _jingren_ , or “purity-keepers.”30 These purity-keepers were, according to these sources, recruited to carry out chores that were considered “impure” and forbidden for the monks, hence the name—they helped the monks keep their purity.31 The Pali term in the original text is a rather ambiguous _kappiya_ , or “attendant.” Whether we read it as servant, slave, attendant, or purity-keeper, the qualification remains that of someone subservient to the monk.

Given our focus on Buddhist praxis, it is more relevant to examine how Buddhists have perceived caste within their practices rather than solely relying on scriptural interpretations. In this context, a valuable resource from more recent times that can provide important insights is the Brahmi inscription at Alluru. Situated in a tiny town in the Nellore district, the inscription dates to before the second century AD32 and is in the Prakrit language. The epigraph was originally part of a pillar and, besides other things, enumerates the gifts made by a member of the nobility to the local vihāra. The list includes cows, carts, copper utensils, lamps, a water tank, a thousand coins, and most importantly, slaves.33 The word used is _pesa-rupāni dāsi-dāsasa catāli_ which is interpreted as male and female slaves by some and menial servants by others.34 If a gift was made and announced, and the announcement makes no mention of a rejection, it’s only appropriate to assume it was accepted. Which places institutions like slavery or at least menial service right at the heart of the Buddhist society, in a vihāra.

It’s possible that we’re reading too much into what’s probably just a mundane social norm perfectly acceptable at the time even if not today. Perhaps the interpretation itself is wrong and _dāsa_ was just a servant rather than a literal slave. Words change meaning all the time. At some point dāsa may have meant just an attendant, a steward before acquiring its current interpretation of unpaid bonded labor. But none of these concessions prevent the notions of occupational hierarchy and purity. These are ideas expressly rejected in popular Buddhist thoughts today. Apparently, they weren’t always. But we’re not done yet. What if a slave wanted to join the Sangha and get ordained as a monk? Did the egalitarian Buddha allow that? Did the Sangha admit them?

Turns out they did. A slave could join the Sangha and become a Bhiksu, thereby attaining equal status with a Brahmin. However, this egalitarian principle led to a conflict in at least one instance. A slave-turned-monk was once seeking alms door-to-door when his former master confronted him, demanding that he return to servitude.35 The bystanders supported the monk, citing King Bimbisara’s decree that a monk, regardless of his past, was free from bondage.36 The master, discontented, argued that the Sangha had become a refuge for slaves evading their duties and debts.37 In response to this issue, the Buddha instituted a rule requiring slaves to obtain their masters’ consent before ordination.38

So no, slaves could not become monks. And yes, monks could own slaves. Or at least menial workers. Buddha’s egalitarian ideals did not percolate to this particular class, it would seem. In all fairness though, it’s only appropriate to point out that the Buddha expressly prohibited his monks from engaging in five types of trade—weapons, living beings, meat, intoxicants, and poison.39 Since “living beings” includes humans, this prohibition can be seen as a ban on the slave trade. How one reconciles this stricture with the fact that slaves or servants could be freely gifted to the Sangha by a wealthy patron remains a matter of personal interpretation. Let’s just call it complicated and move on.

## Caste and Aśoka

Aśoka is arguably the single most influential figure in Buddhist history after the Buddha himself. While the question of whether he truly converted to Buddhism or just remained a passionate lay patron at large still stands unresolved, his Buddhist association is above dispute. It could be for economic reasons (Buddhist monasteries were powerhouses of international commerce), political reasons, spiritual reasons, or a mix of all three. But the spiritual component is difficult to dismiss. Against that backdrop, any endorsement of the caste system by this figure should at least bring to question the community’s own aversion to it. So what did Aśoka think of castes? Did it matter to him?

We get a glimpse in a medieval panegyric called _Aśokavadana_. The earliest dating of this text places it in the Gupta period with the caveat that oral traditions could well be from Aśoka’s days. At any rate, this is a Buddhist work that speaks highly of the king and was composed not by Hindus or lay Buddhists but by Buddhist monks themselves. In one of the episodes, the work recounts an anecdote where Aśoka met with some Buddhist monks and prostrated himself at their feet to greet them. This was a well-established way to greet one’s superiors, especially in terms of caste, in Ancient India. But Aśoka was a king. Nothing in his realm could be superior to him. So why prostrate to renunciants? While these monks were outside the pale of caste, they could still have originated in any _varṇa_ , including Shudra. A king unwittingly prostrating before a Shudra, even if the latter were no longer one, was an impeachment of his exalted station.

At least that’s how one of his ministers, Yaśas, saw it. And was so bothered, he even took this up with the king and pointed out to him how the prostration was unbecoming of his status. Now, we don’t know if Yaśas was Buddhist too, so his views on caste doesn’t matter. But since Aśoka is important to the community, his stand does. According to Aśokavadana, the king responded with a mild chastisement for his minister’s pride and arrogance. He tells him that by being preoccupied with caste hierarchies, he was harming both himself and others. But just before we draw the obvious conclusion, he adds that caste matters at the time of wedding.40

The whole discourse is to drive home the idea that virtues and not caste identity decided one’s worth. But that ever-so-subtle exception of marriage ensures we know where the line ought to be drawn. Marrying across caste lines, it would seem, was still frowned upon at least by one most prominent Buddhist figure. Does this reflect societal norm or was it Aśoka’s personal preference?

Do note that as we’ve discussed in an earlier article on the history of caste, marriage insularity would not become a prominent Indian feature until the Common Era. That’s what genetic studies have shown anyway. But it’s not improbable that some amount of endogamy was practiced at least in the royal or aristocratic class. Understandable since a prince preferring a princess over a commoner helps politics and diplomacy. Coming back to Buddhism, it’s evident that caste endogamy was not exactly a social taboo in its praxis as it says on the tin.

The idea of endogamous royalty gets even stronger support in the Buddha’s own lineage, so while this section is about Aśoka, let’s spend a couple of minutes on Siddhartha Gautama too. For that, we’ll circle back to the story of Ambaṭṭha from Dīgha Nikāya. We know that he complained to the Buddha about his insult at Kapilvastu, and that the latter then responded with a long sermon on the equality of castes and, ironically, superiority of Kshatriyas over Brahmins. Part of this sermon was also a quick overview of the origins of his own clan.

The story goes that long ago there was a king named Okkāka (Pali for Ikshvaku) who ruled over Kosala from his capital at Sāvatthī or Śrāvastī. Concerned about securing his son’s succession, he banished his brothers to prevent them from claiming the throne. These exiles settled in the Himalayan foothills of Kapilvastu near a lotus pond and a _sāka_ or teak grove. To preserve their royal bloodline, these siblings took the unconventional step of cohabiting with their own sisters.41 Later when Okkāka learned of them from one of his ministers, he commended them as “strong as sāka,” which is how the name Śākya came to be.42

Of course, the legend is just that, a legend. But it does underscore the Buddha’s own preoccupation with lineage. One may argue that perhaps Buddha did not approve of this and merely related the story to humble Ambaṭṭha, but nowhere in the discourse is there any sign of such a disapproval. Also, the choice of such a bizarre narrative seems extreme even for the purpose of erudition. Unless it weren’t considered bizarre in the first place. Or maybe we really are reading too much into a concocted anecdote. A third possibility is that this particular section was a later, Common Era interpolation which would explain the almost doctrinal sanction for caste endogamy. Again, endogamy wasn’t mainstream in India at the time of Buddha as genetic studies have already shown. Two things are clear as daylight here— _**a)**_ The text was composed not by a Hindu but a Buddhist, because a Hindu scribe would never bring incest to even a cadet branch of the Ikshvaku line, and _**b)**_ whoever composed it, placed a great deal of importance on caste. Clearly, the rhetoric of caste equality was not actualized in real life.

## The Outcastes

A renowned episode from _Śārdūlakarṇāvadāna_ , a work coeval with and in the same genre as Aśokavadana, narrates the tale of a Matanga girl who becomes a nun.43 In this account, the Buddha uses her story to challenge the caste system in a conversation with King Prasenajit of Kosala. The central plot revolves around Ānanda’s encounter with Prakṛti, a _Matanga_ girl who falls in love with him and later seeks ordination from the Buddha.44 The Buddha invites her to embrace a celibate life as a nun, despite fierce opposition from the Brahmins of Śrāvastī.45 This narrative highlights the Buddha’s rejection of caste distinctions and his commitment to egalitarianism, as both _Caṇḍālas_ and Matangas were positioned at the lowest rung of the social order, beneath even the Shudras.

A similar story appears in the _Kalpanāmaṇḍitikā_ , a non-canonical third-century text, where the Buddha elevates a dung-worker named Nītha to monkhood.46 The narrative focuses on Nītha’s attempts to flee from the Buddha, who magically appears at the end of every alley.47 Eventually, Nītha gives in, undergoes a spiritual awakening, and seeks ordination.48 Just like the story of Prakṛti, this one too serves to emphasize the Buddha’s anti-caste stance in the context of spiritual elevation. Here too, the radical move scandalizes the Brahmins of Śrāvastī who rush to King Prasenjit with their reservations against the idea of a low-born dung-worker entering their homes as an ordained Bhikshu. To which the Buddha responds with his sermon of egalitarianism and enlightenment.

These are not the only such stories in Buddhist works. The theme has cycled through all kinds of canonical and non-canonical texts, often as multiple variants of the same story. But before we rush to draw the final conclusion, there’s a subtext we must reexamine here. Let’s go back to Prakṛti’s story first. It tells us how despite her Matanga background, she was embraced as an ordained nun into the Sangha. We get the story from a rendition by Sujit Mukhopadhyaya of a Sanskrit manuscript. Nothing stands out in this rendition itself, but in a footnote, Mukhopadhyaya adds a snippet that he says is part of the Sanskrit manuscript but omitted in Tibetan and Chinese translations. This snippet tells us that the ordination involved a “purification ritual” where the Matanga girl was cleansed of all the sins of her “past lives” and liberated from her Matanga identity.49 The snippet can also be found in a rendition of Divyāvadāna and allied works by the nineteenth century English Orientalist Edward Byles Cowell.50

This purification ritual shows that in the process of liberating a lay follower from his caste identity, the Buddha is also ironically reinforcing the very institution of caste. In other words, an untouchable remains an untouchable unless purified and ordained as a monk. In light of the ritual’s incompatibility with the broader Buddhist ideals and its complete absence in non-Indian renditions, it’s often assumed to be a later interpolation. At least that’s what Mukhopadhyaya believes. But that’s just one story.

In Nītha’s story, again, while the ordination is challenged and the challenge overruled by Prasenjit, the overrule still comes with a blink-and-miss subtext that reveals a little more nuance to the Buddhist attitude toward caste. The king clarifies that caste is no object when it comes to entering Buddha’s fold, but also adds that it sure is when it comes to marriage.51 This is reminiscent of what we saw earlier in Aśokāvadāna where Aśoka says pretty much the same. Even if these caveats were to be dismissed as later interpolations, they reflect the social tendencies in favor of caste-based endogamy. Don’t forget, Aśokāvadāna was composed not by lay followers but by active members of the Buddhist clergy. In a sense, what Buddha thought of caste becomes immaterial to what caste means in Buddhist praxis.

The theme of caste hierarchy in social life is not confined to non-canonical works. As we have already seen in earlier sections, even the canon is replete with such discriminatory expressions. While the Buddha tried associating caste with actions, we have, for example, the Pali Vinaya that openly segregates birth into low and high.52

    “Birth means: there are two kinds of birth: low birth and high birth. Low birth means: birth as (a member of) a despised class, birth as a bamboo-plaiter, birth as a hunter, birth as a cartwright, birth as a refuse-scavenger—this means low birth. High birth means: birth as a noble, birth as a brahmin—this means high birth.” (trans. Horner)

And this isn’t even Mahayana which many associate with Buddhism’s “later corruption.” The Pali canon is integral to the Theravada tradition. Thus, it can safely be concluded that pretty much across the board in Buddhism, it’s not a nice thing to be of a lower caste, even less so to be an outcaste. There are dozens of other examples littered throughout the Pali and Tibetan canons that reinforce this conclusion. The Buddha shall emancipate you, sure, but short of that, you remain a contemptible Caṇḍālas who is serving the karmic sentence for his past life sins. In one bizarre tale in _Karmaśataka_ , a non-canonical Mahayana work from the fourth or fifth century AD, a Caṇḍāla is ordained as a monk but then fated to be reborn as a Caṇḍāla five hundred times.53 His infraction? Losing his cool and calling other monks Caṇḍālas.54 So Caṇḍāla is such a taboo idea that even calling someone that is a severe infraction. And Caṇḍāla is such a taboo idea that being born as one is a legitimate punishment for such infractions. Not once but five hundred times.

In the famous Lotus Sutra or _Saddharmapuṇḍarīka_ , a Bodhisattva is prohibited from engaging with a set of undesirables in order to maintain his piety. And while this set understandably includes meat vendors, prostitutes, musicians, wrestlers, it also includes servants and Caṇḍālas.55 In yet another Mahayana text, _Śrāvakabhūmi_ , a Caṇḍāla’s hut is one of the five places a monk must not visit, the other four being a king’s palace, a bar, a brothel, and a butcher shop.56 The Great Dharani Sutra of the Tibetan Mahayana canon lists a number of things a _dhāraṇī_ chant immunizes one against. This list includes negative elements like poison, fire, weapons, wild beasts, disease, robbers, thieves—and Caṇḍālas.57 In Tantrik Buddhism, the Tibetan mainstay, Caṇḍālas join demons and ogres as subjects of nightmares should a monk mispronounce a mantra or err in his rituals.58

Even in the context of ordination things remain ambiguous. While _Avadāna_ and Pali works maintain universal eligibility to the Sangha, as we saw in the case of Prakṛti and Nītha, later Mahayana works don’t. One such work is Guṇaprabha’s _Vinayasūtra_ , which expressly excludes Caṇḍālas, cartwrights, and other lowborn individuals from ordination.59 The observation that those of “lowly births” had a challenge entering the Buddhist orbit has also been made by figures of eminence typically associated with Marxist historiography and seen as generally sympathetic to Buddhism. Even a prominent historian like Kosambi notes that slaves, tribals, and Naga aborigines were barred from the Sangha even before the schism that birthed Mahayana.60

Evidently, the outcaste is the embodiment of negativity and unworthy of any physical or intellectual engagement in all forms of Buddhism, including but not exclusive to Mahayana. A closer scrutiny also reveals a gradual progression in this association—subtle in the Pali canon but extremely pronounced in mid-first millennium Mahayana corpus. This coincides perfectly with the advent of widespread endogamy in the Common Era.

## In Contemporary Buddhism

An overwhelming majority of India’s Buddhists are modern converts to a newly created Navayana sect, disparagingly called neo-Buddhists. The sect was founded by B. R. Ambedkar to liberate India’s Dalits from the Hindu caste ecosystem. With reference to the 2011 census, these converts are pegged at 87% of India’s Buddhist headcount,61 the rest primarily confined to the Himalayas and its foothills. Since we’re on the subject of active Buddhist praxis, it’s more relevant to talk about how the community sees caste today, than how it did centuries ago or how it does in the scripture. It’s always possible that things that happened in Buddha’s days are no longer seen as fit for practice today, as is the case with all world religions. So, the question is, does Buddhism follow caste discriminations today?

In the context of Navayana, we can safely assume the answer is no. The whole idea of Ambedkar’s move was to help people escape caste. So, it wouldn’t make sense for anyone entering its fold to bring their caste baggage along. Much can be said of the movement’s political implications in the other direction, however, but that’s not the object of this discourse so we’ll leave it at that. Long story short, no, Navayana Buddhism which makes up nearly 90% of India’s Buddhism, does not practice caste discriminations, although questions can and have been raised on whether Navayana even qualifies as Buddhism to begin with. But what about others?

Caste-like divisions have been noted by several scholars in several other forms of Buddhism across the world, most notably in Sri Lanka. Much has been written on this subject by British Indologist and Buddhist scholar Richard Gombrich. In Sri Lanka, the dominant Sinhalese castes is that of _goviyо̄_ or rice farmers, called _goyigama_ , and it comprises nearly half of the island country’s population. They are Buddhist. There are other castes too, most carved along feudal and occupational lines. For instance, a ritual tied to a monk’s formal visit to a lay follower’s home includes the monk walking on white cloths laid out by a member of the washerman caste.62 An unambiguous hierarchy is instantly established here. Similarly, there are _navandannо̄_ (craftsmen),63 _beravāyо̄_ (drummers),64 and others. The latter also seem to double as exorcists or _kaṭṭaḍiyā_ in the rural environs of the Low Country.65 Interestingly, Gombrich informs, the typical spells during these exorcism rituals begin with something like _Ōṃ Buddhāya_ , a brief invocation to the Buddha.66 Even the Sinhalese monastic landscape is replete with caste divisions as observed by Gombrich, who noted the existence of numerous caste-based groups or _Nikāyas_ within the order.67

In conversations within goyigama circles, a clear distinction is made between _honda minissu_ or “good men” and _aḍu kula_ or “lesser men.”68 Features seen in rural Indian households, such as lower seating for lower caste, separate utensils, food segregation, and endogamy, are also evident in Sinhalese Buddhist communities.69 This caste distinction also extends into the popular Sinhalese grammar where the goyigama use different pronouns for different caste levels, which also happens in India. Gombrich noted the use of pronoun _ū_ for both animals and members of a lower caste.70

Outside of Sri Lanka, caste has also been detected in Buddhist societies in Tibet and Nepal. Both used to be highly feudal societies until not too long ago, so the vestiges of the past are understandable. English Indologist and founder of The Pali Society, Thomas William Rhys Davids is often quoted as having observed and documented caste practices in over a hundred Buddhist communities around the world, but it’s futile to spend too much energy upholding it until such a time a first-hand account from him is found.

Having said that, it won’t be fair to place the problem of caste in today’s Buddhism on an equal footing with its Hindu counterpart. Most Buddhist communities have largely shed their feudal past and gone beyond caste identities. This is true for Sri Lanka, but more so for places like Tibet, China, and Korea where the overwhelming influence of both Communism and Capitalism have played a role in forcing caste hierarchies out of routine practice. In India too, only to a lesser degree. Anyway, this discourse is not a social commentary, so we’ll leave it at that.

Before we wrap up, there’s a radical new observation that merits discussion. Alright, not exactly new because the observation first appeared in the Imperial Gazette of India as early as 1907, but certainly new to many readers of this article. It comes from Scottish Indologist William Wilson Hunter, who also once served as a civil servant in British India. What he argues is that not only did Buddhism uphold the prevailing caste order but played an active role in propagating and mainstreaming it.

While the take may scandalize at first blush, it makes sense once we rope in the fact that caste distinctions were not as pronounced in the Vedic Age as they are today (I have discussed this at length in the other article on the history of caste). We also need to remember that endogamy, the most enduring and defining feature of caste, did not go mainstream until around the Gupta centuries. To this, we can then add the Buddhist emphasis on purity and karma, and we get what starts to look like hardline caste-like orders. This is not to say the identities did not exist in the pre-Buddha world, they just weren’t as pronounced and impregnable as they would become later. Hunter rightly posits that caste as a social identifier was only beginning to take shape in the early Buddhist years.71

By the time Buddhism crystallized as a doctrinally independent philosophy, it had acquired two characters that set it apart from the prevailing Vedic systems—it was evangelical, and it was mobile. Buddhism has always been a philosophy of energetic propagation, but it has also been a highly mobile community which is how it came to ace the mercantile landscape most notably along the Silk Road. And as the community grew and spread, it brought the ideals of purity and karma to newer domains. If you’re born low, that’s sins from a past life. If you wish to secure a noble birth in the next, strive to live pure. Hunter adds that these elements brought caste and caste-like social distinctions to places that didn’t have those before.72

Away from Sri Lanka, we find communities of untouchables and menials in a multitude of Buddhist communities. In Tibet we have the _Ragyabpa_ who comprise lowly professionals like fishermen, executioners, and corpse disposers. They are an integral part of the Tibetan Buddhist fold. A similar class of low-caste community, albeit superior to Ragyabpa, is that of the _Nangzan_ bonded labor, also part of the Tibetan Buddhist fold. In China we come across the _Jiànmín_ class that has traditionally comprised courtesans, musicians, actors, and prostitutes. The term itself means worthless people. A Jiànmín was forbidden from marrying a commoner and obliged to carry out the profession of his lineage. Past tense because these distinctions have largely disappeared with the advent of militant Communism. At the bottom of the Japanese social pyramid are the _Burakumin_ people. As late as 2003, as many as 5% of Tokyo residents reported they would actively avoid a Burakumin neighbor. This is also Buddhism. Yet another example of caste discrimination in active Buddhist praxis comes from Thailand where the _Precariat_ community remains the most deprived on all socioeconomic parameters due to the largely underreported parochial views of Thai Buddhists. Back home in India, we have such examples in Ladakh. The largely Buddhist communities of _Garba_ (smiths), _Mon_ (musicians), and _Beda_ , although some have converted to Islam, still get separate cups for tea at village gatherings. Thus, while swiftly vanishing with improved economics, caste is as much a daily reality of the Buddhist life as it is in Hinduism.

## Conclusion

So, where does that leave us? Right where we started? Not really. There’s clear, undeniable evidence of Buddha embracing members of all castes to his fold. Of someone as low as a dung-worker being eligible for monkhood without reservations. But there’s also clear, undeniable evidence of caste being an overarching reality in both Buddhist doctrine and Buddhist praxis. It’s true that even low-caste members were admitted to the order in Buddhist scripture, but it’s just as true that several groups like tribals, aborigines, and slaves weren’t. It’s true that the Buddha preached equality across caste lines, but it’s just as true that Buddhist scripture does not prohibit the gifting of slaves to the Sangha. It’s true that a dung-worker was ordained by the Buddha himself, in the face of great protestations. But it’s just as true that he did so only after first _purifying_ her of her caste. It’s true that a monk was above worldly distinctions and caste hierarchies in the scripture. But it’s just as true that he was prohibited from engaging with the likes of Caṇḍālas and prostitutes to maintain his _purity_.

In the real world of today, the world outside scripture, caste holds a less significant value in the Buddhist society. But it would be erroneous to say it doesn’t. As Gombrich has pointed out with generous examples, Sri Lanka remains a caste-riddled society despite there being virtually no Brahmin or Kshatriya class on the entire island and the Vaishya goyigama being the highest echelon. In India, the prominent sect is Navayana, which was itself borne out of sociopolitical compulsions. So, it’s understandable that caste plays little role in its lifestyle. But elsewhere, communities with better affinity to traditional Buddhism, such as Tibetan Vajrayana, caste had held fort until fairly recently. The _Cheonmin_ of Korea and the _Senmin_ of Japan, both low-caste members in their respective countries, attest to this dying reality.
