# Were Harappans Buddhist or Hindu?

*A Critical Examination of Marshall, Banerji, and Misread Artifacts*

Well, “Harappan” may be misleading here since much of the premise that the folks of the Indus Civilization practiced Buddhism, comes not from Harappa but from a single structure more than 400 miles to the South in Mohenjo-daro. But while a Harappan is technically someone from Harappa, the term has also come to be used as a demonym for the entire civilization, so that’s the term we are going to stick to.

In 1922, Rakhal Das Banerji began excavations on the northwestern mound of Mohenjo-daro, the site’s loftiest elevation. His work, later continued in 1925–26 under B. L. Dhama and directed by John Marshall, revealed a Buddhist complex that Marshall documents in detail. At its core stood a quadrangular courtyard dominated by a central _stupa_ and enclosed by monastic cells, rebuilt several times at progressively higher levels. The original stupa, over fifty feet wide, included an elaborate eastern stairway and a prominent image niche that once housed a seated Buddha. Although its dome has disappeared, the circular drum survives to a height of eight feet, while earlier treasure-seekers had disturbed its hollow core and uncovered fragments of an alabaster reliquary.

Banerji suggested that the monument was designed as a hollow structure, plastered and painted within, yet Marshall questioned this interpretation. Finding no clear traces of interior decoration, he argued that the stupa, like others in India, was more likely filled with debris and capped by a dome, with only a modest relic chamber at its heart. Constructed of reused bricks set in mud mortar, the monument displays rough workmanship compared with the earlier Indus remains it overlies. Still, it stands as evidence of the long persistence of Buddhist activity at Mohenjo-daro, imposed upon the ruins of the city’s prehistoric past.

Or does it?

So, for the longest time, Mohenjo-daro was a desolate wasteland with nothing but arid dirt and earth covering whatever might lay underneath. Its “citadel” jutted out of its surrounding like an invitation to archaeologists to dig it out of centuries of neglect, but the Archaeological Survey of India had its hands full with Harappa, a site it had started digging only months before. The unusual outcrop, after which Mohenjo-daro or “Mound of the Dead” got its name, was casually read as a stupa from the Kushan era. These rulers, especially after Kanishka, were heavily into Buddhism and ruled the area roughly between 75 and 250 AD. In fact, the region had been under Buddhist influence even before the Kushans arrived. The Parthians, the Greeks, the Maurya, had all ruled the area before the Kushans and all of them patronized Buddhism in their own way. Therefore, the theory of an ancient Buddhist stupa in the region had no reason to invite scrutiny.

Banerji had surveyed the area in 1919 and was keen on digging. But he only got the chance in 1922 when he finally put the trowel to the mound with a team of excavators. Worth reiterating and bearing in mind here is that the mound was assumed to be a stupa long before its excavation. This is important. Banerji’s boss was none other than the ASI Director-General John Marshall.

Before we proceed with the nature of the “stupa,” it’s important to establish the necessity of such establishment. Ideally, it would be a mere subject of curiosity and exploration, but as most things Indian, this one too comes with a great deal of political and ideological baggage. IVC is where the Indian society took roots. Hence, there’s much to be gained ideologically from appropriating its cultural and more importantly religious legacy. Whichever religion the Harappans practiced automatically becomes India’s primary religion. There are two major contestants right now, Hinduism and Buddhism. It’s the classic “alien versus sons-of-soil” argument. Of course, this also fits neatly within the Indian “Left versus Right” discourse where the Left gains from a Buddhist IVC and the Right from a Hindu or Vedic IVC. The third alternative that it could be neither, given that we’re yet to crack its code, is far less glamorous and finds few takers in this ideologically charged debate. The attempt here is to, as always, stick to the verifiable and discard the unverifiable. Let’s continue.

## It’s a Stupa

John Marshall published his seminal _Mohenjo-daro and the Indus Civilization_ in 1931, almost a decade since the start of excavations. In this work, he reaffirmed what was always known to be the case—that the brick structure under the mysterious mound was a Buddhist stupa. In his work, Marshall rightly identifies not a single monolith but a prehistoric base with multiple successive accretions.1 This work of his, in his own admission, draws upon Banerji’s inputs, and describes the edifice as a “spacious quadrangle open to the sky, with a lofty stupa in its middle and rows of monastic buildings enclosing it on the four sides.”2 But does that mean it _was_ a stupa?

A Kushan-era stupa cannot be ruled out at any rate because among Banerji’s finds were coins of the Shiva and Bull type from Vasudeva I,3 a Kushan king who ruled in the first quarter of the third century. Since the Kushans were a Buddhist polity, it’s quite plausible that the structure in question was Buddhist in nature. But that still doesn’t mean the underlying Indus structure must also be Buddhist. We need more, and Marshall recognizes that. That’s where the third exhibit comes in handy—burial urns.

Archaeologists found two big clay jars in this spot. Inside each of these were smaller pots or urns, and inside those urns were human bones placed in crude, bowl-like terracotta containers. These bones had not been burned (they were uncalcined), which means they were placed directly after death rather than cremated first. Once the team cleared away these urns, they found something unusual—a simple stone casket made from coarse sandstone. It was small, about six inches across, with a shallow round hollow in the middle (about an inch deep). This hollow was covered with two lids, first a tiny lid made of conch shell, and above that a larger marble lid. In the larger burial jars of the room, they also discovered bigger bones that couldn’t fit into the smaller, pointed-bottom urns.4 Stupas are essentially burial mounds. discovery of funerary remains inside a structure, therefore, can be a strong indicator of the said structure being a stupa, even if not conclusive. But again, that does not establish its stupa character in deeper antiquity. The pointed-bottom urns hold the key.

Similar pointed-bottom jars were also identified as Indus artifacts, dating to far older timelines than the Kushans. Banerji believes both Indus and Kushan jars to be funerary urns whereas Marshall believes the Indus urns to be drinking goblets. But these artifacts are two thousand years apart. Were the Indus jars in continuous use for two thousand years? Or were they preserved as some kind of collectible or reliquary by the Kushan-era monks? If burial urns, the case for stupa gains weight. If not, we’re back to the drawing board. Marshall seems to dither. So far, all we know is that it was a stupa by the third century AD. Now let’s see the arguments against an Indus stupa.

Before all else, let’s establish something fundamental for the lay reader. A stupa is never something you _enter_ ; it’s something you circumambulate on the outside. That’s because there’s no _inside_. Contrary to what the layman believes, these are not hollow spaces with a dome, these are solid mounds filled with earth or bricks. The relics are placed at the center. In other words, there’s no nondestructive way to access the relics once the stupa is built.

The structure at Mohenjo-daro, if you’d recall, was hollow. The dome, if any, had long fallen off and what remained was a drum-shaped walled structure open to the sky. That instantly rules out a stupa. Then we have the presence of multiple funerary remains. Since a stupa is built once and never possible to be entered again, it’s difficult to imagine one with multiple artifacts belonging to multiple timelines inside of it. This calls into question the identification of the structure as a Buddhist stupa even during Kushan times. But let’s stick to the Indus identification here. If the hollow architecture isn’t enough on its own, we have the quadrilateral base.

Stupas are customarily built on square bases. This jives well with the idea of circumambulation around a domed structure. The best fit for a circular structure is a square. But the structure in Mohenjo-daro is rectangular. A 2007 paper by Giovanni Verardi and Federica Barba also notes that no cultic objects identifiable with Buddhism has ever been recovered from these ruins.5 This difference is not a minor detail but a fundamental departure from Buddhist architectural norms. The absence of moldings, gateways, votive stupas, reliquaries, or any other ritual objects that typically accompany Buddhist monuments further undermines the stupa identification. Instead, the building style is entirely consistent with that of the Indus Valley civilization, suggesting continuity with the city’s protohistoric character rather than a later Buddhist phase. Verardi and Barba argue that the so-called stupa is better understood as a stepped platform or pyramid-like structure, possibly serving a ritual function within the sacred precinct of Mohenjo-daro, but wholly unrelated to Buddhism.6

An interesting question Marshall raises is that of support. The mud drum of Mohenjo-daro spans more than thirty feet in diameter. A hollow domed structure of that span is untenable and would quickly collapse in the absence of any support. But if it weren’t hollow, it would be a stupa, right? But more importantly, if it weren’t hollow, how could subsequent urns get in? Marshall closes this loop with a supposition that the Kushana structure must have been filled with debris and earth before being sealed off. The debris that were used might have contained older Indus artifacts as part of the aggregate. Not only that, Both Banerji and Verardi argue that the original wasn’t hollow either. Instead, it was a stepped structure meant for ritual offerings, much like the ziggurats of Mesopotamia. We do not therefore need a stupa to explain the architecture at all.

But if not stupa, what was it?

## It’s a Temple

Marshall’s 1931 report on Mohenjo-daro was not the first. It was only the first that saw the light of the day. But the truly first documentation of this site comes to us from none other than Banerji himself. This was a typewritten collection of papers he had sent to Marshall for review. The collection included a number of diagrams and photographs. Unfortunately, Marshall found the research lacking in accuracy and deferred his opinion for a good four years. Then when he finally returned the copy to Banerji, in 1930, with his approval for printing, he held back the illustrations and photographs. With no support from the ASI or Marshall, Banerji never got to publish his paper and gave up. He died the same year.

It was only in 1984, more than half a century later, that the report resurfaced and was published in facsimile under the title _Mohenjo Daro: A Forgotten Report_.7 In it, Banerji advanced a markedly different theory—that the structure was a Hindu temple. His argument rested on several premises, the first being the significance of its location. He noted that Mohenjo-daro stood on the southern bank of an early bed of the Indus, where small river islands provided natural platforms for shrines and religious sanctuaries. He argued that the so-called stupa occupied one such island, in keeping with a long tradition of island-based temples in Sindh, exemplified by Khwaja Khizr, Sadh Belo, and Satiyan Jo Asthan. The elevated setting, he suggested, was not accidental but deliberate, intended to safeguard the shrine from seasonal floods that regularly swept the region. Evidence of ash layers indicated that an earlier sanctuary on this spot had been destroyed by fire, after which a new brick platform was raised, its great height the cumulative result of rebuilding and debris over centuries. The surrounding rooms, he further maintained, were originally shrines rather than residences, as suggested by the presence of oblation tables, libation cups, and an E-shaped altar—features consistent with ritual practice rather than monastic life.

Another line of reasoning came from the marble architectural fragments uncovered in the mound. These included polished rings and oval slabs, many with grooves and channels and joined together by copper bolts. While some had dismissed these objects as mace-heads, Banerji insisted that their true function was ritual—joined end to end, they formed conduits for liquid offerings, a practice deeply embedded in early Indian cults. He drew comparisons with similar installations still preserved in shrines at Sehwan, Indore, and Alwar, reinforcing the case for Mohenjo-daro as a religious sanctuary rather than a monastic residence.

The discovery of miniature pottery, faience vessels, and figurines added to this picture. Banerji emphasized that these were not domestic wares but votive offerings, placed as acts of devotion. Their presence within the complex, along with relic caskets fashioned from stone and marble, confirmed to him the cultic function of the site. Even the arrangement of altars and libation tables within adjoining chambers suggested ceremonial use, marking these spaces as chapels rather than cells.

Finally, as we have already suspected, Banerji stressed that the very design of the mound was inconsistent with that of a Buddhist stupa. Unlike the solid brick core of a true reliquary, the Mohenjo-daro structure contained chambers, passageways, and ritual installations. Its layout, in his view, was incompatible with Buddhist architecture but entirely consistent with a shrine or temple complex. Later frescoes and inscriptions in Kharoshthi and Brahmi, which clearly belonged to the first or second century AD, indicated that the Buddhists had appropriated an older sacred site rather than establishing a new one. The so-called stupa, then, was not originally a Buddhist monument at all but a temple of far greater antiquity, its sanctity preserved across successive faiths.

While much of Banerji’s conclusions may be at best qualified as educated guesswork, the odds of the structure being Buddhist in nature are rather slim on multiple grounds. Is it possible that the idea of Buddhist stupas itself may have evolved from an earlier architectural tradition similar to what we see in Mohenjo-daro? Sure. Not just possible, it’s rather natural for a later tradition to be an evolution of an older one. Stupas could absolutely be an evolved form of earlier megaliths or burial mounds that were common to most Bronze Age traditions.

While we have grounds to lean in favor of a shrine, we still don’t have enough to be confident of a _Hindu_ shrine. All cultures of remote antiquity built shrines to various local deities. But not all were Hindu or Vedic. The Mesopotamians weren’t Vedic, nor were the Egyptians. So it would be premature to close Mohenjo-daro’s case with a Hindu conclusion. For the shrine to be Hindu, the Harappans would have to be Hindu. And for them to be Hindus, there ought to be a sufficient number of Hindu-identifiable artifacts _outside_ of the shrine itself.

## Proto-Shiva or Pashupati

A soapstone seal, just under an inch and a half square and a quarter of an inch thick, was recovered from Mohenjo-daro about six years after excavations began. At its center sits a human figure on a low platform, legs drawn up with heels together and toes pointing downward, arms extended outward to rest lightly on the knees. The figure is laden with ornaments—eight small and three large bangles on each arm, layered necklaces across the chest, and a double band around the waist—and crowned with an elaborate headdress marked by a fan-shaped crest flanked by striated horns. Surrounding the figure are four wild animals—elephant and tiger to one side, water buffalo and rhinoceros to the other. Beneath the platform two ibexes turn their heads backward, their curved horns nearly meeting at the center. Above the scene runs a row of seven Indus script signs, the last pushed downward for want of space. They call it the “Pashupati seal,” often also “Proto-Shiva.”

That Shiva is Pashupati is established convention. The name has appeared in Hindu scripture since as far back as the Samhitas. If Shiva was being worshipped in Mohenjo-daro, Vedic was the religion without much in the way of counterargument. But is the Shiva? Ironically, the same John Marshall who identified the mound as a Buddhist stupa happens to be the one who identified this seal as Pashupati Shiva. Pashupati is a Sanskrit compound of _pashu_ , meaning animal, and _pati_ , meaning master—Master of Animals. Put a pin on this term, we’ll return to it in a bit.

For the man in the seal to be Shiva, the following must be true beyond doubt:

  1. Shiva being uniquely identified as Pashupati

  2. Shiva’s identification as Pashupati being of absolute antiquity

  3. Man surrounded by animals being uniquely Indic




Let’s start with the first. Is the appellation of Pashupati unique to Shiva in Hindu scripture? Today, yes. But wasn’t always the case. Case in point is the _Taittiriya Samhita_ of _Krishna Yajurveda_. The second verse of 3.1.4 uses the term Pashupati for Agni, the fire deity of the Vedas:8

     _imaṁ paśuṁ paśupate te ’dya baghnāsygne sukṛtasya madhye,
    anū manyasva suyajā yajām juṣṭaṁ devānāmidamastu havyaṁ._
    “This victim, O lord of cattle, for thee to-day,” (trans. Shastri and Shastri)

At the same time, it’s worth noting that there is no Pashupati in Rigveda. None whatsoever. There is Rudra, but no Pashupati. No Shiva either. The first time Rudra is identified as Shiva is in Vishnu Purana, a work of much later antiquity. Sure the word _shiva_ appears in a couple of RV hymns, but only as an adjective meaning “auspicious,” and not even exclusive to Rudra. RV 10.169.4, for instance, goes:9

     _prajāpatir mahyam etā rarāṇo viśvair devaiḥ pitṛbhiḥ saṃvidānaḥ,
    śivāḥ satīr upa no goṣṭham ākas tāsāṃ vayam prajayā saṃ sadema._
    “May Prajāpati bestowing these (Cows) upon me concurring with the universal gods and the Pitara,
    bring the auspicious cattle to our cow-pen, may we be in possession of their progeny.” (trans. Wilson)

This hymn is not addressed to Rudra. It’s addressed to Prajapati, a name variously denoting Indra, Agni, or even Savitr (Sun), depending on the context, in the role of a primordial Creator deity. But Shiva is not what we’re concerned with here. Our preoccupation is with Pashupati, of which there’s no mention in the entire work.

The term only makes its debut in later Vedic works like the one we saw earlier and even then, not exclusively for Rudra. The Rudra-Shiva-Prajapati association would not crystallize until the much later Puranic times. So Pashupati has already failed the first two tests, of exclusivity and of antiquity. That leaves us with the one point on which we had dropped the pin before—Master of Animals. Let’s address it now.

The Master of Animals is a motif that’s surprisingly far from exclusive to Indic traditions, and understandably so. The further back we go in time, the more man’s dependency on animals, either for food or for protection. This dependency necessitates an all-encompassing force that could be propitiated to exert control over all creatures, big and small, tame and wild. Naturally, most animist and polytheistic systems have animal-deities as part of their pantheons too. The Pashupati seal has been dated to the Mature Harappa phase, which places it between 2500 and 2300 BC. But there have been far older motifs depicting man surrounded by animals in other parts of the world too. One example is an artifact known as the Gebel el-Arak Knife that sits in the Louvre today. Dated to as far back as 3300 BC, this specimen depicts a man who looks Mesopotamian flanked by two upright lions on either side and what appear to be two bovine animals below. This comes from predynastic Egypt and has nothing to do with Hinduism.

Another artifact of a similar vintage, also currently in the Louvre, shows an ibex-headed character with one giant serpent and one goat on either side. This is a soapstone seal, the same as Pashupati, but the provenance is an entirely unrelated Lorestan.

Several similar specimens exist in all parts of the world, from Egypt to Mesopotamia to Levant to Greece, each as unrelated to Hinduism as the rest. This isn’t the only such situation, by the way. We also have a similar case with the Swastika where the oldest specimens on record are not even Indic. So, a man being surrounded by beasts is not grounds enough to designate him as Pashupati. And even from designating him Pashupati to identifying him as Shiva is nothing short of a leap. But we still have the lingam to contend with.

## The Sacred Phallus

As Banerji and his colleagues were unearthing the Pashupati seal at Mohenjo-daro in the late 1920s, Daya Ram Sahni had already brought to light a remarkable find further north at Harappa. In 1924–25, while excavating Trench A(i) of Mound F, he recovered a sandstone obelisk-like object that he interpreted as a Shiva lingam. His successor, Madho Sarup Vats, later added to this record with the discovery of three cylindrical stone objects, ranging from just a few inches to nearly a foot in height, which bore a striking resemblance to the lingam still revered in Shaiva practice today. John Marshall noted that comparable specimens at Mohenjo-daro fell within the same size range, reinforcing the impression of a shared symbolic tradition across the Indus cities. In his monumental _Mohenjo-daro and the Indus Civilization_ , he devoted an entire section to the subject of phallic and baetylic stones, stressing how difficult it is to distinguish between objects of cultic significance and those that might have been mere game pieces or weights.10 He observed that the reverence paid to stones in India was both ancient and widespread, ranging from natural forms regarded as inhabited by spirits to highly conventionalized idols that embodied divine energy. Within this continuum fell the linga and yoni, which, in his estimation, were already well represented in the Indus cities. Question is, was he right?

We’ll approach the lingam in the same way we did Pashupati. The interpretation must be exclusively phallic, and phallic practices must be exclusively Indic. If we establish these two, the artifact is a lingam. And if there are lingams in Harappan cities, they are extremely likely to have been a Vedic people.

Marshall divided the finds into three broad classes. The first comprised small to medium-sized objects, some modeled with unmistakable realism, which he regarded as phalli. Their presence, he argued, proved conclusively that phallic worship in India had a pre-Aryan origin, dismissing the once fashionable notion of a later Greek or foreign importation. Some were large enough to serve as cult objects, others small enough to be worn as amulets, much like miniature lingas still carried by Shaiva devotees today. The second group, more conical in form, bore a closer resemblance to the baetylic stones of Western Asia. Here, Marshall cautioned against assuming phallic symbolism too readily, suggesting instead that later Shaivism may have absorbed and reinterpreted these older baetylic cults. Finally, he identified a third class, the so-called ring-stones, ranging from a few inches to several feet in diameter. Some, he noted, could hardly have served a utilitarian purpose and were more plausibly understood as yoni symbols or as fetish stones endowed with magical properties.

Taken together, these three classes—the phallic, the baetylic, and the ring-stones—illustrated for Marshall not only the deep antiquity of stone worship in India but also the difficulty of assigning definitive meanings across millennia. While some specimens almost certainly represented the linga and yoni, others might equally have functioned as protective amulets, gaming pieces, or memorial stones. What could be said with greater confidence was that the symbolic tradition of aniconic worship, later to be central to Hindu practice, had roots traceable at least as far back as Harappa and Mohenjo-daro.

But that’s Marshall’s interpretation. Conjecture. Let’s now explore the exclusivity of phallic symbolism to Indic traditions. Like Swastika and Master of Animals, could this too be a more universal motif? If so, the association with Shiva becomes a little shaky.

Turns out it might just be. The oldest Harappan specimen goes back to within 2500 BC. More than 20,000 years older is an eight-inch artifact recovered from the Hohle Fels Cave in Germany that has universally been identified as a phallus.11 The jury still remains out on whether it’s cultic or utilitarian. Or even a mere sex toy? At any rate, it remains the oldest material representation of male sexuality ever discovered. But given the ambiguity on its religious status, we need more. And it just so happens that we have many more.

Phallic iconography is part of the whole fertility trope which itself is a most ubiquitous feature across time and space. The Mother Goddess theme is also part of the same set. The oldest specimen clearly identified as ritualistic has to be the clay phallus of the Cucuteni Culture.12 It dates back to as early as 3000 BC. Besides, we find phalluses in almost all cultures of remote to late antiquity, from Minoan Greek to Roman to Egyptian to Japanese and many more. The Sumerian charter myth, in fact, posits the Euphrates itself as a river made with Enki’s ejaculate.13 Egypt, similarly, saw the Nile as Osiris’.

This weakens the case for Harappan Shiva. But all hope isn’t lost yet. Phallus worship elsewhere does not inherently disprove phallus worship here. The Egyptians and the Mesopotamians may very well have their own phallic cult, we did too. Theirs was associated with Osiris or Enki, ours could be just as associated with Shiva or Rudra. The only way to affirm this, however, is to establish beyond doubt the antiquity of Shiva’s phallic association. As we’ve already established, Shiva itself is a later concept, absent in the early Vedas, so let’s go with Rudra. So the question can be articulated thus:

Was Rudra associated with the lingam?

We could even broaden the question to say, was _any_ Vedic deity associated with the lingam? To further simplify, does the cultic phallus exist throughout the Hindu scripture?

Unfortunately, no. There is no mention of the linga in any sense of the word in any of the four Vedas. The first time it’s hinted at is only in the _Shvetashvatara Upanishad_ in mantra 4.11:14

     _yo yoniṃ-yonim adhitiṣṭhaty eko yasminn idaṃ saṃ ca vi caiti sarvam,
    tam īśānaṃ varadaṃ devam īḍyaṃ nicāyyemāṃ śāntim atyantam eti._
    “(One) attains this highest peace, having thoroughly known Him, the Governor, the Giver of gifts, (including salvation), the Luminous and praiseworthy Being, who, being one, governs (or supports) every cause, (primary as well as secondary), in whom this whole world is absorbed (by destruction) and assumes various forms (at the time of creation).” (trans. Shastri)

Although this too is an indirect reference and does not specifically mention the word “linga,” it can be read as an indirect reference since it speaks, albeit metaphorically, of God presiding over every “yoni.” But Shvetashvatara Upanishad has only been dated to the later centuries of the first millennium BC even by the most generous of estimates. If the Indus phallus were Shiva, wouldn’t it find copious mention in earlier texts?

Eminent Indian orientalist Sir Ramakrishna Gopal Bhandarkar goes even further and posits that the lingam association was alien even to Patanjali, himself a contemporary of Pushyamitra Shunga from the second century BC.15 He also references Vima Kadphises’ “Shiva and Bull” coins to question the association during at least early Kushan years.16 This however does not sound terribly conclusive because it’s based on a very tiny handful of representations and not a holistic array of textual and material evidence. It’s like looking at one image of a standing Shiva today and concluding that since he’s represented in a human form and not phallic, the lingam didn’t exist. That’s deficient. But the fact that the association did not exist in Vedic works remains uncontested. Before we draw the final conclusion, however, let’s revisit the Rigveda for something more interesting.

Lingam is the Sanskrit name for the male genital, but not the only one. A lesser-known synonym is _shishna_.17 While the Rigveda does not mention lingam, it does mention shishna in the compound _shishnadeva_ in 7.21.5:18

     _na yātava indra jūjuvur no na vandanā śaviṣṭha vedyābhiḥ,
    sa śardhad aryo viṣuṇasya jantor mā śiśnadevā api gur ṛtaṃ naḥ._
    “Let not the rākṣasas, Indra, do us harm; let not the evil spirits do harm to our progeny, most powerful (Indra); let the sovereign lord, (Indra), exert himself (in the restraint) of disorderly beings, so that the unchaste may not disturb our rite.” (trans. Wilson)

In this verse, shishnadeva is used as a common noun for those who worship the phallus.19 The verse itself, as evident, is an ode to Indra, the ruler of the Vedic pantheon. Isn’t it interesting that Indra is being invoked against “unchaste” phallus worshippers? Clearly, these phallus worshippers are outside the Vedic fold, possibly even Aryan fold. What’s even more interesting is that Rudra is always spoken of in exalted terms throughout the Rigveda. The conclusions safe to draw at this point, then, are that a) Rudra isn’t associated with the phallus and b) the phallus isn’t seen reverentially by the Vedic community. To further reinforce this readon, we get in 10.99.3:20

     _sa vājaṃ yātā́paduṣpadā́ yant svarṣātā́ pari ṣadatsaniṣyan,
    anarvā́ yac chatadurasyá vedo ghnañ chiśnadevā́n abhi varpasā́ bhūt._
    “Going to the battle, marching with easy gait, desiring the spoil, he set himself to the acquisition of all(wealth). Invincible, destroying the phallus-worshippers, he won by his prowess whatever wealth (was concealed in the city) with the hundred gates.” (trans. Wilson)

Here, the deity is literally being called upon to destroy those who worship the phallus. Clearly not a very reverential attitude. Do note that while the 7th _mandala_ is old, the tenth is the one with the newest vintage.

In sum, phallic worship may have been part of the Indus culture as was the case in most unrelated animistic and pagan societies of the time. But no reading of available pieces of evidence indicates that it had anything to do with Shiva as we know him today. If anything, it’s more likely that the motif was absorbed into the Rudra-Shiva iconography in much later post-Vedic times. Such assimilations and diffusions are extremely common in all situations involving cultural contacts.

## Conclusion

So, if the “Pashupati” isn’t conclusively Shiva, the phallus isn’t conclusively Shivalinga, and the Mohenjo-daro mound isn’t conclusively a stupa, what could be said about the nature of Harappan faith? It was certainly animistic, no doubts there. And it had a number of elements we continue to have as part of our own tradition today, both Hindu and Buddhist—the Mother Goddess that we now worship as Durga, Kali, etc.; the vermilion or _sindoor_ , which to this day Hindu women apply to their forehead upon marriage; the holy fig tree or _peepul_ , which is still worshipped as divine; the Swastika, which is still held sacred by both Hindus and Buddhists alike; and so on.

Yet these continuities, however striking, do not constitute proof of direct religious lineage. Similar symbols and practices emerged independently across numerous ancient civilizations, suggesting universal human responses to the sacred rather than exclusive cultural inheritance. The Egyptian ankh, the Mesopotamian tree of life, the Celtic sacred groves—all speak to humanity’s shared spiritual vocabulary without implying direct transmission. What we observe in the Indus Valley may well represent the foundational layer from which later traditions drew, but this does not make the Harappans proto-Hindus or proto-Buddhists any more than it makes them proto-Egyptians.

The archaeological record suggests a sophisticated urban civilization with complex ritual practices, but one that remains fundamentally alien to our understanding. The undeciphered script holds the key to their worldview, and until that lock is opened, we can only speculate about their spiritual beliefs. What seems certain is that they possessed a rich symbolic culture involving sacred animals, fertility imagery, ritual bathing, and hierarchical social structures—all elements that would later find expression in various Indic traditions.

Perhaps most importantly, this analysis reveals how modern ideological needs have shaped archaeological interpretation. The eagerness to claim the Indus Valley for either Hindu or Buddhist heritage reflects contemporary political anxieties rather than ancient realities. Both traditions have legitimate claims to antiquity within the subcontinent, but neither requires validation from Harappan civilization to establish their authenticity or importance. Rather than forcing these ancient peoples into modern categories, we might better honor their memory by acknowledging the limitations of our knowledge and resisting the temptation to conscript the past into present-day ideological battles.

The Harappans built great cities, developed sophisticated technologies, and created enduring symbols of human spiritual aspiration. That should be legacy enough without demanding they also serve as the ancestors of any particular contemporary faith. In the end, the most honest conclusion may be the humblest one—we simply don’t know what the Harappans believed, and our ignorance says more about the complexities of human spirituality than any confident identification ever could.
