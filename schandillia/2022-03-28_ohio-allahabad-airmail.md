*How an Indian City Found Its Spot in Postal and Aviation History*

For some intriguing albeit seemingly unrelated background, we’ll start with America. The year is 1910, seven years since Orville Wright’s first flight into aviation history. By now, flying is to the Wrights what driving would be to us later.

Enter Max Morehouse, an aviation enthusiast and a reasonably influential businessman in Columbus, Ohio. At the time, Morehouse ran the _Rowland, Morehouse and Martens Company_ , one of Columbus’ largest modern departmental stores of the time selling high-end merchandise. Actually, wait, before we go any further with Morehouse, let’s talk a bit about the Wrights. Over the past seven years, the brothers had built several _Wright Flyers_ , which is what they called their flight contraption. But to make any money out of those, they needed to make more pilots. So they had an idea.

In March 1910, one of the world’s earliest flying schools, the _Wright School of Aviation_ took flight. This was at an open field near Montgomery, Alabama. We still know the site as the _Maxwell Air Force Base_. The first batch of trained pilots had graduated by summer. Now it was time to showcase the skills to attract further traction for the idea of flight. This was crucial because, at the time, the layman still viewed human flight with an understandable amount of suspicion and dread. Someone had to demonstrate how safe it was. To that end, the _Wright Exhibition Company_ was formed. The idea was to have a select group of star pilots go from town to town performing public flight exhibitions and injecting confidence. These exhibits would also serve as a secondary revenue stream through ticket sales. Now back to Morehouse.

## The Spectacle in Ohio

One October morning, the Wrights received a letter addressed to their exhibition company. It was from Max Morehouse. It was a request to airlift a bolt of silk ribbons from Dayton and drop them off at Columbus. Every October, Morehouse ran an annual autumn sale at his Columbus store and silk ribbons were a big attraction. Coincidentally, Columbus was also the site of a Wright exhibit in 1910. So he thought of riding this spectacle to maximize press coverage. The request involved an amount of aerial acrobatics over the city of Columbus along with the original transport assignment. The Wrights readily agreed as it’d garner them much attention too.  
  
Everything in place, the flight took off at 10:45 AM on November 7. It was a _Wright Model B_ and carried a twenty-pound payload, all silk. The flight path traced the Dayton–Columbus railroad line over a distance of seventy miles. Landing shortly before noon, the sole person on the flight, the pilot, was greeted by a crowd of over three thousand. The whole operation cost Morehouse an eye-popping $5,000 in 1910 money, almost $162,000 today. But the bet proved so successful he broke even in less than three days. November 7 went down in history as not just the day of the first cargo flight ever but also the first chartered service.

So this was the story of history’s first air cargo. But the first-ever airmail service was still three months and eight thousand miles away. Before we get to it, though, we need to go back once again, back to the year of Orville Wright’s first flight. Actually, let’s go even further—to 1898.

## Europe in the Skies

Remember the Cold War race to the Moon between the Americans and the Russians? A similar race was in play between America and France at the dawn of the twentieth century, this one over powered flights. Whoever conquered the skies, would win this round. Spearheading the French side was a lawyer and flight enthusiast named Ernest Archdeacon. Having completed his first balloon flight in 1883, a century since the Montgolfier brothers’ first, Archdeacon was determined to make France the first to tame the skies. To that end, he founded the _Aéro-Club de France_ in 1898. Over a century down the line, it still remains the apex body of sports aviation in the country. Upon inception, its mission was “to encourage aerial locomotion.” Two years later, the club announced a prize of 100,000 francs, more than half a million euros today, to stir things up. Sponsored by a Jewish oil magnate, this prize was for anyone who could complete a round-trip flight from Saint-Cloud to the Eiffel Tower and back in under half an hour. One can cycle that distance in under half an hour today but back then, it was no mean feat to accomplish flying. A Brazilian aviator won the contest.

Did you know that just like a father of everything else, there’s a “father of avionics” too? And did you know it isn’t either of the Wrights? That title goes, instead, to a French-American civil engineer by the name of Octave Chanute. Chanute didn’t invent flying machines, he just mentored those who did. The Wrights couldn’t have achieved any of what they did without both technical and commercial help from this man. In 1901, an article by Chanute showed up in the June issue of _Revue Scientifique_. Of the many who read this piece was Louis Ferdinand Ferber, a pioneer of French avionics. Hooked on the idea, he wrote to Chanute and learned of the Wright brothers’ experiments. What followed was a building frenzy for the first powered aircraft ever.

Two years later, Ferber wrote to Archdeacon demanding him to “not let the aeroplane be achieved in America first.” Archdeacon agreed that the first powered flight _had_ to be French. This, of course, didn’t quite work out as planned and the Wright brothers ended up on top. The endeavor, however, wasn’t entirely pointless. What Archdeacon and Ferber kickstarted in France attracted attention from as far as across the English channel, but more on that a little later. First came Gabriel Voisin, who was introduced to Archdeacon by Ferber.

Under Archdeacon’s patronage, Voisin went on to create Europe’s first aircraft powered by an engine. The craft took its first successful flight on Jan 13, 1908, a good five years after the Wright Flyer. The pilot’s name was Henry Farman, an Anglo-French aviator and inventor. Farman marks the beginning of English interest in avionics. Soon after this flight, he and his brother started modifying Voisin’s aircraft and by the following spring, came up with _Farman III_ , a pusher biplane that became the template for many contemporary airplanes including the _Sommer 1910_ biplane. Designed by the French–Belgian aviator Roger Sommer, this was one of the most successful Farman-class flying machines. One of these was owned by no less than Charles Rolls, the Rolls of Rolls-Royce.

The same year that Farman took Voisin’s biplane on its maiden spin, about five hundred aristocrats, inventors, scientists, and engineers gathered at the Claridge’s Hotel on a wealthy, forty-year-old Englishman’s invitation. His name was Walter George Windham. At the time, Windham was a King’s Messenger his job being to secretly hand-deliver documents of grave importance to British embassies. Having once attended an event in Paris the previous year, he was excited to witness the progress the French had made in avionics. It’s this excitement that led to the creation of the _Aeroplane Club_ , an assortment of some of the best brains and most generous patrons brought together in the pursuit of placing Britain in the skies. The huddle at the Claridge’s was this club gathering for the first time.

On August 10, 1909, French aviator Hubert Latham delivered to Windham what’s believed to be the world’s first mail delivered by air. Latham, by the way, was also the world’s first pilot to have crash-landed on water, but I digress. Remember Sommer’s wildly successful Farman-class machine? A now-defunct British automaker named Humber brought it to England. Not the machine itself, just the design. Under Sommer’s license, Humber built the first English prototype called Humber-Sommer.

## The Spectacle in India

England, in those days, ruled over much of the world with colonies on nearly every continent. Among these was India, the wealthiest of them all. Initially owned by a private chartered business, the subcontinent had been under direct Crown rule for a while now. While Britain’s money bag was India, India’s money bag was two territories named Agra and Oudh. In 1902, the imperial government decided to merge the two into a single administrative unit—the United Provinces of Agra and Oudh, United Provinces for short. Much of India's affluence and influence at the time could be narrowed down to pockets within this territory. One such pocket was Allahabad. And Allahabad’s affluence was mostly concentrated in one stately mansion recently acquired by a lawyer of Kashmiri Pandit descent.

Motilal Nehru’s father had migrated to Agra after having lost everything in Delhi during the mutiny of 1857. Having lost his father at an early age, Motilal had worked hard to cut himself a stable career as a civil attorney in Allahabad. His windfall came in 1909 when he secured approval to appear in the Privy Council of Great Britain. This earned him unprecedented clout and turned his fortune around almost overnight. His son, Jawaharlal, was then in London studying law at Inner Temple Inn.

It’s against this backdrop that Allahabad hosted the 1911 _Annual United Provinces Industrial and Agricultural Exhibition_. Commander Walt Windham had, by then, shored up a considerable reputation as a skilled driver and one of the pioneers of British aviation. The organizers of the Allahabad exhibit expectedly wanted the maximum return on their investment the only way to accomplish which was by increasing the footfall. But that called for a star attraction. Something unprecedented, unexpected. A showstopper, if you will. So they reached out to Windham.

The idea was to have him perform a bunch of aerial acrobatics to enthrall visitors. Nobody in India knew airplanes then. This was bound to be an unparalleled hit. Windham agreed readily. He had a fleet of several flying machines of his own, among them, a Humber–Sommer biplane. He sailed to India with two pilots, two mechanics, and six airplanes in large wooden crates. Once here, he had the crates transported to Allahabad by railroad and special trucks. One of the pilots was a French guy named Henri Péquet.

One day during his stay in Allahabad, Windham had a visitor. It was the chaplain of the Church of the Sacred Trinity, the second oldest church in all of Allahabad and around—Reverend W E S Holland. The Church was raising funds for a hostel and Rev. Holland wanted Windham to carry some mail as part of his payload. The idea was to let people write letters and have them carried by air. At least part of the distance if not the entire stretch. Being a novelty, he could charge enthusiasts a premium. This premium, about four shillings above regular postal rates, could then be used toward the hostel.

This sounds like what Max Morehouse did in partnership with the Wrights some three months earlier in November 1910. The only difference, though, is that Morehouse had a commercial interest unlike Rev. Holland’s philanthropic. But in those days you couldn’t carry mail without the administration’s prior approval. So Sir Geoffrey Charles, the postmaster of the province, was approached. He said yes and so did the Director-General of the Indian Postal Service. The “surcharge” was fixed at six shillings, about forty paise. The flight was to be from a polo ground in Allahabad to Naini, a railroad junction six miles away across the Ganges. A patch had been cleared for the airplane just outside Naini jail.

Naini was chosen for a reason. It was the closest such place to Allahabad from where you could take long-distance trains for places like Calcutta, the capital, and Bombay, the harbor. Thus, letters addressed to virtually any part of the world could be carried from Naini to Bombay by train and onward by ship. There were rules around what you could and could not send. Only letters or postcards were allowed, each weighing not more than an ounce. You had to get your mail to the church where, upon payment of all fees and surcharge, your mail would be stamped with _First aerial post_ , and received. The flight was to take off on the first day of the exhibition from the polo grounds, also the exhibition’s venue.

Péquet was to fly a Humber–Sommer with the letters and postcards. By the time the submission window closed, the payload stood at over six thousand letters and postcards and weighed about thirty-three pounds or fifteen kilos. These were addressed to some of the most exotic recipients on Earth—King George V of England, King Albert I of Belgium, King Victor Emmanuel III of Italy, Queen Wilhelmina of the Netherlands, and many more. Of course, there were also letters to friends and family. Amongst these was a letter from one of the event’s biggest patrons. He was writing to his son who was then studying law in London. Motilal Nehru was known to be an early adopter of all things new in town. At one time, he owned the only car in Allahabad.

On the designated day, Péquet took off in his biplane at about 5:30 PM. Flying at thirty-eight miles an hour following the road through the woods, Péquet took about half an hour to reach Naini. The altitude throughout the flight remained no more than 160 feet, roughly sixteen stories. Years later when Péquet recalled the flight in an interview, he spoke of how crossing the Ganges was the most nervous part of the trip. “It was not the water,” he said, “it was the crocodiles.”

The chaplain’s idea proved to be a spectacular success. His church raised a total of 2,500 rupees in surcharge and donations. Doesn’t sound much today, but a gram of gold was less than two rupees that year. The church got its hostel, and the world got its first official airmail—on February 18, 1911.

So that’s the story of how a French engineer helped an American invention, how copies of which were made back in France, how one of which was brought to India by an English aviator, and how he then gave the world its first airmail to raise funds for a church. One caveat—the first airmail _that was carried in an airplane_. This qualification is because technically, mail had been traveling by flight since 1859 in America. Only, not in airplanes but in balloons.
