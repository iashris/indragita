# Who Ended Nalanda?

*A Critical Analysis of the Sources and Debates on the Fate of the Mahavihara*

- audience: only_paid
- url: https://www.schandillia.com/p/nalanda

---

The destruction of Nalanda is among the most hotly contested topics in the subcontinent’s history. While the most debated ones like Aryan origins, beef in ancient India, and the antiquity of Sanskrit confine their jurisdictions to within the Indic ecosystem, this one represents a violent injection of a non-Indic one. The prevailing narrative, especially in Hindu nationalist circles, accuses a Turkic invader named Bakhtiyar Khilji. It posits the Delhi Sultanate’s eastern campaign that brought along a wave of repeated raids on the cities and monasteries of Bihar, Nalanda among them. This position, however, has been met with a serious challenge from the other side of the ideological spectrum that argues for a more indigenous, sectarian conflict as the cause of destruction. In this framework, Nalanda was a Buddhist stronghold envied and eventually destroyed by antagonistic Brahmins of the region. Both hypotheses enjoy widespread endorsement within their respective ideological confines which is what makes it a highly polarizing subject. Here, we’ll attempt to evaluate the substance in both scholarships and determine the winner on purely non-ideological grounds.

Before we get to its destruction, we must understand its inception. Not just for the sake of learning, but for the sake of the argument. How Nalanda was born, believe it or not, has a strong bearing on how it died and we’ll eventually see how. Contrary to what many believe, what we’re talking about here isn’t something all that ancient. While Taxila goes back to the fifth century BC, Nalanda only appeared in the fifth century AD which is when Taxila ultimately fell to the Huns and ceased to exist.

So our story starts in the first half of the fifth century when much of North India was under the Guptas. Today’s Bihar was then Magadha and formed the core of the Gupta polity. Pataliputra, today’s Patna, was its capital. On the throne was Kumaragupta I, son of the dynasty’s best-known name, Chandragupta. The Guptas were a Viaishnavite dynasty, a sharp departure from the prevailing Buddhist dominance in the region. The Mauryas, the Greeks, the Kushans—ever since Ashoka’s conversion, except for a few brief pockets of uncertainties, North India had remained under a largely Buddhist rule. The Guptas were the first major power that actively practiced and patronized a Vedic religion.

This, however, did not translate into any anti-Buddhist hostility, at least not as a state policy. By the time Kumaragupta took the throne, Taxila was already gone or at least in its death throes. Besides, it was impractical far from the Gupta capital in Pataliputra. So he decreed the establishment of a similar center of learning, equal in grandeur, not too far from the capital. This was to be a multidisciplinary residential institution with instructions in both Vedic and Buddhist studies. A site rich in lotus stalks or _nāla_ , was chosen about fifty miles southeast of the capital and soon enough, the _sangharama_ or monastery of Nalanda was born. Buddhist sources record Kumaragupta’s name as Shakraditya, likely an honorific. Two centuries later, the university would host the famous Chinese pilgrim Hsüen Tsang and find corroboration in his works. By then, the small monastery would’ve burgeoned into a full-blown _mahavihara_ or university. Throughout this period, the university enjoyed more than generous monetary as well as material patronage from successive Gupta monarchs, something corroborated by seals, coins, and other epigraphical artifacts recovered from its ruins. Patronage also came from non-Gupta sources in the form of village grants and endowments. At its peak, the institution hosted students and teachers from as far away as Korea, Indonesia, Afghanistan, Persia, and even Turkey. Hsüen Tsang pegs the resident headcount at 10,000 students and 2,000 teachers which may have been an exaggeration. The number may have been closer to 3,000 as posited by another Chinese pilgrim, Yijing. These students studied both Buddhist as well as Vedic philosophies, both Pali as well as Sanskrit texts.1 We know this from the accounts of the famous Chinese pilgrim Xuanzang, who visited the university in the seventh century (more on him later).2

While the Guptas were inclusive in their religious outlook and patronized Buddhism with the same vigor as Vaishnavism, as Nalanda demonstrates, it must still be noted that this was a period of dramatic doctrinal shift. The Buddhists were losing political influence after centuries of dominance. Of course, there was an undeniable element of Brahmin-Buddhist animosity in the society. How this animosity expressed itself remains a matter of debate. We do know that in the middle of the sixth century a major spate of violence, particularly directed at Buddhists, swept through the region in the form of Mihirakula. His father was none other than Toramana who had destroyed Taxila earlier. Other than that, not much can be said with confidence about sectarian hostilities under the Guptas. What remains unimpeachable, however, is the enthusiastic Gupta patronage of Buddhist institutions despite their Vedic affiliations. And Nalanda is an important attestation to this fact, albeit it’s a misconception that the university curriculum was confined to Buddhism.

So this was the story of Nalanda’s birth and the circumstances of the time. Now that we understand the state of the society and the attitudes of the royalty surrounding Nalanda’s development, let’s finally get to its destruction. While mainstream scholarship still maintains Muslims as the perpetrators, Bakhtiyar Khilji in particular, there has been significant pushback to that position in recent years. In fact, the idea of non-Muslim perpetrators goes back centuries but now with the democratization of history, the position has gathered unprecedented steam. Therefore, we’ll first examine this position and then move on to the other, more mainstream one.

## It Was Brahmins

This school of thought goes back to at least the seventeenth century, if not further, and a prominent voice in this camp is that of Dwijendra Narayan Jha. A celebrated scholar and member of the Indian Council of Historical Research, Jha is best known for his controversial work on India’s relationship with beef in remote antiquity. He passed away in 2021 but left behind a significant body of work for us to pore over for valuable insights. One such insight pertains to Nalanda and comes from his address at the 2006 Indian History Congress, albeit as a fleeting reference in a much longer discussion on Brahminical excesses against Buddhists in early medieval India.

Jha posits that not only was Nalanda destroyed by the Brahmins, but also that this was a mere continuation of something that had been the norm for centuries. To that end, he speaks of a Kalachuri king perpetrating several destructive campaigns against Buddhist monasteries in Magadha in the eleventh century.3 He probably means the Kalachuris of Tripuri, also known as Later Kalachuris, because the original folded around the middle of the seventh century. Both Kalachuri polities were Shaivite and it’s easy to see why they could be generally hostile toward Vaishnavite Guptas and particularly hostile toward Buddhists. But that doesn’t seem to be the case in reality, for the Kalachuris in both iterations, just like the Guptas, are recorded to have maintained an overall amicable relationship with non-Vedic communities like Buddhists. Jha references Tibetan traditions as the source of his claim. He also finds further corroboration in the writings of B. N. S. Yadava, Ram Sharan Sharma, and Krishna Mohan Shrimali, all historians of great stature and all of whom agree with the line of reasoning taken by historian and archeologist D. R. Patil, another name Jha offers as his source.4

Between the eighth and the twelfth centuries, the period leading up to Nalanda’s destruction, the Magadha region was ruled by Bengal’s Pala dynasty which had taken over from the Kalachuris without much effort. It was under this rule that centers like Vikramshila and Odantapuri (now Biharsharif) emerged to supplement Nalanda. In _The Antiquarian Remains in Bihar_ , Patil argues that this was a particularly detrimental phase for Buddhism, due partly to new sectarian fractures and partly to Muslim incursions into Magadha.5 He even mentions Bakhtiyar Khilji as a prime antagonist but is also quick to de-hyphenate him from Nalanda. According to him, it’s Odantapuri, which Muslims erroneously referred to as the city of Bihar, that Khilji destroyed and not Nalanda. In defense, he points to a conspicuous absence of any Islamic structures in Nalanda. Since it was common for Muslim invaders to build atop destroyed pagan structures, Patil concludes that Nalanda was likely untouched by Khilji.6

But this isn’t all. In the same work, Patil also defers to a Tibetan source, the one Jha is likely to have meant while addressing the Congress in 2006, that speaks of a scuffle between the Buddhists and the Brahmins at Nalanda. This source is _Pag-sam Jong-zang_. It was written by a Tibetan Buddhist monk named Sumpa Khan-Po Yece Pal Jor and it forms the basis of almost all _Brahmins-destroyed-Nalanda_ advocates. The work recounts an episode where the Brahmins, upset with the Buddhists over something, meditated for twelve years and burned down the library at Nalanda with magic embers.7 This is forwarded as a strong allusion to the hostile, often violent relationship between the two communities in general and Nalanda’s destruction being a result of such an episode in particular.

Among the illustrious names that vouch for this Tibetan account is Satish Chandra Vidyabhusan a noted Sanskrit and Pali scholar from the early nineteenth-century Bengal. In _A History of Indian Logic_ , Vidyabhusan elaborates that the mendicants were furious over being insulted by a bunch of novice Buddhist initiates during a religious sermon at the newly erected Sun temple. He names Kukutasiddha, a Magadhan minister, as the man behind the temple.8 Quoting Vidyabhushan verbatim is Radha Kumud Mookerji in his work, _Ancient Indian Education_.9 Historian Sukumar Dutt is another prominent figure who defers to the Tibetan account in favor of this theory.10

The case for Brahmins as perpetrators seems pretty much airtight here. In sum, the argument comes on two fronts. First is that Nalanda already went into decline with the rise of centers like Vikramshila and Odantapuri and the attrition that followed. This claim hinges on the position that imperial grants to Nalanda severely tanked under the Palas who favored Vikramshila over Nalanda. The Islamic incursion, if any, was only the final blow to an already moribund institution which would have died an organic death anyway. The other front is that the decline of Nalanda went in tandem with the decline of Buddhism in North India and that much of it was attributable to the ever-increasing Brahmin-Buddhist hostilities. The most defining episode in this regard was that of Brahmin mendicants setting the library ablaze in response to an insult by the Buddhists. So this is where the camp represented by the likes of D. N. Jha stands. Now let’s explore the other side of the argument, the one that blames the Muslims.

## A Scrutiny of Patil

Before we examine the proponents of this position, it’s worth seeing if the one we discussed is truly as airtight as it seems. D. N. Jha, if we’d recall, speaks of the Kalachuri campaign into Magadha in this context. At first crack, this may seem like a reasonable point given the Kalachuris’ Shaivite leanings and the general anti-Buddhist sentiments among the Shaivites of the time. Per Tibetan sources, it was a Kalachuri king named Lakshmikarna—Karna for short—who mounted the invasion. Magadha was then ruled by the Palas and saw several of its Buddhist monasteries destroyed in this episode.11 But is it likely that Jha overrated this destruction in both scale and quantum? The same Tibetan accounts that speak of Karna’s invasion also say that it was a solitary campaign, in which a grand total of five Buddhist monks lost their lives, and which resulted in a truce between the two parties.12 Noted Indologist Vasudev Vishnu Mirashi points to a stone inscription at Sarnath and confirms that Buddhist monasteries did quite well under Karna.13 The inscription, dated to October 4, 1058, records the imperial commissioning of a project where a Buddhist text was copied and made grants were made to a monastery for its recitation. The king named as patron is Karna.14 To further support his assertions, Mirashi offers several Buddhist figures recovered from the Chedi country, some even from the capital.15 Despite their Shaivite leanings, the Kalachuris not only regarded Vishnu but also Buddha himself, as the tenth avatar thereof.

Although we’re yet to touch upon Nalanda, the general Kalachuri attitude toward religions, Buddhism in particular, makes it difficult to associate with them any large-scale anti-Buddhist mission that could damage an institution of Nalanda’s stature. So if not the Kalachuris, then who? Jha doesn’t specify. In a similar vein, the problem with Patil’s claims is that he relies too much on the Palas’ Shaivite affiliations and negligence of Nalanda as the reasons for its decay. History, however, tells us that like the Guptas and the Kalachuris, the Palas too were mighty expansive on their religious patronage. Buddhism not only flourished under them but enjoyed all kinds of royal endowments. He mentions Dharmapala, the king who founded Vikramshila, and his son Devapala as the ones whose negligence dealt the biggest blow to Nalanda.16 Contemporary records, however, fail to bear this out.

Nalanda and other sanctuaries in Magadha had always attracted curious visitors from distant lands, especially China. A most recognized name in this regard is Xuanzang, who visited in the seventh century and spent more than a decade gathering manuscripts for his way back home. In his accounts, he described Nalanda as a “veritable monastic city, consisting of some ten monasteries, within a brick wall enclosure, and comprising, along with the dwelling-places of the monks, a number of halls for meetings and prayer.”17 But, much of Xuanzang’s experiences come to us not from his own writings but from those of one of his contemporaries named Hui Li who, in a biographical work titled _The Life of Hiuen-Tsiang_ , states that Nalanda was endowed with revenues from a hundred villages.18 Shortly after Xuanzang, Nalanda received yet another Chinese traveler, a monk named Yijing. Like his predecessor, Yijing too left generous accounts of his stay in the bustling _mahavihara_. Interestingly, though, his accounts double the number of villages under Nalanda to two hundred.19

So this was the state of Nalanda in the middle of the eighth century when it was likely under Harsha’s influence. The Palas took over before the end of the century. Question is, how far did Devapala’s negligence hurt the mahavihara? And how bad was the hostility against Buddhists at the time? As for Devapala’s attitude toward Nalanda, we don’t have a whole lot of artifacts to work with. There is, however, one copper plate that goes back to the middle of the ninth century and bears his name. This inscription has been identified as an imperial charter announcing the grant of five additional villages to the monastery at Nalanda upon the request of a Sumatran king named Balaputradeva.20 Note that this was besides the two hundred already assigned as per Chinese reports. There is no record of a Pala king ever withdrawing an endowment made by their predecessors.

Yet another attestation to Devapala’s active patronage of Nalanda comes from a figure of Tara, an important female member of the Buddhist pantheon, recovered from a site not far from Patna. It bears three inscriptions in crude Sanskrit that record the installation of the idol at Nalanda by one Manjusrideva in the thirty-fifth regnal year of Devapala.21 The king’s active participation in the oversight of Nalanda’s affairs finds further affirmation in the “Ghosrawan inscription,” thus named after the place of its finding. Discovered in 1848 by the British archeologist Captain Markham Kittoe who misread Ghosrawan as Pesserawa,22 announces the appointment of one Viradeva as the rector of Nalanda by the “Lord of the land, Sri Devapala.”23 These are just from Devapala’s reign, plenty of similar artifacts exist for other Pala rulers as well. Thus, as evident, the polity was anything but negligent to Nalanda. As for their society’s relationship with Buddhism, it was customary for Pala copper inscriptions to open with a reference to the king as _parama-saugata_ or “a devout follower of Buddha,” Sugata being a synonym for Buddha.24 While this does not conclusively negate all Brahmin-Buddhist animosity, it sure negates to a significant extent the possibility of such animosity leading to the destruction of a sanctuary like Nalanda. An endeavor of that scale simply wouldn’t be possible without imperial consent if not participation.

## Tibetan Sources

A large part of Jha’s assertions is based on Tibetan sources. That, in fact, is the case with everyone in the camp, from Vidyabhushana to Mookerji to Patil to Yadava. The source in question, as we’ve already noted is Sumpa’s Pag-sam Jon-zang. Let’s recall its account once again to better judge its viability before we arrive at any conclusion.

So a Magadhan minister by the name of Kukutasiddha had erected a Hindu temple at Nalanda after a recent attack by _Turushka_ raiders. It’s at this temple that two Brahmin mendicants showed up during a religious activity shortly after consecration. Out of disdain at their destitution, some Buddhists tossed dirty water at them to shoo them away. Insulted, the mendicants vowed revenge and performed penance for twelve years to appease the Sun God. Once done, they performed a propitiatory _yajna_ and tossed the resulting embers into the Buddhist structures of vihara setting them ablaze in a massive conflagration. While the library burned to the ground, the manuscripts miraculously escaped harm thanks to a last-minute divine intervention.

That’s the story related by Sumpa. An immediate problem with this account is its fantastical character which makes it hard to take it as literal. It sure could be an allegory for an inter-communal friction prevalent in the society, but beyond that, any conclusion would be far-fetched. The presence of a Hindu temple on the premises doesn’t help much either since both Kalachuri and Pala royalties professed Shaivism. An interesting element in the story, however, is the mention of Turushka raids preceding the erection of Kukutasiddha’s alleged temple. Turushka is a corruption of Turks and is near-universally admitted as the medieval Indian name for Muslim invaders. This messes up the timelines significantly. The Islamic invasion of Magadha only happened toward the final years of the eleventh century. That would place Kukutasiddha’s temple comfortably in the twelfth century. By then, Buddhism and Nalanda were already in decline, so Sumpa’s account should have no bearing on the monastery’s fate. Note that Kukutasiddha’s identity remains a mystery. But even that isn’t the biggest problem with Sumpa’s story.

The biggest problem is its timeline. Sumpa lived as recently as the eighteenth century which makes his work far from primary. Now the question is, where did Sumpa learn of this account? Frankly, we don’t know. But there does exist an older work by yet another Tibetan lama named Kun-dga'-snying-po, better known by his Sanskrit pseudonym, Taranatha. His work _dpal dus kyi 'khor lo'i chos bskor gyi byung khungs nyer mkho_ (History of Buddhism in India) tells us of the exact same episode, just with a few additional details. While in Sumpa’s account it was only washwater, in this one there were also dogs. Taranatha’s account also embellishes the twelve-year penance with a little more drama where one of the mendicants threatens his companion with death should he emerge from the penance without any superpowers. Overall, however, it’s the same story—Buddhist monks offend Brahmin mendicants who then burn down the university in revenge.25

But Taranatha doesn’t help much, for he too is no primary source. His work only predates Sumpa by about a century. We still have a gap of more than four hundred years to reconcile with. And nothing older exists to corroborate this story. There’s also this supernatural element to contend with anyway. There’s also this uneasy reference to Turushkas that warrants explanation. Just like Sumpa would later, Taranatha too speaks of Turushka raids in the lead-up to the alleged conflagration. Who are these Turushkas?

At the time of Nalanda’s death, Bengal was ruled by the Sena dynasty and the man on the throne was Lakshmana Sena. But Taranatha qualifies Turushkas as “petty rulers of Bhamgala and other places” and names the king as _zla-ba_ , Tibetan name for Chandrasena.26 History says the Sena polity pretty much disintegrated after Muslim invasions in the first half of the thirteenth century, but petty dynasts continued to rule as puppets of the Sultanate. The mainstream Sena line stops with Keshava Sena whose successors ended up as vassals to the neighboring Deva dynasty for a short while before coming under direct Sultanate suzerainty in the later decades of the thirteenth century. Chandrasena was from this tail-end of the line. That would place the Tibetan account well within the period of Sultanate rule over Bengal and its surroundings. Thus, Taranatha’s Turushkas are either these petty vassals of the Sultanate or the Sultanate itself. Either way, it has to be in the latter quarter of the thirteenth century whereas the destruction of Nalanda has been dated to the final years of the twelfth. This makes the account far from reliable as a historical insight even if one disregards the supernatural elements therein. Political historian Harbans Mukhia holds similar doubts over the historical accuracy of Taranatha’s accounts in a note to a translated work.27

If, on the other hand, we were to ignore the problem with timelines, there’s still an issue with Taranatha’s account. He names Odantapuri and Vikramshila as two other monasteries destroyed in the same Turushka wave that destroyed Nalanda.28 This piece is extremely crucial and we’ll revisit it a little later. Put a pin on it for now.

Before we let go of Taranatha, a little more light on the Sanskrit term Turushka. While proficient in Sanskrit Taranatha wrote in Tibetan and never used the word. He used the Tibetan term _Gar-lóg_. How do we know the word translates into Turushka? Nineteenth-century German Tibetologist Heinrich August Jäschke offers two meanings. One is a marauding mountain tribe from northeastern Tibet, and the other is Turushka.29 The interpretations are also acknowledged by fellow Tibetologists Franz Anton Schiefner30 and Helmut Hoffmann.31 The latter, however, leans in favor of Turushka, in agreement with Sarat Chandra Das who, in his commentary on Sumpa’s work, identifies Gar-lóg with Muslims32 and categorically denies its application to the marauders of northeastern Tibet.33 And it makes sense too. Between a mountain horde from faraway Tibet and a Muslim army that’s already in the region, the latter is, however one sees it, far likelier.

To conclude, the only two Tibetan sources we have are a) far removed from the events they describe and too fantastical to be of significant historical value, and b) allude to Turushka invasions even if taken on face value. Since we already put a pin on Taranatha’s Turushkas earlier, we will be returning to him one more time before the end of this article. First, let’s examine some secondary sources that blame it on the Muslims.

## It Was Muslims

The first name on this list has to be Jadunath Sarkar, practically the unofficial champion of the Muslim-perpetrator hypothesis. The first time he brings up the idea is in his 1928 work, _India Through the Ages_. He blames Muslim invaders for not just destroying monasteries but also wiping out Buddhism from all of North India.34 He gives gory details of wholesale destruction and slaughter at the viharas of Bihar that prompted most surviving monks to flee the place for safer havens like Nepal and Tibet. The manuscripts they carried with them for safekeeping would later be collected by the British Resident at Kathmandu and sent to Paris.35 Note that he does not name Nalanda here.

Sarkar reiterates his point a second time in his 1948 work, _The History of Bengal_. Here, he clearly mentions Bakhtiyar Khilji but still refrains from naming Nalanda. Instead, he speaks of Odantapuri and Vikramshila both being reduced to rubble by Khilji’s army in the years 1199 and 1200.36 But just before he ends, he also mentions a renowned Buddhist monk from Kashmir who visited Magadha in 1200. The monk, he claims, was so alarmed at the state of ruination at Vikramshila and Odantapuri that he fled to another vihara in northern Bengal for safety.37 No mention of Nalanda less than ten miles from Odantapuri, but it’s worth wondering why the monk didn’t flee there instead. If Taranatha’s timeline were to be followed, Nalanda should be operational in 1200, the year of this visit. Why then flee to a faraway North Bengal? Maybe there was no point, for the wave that destroyed Vikramshila and Odantapuri, also destroyed Nalanda? One can only guess.

Historian André Wink goes even further and posits Lakshmana Sena as a formidable adversary of Muslim invaders.38 This runs starkly against the earlier hypothesis that positioned the Senas as a possible Sultanate ally who invaded Magadha for the Muslims. Lakshmana Sena, if we’d recall, was the Sena dynast ruling Bengal at the time of Nalanda’s alleged destruction. Wink describes him as the last man standing against the invaders even after most of his courtiers and citizens had fled east. Khilji ultimately took possession of the kingdom in 1204 driving the king out.39 But later, he too left. From that point on, the Senas continued to rule in pockets for a few generations, likely as vassals first to the Devas and then to the Sultanate as we already discussed earlier.

Buddhologist Luis O. Gómez takes a more nuanced approach in his 2018 work on the history of Buddhism. According to him, Buddhism was already moribund when the Muslim invaders arrived. Among the major factors of its decline was a rapid assimilation of the Buddhist creed into the Hindu mainstream.40 The general scarcity of non-royal patronage and the monasteries’ reluctance to proselytize were two other factors in his views. The injection of Tantrik practices, often seen as degeneracy by Buddhist orthodoxy, has also been offered as a possible reason elsewhere. Having said that, Gómez doesn’t dismiss the role played by violent Islamic incursions either. He calls them the _coup de grâce_ that finally ended Buddhism.41 While Sarkar and Wink refrain from unambiguously naming Nalanda as the subject of Khilji’s plunder, Gómez doesn’t. And not just one but two raids as he writes that “the Turks mounted _another_ raid to further ransack what was left of Nalanda.”42

But these are all secondary sources. Sarkar, Wink, and Gómez, all rely on sources closer to the alleged destruction to make their assertions. There are two, one Indian, the other Muslim. There’s also Taranatha to revisit. Let’s see what they have to say, first the Muslim.

## Minhaj al-Siraj Juzjani

Also known as Minhaj-i Siraj or simply Minhaj, Juzjani was a Persian historian from Afghanistan who arrived in Delhi in the third decade of the thirteenth century and quickly became the imperial chronicler of the Mamluk Sultanate. In 1260, he completed his best-known work, a biography of Sultan Nasiruddin Mahmud Shah titled _Tabaqat-i Nasiri_. In this work, he also recounts the eastern campaigns of Bakhtiyar Khilji whom he personally accompanied on several occasions,43 which makes him a most valuable primary source in our conversation—Minhaj was an eyewitness to Khilji’s exploits in Bihar.

Minhaj tells us that Khilji led an army of two hundred horsemen to a violent charge upon a fortress he simply calls Bihar.44 The assumption was that it was an armed fortified city that must be conquered. The conquest was easy and all its inhabitants, whom Minhaj mistakenly identifies as Brahmins, are slaughtered. Only later when Khilji finds thousands of books and no soldiers, do they realize that what they just destroyed was in fact a university, a vihara.45 The shaven-headed inhabitants that Minhaj identified as Brahmins were actually Buddhist monks. At times, we find the name “A-dwand Bihar”46 which has led some scholars to identify the vihara as Odantapuri, today’s Biharsharif. Evidently, Minhaj isn’t familiar with the name Nalanda which is why the word doesn’t find a single mention in his work.

The question is, are we right in reading the “city of Bihar” as A-dwand Bihar and A-dwand Bihar as Odantapuri? The identification of A-dwand Bihar as Bihar seems straightforward, the latter could just be a shortened form of the former. But that doesn’t seem to be the case. In his notes to his translation of Minhaj’s work, Raverty informs that Bakhtiyar Khilji’s son later presented himself to the Sultan in Delhi as from not Bihar but A-dwand Bihar, implying that the Khaljis consciously drew a distinction between the two names.47 Going by that line of reasoning, it would seem that the vihara misidentified as the city of Bihar was not Odantapuri but something else. Khilji did sack Odantapuri, it’s just not clear if that’s the sack Minhaj is talking about. If not, it could be the vihara of Nalanda misidentified as Bihar. Even if we do admit the Odantapuri hypothesis for the sake of the argument, it’s far from unlikely that the invaders would have missed another monastery less than ten miles away. A case in point is the monk from Kashmir who fled to North Bengal instead of Nalanda. Archeologist Amalananda Ghosh also concurs and reads Minhaj’s city of Bihar as Nalanda in his seminal work, _A Guide to Nalanda_.48

Be that as it may, Minhaj on its own may not be enough to conclusively settle the Nalanda debate, which is probably why Sarkar too refrains from specifically spelling out the place, for his source is none other than Minhaj.

Now is a good time to circle back to the pin we dropped on Taranath earlier. His work, as we’ve already seen, informs us with clarity that Nalanda was destroyed by a Turushka horde. We have already gone through the exercise of identifying Turushkas with Turks, but let’s put that aside for a moment. What’s of immediate interest here is the fact that the same Turushkas also destroyed Odantapuri and Vikramshila in his accounts. Therefore, if Minhaj’s city of Bihar is Odantapuri, then Khilji destroyed Odantapuri. But if Khilji destroyed Odantapuri as per Minhaj, he also destroyed Nalanda as per Taranatha! This further strengthens the identification of Turushkas with Turks rather than Tibetans as sometimes presumed. While the case in favor of Muslim destroyers is already closer to airtight than otherwise, we have yet another primary source to chip in, the Indian witness.

## Dharmasvamin’s Account

Chag Lo-tsa-ba Chos-rje-dpal, better known as Dharmaswamin, was a Tibetan lama who spent two years in Magadha after the first wave of Turkic raids in the region. His biography, written by a companion named Chos-dar, offers valuable insights into not just his activities in India but also the state of Nalanda during the upheavals of the thirteenth century. This is the only primary source we have of the episode other than that of Minhaj. While Minhaj wrote from the invader’s perspective, Dharmaswamin’s accounts give us the same story from the victims’ vantage.

Dharmasvamin’s sojourn in Nalanda came decades after the first alleged raid on the mahavihara and lasted about six months. Before Nalanda, the lama also visited Tirhuta, Bodhgaya (Vajrasana in his records), and other places on the Magadha Buddhist circuit. The biography reports wholesale destruction throughout the region at the hands of Turushkas whom, just like Taranatha, he calls Gar-lóg. The first glimpse of this terror comes from Tirhuta, Dharmasvamin’s first pit-stop in India. More specifically, it was the township of Patala.49 Here, they had built no fewer than seven defensive walls around the settlement which comprised more than half a million homes. The king’s palace stood just outside the walls and was similarly fortified with walls, gates, and ditches. The monk was told this was all in preparation for potential Turushka raids into the kingdom, one of which they’d suffered just recently.50

From here, Dharmasvamin joined a caravan of three hundred pilgrims headed to Bodh Gaya (called Vajrasana in his biography). The condition here was even worse. The entire town was deserted, save for four monks still occupying the vihara. One of them is quoted as categorically spelling out the reason to the visitor—Turushka.51 They had bricked and plastered the shrine’s door to hide the Mahabodhi image from the iconoclasts.52 Also inhabiting the town in hiding was Buddhasena, the _Raja_ of Magadha according to Dharmasvamin. The name should ring a bell because it also finds a mention in the work of Taranatha who mentions him as a weak Sena dynast and a vassal to the Turushkas.53 The chapter on Vajrasana also informs that when Dharmasvamin visited the monastery at Vikramshila, he found nothing but ruins. The Turushkas had destroyed the viharas and thrown the foundation stones into the Ganges.54

The monk’s final stay before his return home was in Nalanda which, he reports, was damaged but still somehow functioning as a small scholastic center with a nonagenarian rector named Rahula Shribhadra and his seventy-odd disciples.55 The center enjoyed patronage from Buddhasena who now lived in Bodh Gaya. There were some eighty small viharas in the township all built by Buddhasena and his queen. Some stood relatively unharmed while others were damaged by the Turushkas. At any rate, most stood in neglect as there weren’t many residents left to maintain them.56 A little further, the monk even gives us a more precise number and says that of all the viharas on the premises, only two, named Dha-na-ba and Ghu-na-ba, were in “serviceable condition.”57

Dharmasvamin placed himself under the tutelage of Rahula Shribhadra and spent about six months at the mahavihara. Chos-dar’s account tells us that the neighboring center of Odantapuri had completely ceased to exist as an academy and had come to be occupied by Turushka military officers. Nalanda was constantly on the edge given its proximity to this veritable military outpost. The fears weren’t unfounded, for one day, news came of an impending invasion from Odantapuri which resulted in most residents fleeing the place for safer alternatives. Only Dharmasvamin and his teacher stayed back in hiding. The Turushkas indeed came—no fewer than three hundred soldiers—and left having found nobody.58 Dharmasvamin would complete his sojourn and subsequently return to Tibet with all the manuscripts he could carry.

These accounts make two things amply clear—that Nalanda had suffered not one but multiple Muslim invasions, and that the Muslims had ravaged the entire length and breadth of Magadha. Tirhuta is in the extreme north, almost bordering Nepal, and Bodh Gaya is way south. And then we have Vikramshila which is way too east. Although in his introduction to Roerich’s translation, noted historian and archeologist Anant Sadashiv Altekar asserts that Nalanda partially survived Muslim incursions as it didn’t fall on the highway from Delhi into Bengal59 (Jha and others have always used this to absolve Muslims in general and Khilji in particular of Nalanda’s fate), it doesn’t help Khilji’s case much. For one, Atlekar does not exactly absolve Khilji. All he says is that Nalanda was not completely destroyed in the early years of the thirteenth century, which of course is true, in light of Dharmasvamin’s account. All this proves is that there was one big blow at the turn of the century, followed by multiple smaller incursions, one of which took place right when Dharmasvamin was still in Nalanda. In fact, the word Atlekar uses for this first wave is “avalanche” and there’s also this affirmation from him that “practically the whole province had been overrun by the Muslims.” The monk makes it abundantly clear that the university was barely functional having lost most of its viharas, temples, and occupants to Turushka invasions.

As for the site being out of Khilji’s way, Tirhut doesn’t exactly fall on the highway to Bengal either and it still failed to escape the destruction. Nor does Bodh Gaya which again met the same end. Besides, one place that isn’t in any kind of dispute is Odantapuri. Being less than a day’s walk from Nalanda, if this town could face Khilji’s attack, it’s absurd to think Nalanda wouldn’t. To reiterate, both Dharmasvamin and Atlekar agree that Muslims had invaded and destroyed the entire Magadha region. One way to interpret Atlekar’s route hypothesis is that after the first “avalanche,” there wasn’t enough left of Nalanda to warrant a separate large-scale campaign even though smaller day attacks from the base at Odantapuri were evidently routine.

## Conclusion

While the argument may continue until Rapture, it’s only thanks to its ideological ramifications. Should history be the sole arbiter, there’s little that absolves Khilji. Unless we were to someday discover Muslim invaders other than Khilji who happened to be in the region at the time. Minhaj confirms Bihar which may or may not be Odantapuri, Raverty indicates Bihar isn’t Odantapuri, and Dharmasvamin confirms Nalanda’s destruction by Turushkas. That, in all aspects, Turushkas refer to Muslims and their Hindu vassals, is also abundantly clear, both lexically and historically. Taranatha’s account is neither historical nor primary and yet doesn’t shy away from putting the Gar-lóg or Turushkas on the docks for Nalanda’s desecration. The fantastical parts about Brahmin mendicants, at best, indicate a sectarian rivalry with little bearing on the Islamic invasions in question.

To conclude, if not Bakhtiyar Khilji, then who? The answer cannot be a non-Muslim if all historical indicators were to be satisfied.
