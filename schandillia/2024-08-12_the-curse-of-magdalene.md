In 2005, in an online article in the Swiss daily _Neue Zürcher Zeitung_ , a climatologist at the Institute of Geography of the University of Bern wrote that under normal weather conditions—although the jury remains out on what’s “normal” anyway—it takes the elements two thousand years to wash away thirteen billion metric tons of soil.1 Of course, that’s a rather simplistic number because the total amount of erosion depends, above all, on the total area in consideration. For example, while the NZZ piece quotes what comes to less than seven million tons a year, a 1998 study posited nearly a thousand times as much for China.2 The value in the NZZ article is for Western Europe, and even that number changed dramatically in a 2015 Global Agriculture piece that claimed nearly a billion tons annually.3 At any rate, thirteen billion tons is a lot of arable earth, and even the most pessimistic of estimations would allow at least a decade for an erosion of that volume across the size of western Europe. A flood in July 1342 did it in a matter of days.4

A deluge of this magnitude should be expected to have lasting consequences. In this case, the consequences were historic, timeline-altering. Not just for Europe but the whole world. This story attempts to answer two questions—what triggered the flood and what came out of it. We explore this subject because its significance in the course of world history is comparable to none other than the Bronze Age collapse of the twelfth century BC and the volcanic winter of 536 AD. We explore this today because despite its paradigm-shifting role, it’s rarely spoken of in the larger conversation on world or even European history.

## The Big Freeze

Three volcanic eruptions had triggered the big freeze of 536 AD which led to the Fall of Rome in the west and the Guptas in the east; and brought “Kaliyuga” to India. History, especially in the context of climate, is cyclical. It repeats. That’s what happened in 1257 when an Indonesian volcano named Samalas went off leaving behind a caldera that we now call Lake Segara Anak. The VEI7 eruption is counted among the largest of the Holocene and it triggered, as expected, a spell of catastrophic freeze all over the world also known as the Little Ice Age or LIA. The initial phase lasted twenty-five years through 1300. This is unusually long disruption for a single eruption. Which is why a 2012 paper suggests there were three more during these twenty-five years, further accelerating the freeze.5 Then came a little thaw and with it, torrential rains that washed away swathes of farms all over Europe. All of this culminated in the Great Famine of 1315–1317, also referred to as The Great Hunger.

* * *

The spring of 1315 was especially harsh, not so much due to the thaw, but rather because of the heavy rains. Excessive rains and cool temperatures stunted crop growth, leading to successive harvest failures. The situation persisted through 1316 and into the summer of 1317. Europe would not fully recover until 1322. To add further to what was already a mountain of miseries, a severe outbreak of cattle disease, likely a rinderpest infestation, dramatically reduced livestock populations, particularly sheep and cattle between 1319 and 1320.6 In England, the worst affected, they called it the Great Bovine Pestilence.7 Here, the scarcity of milk and dairy would last through as late as 1332.8 And then came the locusts.

Less than two decades after the famine, a colossal swarm of locusts rose in Hungary and fluttered its way up the Danube in search of food.9 By August 1338, it had reached Lower Austria. Contemporary accounts, particularly the autobiography of Holy Roman Emperor Charles IV, peg the swarm’s spread at an apocalyptic seven miles.10 It was so dense, he wrote, it blocked out the sun. From Austria, the swarm continued its relentless advance through Europe, along the Rhine, gobbling up farm after farm in Bohemia, Franconia, Lombardy, and in his words, all over the world.11 Such was the scale of devastation that the 1347 seal of one Niklas Fronauer, a nobleman from Lower Austria, features a coat of arms with a locust.12 The Fronauer family lived in the first half of the fourteenth century near Krumbach and Würflach, not too far from Vienna and close to today’s Austria-Germany border.13 This was one of the worst hit parts of Europe. Heraldic research reveals that the use of a locust as a heraldic charge is quite rare as it does not typically reflect canting arms.14 Most heraldic instances of locusts found in databases and collections are either undated, from the early-modern period, or later.

The “Ice Age” was not done though, the thaw was fatally deceptive. The winter of 1341-42 brought another devastating spell of snowstorms. The LIA was a period of intense glaciation, a phenomenon that only compounded further during winters. Advancing glaciers obliterated towns and villages across Scandinavia and the Alps.15 Artworks from the period show people ice-skating on the Thames and on Dutch canals.16 These were relatively ice-free areas before and after the LIA.17 People struggled to make sense of this long winter and with locusts, the end times must have seemed near. Some thaw arrived in spring which at first seemed like a welcome relief from the hopelessly frigid winter, but soon turned out to be a whole other stratosphere of devastation.

## The Genoa Low

The spring of 1342 brought warmth and thaw. This proved problematic since vast swathes of Europe had by now accumulated tons of ice. The thaw created enough meltwater to flood almost every major river and lake on the continent. And then came the rains. Everything that year was superlative. Including the rains. The torrential downpour further augmented the already swollen meltwater volume. The thaw alone would have probably still been manageable, but the intense pluvial activity ensured irredeemable catastrophe. This compounding was the result of a phenomenon called the Genoa low. But before we discuss the phenomenon, we need to understand its geography.

In the northern expanse of the western Mediterranean Sea lies the Ligurian Sea, delineated to the north by the French-Italian coastal arc and to the south by the island of Corsica. The northern sector of this sea is named the Gulf of Genoa after the eponymous coastal city to the north. On the French segment, that is the west, of the aforementioned arc, three principal ridges radiate northward from the coastline, interspersed by two major rivers that carve valleys between them before pouring into the Gulf. These ridges, from west to east, are the Pyrenees, the Massif Central, and the Alps. The Garonne River flows between the Pyrenees and the Massif Central, creating the Carcassonne Gap, while the Rhone River flows between the Massif Central and the Alps, forming the Rhone Valley.

The Carcassonne Gap and the Rhone Valley form natural corridors for the cold, moist North Atlantic airmass headed to the Mediterranean during the winter months. This happens because water retains heat longer than land, and as this retained heat transfers to the air above, it rises. This creates a low-pressure area above the water which, in this context, is known as the Genoa low. To equilibrate this low-pressure zone, the cold, moist North Atlantic wind streams through the Carcassonne Gap and the Rhone Valley toward the sea. But the stream doesn’t get far before hitting a large granite wall to the southeast, the Corsican highlands. The wall deflects the air mass to the northeast, i.e. toward Italy. We call this deflected airmass the Libeccio, which is Italian for Libyan, because to those on the Italian mainland the wind seems to be coming from Libya. Now the Italian peninsula itself has a mountain range running along its entire length like a midriff. It’s called the Apennines, and it forms the barrier that breaks the Libeccio upon landfall.

As expected, these double deflections result in heavy downpours in both Corsica and western Italy, but the Genoa low also creates something far worse because while all this is going on in the lower atmosphere, another violent interaction is afoot in the upper reaches. There, the polar jet stream dashes into the subtropical jet stream swirling the wet air around like a cyclone. The concave southern face of the Alps further concentrates the airmass hitting it back to the heart of this cyclone, further intensifying its effects. With all three ingredients in place, the Genoa low turns into a devastating cyclone ready to dump millions of tons of Mediterranean waters on whatever it touches.

Once the cyclone forms in the lee of the Alpine ridge, it begins to move eastward along the concave southern face.18 Upon reaching the eastern flanks of the ridge, the cyclone sharply veers northeast.19 This trajectory is known as the Vb-track, named after the nineteenth-century German meteorologist Wilhelm Jakob van Bebber. The intensity of polar winds initiating this process can lead to severe flooding of major European rivers such as the Elbe, Rhine, and Danube during the summer months.20 Essentially, it’s born over the Ligurian Sea and dies over the Adriatic on the other side of the Apennines, carrying seawater from Italy’s west and pouring it over its east. Along the way, the cyclone dashes and brushes against the Alps and drops a bit of its payload over Italy as a result. Just like the monsoons do when they hit the Himalayas. The 2014 Southeast Europe floods that recorded the heaviest rainfalls in 120 years were the latest example of this phenomenon. What makes Genoa lows so deadly is that they are not necessarily an annual event but can and often do form two to even three times in a single season. One such Genoa low hit right after the thaw of 1342 and carried a little more water than usual.

## The Deluge

The flood occurred in two phases. The first, in February, was triggered by the spring thaw and primarily consisted of meltwater.21 This phase chiefly impacted the Elbe and Danube rivers, causing significant damage to the bridges in the region.22 The most notable example comes from Prague where Vltava, a tributary of Elbe, swelled and washed away the 170-year-old Judith Bridge. Construction on a replacement would not begin until fifteen years after the flood. This replacement, made of stone, still stands and is now known as the historic Charles Bridge, named after the same Charles IV, who had earlier documented the locust swarm of Austria in the lead up to the great flood.

The second phase that came in the summer was the result of the Genoa low we just discussed and primarily affected Main, Rhine, Wesser, and Danube.23 The rains didn’t last long, six days at best from July 19 thru 25.24 But as much water was dumped on Central and Western Europe during those six days as is in half a typical year. Not to forget, this was in addition to an already catastrophic amount of meltwater. Now, Genoa lows are no strangers to Europe, nor are the floods they bring. These cyclones have flooded the continent in the Julys of 1897, 1927 and 1997, just to list a few.25 And these are only the major ones. So the flooding is expected. But 1342 was different. The amount of runoff this year was significantly higher than any year in a thousand. Water levels in every European river rose to levels not recorded before. Initial reconstructions estimated that the maximum surface runoff in the catchment area of the Oder River, which forms part of the border between Germany and Poland and pours into the Baltic, was between fifty and one hundred times higher than in 1997.26 A similar study by German geographer Hans-Rudolf Bork in 1998 posited 100 mm for the farmlands in the Main River catchment area, a value that rarely exceeds 3 mm under normal circumstances.27 Under normal circumstances, it rarely exceeds three millimeters. In a 1967 study, Hermann Flohn, a climatologist from the University of Bonn, reported that the deluge of July 1342 surpassed all other floods of the past 1,500 years in both magnitude and significance.28

The flood is presumed peaked around July 22, which happens to be the feast of St. Mary Magdalene. For this reason, the event earned the name of _Magdalenenhochwasser_ , literally “Magdalene’s high water” or the “Magdalene flood.” More recent studies, however, have challenged the notion of a peak on July 22, noting that rivers continued to swell in the days that followed and that the tidal peak did not reach Frankfurt until the night of July 24-25.29 Nonetheless, the association persists due to its reinforcement by popular memory.30

Germany, by most medieval accounts, was the worst-hit due to its numerous rivers. Nearly all major bridges on the Danube, Rhine, and Main were swept away, and the churches submerged, including their spires. One river, for example, recorded water levels reaching twenty-four feet. A tributary of Main had already claimed Prague’s Judith Bridge in the spring. This time, the Main itself took out the famous _Steinerne Brücke_ or Stone Bridge in Würzburg.31 The Elbe did the same with bridges in Dresden and Meißen.32 As did the Weser River in Minden. The same fate also befell countless bridges in Switzerland and other Western European countries.33

Main, the river that feeds cities like Frankfurt, Mainz, and Wiesbaden, moves an average of less than 300 gallons of water a second near Frankfurt in a typical July.34 Even when it finally meets the Rhine near Mainz, its total egress remains 52,000 gallons a second. In the July of 1342, this value was almost a million gallons per second at Frankfurt.35 A swell of this magnitude is estimated to occur only once in four to ten millennia.36 Similarly, while the Rhine typically discharges an average of 800,000 gallons per second, it surged to nearly three million gallons per second at Cologne during the 1342 flood season.37 Two separate accounts of the city from the time speak of water levels so high that boats freely floated above the city walls.38 This, however, may be the result of a reporting error, a point that factored into a 2014 study that brings to question the magnitude of impact the Magdalena flood alone had on the overall destruction of European farmlands. The paper posits that the impact is likely exaggerated, and that the bulk of the destruction is actually owed to spring meltwater rather than summer precipitation.39 In radical departure from conventional scholarship, this study downplays the Geneva low as the prime weather villain of the year.40 At any rate, it maintains that the floods of 1342 were catastrophic without parallel41 the only debate being whether it was the rain or the thaw.

Back in Frankfurt, the _Alte Nikolaikirche_ or the Old St. Nicholas Church offers valuable insights into the extent of the cataclysm. It stands barely over a hundred yards from the Main River and expectedly suffered the full force of nature during the flood of Magdalene. Fortunately for us, despite the enormous civic destruction through the two world wars, much of the original structure still stands unhurt. In the past, it was a common municipal practice to mark the _Hochwasser_ or high-water levels on prominent buildings during episodes of major inundation. Over time, these markings have become a reliable record of the magnitude of such events. Those on the Old St. Nicholas Church indicate that the water level reached six old Fuß above the base.42 The Fuß was a pre-metric German unit of length approximately equivalent to the English foot but varied between cities. In Frankfurt, it measured about 28.3 centimeters. Given that the church’s base sits 96.88 meters above sea level, the water level would have approached 99 meters above sea level.43 A similar story is also told by the _Eiserner Steg_ or Iron Footbridge that spans the river next to the church, but since it was built only in the nineteenth century, its markings are a reconstruction rather than contemporary artifacts.

About thirty miles downstream from Frankfurt is the city of Mainz which sits at the spot where the Main River pours into the Rhine. Here, a high-water mark at the landmark _Mainzer Dom_ or Mainz Cathedral gives a value of three Fuß.44 The Mainz Fuß is slightly longer than the Frankfurt Fuß and closely approximates a third of a meter. With the elevation of the building’s base in account, the value comes to 88 meters above sea level.45

## Human Factors

The inundation of 1342 stood out not only in the immediate damage it wrought but also in its long-term impact. It is reported that a third of all soil erosion over the preceding millennium and a half occurred during the one-week period starting July 22 of that year. This erosion was facilitated by, among other obvious factors, terrible land management in medieval Europe. The disaster was as anthropogenic as it was meteorological.

Forest covers prevent floods. They intercept and absorb rainwater through their canopy and soil, which reduces the volume of water reaching the ground and arrests its flow. Tree roots stabilize soil, preventing erosion and maintaining riverbank integrity, thus reducing sediment runoff that can exacerbate flooding. Forests also enhance the infiltration of rainwater into the soil, decreasing surface runoff and thereby reducing the volume of water flowing into rivers and streams. By regulating the flow of water in watersheds and maintaining a balanced hydrological cycle, forests contribute to a more stable and predictable water flow, mitigating the risk of sudden and overwhelming floods. Goes without saying, then, that reducing forest cover beyond a certain point is not a terribly wise move, especially in a region prone to extreme weather conditions. But some lessons must be learned the hard way.

Today, a little over 28 million acres, or nearly 32%, of Germany is forested.46 But it wasn’t always the case. The country has a long history of deforestation, reforestation, and silviculture that dates back to the Late Roman period.47 Those days the cover was an overwhelming 90%.48 But then deforestation started. Wood was the primary fuel source for the rapidly growing population, and to that end, trees were felled faster than they could regrow. This context provides the backdrop for the woodcutters in tales like Little Red Riding Hood and Hansel and Gretel. A growing population brings more mouths to feed which means not just more firewood but also more agriculture. When forests weren’t being felled for firewood and lumber, they were being cleared for farming. As agriculture expanded, it led to increased prosperity, which in turn fueled further population growth, creating a vicious self-reinforcing loop. By the time Germany entered fourteenth century, its green cover had shrunk to just under 20%. In the decade of the calamity, i.e. from 1340 thru 1349, it was closer to 15%.49

This wasn’t limited to just Germany. Aggressive assarting became a feature all over Europe. When Rome fell, Western and Central Europe was a whopping 80% forest. By the century of Magdalene’s flood, it was down to less than 30%.50 In those nine centuries, more than a hundred million acres of woodlands had disappeared from the continent.51 Not all of this was food and housing either. A great deal of deforestation is owed to a far darker reality of human civilization—war. That the aftermath of Rome’s fall was politically tumultuous, would be an enormous understatement. First it was the violent restructuring in the vacuum left by the Romans, then it was the Crusades. The first came in the tenth century, then two more came in the twelfth. The thirteenth century brought no fewer than twenty-two, depending on how one counts. And then there were other wars between the myriad kingdoms and principalities litters across the continent. It was a period of extreme instability and violence. The was hardly a year without war between the fifth and the fourteenth centuries. And wars demand resources.

The war industry flourished as the demand for war implements refused to abate. Everything from armors to swords was made from steel. And steel came from smelting iron ore, which demands heat and fuel.52 A great deal of wood therefore ended up in the blacksmiths’ kilns and furnaces. More wars directly meant more deforestation. And we need no lessons in what deforestation does to topsoil, particularly during violent weather events like the Magdalene flood. The flood washed away so much topsoil that acres upon acres of what were once fertile farmlands remain barren and non-arable to this day, with many expected to stay that way until the next glaciation period, at least ten thousand years away. Do note that this occurred less than three decades after the Great Famine, which had devastated livestock numbers and driven desperate Europeans to extreme measures, including cannibalism. In less than two generations, here was yet another climate crisis triggering yet another famine. But something far worse than famine was in store this time.

## Crisis in Constantinople

The Fall of Rome in the fifth century was not exactly the end of the road for the Roman regime. It was just the end of the western half. The eastern half, often referred to as the Byzantine Empire, continued as the heir to the thousand-year-old legacy. In simpler terms, “Rome” had shifted from Western Europe to Anatolia. At the time of the great fluvial crisis of Europe, the Byzantine regime had just entered a bitter civil war between two contenders that would last six years and involve parties as disparate as Greeks, Serbians, Bulgarians, and even the Ottomans.

By the time it ended in 1347, John VI Kantakouzenos, also known as Cantacuzene, was on the throne in Constantinople. He fathered six children, an equal number of boys and girls. Among them was Andronikos, who was born while Cantacuzene was still serving as the _mégas doméstikos_ , or Grand Domestic, a title that designated him as the commander-in-chief of the Byzantine army and made him the most powerful figure in the empire, second only to the emperor. Unfortunately, Andronikos did not have long to enjoy his father’s royalty. Cantacuzene was crowned in the summer of 1347. By fall, his son was dead. Cantacuzene’s own writing says that it was some mysterious illness.53 The boy was only thirteen. Nobody could make sense of what happened.

At the same time, Western Europe saw a heavy influx of people fleeing from something. Most of these refugees were traders from Genoa, many of whom were ill with the same mystery disease that claimed young Andronikos. Something was going down in Genoa. Or so it seemed, for the true source of the affliction lay elsewhere. But before we go there, a bit more on Constantinople.

Outside the palace walls, an apocalyptic pestilence unfolded, unseen since the days of Justinian. Cantacuzene paints a grim picture of death and desolation all over the Byzantine realm and writes of a strange illness that arrived in ships carrying “Scythians from Hyperborea.”54 We need not dwell on the precise meaning of the term Hyperborea, a mythical northern realm in Greek legends, as its geographical location has changed significantly over time. The broad takeaway is that the malady spread via maritime routes. Cantacuzene notes that it first hit coastal cities and only then moved inland.55 As emperor, he must have received reports from various parts of the known world, for he further adds that it was not just Anatolia and Macedonia but also Greece, Italy, Egypt, Libya, Judea, and Syria.56 In sum, it was a pandemic, and people were dropping dead wherever they lived.

The curse seemed absolute because it killed the weak and the strong in equal measures and neither diet nor medicine proved any good.57 And it killed quickly. Some died within hours, others within days.58 The emperor’s own son lasted just three days.59 Even those who seemed to recover at first, as if by some divine intervention, they struggled to speak and eventually died anyway.60 The worst affected organ was the lung where internal inflammations triggered sharp pains in the chest and spittle of blood.61 And there was the thirst, the unquenchable thirst. No matter how much water you drank, the throat remained parched.62 As if that weren’t hellish enough, a devilishly foul stench enveloped the victim at all times, which made medical attention and even palliative care a challenge.63 The contagion was so potent that anyone who came in contact with the patient’s bodily discharge was certain to contract the curse within days.

Even this wasn’t the end of the torment. As the illness progressed, the victim’s body would break out in countless foul-smelling and excruciatingly painful pustules, black in color and of all sizes.64 Some patients developed large abscesses in the thighs and when those were cut, much foul matter flowed out which seemed to somehow alleviate the illness.65 But even this didn’t work for everyone and what cured one made things worse for another.66 Nobody had a clue and people continued to die in large numbers. Several homes lay empty after entire families had perished, along with their livestock.67 The Black Death was now in session, although the name itself would not come about until several centuries later. This is what those Genoese merchants were fleeing from back in Europe.

## Black Death

The most detailed account of what followed in Europe comes from a lesser-known Medieval chronicler Michele da Piazza. From his writing we learn that sometime in the beginning of October 1347, shortly after the first deaths in Constantinople, a fleet of twelve Genoese galleys arrived in the Sicilian port of Messina.68 But instead of the usual merchandize, the cargo this time was death. By the time the fleet docked, all crew members were either dead or dying.69 They were escaping something horrible and had arrived at Sicily in hopes of safe harbor. But neither they nor the Sicilians had any that Europe’s fate had just been sealed.

Everyone who came in contact with the merchants contracted the malady. Same symptoms as those recorded by the Byzantine emperor. Michael notes that death was swift and came after three excruciating days of coughing and spitting blood.70 Dark pustules the size of lentils developed all over the body71 and the afflicted emitted such a foul stench that only the strongest willed dared to approach. The illness spread throughout Messina in no time and soon people were dying in numbers. Doctors and caregivers too fell as they contracted the plague without a cure.72 Although the residents figured out that the infirmity came from the Genoese and promptly expelled them from the city to stem the contagion,73 they just weren’t quick enough. The disease was already in town, and it no longer mattered if the Genoese stayed.

According to Michael, the curse spread not only through touch but also breath. Even coming in contact with a patient’s belongings was enough. Just as in Constantinople, death proved indiscriminate. The disease killed them all, man and beast alike.74 Corpses lay rotting in homes with doors ajar as entire families ceased to exist. Priests who dared to visit for confessions met the same fate, as did the thieves who took the opportunity to pillage homes with no living occupant.75 A number of Franciscan and Dominican priories lay deserted as priests succumbed to the curse while granting absolutions and hearing confessions.

The following year, the plague reached Siena in the far north of the Italian peninsula. Here, a shoemaker and tax collector named Agnolo di Tura was among thousands at the receiving end. He wrote a detailed account of the calamity that claimed his own five children.76 Such was the calamity here that many died without a funeral. There was no priest to perform the rites, nor any sextant to dig the graves. Even the bells didn’t toll.77 The dead either just decomposed in their homes or got dumped in mass graves.78 New corpses emerged day and night. Agnolo recorded a total of eighty thousand deaths in Siena and surrounding villages.79 In the end, fewer than ten thousand remained in what was a bustling mining metropolis until only a year before.80

As months passed, the illness snaked its way up north, swallowing villages and cities without discrimination in its wake. By the summer of 1348, it was in Venice, having desolated Florence, Bologna, and Padua on its way. There was no respite so far. But in Venice, the authorities came up with a novel idea to contain the infection. They ordered every new arrival to stay in isolation for a certain number of days before making any contact with the townsfolk. The period decided was forty days or _quaranta giorni_. That’s how long arriving ships had to stay in anchor before anyone was allowed disembark. We now call it quarantine. But the Venetians did not invent the practice of isolation. Before them, the city of Dubrovnik in Croatia had implemented a similar method but for thirty days instead of forty. In fact, the practice goes all the way back to Biblical times.

The quarantine, however, failed to entirely prevent the deaths. Some 100,000 Venetians,81 making up over than 70% of the city’s population,82 perished in the pandemic. As did twenty out of twenty-four excellent physicians.83 In Genoa, on the other side of the peninsula, barely one out of every seven survived.84 In Padua, says a contemporary account, a single visitor brought the contagion which ended up killing a third of the entire region’s population.85

From Italy, the contagion spread rapidly in all directions and by the end of 1351, everything from France to Siberia was in its grip. The fact that Europe, particularly the western and central regions, had barely recovered from a devastating famine caused by the once-in-millennia inundation of 1342, meant the poor had neither immunity nor the physical constitution to escape any epidemic, much less one of such Biblical proportions. What the flood couldn’t kill, the famine did. And what the famine couldn’t, the plague did.

But the question remains, where did it come from? All contemporary European accounts point east. The Byzantine account says Hyperborea which points north. The answer can only come from our dead Genoese merchants whose arrival in Messina set off the first domino in Europe when the plague had just started claiming lives in Anatolia. An easy explanation could be that they got it from Constantinople which is to the east, and Constantinople got it from some place to the north. But that’s too vague. Where in the north?

## The Black Sea

Remember we made a fleeting mention of Dubrovnik in Croatia? That was the first city in the world to implement physical isolation as a measure against the Black Death. Or at least one of the first cities. Here, the period was thirty days instead of forty. Could this city be ground zero? Unlikely, as it is not situated far enough north to Anatolia to merit the name Hyperborea. The merchants of Genoa routinely sailed to destinations in the east, which is well established. Among the northernmost of these destinations was a port city across the Black Sea on the Crimean Peninsula known as Kaffa. Today, it is called Feodosia, a name given by the Russians after their conquest of Crimea in the eighteenth century. But this is no Russian name, its etymology goes back to Greek Theodosia.

As the etymology suggests, the city was founded by Greek colonists from Anatolia some 2,500 years ago. After about a thousand-year run, the city suffered destruction when the Huns sacked it in the hundred-year lead up to the Fall of Rome. After the Huns, the region would shuffle between Turkic and Byzantine powers for almost a thousand years before finally entering the Mongol sphere in the first half of the thirteenth century. It’s toward the end of that century, in 1266, that the merchants of Genoa entered the scene. They purchased the city from the Mongols, developed it into a Genoese trading outpost, and named it Kaffa after a nearby Greek settlement called Kaphâs.

Over the following eight decades, Kaffa developed into a major port city with a bustling slave market, one of the largest in all of Europe. It also helped that the western end of the Silk Road terminated here. But this prosperity also attracted attention, not all of it positive. The Mongols of the Golden Horde soon came to experience what’s called the seller’s remorse. Now they wanted Kaffa back. The Khan of the Golden Horde at that time was one Jani Beg, a second-generation Sunni Muslim who had taken the Mongol throne in the same year as the Magdalene Flood after eliminating his sole rival and older brother Tini Beg. With help from none other than their own mother.

For the new Khan, the recapture of Kaffa was the first order of business, and preparations began in earnest. The siege finally began the following year, sparked by a skirmish between Tartar or Turkic Muslims and Genoese Christians in the Genoese settlement of Tana, which provided the Mongols with a pretext. For the purpose of this discussion, Turkic, Tartar, and Mongolic are synonymous. Tana emerged more than a thousand years earlier as the Greek settlement of Tanais in the Don River delta, about 350 miles to the northwest of Kaffa. From the fifth century onward, Tanais went through several occupants, first the Huns then the various Turkic peoples. Its first name change came in the eleventh century when the Kipchak Turks took control of the fort and renamed it Azaq which means lowlands in their tongue. The Kipchaks held the place for a couple of centuries before giving way to the Golden Horde which had quickly grown to take over much of the Black Sea coastline. They, in turn, gave away the town to the merchants of Venice and Genoa to develop into a trading outpost. The Italians rebuilt the place and restored it to the original Greek name, Tanais or, in their dialect, Tana. Today we call it Azov, a corruption of Azaq.

Under the pretext of rescuing the Muslims of the town, the Tartars of Jani Beg besieged Tana. Encamped outside the city walls, the Mongol general took note of mounting casualties in his camp. This was not the usual battlefield casualty but something else. An inexplicable affliction seemed to be running amok among his men, killing everything it touched. No cure worked and survival, once struck, was nothing short of a miracle. At least one contemporary account by an Italian lawyer named Gabriel de Mussis, names this as the reason for the siege.86 The Tartars were dropping dead and looked to Tana as a safe refuge for some reason. Once the city fell, the surviving Christians fled in armored ships to the only safe harbor they could think of, that is Kaffa.87 Unfortunately, Kaffa too was on Jani Beg’s list. Soon, the Tartar horde was at the city walls and the Siege of Kaffa began. Kaffa, however, proved much tougher than Tana, for the Italians would receive an abundance of reinforcements from Italy. With a brief respite in 1345, the siege lasted well into 1347. The Genoese appeared unbeatable. The walls seemed impregnable. And then came a turning point.

This turning point was the handiwork of the plague that had followed the Tartar army all the way to Kaffa. Deaths continued to mount in their camps and as before, no known remedy seemed to work. Whoever came in contact with the dead, died. Whoever tended to the sick, died. From this point the story goes one of two ways depending on who we read. In some accounts, the Tartars were forced to lift the siege after too many of them succumbed to the malady. A compromise was struck, and trading resumed. In this version, the Genoese grain shipments leaving the port of Kaffa would later bring the plague to Sicily. The other theory, which comes from Gabriel de Mussis’ account, is a little grimmer. In this one, Jani Beg weaponized the contagion and innovated what is often understood to be the world’s first instance of biological warfare.88 He had corpses of his men catapulted across the city walls in hopes of spreading the disease among the Genoese.89 The plan worked, and the city fell after residents started dying in large numbers.

Either way, this is what the Genoese were fleeing from when they sailed into Messina in the fall of 1347. Unfortunately, most of them couldn’t make it but the curse had just found a target-rich environment in a severely impoverished and emasculated Europe. Having said that, some modern scholars dispute the notion of a single point origin. They state that while the Genoese refugees did bring the disease to Sicily, they were only one of several channels. The transmission was in stepwise fashion and probably also traveled aboard a multitude of trading vessels that were rich in flea-riddled rodents.90 Some of these must have also headed to destinations in Anatolia which is how the curse reached Constantinople.91 At any rate, the Black Death was finally out of the Black Sea. But the question of its origin remains unanswered.

## The Rats of Karakoram

A paper published in 2015 announced the conclusion of a study of more than seven thousand plague outbreaks along with climate data gleaned from tree-ring patterns across Europe and Asia.92 After excluding a majority of these episodes on various grounds, sixteen were ultimately sampled. Some of the climate proxies studied were in the Karakoram mountains. Climate proxies are natural recorders of climate variability. They include things like tree rings, ice cores, and sediment layers, which scientists analyze to infer past climate conditions. These proxies help reconstruct historical climate data when direct measurements aren’t available. In the context of this study, the proxies were tree rings.

The Karakoram proxies revealed something interesting. The climate fluctuations that positively affected tree-ring growth in the juniper trees here were followed by a decline in conditions, which in turn led to significant increases in rodent populations that could carry the plague. The study found that these climate events consistently preceded plague reintroductions into Europe by approximately fifteen years. The analysis of the tree-ring data indicated that during periods of favorable climate, there would be an increase in rodent populations, which are the primary reservoirs for the plague. When the climate conditions became less favorable, it would lead to a collapse in these rodent populations, prompting their fleas to seek alternative hosts, which could facilitate the spread of the plague. A total of sixteen episodes of plague outbreaks were found to have occurred shortly after climate events in the Karakoram.93 They found a consistent pattern where certain climate events in the Karakoram mountains of Central Asia happened about fifteen years before plague outbreaks were reintroduced into Europe.94

In other words, when the climate was favorable for tree growth in that region, it often led to an increase in rodent populations that carry the plague, which then spread to Europe about fifteen years later. 1346 was one such episode. The study concluded a total of twenty-eight qualifying climate events in the Karakoram between the thirteenth and nineteenth centuries, most of them concentrated within the first two.95 The fact that the first on the list occurred in the middle of the thirteenth century holds meaning. Any guesses what else happened in that period?

We don’t know for sure if Samalas eruption that triggered the Little Ice Age in Europe also affected the climate in the Karakoram, but the coincidence is notable. Now let’s talk about Karakorum, the city. It was founded by Genghis Khan himself as a yurt town in the year 1220, more than 2,000 miles away from the eponymous mountain range. Under his son and successor Ögedei, the city developed further and became the capital of the empire. The eruption occurred a mere two decades later in 1257. Interestingly, Hülegü Khan sacked the Abbasid capital of Baghdad the very next year. Could there be a connection? A 2021 study suggests so. It posits that the post-eruption climate anomaly strongly dictated the Mongol campaigns into Syria and the Middle East—of which the Siege of Baghdad was a part—and later their withdrawal.96 More than a hundred thousand Mongol soldiers and about five times as many horses found themselves at the receiving end of the catastrophic changes.97

So, the Mongol Empire entered a period of intense civil war shortly after the death of Genghis Khan’s grandson Möngke, because the latter did not name a successor. By the time things settled, the empire was gone and in its stead were four smaller khanates—the Ilkhanate in Persia, the Kipchak Khanate or Golden Horde in southern Russia, the Chagatai Khanate in Central Asia, and the Great Yuan in China. Three of these would eventually convert to Islam leaving the Yuan dynasty as the only non-Muslim successor to the Chingisid line. Of these, with an eye on Byzantium itself, the Golden Horde proved to be the most ambitious, especially under Hülegü. But why? Of course, the Mongols were a territorial people, but there was possibly another imperative at play here. The climate anomaly had brought a long spell of dry weather to the Central Eurasian grasslands which had a direct impact on the Mongol way of life. Their intimate dependence on horses for everything from defense to nutrition meant a most pressing need for newer pastures. This is posited as at least a strong reason, if not the only one, for Hülegü’s advance toward the mountains. And this is likely when the mountains came to acquire the Mongol city’s name. The post-Möngke split had dealt a blow to Karakorum’s prominence as all four entities moved their capitals to other cities. The city quickly declined both politically as well as commercially. Hülegü might have also hoped to reclaim it as part of the campaign.

The Karakoram range, especially the northern face, is typically an arid zone with negligible vegetation. But on the other side there’s ample grasslands thanks to the exposure to the moist rain-bearing winds from the Indian Ocean. Although the Horde’s westward campaign was originally driven by the usual Mongol thirst for conquest, after the eruption of 1257, it became a matter of survival. Climate simulations in the 2021 study show an unusually wet summer in the Middle East between 1258 and 1260.98 This would prove immensely favorable to the Mongols whose horses could use all the rainfed vegetation they could get their hooves on.99 The paper directly holds the Samalas eruption responsible for the cooling over the Syrian plains and the subsequent precipitation that crucially helped the Mongol cause.100 By 1260, however, the climate had started returning to its usual arid self in the Middle East which, coupled with overgrazing, severely depleted the region’s grass cover. This triggered large-scale dying among the Mongol livestock which had a direct impact on their campaign. They suffered a crushing defeat against the Mamluks of Egypt at the landmark Battle of Ain Jalut which took place in modern-day Israel in 1260.

While the Mongols blitzed through the Middle East and left in a space of two years, they left something behind that lasted much longer. This goes all the way back to Central Asia. As the draught killed vegetation and triggered extreme food shortages, it wasn’t just the horses that suffered. Many native animals grew desperate for alternative food sources. When the Mongols traversed the barren landscape, these animals found what they needed. The caravan consisted of hundreds of thousands of men and over half a million horses, offering a cornucopia of discarded food. Naturally, these native creatures tagged along as the enormous tent city moved westward. Among them were the black rats of Karakorum carrying the deadly _Yersinia pestis_.101 While the Mongols were gone by 1260, these vectors persisted and over the following century, continued to spread death in every direction from Egypt to Crimea and from Asia to Rome, although things only picked up after the 1346 Siege of Caffa.

## The Aftermath

While there’s no conclusive evidence of the Black Death hitting India or China, an epidemic, which some experts identify as the plague while others as cholera, did kill a large number of Indians in the aftermath of the Samalas eruption. How do we know about this epidemic? From the Moroccan traveler Ibn Battuta, who was in the Delhi Sultanate between 1333 and 1342 when Muhammad bin Tughlaq was on the throne. Of all the places where Battuta stayed, the longest was in India. Naturally, then, it’s India that the bulk of his work pertains to. In his travelog, _The Rihla_ , he speaks of at least two epidemics that killed a large number of Indians within very short durations. The first of these was in 1334-35 and occurred in what he calls Tiling.102 That’s today’s Telangana. Tughlaq was here on a campaign against a rebel governor when a mysterious illness struck his army and killed most of his men.103 According to Battuta, Tughlaq himself was hit but miraculously survived.104 The pestilence killed the rich and the poor alike. The word used for the outbreak here is _wabāʾ_. Now wabāʾ is epidemic. It could be the plague, but it could just as well be any number of other infectious outbreaks. A more accurate term for the plague is _tāʿūn_ while disease is _maraḍ_. We will return to these names in a bit.

The problem with Battuta’s account of the Telangana epidemic is that he does not offer any symptoms. Just the mortality.105 There is, therefore, no way to ascertain if what me describes was the plague that would ravage Europe a decade later. Battuta isn’t the only one who wrote about this outbreak though. There were also Indian writers like Ziauddin Barani, a member of Tughlaq’s court, and Abdul Malik Isami, a member of the Bahamani court. While Battuta locates the outbreak in Bidar, Barani locates it in Warangal, some 150 miles to the east.106 Isami does not give any location. None of these writers say anything about the symptoms. Scholars remain divided on whether this wabāʾ was the plague or cholera. Either way, it killed many.

In the fall of 1344, Battuta arrived in _Mutra_ , present-day Madurai, after a very short trip to Ceylon. This time again, he comes face-to-face with the same wabāʾ that he witnessed in Tiling.107 Was this the plague? We don’t know for sure, but he says the victim died in three days, four at best.108 Which is eerily similar to what European writers said about the Black Death. And yet, in the absence of any symptoms, it’s hard to identify the illness with any measure of confidence. Battuta even speaks of people who were visibly fit one day, then died the very next day. We know both bubonic and pneumonic plagues exhibit extreme symptoms even when they kill quickly. There’s violent blood vomits in both cases and the defining dark pustules in the case of bubonic. On the one hand, deaths without any of these symptoms contradict the possibility of a plague, but on the other hand, Battuta does not mention such symptoms even when describing known plague outbreaks in the Mediterranean.109 Therefore, unfortunately, we’re left with no conclusive answers. We can’t assert it was the plague, but we can’t rule it out either. Nevertheless, the fact that a large number of Indians perished in a violent epidemic that almost coincided with the Black Death in Europe holds meaning.

## Déjà Vu

That the Black Death dramatically altered the course of world history would be a gross understatement. Europe lost a third of its population which drastically reduced the workforce and reconfigured the demographic structures. This sharp decline in population immediately led to labor shortages, which increased wages for peasants and workers. This shift contributed to the decline of the feudal system and the rise of a more market-oriented economy. The death toll exacerbated class tensions and accelerated social mobility. With fewer people in rural areas, many moved to cities in search of work, accelerating urban growth and contributing to the rise of new economic centers.

Another area where the plague upended the prevailing order was in religion. The death and desolation brought about first by the famine, then the floods, and finally by the plague led many to question the authority and even necessity of the Catholic Church. There was an increased interest in mysticism and personal religious experiences. The search for a direct and personal connection with the divine became more pronounced as traditional institutions seemed inadequate. Unable to reconcile with the clergy’s inability to prevent or even explain the calamity, many turned to a more personal and individualistic approach to religion. Personal salvation took precedence over ecumenical practices. This shift in focus contributed to the appeal of reformist ideas that emphasized personal faith over institutional rituals. The trend would continue long after the plague was gone and ultimately culminated in the Protestant Reformation of the fifteenth century.

The disruption of trade routes during the plague and the need for new economic opportunities led to increased exploration and the eventual Age of Discovery, reshaping global trade patterns and expanding European influence. The rise of a wealthy merchant class, eager to invest in exploration, played a crucial role in funding voyages and supporting explorers. Additionally, the need to explore new trade routes spurred advancements in navigation and shipbuilding, with innovations like the astrolabe and improvements in mapmaking becoming essential for long-distance exploration. This is what took Europeans to places like India and Americas turning them into colonial superpowers. In sum, the Black Death helped Europe grow from a collection of inconsequential feudal fiefdoms to global political and mercantile powerhouses.

Another big-ticket outcome of this cataclysm, that’s rarely, talked about is the end of the Abbasids as a power center in the Muslim world. Although the Caliphate would continue for another two centuries or so, but the 1258 Sack of Baghdad drove them not only out of Mesopotamia but also relevance. The fall of the Abbasids in Baghdad also formally ended the Arab hegemony on the caliphal scene. Because from this point on, the Islamic world would increasingly become the domain of the Turks. The first Turkic peoples had already established a presence in Anatolia with the 1071 Battle of Manzikert, but it’s only after the ouster of the Abbasids from Baghdad that they truly emerged as contenders of Islamic supremacy. The Ottomans, the entity that came out on top, would go on to not only conquer Constantinople but establish the longest lasting Caliphate in Muslim history.

The catastrophe was a strange and cruel redux of what had already come to pass almost exactly a thousand years earlier. In the first iteration, a volcanic winter triggered the Late Antique Little Ice Age, which caused massive flooding and famines all over the world, brought Asian hordes—the Huns—into Europe, and led to the Fall of the Western Roman Empire. In this one, a volcanic winter triggered the Little Ice Age, which caused massive flooding and famines all over the world, brought Asian hordes—the Mongols—almost into Europe, and led to the Fall of the Eastern Roman Empire. The first iteration helped the rise of Islam, the second helped bring it to Constantinople. If one had to pick a single example of _déjà vu_ in the entire human history, this ought to be it.
