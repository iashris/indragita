# Why Prakrit before Sanskrit?

*A Deep Dive into Indian Epigraphy’s Most Enduring Paradox*

India has an incomprehensively long history and so does its linguistic tradition. So far, we can only speculate about the language spoken by the Harappans, or for that matter those spoken by the aborigines deeper in the subcontinent. There is no speech specimen to work on and there’s little in the way of inscriptions. We were fortunate with Ancient Egyptian, as its continuity through Demotic and later Coptic allowed us to trace sound changes back to antiquity. This continuity has enabled us to develop a fairly reliable model of how the earliest Pharaohs may have sounded. With the Harappan tongue (or tongues), we have no such continuum to work with. The handful of inscriptions we’ve uncovered so far too remains to be deciphered. All we have is a sharp, abrupt jump from that to Sanskrit, roughly around, by most reckoning, 1500 BC. There’s most certainly plenty of cultural continuity, but we’re talking linguistic continuity here. That gap remains to be filled.

We know that Sanskrit is _the_ oldest Indic language, older than the entire Dravidian family. This debate has been extensively studied and settled in an earlier article on the subject.

* * *

* * *

The traditional view is that most non-Dravidian languages spawned from small set of nonstandard ancestors collectively called Prakrit. These Prakrit tongues are typically contrasted with Sanskrit in prestige, status, and semantic standard. Whatever be one’s position on this contentious subject, learned consensus largely remains that Prakrit follows Sanskrit and not the other way around. In which case, we must address a nagging question—Why doesn’t epigraphy support this order? The oldest specimens of Indic writing, outside of Indus seals and aboriginal cave scribbles, known to us come from the imperial edicts of Aśoka. These edicts are in Pali, Aramaic, and even Greek. But not Sanskrit. This is roughly between 260 and 232 BC. But the first specimen to exhibit Sanskrit, as traditionally (but erroneously) held, wouldn’t come until around 150 AD with Rudradaman’s rock inscription at Junagadh. That’s almost four hundred years between written Prakrit and written Sanskrit.

If Sanskrit emerged before Prakrit, how come Prakrit inscriptions predate Sanskrit inscriptions? This is a linguistic paradox that has been extensively studied and investigated for almost a century now with few clear answers. In this article, we’ll attempt to follow this study and arrive at a working theory that stands the test of reason and logic. In light of available historical facts, of course. In the course of this investigation, we will also tangentially touch upon the history of writing in the Indian context. The epigraphic gap between the Indus collapse around 1800 BC and Aśokan edicts around 250 BC is troubling. We will appreciate this gap, if not fully decode it. The first order of business in that direction is to clearly establish the distinction between Sanskrit and Prakrit. And address some misconceptions along the way.

## The Concept of Diglossia

A proper understanding of the subject at hand warrants a degree of familiarity with a couple of linguistic concepts. One such concept is diglossia but even before that, we ought to understand code-switching. While the term itself might sound unfamiliar, the phenomenon it describes isn’t. Unaware of its technical appellation, we engage in code-switching almost instinctively at all times. At least those of us who speak more than one language. So, what is it?

Code-switching is the practice of alternating between two or more languages or language varieties within a single conversation, sentence, or even phrase. It typically occurs in multilingual or bilingual communities where speakers are fluent in more than one language. Code-switching can happen for various reasons, such as expressing identity, conveying specific meanings, accommodating different listeners, or adhering to social or cultural norms. Or just plain old habit. An excellent example in the Indian context is Hinglish, a seamless nonstandard blend of Hindi and English:

“ _Kal_ meeting _hai_ , so don’t be late.”

In this example, the same sentence is rendered partly in Hindi and partly in English. In fact, even the Hindi part comes embedded with an English word, _meeting_. This practice of instinctively switching between two or languages in the same context, often in the same sentence, is common to all multilingual societies. In some cases, also a marker of social status. Hinglish is widely used in urban India, among younger generations, and reflects the linguistic and cultural interaction between Hindi and English. The phenomenon is often studied in sociolinguistics because it reveals how language interacts with social contexts, helping speakers negotiate different social identities or roles. It also shows how languages influence each other in multilingual settings.

Since code-switching involves multiple languages or dialects, one language or dialect typically stands out as the dominant substrate. It serves as the primary grammatical framework within which elements from other languages are inserted during conversation. This dominant tongue is called the matrix language, the second concept fundamental to the subject at hand. The non-dominant components are called embedded languages. The matrix language controls the sentence structure, word order, and other grammatical rules, while the embedded language provides individual words or phrases. In the case of Hinglish, for instance, Hindi is the matrix language because it dictates the word order and the sentence structure, and English, which contributes little more than rudimentary vocabulary, serves as the embedded.

The matrix language concept is particularly important in studying code-switching because it helps linguists understand how two or more languages interact in bilingual speech, with one language, the matrix, setting the structural norms and the other, the embedded language, contributing lexical items or short phrases.

And now we can finally discuss diglossia. It’s a subset of the above sociolinguistic situation, where two distinct varieties of the same language coexist within a speech community. The varieties can be classified as _High_ and _Low_ , and they often differ significantly in terms of grammar, vocabulary, and usage contexts. The High variety is typically used in formal settings, such as in literature, education, government, and religious contexts. It is usually regarded as the prestigious or “standard” form of the language. The Low variety, on the other hand, serves informal settings, such as everyday conversation, family interactions, and social gatherings. It may be considered colloquial or nonstandard.

Speakers generally do not blend High and Low varieties within the same context. For example, one might use the High variety during a speech or a religious ceremony, while the Low variety is employed in casual conversations with friends or family. Consequently, code-switching is virtually nonexistent between these varieties. Diglossia often mirrors societal attitudes toward different language forms, with the High variety typically associated with education and prestige, whereas the Low variety may be linked to regional identity or informal speech. A noteworthy example in the Indian context is the distinction between the Hindi spoken in Delhi and that in Mumbai or Bihar. The former represents standard, educated speech, while the latter varieties are frequently dismissed or even derided as uneducated vernaculars. Beyond India, this distinction is observed in Greece with Katharevousa and Demotic, though Demotic has now become the standard. In the Arab world, Standard Arabic contrasts with dialects like Egyptian Arabic. Similarly, in German-speaking regions of Europe, _Hochdeutsch_ (High German) is distinguished from _Plattdeutsch_ or _Niederdeutsch_ (Low German). Examples also exist in English, including within subsets like American English, where the East Coast dialect enjoys more prestige than Ebonics or Texan English. Similarly, in British English, there are distinct class differences between Queen’s English and vernaculars such as Geordie or Cockney.

While code-switching is not a characteristic of diglossia, the concept of matrix language remains relevant. In this context, the matrix language refers to the more dominant language in everyday speech—the register most commonly used in informal communication. Typically, this is the Low variety specific to a region, such as Bihari Hindi in Bihar or Cockney in certain parts of London. The Low or matrix dialect is the one most speech communities default to unless in formal settings that require the High or “educated” variant. With these fundamentals in place, we are now sufficiently equipped to discuss Sanskrit and its relationship with Prakrit.

## What is Sanskrit?

The Rigveda is universally acknowledged as the first usage of the Sanskrit language. If anything was produced before, we haven’t found it yet. It’s logical to assume, however, that the language must have been spoken before the composition, even if we have no records thereof, otherwise what’s the point of the composition at all? Rigveda is dated to roughly around the middle of the second millennium BC, so that’s at least how old Sanskrit ought to be. This Sanskrit looked and felt very different from the one we are familiar with today. It’s natural since languages evolve. In the case of Sanskrit, this evolution was catalyzed by a man named Panini who produced its first known grammar. The _terminus ad quem_ for Panini is typically placed in the fourth century BC, so we’ll go with that although the _terminus a quo_ goes as far back as sixth century BC. The Sanskrit as found in the Rigveda is called Vedic Sanskrit and that formalized by Panini, which is very different, is Classical Sanskrit. Simplistically 

It is understood that several formalization efforts existed before Panini but it’s only the latter’s that exist to this day. Panini himself affirms this view by naming ten grammarians he drew upon. So, Vedic Sanskrit before mid-first millennium BC and Classical Sanskrit after mid-first millennium BC. But here’s something interesting—Panini never named the language. The word _saṃskṛtaṃ_ appears in _Aṣtādhyāyī_ a grand total of three times, all in book 4—2.16,1 4.3,2 and 4.134.3 Not one is in the context of language. In other words, nowhere in all of Aṣtādhyāyī does the word Sanskrit, or saṃskṛtaṃ, appear as the name of a language. In fact, the first time such an appellation is made is in Valmiki’s Sundara Kāṇḍa, 28.17.4 Depending on how we date the epic, it may or may not be coeval with Aṣtādhyāyī.

At any rate, the conspicuous absence of the term from Panini’s work raises a question. Was Sanskrit even a language? Or was it a “High” variant of a broader language? If so, what was that that broader language? Panini uses the more generic term _bhāṣā_ , or its derivative _bhāṣāyām_ , but this has been interpreted to mean the colloquial variant in Panini’s times, _i.e._ the register Panini contrasts to that of the Vedas.5 What this suggests is that the language of the Rigveda had already corrupted to a Low register by the time of Panini and Valmiki. Panini acknowledged this corruption and referred to it as bhāṣā while also retaining separate semantics for the High register of the Vedas. This High register is what Valmiki likely referred to as Sanskrit in his work. If the hypothesis holds, Prakrit vernaculars are likely to be regional derivatives of this Low register rather than independent languages. Which would make Prakrit tongues like Pali, derivatives of Vedic Sanskrit. Panini is careful to keep the two registers segregated wherever possible. We seem to have on our hands, one of the earliest known examples of diglossia in the history of language. But this is just hypothesis. We need more to be sure. Let’s seek solutions in the Rigveda.

One case in point is the verbal root _kṛ_ , “to do.” Sanskrit is a heavily inflected language, which means it is heavy on conjugations and case inflections. Throughout the Rigveda, the root in question is rendered as _kṛṇu_ to form the present stem. With two exceptions, both in the tenth book or _maṇḍala_. These instances use an alternative form _kuru_ instead. Now, it’s widely acknowledged that the tenth maṇḍala is the last maṇḍala in chronological order. With that in mind, the logical inference would be that kuru was a later corruption of kṛṇu. But there is a problem.

The same maṇḍala that is supposed to prefer kuru also uses kṛṇu in one instance. It would seem that both forms coexisted at least at the time of its composition. To understand what this implies, let’s examine each usage. The first is in 10.19.2,6 in the context of cattle returning home in the evening. The second is in 10.145.2,7 in the context of a jealous wife seeking to corner her husband’s affection from his other wife. In the first case, it’s an ordinary cattle herder calling out to his cows,8 in the second it’s a jealous wife conjuring against her cowife.9 The instance that returns kṛṇu instead of kuru is in the hymn 10.95, two instances actually—95.1 and 95.2.10 This hymn is different. It’s not about ordinary people. It’s about a superhuman being. It’s a dialog between Urvaśī, an _apsarā_ , and Purūravas, her human husband.11 And there lies the clue.

A lowly cowboy uses kuru, as does a jealous mortal woman. But a divine Urvaśī opts for kṛṇu, and so does her mortal husband when addressing her. Clearly, a distinction is being made on grounds of social status, kṛṇu signifying a High register and kuru, a Low register. Both forms coexist at the time of this maṇḍala. As this pattern continues, it appears that kuru and its derivatives dominate the Atharva Veda, consistent with the observation that Low registers often outlast High ones. By the time of Pāṇini, kṛṇu would have been relegated to the status of an archaism, a Vedic relic with no currency outside of liturgy. Yet, during the period of the tenth maṇḍala, both registers were available as two distinct levels of speech within the same language. The High dialect, associated with prestige, was reserved for divine beings, while the Low dialect was used by women, who were socially considered inferior to men, and men of lower class and education. This demotic character of Prakrit continued well into the Common Era when the Jain mendicant Haribhadra Suri called it the language of the slow-witted, the uneducated, children, and women.12 Austrian linguist Manfred Mayrhofer traces the vernacular form, kuru, to Vedic kṛṇu via a Middle-Indo-Aryan or Prakritic intermediary, _*kuṇu_.13

All of this culminates into the aforementioned conclusion that originally, Prakrit and Sanskrit were dialects of the same language. The vernacular dialects were called Prakrit or natural because they seemed more “natural” to the lay speakers, and the “refined” dialect was called Sanskrit or cultured because it was associated with the social elite. Even today, in all languages, the High register is always considered more contrived and the Low, more natural. Again, do note that Sanskrit as a designation for this High register exists in neither Aṣtādhyāyī nor _Mahābhāṣya_. The most crucial takeaway here is that the two registers, Sanskrit and Prakrit, should we choose to identify them as such, existed _side-by-side_. Asking whether Prakrit came before or after Sanskrit would be akin to asking whether Yorkshire English came before or after Queen’s English. There may have been some kind of a chronology, but it’s immaterial to the larger discourse. And with that, we can now get to Aśoka’s language.

## Language during Aśoka

Aśoka is crucial to this discussion not only because his edicts are the first substantial specimens of written language in India, but also because he happened to live in a rather interesting period between Panini and Patanjali. The language described by these two focused exclusively on the high-prestige version of Sanskrit that was prevalent among the elite of their time. However, linguistic usage during that period encompassed a broad spectrum of forms, from more colloquial, Prakritic varieties to different approximations of the high standard Sanskrit.14 For example, alongside the classical form _bhavati_ , meaning “he is,” vernacular forms like _bhoti_ and _hoti_ were also in use.15

This linguistic diversity can be inferred from the inscriptions of Aśoka, dating to the third century BC, which demonstrate a range of contemporary spoken dialects spread across the Indian subcontinent. Since these inscriptions fall between the periods of Panini and Patanjali, it’s fair to infer that while grammarians like Paṇini aimed to document the most prestigious registers, they intentionally excluded many of the lower prestige forms, referred to as _apaśabda_ (incorrect or non-standard forms).16 Despite this, Patanjali’s commentary contains occasional references to these lower forms, offering insights into the broader linguistic landscape of the time. The grammarians’ focus on high-prestige Sanskrit meant that more informal variations of the language were largely left unrecorded in their works.

So what’s the linguistic situation during Aśoka’s reign? For perspective, the Rigveda is now more than a thousand years in the past, and Panini about a quarter of that. It’s safe to assume that the language spoken by the masses would have changed significantly over this time. In other words, what was “substandard Sanskrit” in the Vedic Age was in all likelihood a bunch of reasonably developed Prakrit vernaculars in the Mauryan Age. These included most notably Pali, but also Gandhari, Maharashtri, Ardhamagadhi, Paishachi, and others. The seeds of Shauraseni Prakrit, which would later evolve into the Standard Hindi register as spoken in and around Delhi, are also understood to have been sown during this period. This vernacular continuum that started around the time of Panini and lasted through 1000 AD is referred to as Middle Indo Aryan or MIA. Afterwards, these would crystallize as Bengali, Marathi, Magahi, Oriya, Dardic, and other languages spoken in various parts of the subcontinent today. Before MIA, all we had was Vedic Sanskrit and its various colloquial variants that would later evolve into Classical Sanskrit and various Prakrit tongues respectively.

The question, then, is why Aśoka inscribed his edicts in Prakrit, and even Greek, but not Sanskrit. The answer may not be as complicated as it seems at first blush. To answer this intuitively, we should consider what he was communicating in the first place. He was an emperor, governing the largest empire the subcontinent had seen until that point. How does one manage a domain this vast, this diverse, and consequently, this unwieldy, especially in an era without newspapers or media? Most premodern polities managed with town-criers, but for an empire this large, that wasn’t always the most effective vehicle of dissemination. That’s where the edicts come in.

Imperial inscriptions were not a Mauryan innovation. Long before the Mauryas, the Persians were erecting large edicts for various purposes throughout their realm, including the Indus region. In fact, the very origin of the names Hindu and India traces back to one such inscription, made over a century and a half before Chandragupta. It’s from these Persians that Aśoka borrowed the idea of rock edicts as a medium for imperial dissemination. Needless to say, these inscriptions weren’t easy to make. They were both labor and cost intensive, hence only used for announcements that were extremely crucial for the empire. Things like major royal orders, important conquests, new rulings, and so on. In sum, administrative broadcasts. Since these were intended for maximum outreach, they had to be in a language most people understood. The only way to ensure that is to adopt the matrix language of the region in question.

In Aśoka’s days, that matrix language was not Panini’s High Sanskrit but any of the various descendants of its Low register we collectively call Prakrit. Of course, this depended on the location—Gandhari, Aramaic, and Greek in the far northwest, Old Ardhamagadhi in the east, and a generous spattering of Aśokan Prakrit and Pali elsewhere. These were the languages of the masses. An edict in Vedic or even Paninian Classical Sanskrit would fail to reach the maximum audience the same way a Royal Gazette in pristine Anglo-Saxon or Old English would today.

Had Aśoka lived half a millennium earlier, his edicts would have likely been in Sanskrit or something very close. But how are we so sure if there were no edicts before him? And that’s what brings us to our next point of discussion, pre-Aśokan epigraphy. But wait, let’s dwell a bit more on the relationship between Sanskrit and Prakrit first. Put a pin on pre-Aśokan epigraphy for now, we’ll come to it in a bit.

## The Sanskrit-Prakrit Continuum

Were the Prakrit tongues during Aśoka’s time, especially Pali and Aśokan Prakrit, as different from Sanskrit as two distinct languages today? Or were they similar enough to be considered dialects of the same language? Some scholarship suggests that it was a spectrum and that the three languages, should we call them that, were mutually intelligible. In other words, the various Prakrit languages were mere grades of dialectical separation from Sanskrit. To test this hypothesis, let’s refer to a post-Aśokan work on Sanskrit grammar, Patanjali’s Mahābhāṣya.

In one specific example, Patanjali prescribes against usage of corrupt speech, lest one be perceived as a _mlechha_ or barbarian. He says Brahmins must speak _proper_ Sanskrit to maintain their status as distinct from the _asuras_ :17

    be mlecchas.” (trans. Dasgupta)

Here, the distinction between “mleccha speech” and Sanskrit is crucial, but visibly weak. So weak that mispronunciation is easy, hence the warning. This warning parallels a story in the _Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa_ , an older text, where the _devas_ (gods) and asuras compete in a ritual. The asuras lose the support of Vāc, the goddess of Speech, and shout a phrase that varies slightly in wording but maintains the same meaning. The important point here is that the languages ascribed to the asuras and the gods are not mutually unintelligible.

In yet another example in the same chapter, Patanjali speaks of a group of sages who speak pristine Sanskrit during Vedic rituals but fall back to corrupt pronunciations during quotidian activities. The expression of interest here is _yad vā nas tad vā naḥ_ , which translates to “whatever happens to us, let that happen to us.” That’s how the sages pronounced it during rituals, but at other times, they pronounced it as _yarvāṇas tarvāṇaḥ_.18 The only difference between the “Sanskrit” and the “vernacular” here was the switch from _/d/_ to _/r/_.

If this was the difference between the registers during Patanjali’s time, it can safely be presumed that Sanskrit and Prakrit vernaculars weren’t separate languages until very close to Aśoka’s time. The Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa is generally placed somewhere in the eighth century BC. And during its time, there was certainly no independent language as Pali. So whatever was written down, if anything was, it can safely be assumed to be Sanskrit. Maybe Low Sanskrit, the Sanskrit of the “mlecchas,” but Sanskrit. And now we can finally get back to the pin we dropped on pre-Aśokan writing.

## Writing before Aśoka

The widely accepted belief is that the early Aryans were not particularly disposed to writing, relying instead on a robust oral tradition to transmit knowledge across generations. Written work can be classified into two broad categories—monumental and literary. Monumental writing serves practical purposes, such as administrative tasks—imperial decrees, legislative changes, and the like. Examples include the inscriptions of Darius, Aśoka’s edicts, and the Code of Hammurabi. With these, accuracy and reach are crucial, oral transmission cannot suffice. The other category is literary, encompassing scripture, folklore, and drama. These, unlike monumental texts, lend themselves more easily to oral transmission. If a piece of folklore is lost or altered in transmission, it hardly threatens the stability of an empire. They don’t demand to be committed to writing. Yet, most non-Indian cultures—Mesopotamian, Egyptian, Chinese—chose to write down such literary works, using various media, both perishable and imperishable, such as papyrus, bones, temple walls, and rock surfaces.

Aśoka had no choice but to commit everything to writing due to administrative imperatives. This likely explains why the Vedas go back more than three thousand years and yet the oldest manuscript only dates to later than 1000 AD. Writing non-essential material wasn’t a typical Indian practice, for reasons unknown. But how are we so sure? Sure, no examples have survived, but what if non-monumental writings were recorded on perishable materials like barks and leaves? There was, for instance, no pressing imperial need to inscribe the epic of Ramayana on a boulder. Putting it on, say, pieces of birch barks could have sufficed? Such organic substances wouldn’t have lasted this long. In this case, it’s reasonable to assume that the absence of evidence doesn’t necessarily indicate evidence of absence.

What we are left with at this point is educated guesswork. Speculation. _Maybe_ they didn’t write. _Maybe_ they wrote but no specimen survived. We need more. Although not much from the Vedic times, we do have a crucial hint in the writings of Megasthenes. We know he was in Magadha during Chandragupta’s reign and extensively documented his experiences in what can be considered one of the world’s first travelogues. Among his observations were remarks on Indian laws. At one point, while praising the relatively low crime rates in Pataliputra, he notes that no Indian laws were committed to writing and adds that these laws were transmitted and enforced solely from memory, as the Indians were unfamiliar with written words.19 This commentary provides important insights into the state of Indian epigraphy before Aśoka. Megasthenes served as a Seleucid envoy at Chandragupta’s court. Given that the treaty between Chandragupta and Seleucus has been dated to 303 BC, it is reasonable to consider this as the _terminus a quo_ for Megasthenes’ visit to Magadha. He likely remained there until the arrival of his successor, Deimachus, who visited during Bindusāra’s reign, approximately 298 BC or later. This gives Megasthenes at least half a decade in the capital of India’s heartland, making his observations anything but cursory. This indicates that, at least in the third century BC, Indians were not writing or reading, lending credence to the notion of Aśoka as the pioneer.

But Megasthenes wasn’t the first to make that observation. A couple of decades before him a similar observation was recorded by someone who visited the Indus region as part of Alexander’s eastern campaign. Nearchus was a Cretan naval officer and is best known for leading a naval expedition from Jhelum to the Persian Gulf. Although, unlike Megasthenes, he was confined to the northwest, far from the Magadhan heartland, Nearchus too recorded that Indians did not have written laws.20 Now we have two solid sources from two entirely unrelated parts of the subcontinent saying the same thing. But before we rush to conclude, Nearchus adds, in the same passage, that Indians wrote on tightly woven _σινδόσι_ , _/sindosi/_ , or canvas.21 Now, this muddies waters.

The only way to read this is by seeing quotidian texts as separate from imperial texts. Maybe they did commit to writing the former on a personal level, at least in certain pockets, but not the latter? If as Nearchus claims, Indians wrote on fabric, there’s no way they could’ve survived the centuries of weathering to be extant. What still hurts this conclusion is the region Nearchus visited. At the time, the area had been under the Achaemenids for nearly two centuries, with a sizeable demography of Aramaic speakers among the inhabitants. Even if we assume that people were writing on canvas in those parts, it’s still possible they were writing Aramaic and not Sanskrit, High or Low. Aramaic had been an official language alongside Old Persian in the Achaemenid court since the time of Artaxerxes I, _i.e._ mid-fifth century BC.

To address the question, we might try revisiting someone native to the region. Someone who studied and taught at Taxila. In Aṣtādhyāyī, Panini drops two words that are of interest to this discourse. One of them is _lipi_ , and its variant _libi_. Both are Sanskrit for “script.”22 Another word that alluded to written work in Aṣtādhyāyī is _grantha_ , which means book.23 If Panini spoke of script and book, surely he was familiar with the concept of writing? And if there was writing, there might as well be written Sanskrit? Those who disagree with that interpretation, however, point to the etymology of lipi. The word is understood to have derived from Old Persian _dipi_ which itself has Semitic, rather than Aryan origins. In light of the Achaemenids, the theory does check out. It’s quite likely that the writing Panini saw was in the Aramaic script rather than anything Indic.

But what if they were writing Sanskrit in the Aramaic script? Or, what if they had a script derived from Aramaic to write Sanskrit and accommodating its vowel range? Kharosthi? If Panini seems ambiguous, we can turn to the Buddhists. The _Jātakas_ are a voluminous body of literature that form part of the canonical scriptures of Theravada Buddhism. While they’re typically placed in the third century, which makes them more or less coeval with Aśoka, oral traditions are understood to go further back several centuries. And these are replete with references to writing.

Several Jātaka stories speak of correspondence through letters, both private and official. To quote one such example, a servant named Katahaka forged a letter purportedly by his master to secure a girl from a neighboring kingdom as his bride.24 Another story speaks of letters exchanged between a teacher at Taxila and his ex-pupils.25 In yet another a king, having renounced his throne, writes to his brother and successor to waive off taxes for people who had treated him favorably.26 There are a number of other examples, but it’s already quite clear that even during Aśoka’s times, rock edicts weren’t the only kind of writing going on. Perhaps, when Megasthenes wrote of people being unfamiliar with written words, he meant it in a very narrow context? Or maybe only the elites could write? But even this doesn’t conclude our debate because the Jātakas were all in Pali. Even if it establishes that writing existed before or outside of imperial edicts, it still doesn’t establish that the language of choice for such non-monumental writing was Sanskrit.

A reasonable assumption can still be made that if writing in Pali existed, some could have also written in Sanskrit. If portable writing materials like birch bark or palm fronds were used during Aśoka’s time, despite the lack of material evidence, such writing could have existed before Aśoka, when the distinction between Prakrit and Sanskrit was less pronounced. The fact that Aśoka put up edicts in popular vernaculars itself indicates that the layman was able to read. To wit, writing must have existed.

## Conclusion

It is established beyond doubt that the vintage of Indian epigraphy goes far beyond Aśokan edicts as often presumed. While literacy might have been confined to a smaller pocket of village elites, its existence itself stands beyond doubt. Without literacy, the edicts become redundant. And literacy isn’t possible without written work. As the likes of Nearchus and Panini testify, writing as a form of dissemination predates the Mauryas by several centuries and the older we place them, the likelier they are to be Sanskrit. Since these primordial works were on ephemeral media, such as birch bark, palm leaf, or as Nearchus noted, fabric, they shouldn’t be expected to be physically extant. It is also likely that the earliest writings were in Kharosthi or even Aramaic.

Since the Prakrit vernaculars derive from a Low register of the same language whose High register was designated Sanskrit, anything written before, say, 500 BC, would likely be indistinguishable from what we recognize as Sanskrit today. There were no edicts before Aśoka because there was no empire before Aśoka. And since Sanskrit was not the matrix language in any part of his empire, it did not find space on his edicts.

[courtesy James Burgess in “Plate LI,” _Report on the Eleura Cave Temples and the Brahmanical and Jaina Caves in Western India_ , by Jas. Burgess, Trübner & Co., 1883.]

So to answer the question, why Sanskrit doesn’t predate Prakrit in writing if it does in speech, the reason is a combination of utility and chemistry. At any rate, don’t forget that the first extant inscription to bear Sanskrit, the cave inscriptions of Naneghat, is less than a century younger than Aśoka’s edicts. It’s impossible that Sanskrit became the preferred language of mass communication this abruptly. If anything, it would have been a medium for centuries leading up to that point, just not the matrix tongue.

It’s true that early Aryans were averse to writing and that the Rigveda was tailormade for oral dissemination. Scholars like Jack Goody even point to a taboo someplace in the Mahabharata against writing the Vedas.27 But the same Jack Goody also very much doubts the possibility of a work as complex as the Rigveda, along with the Brahmanas and the epics, to be entirely products of oral tradition. He doubts that even more so in the case of Panini. We, however, have nothing to base that conclusion on other than awe. Having said that, it’s far from unlikely that some kind of writing would have been in praxis at least by the time of Panini if not before. In fact, German Indologist Georg Bühler even dismiss the notion that Aśoka invented the Brahmi script. Sure, he must have modified it to serve his linguistic purposes better, but a “proto-Brahmi” script did exist long before him. Centuries before him. This proto script, Bühler places in the eighth, even ninth century BC.28 That’s over half a millennium before the first Mauryan.

If Bühler’s idea holds water, Indians were already writing long before Prakrit had diverged from the Sanskritic mainstream as an independent language family. What language could the Aryans of 800 BC be writing? Since Brahmi evolved from the Aramaic script and developed in the extreme northwest, the Aramaic language is a reasonable contender. But that’s also part of the realm of Vedic Aryans, Gandhara was a prominent _janapada_ or republic in the Vedic Age and later even became a _mahajanapada_. Which makes Sanskrit a far strong contender. Remember, there’s no independent Prakrit language at this point.

It must be pointed out here that Aramaic is both a language and a script. And just because something is written in the Aramaic script or a derivative thereof, does not mean it must be the Aramaic language or even a member of its family. Just as Semitic Maltese and Turkic Turkish are written in the Latin script, which is neither Semitic nor Turkic, it’s entirely plausible for the earliest forms of Sanskrit writing to be in scripts like Aramaic and Kharosthi.

Long story short, there was definitely an aversion to writing in the Vedic Aryan communities, Panini definitely knew writing, and before his time, writing was definitely more Sanskrit than what later became Pali or Gandhari. Do we have evidence? Yes and no. Yes, because there’s enough we can triangulate from Nearchus, Panini, etc., and no, because none of the pre-Aśokan Sanskrit writing has survived to this day.
