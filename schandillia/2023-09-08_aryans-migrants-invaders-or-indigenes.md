No other population group inspires the amount of ideological, social, and political hostility that the Aryans do. This is even discounting the uninvited genocidal connotations slapped to the name during WWII. The hot button here is its Urheimat or homeland. Hindu nationalists place it in India, their ideological adversaries, outside. An Indian homeland means they originated here and radiated outward, the Out of India Theory or OIT. A foreign homeland means they originated elsewhere and entered India either violently, the Aryan Invasion Theory (AIT), or peacefully, the Aryan Migration Theory (AMT). AIT is no longer of import, hasn’t been for a while now ever since they discarded it in favor of AMT. Which leaves us with OIT and AMT. So which of the two is it? Without prejudice or ideological investment, let’s find out.

We shall start with paleogenetics, the study of prehistoric genetic artifacts. Among all the things that could settle the debate, this is the newest. And the most decisive, for genes don’t lie. One thing that evolves just like us living beings is language. Naturally, an examination of linguistics can offer a mighty useful clue in our search, so that’s what we’ll look at next. In the same vein, the next smoking gun we’ll examine is literature because texts composed by the men in question can be a breadcrumb trail leading straight to their elusive homeland. Next up is a mysterious potion that pretty much formed the centerpiece of our ancestors’ religious practices; if we could figure out what it was and where it came from, we should easily learn a great deal about where its consumers originated and where they went. With all that out of the way, we’ll go on to hear it straight from the horse’s mouth. Somewhat literally this time. Horses and chariots are crucial Aryan mainstays and have much to offer to this investigation. After horses, we’ll talk hydrology, more specifically a river called Sarasvatī. Opinions stand divided on its existence and identity but the river wields a great deal of influence in the Hindu scripture. Which makes its location a very useful indicator of the original Aryan homeland. Lastly, we’ll examine astronomical and astrological clues hidden in Vedic and post-Vedic verses. Basically, letting the stars show us the way.

## Paleogenomics

Before we discuss paleogenetics in our context, a quick refresher on certain fundamentals is in order. To start with, there’s DNA. These are molecules that help make amino acids which in turn help make proteins. Every human cell has a bunch of DNA in long strings called chromosomes—most in the nucleus but some also in the mitochondria. This distinction is important.

During reproduction, two cells come together to form a new cell with DNA contribution from both. One is sperm, the other is egg. The sperm carries its mitochondria in its tail. During fertilization, the tail dissolves away and with it, the mitochondria within. So, only the mother’s mitochondria end up in the resulting zygote. This makes the mitochondrial DNA or mtDNA an excellent marker of maternal lineage. As for nuclear DNA, it’s packaged into pairs of chromosomes, two kinds of which exist in humans, X and Y. Men carry XY and women, XX. As evident, only the father can pass on the Y chromosome and only to the son. This makes Y-chromosome DNA an excellent marker of paternal lineage.

With that out of the way, we can proceed to our first exhibit, a study from 2005 that involved 15 experts and sampled more than 700 individuals from all over the Indian subcontinent across caste and ethnic groups.1 The objective was to identify the paternal haplogroups that originated in the subcontinent. A haplogroup, in the leanest terms, is a group of individuals whose genes share a specific set of mutations indicating common genetic ancestry. The study in question sampled only Y-chromosome genes. In particular, the haplogroup R1a1, but also R2. R1a1 because it’s commonly associated with the “Aryan gene,” although the association isn’t without contest. In short, if you establish that R1a1 originated in India, you establish that Aryans originated in India. That’s exactly what this study did and upended the very foundations of established Indology.

The study posited that R1a1 not only originated in present-day India and Pakistan but also spread to Central Asia from India as early as 10-15,000 years ago.2 This timeline seems more or less in sync with the prevailing understanding which places its origins between 15 and 20,000 years ago. Essentially, this deals two blows to the AMT—**a)** demonstrates the haplogroup’s indigeneity, and **b)** dates it to thousands of years before the supposed Aryan migration.

And yet, the authors stop shy of making any definitive conclusion. Instead, they posit that R1a1 may not even be the fabled “Aryan haplogroup” in the first place given its multiple back-and-forth movement between India and Central Asia thousands of years before Aryans even existed.

Another blow to AMT came only months later. This time, it was mtDNA, the matrilineal line.3 Seven experts including Kumarasamy Thangaraj and Lalji Singh—put a pin on those names, they’re important—concluded that haplogroup M, the most ubiquitous matrilineal mutation on the Indian subcontinent traces origins well within the subcontinent.4 In other words, Indian mtDNA is entirely, well, Indian. So, if paternal lineage is Indian, maternal lineage is Indian, the Aryans must also be Indian.

Circling back to paternal, a third study led by Swarkar Sharma in 2009 shone the flashlight exclusively on R1a1.5 This study screened more than 600 Y-chromosomes from both Brahmins and scheduled castes6 and further reaffirmed the haplogroup’s indigenous origins. In fact, it even went further to name Brahmins as its originators. Singh’s conclusions were primarily based on the observation that Brahmin samples exhibited a higher diversity of R1a1 than any other.7 A higher diversity certainly is indicative of a haplogroup’s origins, so the conclusion isn’t unexpected.

But just as we rush to bury AMT, some counterpoints warrant consideration.

Just as the 2005 study leaves the door open for further nuance, the 2009 study leaves the door open for further refinement and even refutation. Its concluding remarks clearly state that the sample size studied wasn’t enough to draw any far-reaching conclusions yet. At least three R1a1 subgroups were not even part of the study.8

Also, while it’s true that a higher diversity typically indicates higher indigeneity, the operative word is “typically.” Several factors can skew this seemingly linear equation, population being among them.9 A higher population means a larger gene reservoir which, in turn, means more opportunities for mutation, ergo higher diversity. And our subcontinent has been disproportionately more populous than any other throughout R1a1’s lifetime.

Further corroborating non-Indian origins of R1a1 is one paper from 2012 that studied R1a haplotypes from the Altai region, South Siberia, and the Himalayas to conclude that the mutation originated about 20,000 years ago in Central Asia and entered India more than 8,000 years _before_ the Aryans.10

So where does that leave us now? Significantly more on the side of OIT, but not significantly enough to finally bury AMT. At least not going by paleogenetics and at least not as of 2009. The situation further complicates with evidence of violent gene replacement in Bronze Age Europe. Between 1988 and 1994, a large number of skeletal remains were unearthed on the outskirts of Czechia’s Velim municipality. These remains showed clear signs of extreme violence, likely a result of warfare. Osteological examination identified a dozen children and three women, along with 69 more that couldn’t be identified, at just one site.11 In 2018, archeologists Anthony Harding of Britain and Kristian Kristiansen of Denmark too read this as a sign of widespread carnage and large-scale population replacement around the middle of the second millennium BC.12 Harding pointed to several mass graves in Czechia, besides the one at Velim, in support of this argument.

Later, two separate studies, both involving dozens of experts in a diverse array of fields would seem to lend support to this position in 2015. The first of these, published in March, sequenced 69 genome samples from prehistoric Europe and proposed that Europe was populated by hunter-gatherer groups until the start of the Neolithic period when farming communities from the Near East entered the continent. This was the first major gene injection of two on record. The second happened in the Bronze Age when a non-farming, pastoral people called the Yamnaya entered the pool.13 The second, published three months later, sampled about a hundred individuals from Bronze Age Europe and reaffirmed the previous study’s findings.14

OIT evangelists see in these conclusions a major support for their arguments against an Aryan invasion of the Indian subcontinent. The idea is that if the same Steppe influx was so violent and disruptive in Europe, it couldn’t possibly be peaceful in India. Especially considering both AMT and AIT posit an abrupt and complete genetic shift among the indigenous population. If the Yamnaya were violent in Europe, they ought to have been violent in India as well. But since they weren’t, they probably never came to India. On the contrary, it’s not improbable that they originated in India and massacred their way into Europe instead. We’ll return to these studies and Kristiansen a little later.

Fast forward to 2019. Enter David Reich, an award-winning Harvard geneticist with credentials listing names like MIT and Oxford; he was also part of the March 2015 study we just discussed earlier. That year, Reich was part of a team conducting the single largest undertaking of its kind in the history of Indology.15 It involved more than 100 minds including two we first met in 2005—Kumarasamy Thangaraj and Lalji Singh. The reason we dropped a pin on these two names is that neither Thangaraj nor Singh had been terribly enthusiastic about AMT. In fact, at one point during the study, they even reached an impasse with Reich over the latter’s favor of the controversial theory.16 That’s when the model had to be redesigned to account for the possibility that the mixing of Indian and Near Eastern genes could be a result of Indians migrating outward instead.

They examined more than 500 ancient individuals, and not just partial sequences but complete genomes—all 3.2 billion letters, the whole shebang. These samples came from a wide geographical area including not just the Indian subcontinent but also Central Asia and spanned a timeline going back 8,000 years. If there ever was a paleogenetics tie-breaker on the Aryan question, it was this. So what were its conclusions?

That the Aryan gene isn’t autochthonous. But more importantly, it’s part of every Indian genome, dealing a body blow to the popular notion that only upper-caste North Indians carried an Indo-European ancestry. Reich’s team traced the story all the way to 3300 BC when the speakers of the Proto-Indo-European tongue left their Steppe homeland in search of greener pastures and reached the Indian subcontinent sometime in the middle of the second millennium BC.17 A pit stop in the Oxus Basin brought them in contact with the Bactria–Margiana Archaeological Complex or BMAC populated by descendants of Neolithic Iranian farmers. While there was no genetic admixture between the Iranians and the Yamnaya in BMAC, there was much cultural and social exchange.

Once the group crossed the Hindu Kush and settled in the Swat region, it came in contact with the Indus periphery gene pool whence it continued to move deeper into the subcontinent interbreeding with the indigenes along the way. Do note that the study doesn’t consider any Harappan gene directly, for it found none.18 Instead, it observed genes from settlements on the fringes of IVC that are thought to have interbred thoroughly with the Harappans. This is the stock incoming Steppe pastoralists bred with to produce the early North Indian stock.

Another significant conclusion drawn here is that it’s only men that came from outside, the women were all indigenous.19 That, or only male immigrants were reproductively successful. Either way, this helps reconcile the autochthonous antiquity of mtDNA haplotypes with the relative recency of Y-chromosomal haplotypes.

Besides its sheer expanse and thoroughness, what adds further credence to this study is the fact that at least two known OIT proponents, if not straight-up champions, lent their names to the exercise and endorsed its findings. That answers any possible question of institutional or academic prejudice. Neither has so far proposed anything that challenges the conclusion of this study at least as of this writing.

This still isn’t all. Shortly after this paper, a supplement was published that exclusively studied one individual from Rakhigarhi in Haryana.20 Rakhigarhi is a Mature Harappan site, which places it between 2600 and 1900 BC. The specimen was extracted from a Harappan burial site dated between 2800 and 2300 BC. The study found a complete absence of any foreign gene in the sampled genome. More specifically, no Steppe input.21 Taken out of context, this can lend false support to OIT, but the paper is quick to point out that the genetic profile with no Steppe ancestry _no longer exists_ in modern populations, neither in India nor in Iran.22 Moreover, this paper only proves that there was no Steppe influx at least until 2300 BC, the lower edge of Rakhigarhi chronology. Since the alleged Aryan migration occurred _after_ the Harappan collapse, this finding should not surprise.

But what about Kristiansen and the two 2015 studies that confirmed massive violence when the Yamnaya entered Europe? Well, for starters, it’s absolutely not necessary for a population to behave the exact same way in two different situations separated by thousands of miles. At least not _a priori_. One group can migrate peacefully and another from the same community less so. That is _if_ there is violent interaction in the latter case in the first place. In fact, neither of the two papers that speak of population shift in Bronze Age Europe concludes any violence. They conclude population shifts, they conclude swift population shifts, but they do not conclude violent population shifts. Expecting a community to act consistently across time, space, and circumstances is a slippery slope to, in history communicator David Miano’s opinion, racial essentialism. His 2022 video _When did SANSKRIT appear in India?_ , by the way, was a big inspiration behind this piece. It’s available for free viewing on YouTube and is a must-watch for anyone seeking to explore the Aryan question further.

In fact, it was Kristiansen’s book where the idea took shape on the basis of those Chezia mass graves. These are, however, archeological conclusions, not genetic. And they’re based on a vanishingly small sample size. The mass graves prove violence, but they do not prove violence in all of Europe. We have no way to establish that what happened at those sites happened elsewhere too. We also have no way to conclude that what happened at those sites was a conflict between natives and immigrants and not among natives. In fact, Kristiansen himself came to terms with the deficiencies in his earlier position in a 2017 paper where he admitted that the interaction between the indigenes and the incoming Yamnaya was largely peaceful and the population shift, a result of sustained exogamy over several generations despite isolated incidents of hostility.23

One last consideration before we proceed to linguistics is milk. That’s because a large number of populations all over the world, surprising as it may sound, cannot digest milk in adulthood. The condition is called lactose intolerance. Milk contains lactic acid and the enzyme responsible for processing it is lactase. In some populations, the body stops the secretion of this enzyme past a certain age leading to lactose intolerance. In others, this doesn’t happen. The reason this ties in with paleogenetics is that the secretion of this enzyme is governed by a very specific gene and it is designed to switch off secretion after a certain age. Mutations, however, can always alter how a gene behaves. One such mutation, among others, is 13910T.24 This mutation prohibits the switching off of the enzyme and leads to a situation called lactase persistence, the opposite of lactose intolerance. This mutation is closely associated with Eurasian populations which is why Caucasian individuals are able to digest milk at any age while others aren’t. If the Aryans really didn’t originate in India and came from Central Asia or the Eurasian Steppe, this mutation must show up in modern Indian populations as well.

A study was done to investigate just that in the summer of 1981 and published in the _American Journal of Clinical Nutrition_. It found the subcontinent remarkably divided on lactose intolerance with South Indians exhibiting a far higher incidence than their northern counterparts. In fact, the condition followed a clear continuum along a north-south axis with Punjab exhibiting the lowest incidence at 15%, Hyderabad 50%, and Mumbai 62%.25 While not the final word, for lactose persistence, while primarily featured in the Aryan gene pool, isn’t entirely absent in all non-Aryan populations,26 this study certainly adds to the findings of earlier works by Witzel and others.

Can future studies reverse all of this, though? Difficult, but sure can. As the cost of sequencing full genomes, a prohibitively expensive undertaking, goes down—and it already is, exponentially—more precision will become possible. The study in question sequenced 523 individuals, tomorrow another could sequence a thousand. For now, however, this remains not only the most comprehensive study of its kind but also the most recent. So at least from the paleogenetics standpoint, AMT checks out.

But that’s just one area of study. What about language? Horses? Literature? What about astronomy? For any theory to hold water, it must check most, ideally all, boxes. Does AMT?

## Linguistics

Sanskrit shares a significant amount of linguistic overlap with seemingly unrelated tongues like Persian, Greek, and Latin. This is only possible if all four evolved from a common linguistic ancestor. What this ancestor was and how it sounded can only be derived with educated reconstruction. This has been done and the ancestor, named _Proto-Indo-European_ or PIE. Placing PIE in India would also place the Aryan Urheimat in India. Which makes it a hotly contested issue. One camp places it in India, the other, outside. There’s also one fringe that rejects PIE itself and instead, proposes Sanskrit as the ancestral tongue. That’s as wild as claiming we evolved from monkeys. We didn’t; we only share a common extinct ancestor. Just as Sanskrit and its European counterparts share a common extinct ancestor, PIE. We’ll keep the fringe theory outside this conversation.

Before we proceed let’s familiarize ourselves with _linguistic center of gravity_ , a philological concept propounded by German anthropolinguist Edward Sapir in the first half of the 20th century. The concept is often employed in determining the origin of a given language family in the absence of all other clues. Put simply, the theory suggests that the origin of a language family ought to be the place where it enjoys the highest diversity, not unlike paleogenetics if you’d recall. It also presupposes that descendant languages are more or less equidistant from their family Urheimat, similar to the center of gravity in physics.

A most illustrative example of this in action is the case of the Austronesians. The westernmost Austronesian tongue today is spoken in Madagascar, the easternmost in Rapanui or Easter Island. To add to it, Taiwan is also home to nine out of ten Austronesian subfamilies making it the region with the highest linguistic diversity. The linguistic center of gravity places the family’s origin point someplace in Taiwan. The linguistic center of gravity, thus, situates the family’s origin someplace in Taiwan, a conclusion now well supported by established scholarship and archeological research.

Can Sapir’s theory help place the PIE Urheimat with the same accuracy as it did with the Austronesians? Interestingly, just like the latter, the PIE family also classifies into ten subgroups—Anatolian, Albanian, Armenian, Balto-Slavic, Celtic, Greek, Germanic, Indo-Iranian, Italic, and Tocharian. Of these, only nine survive as the Tocharian branch went extinct more than a thousand years ago. Of these nine, seven trace their homelands between about 20° and 40° longitudes,27 i.e. roughly around the Black Sea. Which neatly corresponds with the Aryan Urheimat indicated by paleogenetics.

But while intuitive, the center of gravity system alone isn’t adequate to make any lasting conclusions. That’s because just like paleogenetics, a number of external factors such as population size and contacts with non-PIE languages could easily skew linguistic diversity. So we need more.

Which brings us to names. Of animals, of trees, of places, of geographical entities. What these things are named should be a fairly reliable indicator of who named them. The idea is simple—any name that has cognates in multiple PIE branches ought to have a name in the original PIE lexicon. Consequently, the habitat native to whatever that name represents ought to also be the region where the ancestral tongue was likely spoken, ergo the PIE Urheimat. The inference seems sound at face value. Take beech, for instance. The tree has names in all PIE branches but only grows in areas west of the Eurasian Steppe. Thus it can be construed with reasonable confidence that the PIE tongue is lore likely to be centered there than in India or Anatolia. This is called the beech argument or _Buchenargument_.

Similarly, there’s a salmon argument or _Lachsargument_. Since the fish has a PIE root, its habitat can also be read as the PIE homeland, which happens to be northern and central Europe. These are just two of many such terms. Wolf, red deer, elk, snow, all refer to entities alien to India and yet have names in the reconstructed lexicon.

So, is the debate settled at least on linguistics then? Not so soon, unfortunately. Because the theory only works at face value. If beech and salmon have PIE reconstructions, so do elephants and monkeys, both alien to Europe and native to India. Same with lion. Could one not, in that case, use these to argue for an Indian homeland? Ireland doesn’t have snakes but Irish, a PIE descendant, has not one but two words for it. How come?

Words get repurposed and reassigned to newer entities all the time. Imagine a community that lives in the Middle East and knows camels migrates to, say, Europe where no camels exist. What happens to the word ‘camel’ then? In the absence of usage, it’d naturally fall out of memory and consequently, vocabulary over generations. That is one scenario. In the other scenario, it’d get reassigned to something that’s reminiscent of camels but isn’t one. This happens all the time. This is what J. P. Mallory and D. Q. Adams argued in 2006.28 And they were by no means the first.

More than 90 years ago, Sanskrit scholar Lachhmi Dhar Shastri squarely debunked the beech argument pointing to the _bakula_ tree native to India which does seem to have a cognate in _buck_ , an English synonym for beech in Scotland. The fruit is still called _buckmast_. Consequently, it could easily be argued that emigrating Aryans carried the root _bak_ and applied it to a new kind of tree in unfamiliar European climes where bakula didn’t grow.29 Citing many more examples, Dhar finally establishes that the flora and fauna of the undivided Aryan homeland need not be represented in every daughter tongue and its environs.30

This might seem like an open-and-shut case, but the European Urheimat finds yet another argument in its favor, the _centum-satem_ divide. This pertains to the number 100. The reconstructed name for the number in PIE is _*ḱm̥tóm_. From this came _centum_ (Latin), _hekotón_ (Ancient Greek), _cant_ (Welsh), _śatá_ (Sanskrit), _sad_ (Persian), _sto_ (Russian), and many more. As early as 1871, German philologist August Schleicher saw a pattern here. In some languages, the word started with a guttural _/k/_ sound, in others, a palatal _/s/_ sound. He called the former group _centum_ (pronounced _/ˈkɛntəm/_) and the latter, _satem_. The centum-satem divide, he found, was essentially a West-East divide with centum confined to Europe and satem to Asia. Since the PIE original also carries a /_k/_ sound, the centum homeland must also be the PIE homeland, he argued.

Schleicher’s views were echoed by several other philologists, mostly European, including Dutch linguist Peter von Bradke. But after several decades of acceptance, the theory faced a serious challenge in the 19th century with the discovery of Tocharian. The language was spoken in what later came to be known as Bactria in Central Asia. In this language, the number was _känt_ (Tocharian A) and _kante_ (Tocharian B). An Asian satem? This was a serious threat. Dhar argued that the pattern was far easier to explain with an Indian homeland—the word originated in the sibilant form in Indian Sanskrit, then as the speakers (Aryans) moved westward, they came in contact with newer languages and the sibilant corrupted to guttural; the first time this happened was someplace in Tocharistan.31

Today, the centum-satem argument is no longer held in favor of AMT. Coming back to vocabulary, Dhar also rejected the notion that the stark lexical parallels between Avestan of Iran and Sanskrit of India indicated an eastward move from Iran to India. He argued that the phenomenon could just as reasonably be explained with a westward move in the opposite direction.32 Dhar made several other arguments to bolster his support for OIT, all as viable as the ones supporting AMT, if not more. His views would later be echoed by a number of future OIT evangelists including Koenraad Elst and Shrikant Talageri.

And now, let’s examine one last example that favors OIT—the rhinoceros. Today we call the animal _gaiṇḍā_ in Hindi. The word derives directly from Sanskrit _gaṇḍaka_. Note the dots below _n_ and _t_? That’s indicative of a retroflexive articulation. You make those sounds by curling your tongue back against the palate, a feature found only in Indic registers. Not even Indo-Iranian, only Indian. But _gaṇḍaka_ is still a post-Vedic term. The word in Rigveda is _khaḍga_. Different word, but still carrying the retroflex _d_. _Khaḍga_ later evolved to mean sword. Perhaps an allusion to its horn? Anyway, the retroflex is key here. As no other PIE descendant features it, one wouldn’t be wrong to conclude that the original PIE speakers must’ve inhabited a rhinoceros habitat and lost the word for it, including retroflex, as they moved westward.

One could argue Sanskrit borrowed the word from a Dravidian register, a completely unrelated family, but if that were the case, the modern Tamil word _kāṇṭāmirukam_ wouldn’t be a Sanskrit borrowing. Thus, _khaḍga_ and _gaṇḍaka_ must have traveled from Sanskrit to Dravidian and not the other way around. This is the conclusion Talageri arrived at in his seminal _Rigveda: A Historical Analysis_33. Problem with this conclusion is, while it’s true that Sanskrit didn’t borrow the word from Tamil, it’s not true that Sanskrit didn’t borrow at all. _Gaṇḍaka_ originally came from a whole other language family unrelated to both Dravidian and Aryan, the Austroasiatics. This Proto-Munda etymology has been common knowledge in scholarly circles since at least 1956 when Manfred Mayrhofer published his exhaustive work on Sanskrit etymology.34 In fact, the very concept of retroflex is a Munda loan.

Long story short, Talageri’s theory doesn’t stand the test of etymological scrutiny, at least in the case of rhinoceros. But we’re yet to have anything irrefutable in favor of an Indian PIE homeland. Given the impasse, we ought to look elsewhere. What linguistics couldn’t settle, literature just might?

Before we move out of linguistics, though, there are two recent studies that warrant attention. The first came out in the spring of 2015 and was published by the Linguistic Society of America.35 It was a phylogenetic analysis of languages in the PIE family aimed at ascertaining a more accurate Urheimat and routes of dispersal. All four researchers were from the University of California at Berkley. Its conclusions disappointed proponents of Indian origin and lent support to the Steppe hypothesis instead.

The other, more recent paper came out in the _Science_ journal36 barely days before this writing and has caught on like the proverbial wildfire, particularly in Indology circles. Known OIT proponents have latched on to the paper’s seemingly radical conclusions and started citing it as a novel piece of evidence in support of their position. The phylogenetic study behind this paper involved more than thirty philologists and geneticists and more than 160 languages—about fifty ancient and the remaining, modern—and concluded that the PIE family evolved much earlier than hitherto thought. While the existing theory places the PIE Urheimat in the Steppes, this study revises it to the south of the Caucasus, i.e. modern-day Armenia, roughly speaking. Another thing it revises is the timeline. Earlier research marked the origin roughly in the vicinity of 4000 BC. This study proposes 6000 BC and beyond instead.

From Armenia, the model proposes multiple linguistic families branching out in two primary directions—one going north into the Steppe onward into Europe to create the Corded Ware Culture, and the other going south eventually branching out into Indic and Iranic subfamilies. By 5000 BC, major European subfamilies, such as Italic, Greek, Balto-Slavic, and Armenian have already started diverging. The southern branch, under the linguistic influence of Iranian agriculturists, develops into the Indo-Iranian subfamily. This subfamily would further split into Indic and Iranic around 3000 BC, likely in BMAC. The paper busts a number of myths surrounding the linguistic aspect of the debate, the most obvious being the age and location of PIE. What it does not say, despite so being the claim in OIT circles, is that Sanskrit developed before the Indus Civilization. The reason that’s an impossibility is that the Early Harappan Period already started at least three centuries before the Indic-Iranic split. Also, assuming the “Sanskrit” spoken in 3000 BC was the same as the one in 1500 BC, one could also argue that the Harappans spoke the same language in 3300 BC that they spoke in Mehrgarh in 6000 BC. These would be absurd conclusions considering language is not a static phenomenon. The Indic tongue spoken by the Aryans immediately after the split from Iranic could in no way be the same language as the Sanskrit Rigveda would get composed in millennia down the line. Equally absurd, at least unsubstantiated, would be the conclusion that since the Indic subfamily came into being in 3000 BC, Rigveda also must be dated to 3000 BC. That’d be classic circular reasoning since there’s no evidence placing Sanskrit in 3000 BC.

With that, the linguistic scale decisively tips in favor of a non-subcontinent homeland. Again, it’s not entirely impossible for future research to completely upend everything we’ve learned so far. But the likelihood of something that dramatic is infinitesimally small. Of course, studies can always be interpreted in mischievous ways to serve a variety of sociopolitical prejudices, but as of now, the conclusion that PIE originated away from India stands on sufficiently firm ground. And now we can move on to the scripture.

## Literature

Here again, enter Shrikanth Talageri, a rather zealous OIT champion who spent years scrutinizing the linguistic side of the debate and evangelized his conclusions in a series of published works including the one we just referenced moments ago. In this work, Talageri has based his entire argument on the antiquity, organization, and linguistics of the ten Rigvedic volumes. And given the sheer vintage and size of the composition, rightly so.

As just noted, Rigveda is a collection of ten books or _mandalas_ in, according to Talageri, the following chronological order: 6, 3, 7, 4, 2, 5, 8, 9, 10 (1 being contemporaneous with 4, 2, 5, and 8). We’re not interested in the antiquity here, it’s the order that matters. Talageri then uses geographical artifacts from each of these to infer that the earliest books were composed in the middle-to-lower Gangetic plains, i.e. Uttar Pradesh and Bihar, and the later ones in Haryana, Punjab, and Afghanistan. Which would clearly attest to a westward migration from a Gangetic homeland.

But to arrive at this groundbreaking conclusion, Talageri goes far beyond Rigveda. Because the Riveda, by itself, leaves out a whole lot of crucial details, names of people and places in particular. For instance, it’s common knowledge that what’s today northern India was once _Āryāvarta_ and what’s today Uttar Pradesh was once _Madhyadeśa_. But these names do not find any mention in all of Rigveda. To then prove that UP is where the original composers of the first Veda lived, is a tad difficult. That’s why he turns to a body of derivative work known as _Anukramaṇī_ or _Anukramaṇikā_.

Think of Anukramaṇīs (there’s several, some extant, others lost) as indices of hymns, kings, authors, and poetic meters for Rigveda. These indices often tell what Rigveda itself omits. The most widely regarded of Anukramaṇīs is the one by Kātyāyana, _Sarvānukramaṇī_. Talageri points to 9.96 and 10.179.2 of this text to establish that an ancestral Bharata (a tribe of early Aryans mentioned in Rigveda) king named Pratardana once ruled Kashi, the site of modern Varanasi. The _Kāśirājā_ epithet for Bharata king Pratardana in Sarvanukramani 9.96 and 10.179 completely backs this proposition.37 Which means early Aryans lived in and ruled over what’s today Uttar Pradesh. Which instantly makes Uttar Pradesh the original Aryan Urheimat.

Having established a UP homeland, it’s time to establish a westward migration. Talageri proposes a two-way migration. One group eastward into Bihar (Magadha), the other westward into Kurukshetra, Afghanistan (Gāndhāra), and beyond. Again, Rigveda does not mention Magadha, although it does Gāndhāra just once. Let’s understand the eastward leg first.

Although Rigveda does not mention Magadha, it does mention Kīkaṭas as a native tribe encountered by the Bharata king Sudās on an eastward campaign in 3.53.14.38 But whatever could this tribe have to do with Magadha? Well, the same verse also names their king, Pramaganda. Which almost rhymes with Magadha because that’s where he ruled. This is the first clue. Talageri advanced two, the other coming from not one but two post-Vedic texts, Bhāgavata Purāṇa and Vāyu Purāṇa, both of which refer to Bihar or Magadha as the Kīkaṭa country. The former marks it as the prophesized birthplace of Gautama Buddha in 1.3.24,39 while the latter even names eastern cities like Gaya and Rajgir in the context of Kīkaṭa in 108.71–74.40 In this conclusion, Talageri seems to echo Indologists Albrecht Weber41 of Prussia and Heinrich Zimmer42 of Germany from a century ago. Almost verbatim.

The case for an eastward migration into Bihar seems airtight.

Only one problem, more than one actually.

To begin with the obvious, the Puranas are much later compositions by the time of which the Aryans were already well-entrenched in the East. Bhāgavata, for one, doesn’t show up until at least 1,700 years after Rigveda. While scholars hold little unanimity on its dating, the earliest we have for an older work Vishṇu Purāṇa is more than two centuries into the common era43 as proposed by Ramachandra Dikshitar. The oldest direct estimate for Bhāgavata is AD 500 from theologian Daniel Sheridan.44 As for Vāyu, Dikshitar dates it to 500 BC.45 Although Dikshitar is known for generously pushing Indic works further back in time than scholarly consensus, even 500 BC makes the text in question significantly later than the Vedas. The Aryans would’ve comfortably settled the lower Gangetic plains by then.

Furthermore, the near–rhyme between Pramaganda and Magadha fails to hold, not just because the rhyme is weak but also because the two words share no etymology to begin with. As early as half a century ago, Dutch Indologist Franciscus Kuiper dismissed an Aryan etymology for Pramaganda on grounds that the prefix _Pra-_ exists neither in Sanskrit, nor in Dravidian. As is the case with retroflex, this could also be a loan from a Munda substratum, he posited. In more recent times, philologist Michael Witzel concurs46 and further bolsters his position with 3.53.11 which recounts an _aśvamedha_ (horse sacrifice) by Sudās.47 The sacrifice was performed after a decisive victory in the legendary Battle of Ten Kings the tail end of which is stated to have gone down someplace on the banks of the Yamuna River in 7.18.19.48 This, Witzel takes as strongly indicative of Kurukshetra, the most widely acknowledged site of the Battle and the victory sacrifice that followed.

Just so we’re clear, Aryans did go east. Just not in the time of Rigveda. They expanded into Bihar, into Bengal, and even beyond. But much later.

And finally, it’s time to talk Anukramaṇīka, the derivative Vedic work referenced by Talageri to arrive at his conclusions of a UP homeland for primordial Aryans. The problem with this approach is the word _derivative_ itself. The Anukramaṇīka Talageri refers to is Kātyāyana’s Sarvānukramaṇī. Sure this text unambiguously situates Madhyadeśa in modern-day UP, but it came almost a thousand years _after_ Rigveda.49 Which, once again, renders unproven the fact that the composers of Rigveda had any knowledge of these environs.

There’s also this westward migration to contend with, even though Talageri’s eastern homeland itself stands on shaky ground.

The place of import in the context of Afghanistan is Gāndhāra (often misunderstood as etymological ancestor to modern-day Kandahar), for this is the only place in all of Afghanistan that finds mention in Rigveda. Talageri points as evidence of a later Aryan presence in Afghanistan to 1.126.6, the only verse in all of Rigveda that mentions Gāndhāra by name.50 As we’ve already noted, this mandala is contemporaneous with 4, 2, 5, and 8. The verse in question falls in the later half of the composition which supports Talageri’s Afghan connection. So if the Bharatas inhabited Uttar Pradesh in earlier mandalas and then show up in Gāndhāra later, a westward migration becomes self-evident.

However, there are problems with this position as well. For starters, Talageri got the verse wrong; it’s 1.126.7, not 1.126.6. But that’s no big deal, what matters is that a mention of the place exists in Rigveda. The exact mention, by the way, is Gāndhāri (an adjective) and not Gāndhāra (a noun), also no big deal. And now we come to the actual argument. If you read 1.126 in its entirety, you’d notice something off about 1.126.6 and 1.126.7,51 the last two verses in the hymn. While the hymn is an ode to the generosity of King Bhāva and his son Prince Svanaya, the last two verses abruptly depart from the theme and show a conversation between the prince and his wife Romaśa. In 126.6, Svanaya speaks of a woman who clings to him like a weasel and offers infinite delight. In 126.7, his wife responds with an amorous invitation comparing herself with a Gāndhāri eve. This abrupt switch in theme coupled with an equally abrupt change in metre, according to English Indologist Ralph Griffith, firmly establishes that the verses are not part of the original composition.52

The only Rigvedic reference to Gāndhāra being a misplaced interpolation significantly jeopardizes Talageri’s idea of a westward migration from Uttar Pradesh into Afghanistan. What seems to further weaken his argument is the lack of any etymological relationship between Gāndhāra and _gandharva_. He points to the word’s mention in 1.22.14, 1.163.2, 3.38.6, 8.1.11, and 8.77.5, among others as an indirect reference to the place in Afghanistan.53 AMT proponents like Witzel reject the connection as speculative in the absence of a concrete etymology.54 After all, even sea is _āb_ (𒀊) in Sumerian, a language isolate, and water is _āb_ and _ap_ in Persian and Sanskrit, both PIE descendants. And yet the two families share no etymology whatsoever. Such coincidences are admittedly rare, but not impossible.

Having said that, gandharva appears in both early (the first being 3.38.6) and later mandalas (the last being 10.177.2). And 9.113.3 even describes Gandharva as a proper noun, a divine bringer of Soma.55 Since Soma (more about it in a bit) is associated with the Hindu Kush and Gāndhāra, along with Swāt, is considered the first Aryan homestead on the subcontinent in AMT circles, the gandharva–Gāndhāra connection might just check out? Furthermore, 1.163.2 calls them the bringer of horses56 (more on these too in a bit). Again, in AMT circles horses were never native to India and were brought from the Steppes by early Aryans. If that’s the case, those horses must have crossed the Hindu Kush and traveled through the Gāndhāra country. So, again, could there really be an undiscovered etymology between the two words? We don’t know, but it’s certainly worth considering and Talageri might after all be on to something there.

Even then the final roadblock that remains in Talageri’s model is Gandharva’s presence throughout Rigveda’s chronology. This would indicate that the Aryans were familiar with the deity all along. AMT can easily explain this as a memory of their early sojourn in Afghanistan on their eastward expedition. OIT cannot.

In conclusion, Rigvedic compositions refuse to sway one way or the other with absolute conviction. There exist arguments that favor AMT and there exist those that favor OIT. The former, however, remain marginally more numerous than the latter since most of Talageri’s arguments stand refuted on etymological grounds.

## Soma

A crucial aspect of the early Rigvedic rite is a pressed plant extract offered as sacrificial offerings to gods and consumed as a divine potion by the worshippers. This extract is referred to in Rigveda as _soma_. The plant’s habitat and identity could viably indicate its consumers’ homeland. Needless to say, this makes it a remarkably contentious subject between those who’d like it placed in India and those who’d prefer it elsewhere. Besides its provenance, where it occurs in Rigveda also holds great significance to the debate.

Interestingly, the plant also finds mention in Avesta, the Zoroastrian canon, as _haoma_. It’s thus fair to conclude that the two words share a common etymology.57 Today, consensus exists on _*sáwHmas_ , the reconstructed Proto-Indo-Iranian root. The _soma–haoma_ transformation follows a known pattern also followed by _Sindhu–Hindu_ and _Asur–Ahura_. With this shared heritage established, it’s safe to infer that the plant and its usage in rituals must belong to a place and time where the two branches were yet to split. Question is, what was that place? Was it India of OIT? Or Central Asia of AMT?

A name that keeps popping up in AMT circles is ephedra. As a consciousness-altering extract from a genus native to Central Asia and the northern Indian subcontinent, ephedra offers a strong candidature. A big reason for this, besides the plant’s psychoactive properties, is its use in haoma offering in Iranian Zoroastrian circles, later among their Indian counterparts. Of course, the plant used in Vedic soma rites, especially in South India, is a different one.58 This led scholars to theorize that ephedra was the original offering which got replaced by more tropical alternatives as Vedic Indians moved deeper into the subcontinent where it was harder to come by.59

This identification of ephedra as the original soma goes back at least a hundred years and remains the dominant theory among Indologists across the board. And that’s for a reason. The continued usage in Zoroastrian rites isn’t the only evidence in its favor, other clues have been found in BMAC sites, understood by AMT advocates as the place where the Iranian and Indian branches split.

The BMAC angle was first noted by Soviet archeologist Victor Sarianidi who discovered traces of ephedra in pots excavated at Gonur and Togolok, two important BMAC cities. The liturgical nature of these remains was apparent from the fact that the pots were excavated from buildings that served as temples.60 This would easily make Margiana the site of proto-Zoroastrianism and, by extension, proto-Hinduism. However, this edifice stands on the assumption that ephedra, and only ephedra, is the plant we’re looking for. Turns out, not everyone agrees.

A few decades before Sarianidi, amateur American ethnomycologist Robert Gordon Wasson had proposed a different candidate, a kind of toadstool whose pileus looks like a bright red burger bun with prominent white spots—fly agaric in common parlance. Besides the physical descriptions gleaned from Rigveda, Wasson also offers an interesting biological argument in his defense, straight from the scripture. But before we go there, it’s important to understand the context. The preparation of soma post-extraction, as described in Rigveda, is a three-step process. Wasson calls these steps, _filters_. The first filter, according to him, is sunlight and involves desiccation. The second filter is wool and involves straining the desiccated concentrate through thick wool to sieve out the solids. And then we come to what he calls the Third Filter.

Verse 9.74.4, illustrates a curious procedure where the strained soma concentrate is mixed with ghee and milk. Then the priests consume large quantities of the concoction to the point that their bladders swell. Then they pass it out in urine.61 This, Wasson proposes is the Third Filter—the metaphorical entrails of Indra. The soma-laden urine is the ultimate potion—potion that grants immortality and is fit for gods. Goes without saying, the idea is extremely controversial.

But this alone isn’t enough to establish the mushroom connection. Ephedra could also be put through these three filters and consumed all the same. And this is where the Third Filter comes in handy. Wasson points to extant communities in eastern Siberia who routinely still do exactly this with the mushroom in question. In their case, the shaman performing the ritual prepares the concentrate and urinates it, then offers the urine to the participants for consumption.62 This is a remarkable finding that indeed places fly agaric at the very forefront of the race. If the Siberians are doing it in the present, and Rigvedic verses spoke of it in the past, there’s little left to doubt.

Besides, Wasson also refers to several Rigvedic verses that describe the soma plant as a “buttress of the great heaven” and reads it as indicative of the mushroom’s upright stalk.63 9.72.764 is just one of several such verses. Some seem to describe the stalk, others the fire-colored pileus.

And yet we have the Gonur findings to reconcile with. Is it possible that the source really was ephedra, and that the Siberians and the Indo-Iranians share only the urine ritual and not the plant? At least not impossible. Only new discoveries could settle the debate. That’s exactly what happened in 2002 in Tarim Basin.

Tarim Basin is a region in China’s Uyghur country partly drained by the eponymous river cutting through its northern section. The region has been an archeological attraction since the earliest years of the 19th century, especially for its mummies. The desiccating desert clime has kept the mummies from spoiling and most corpses from as far back as 2000 BC are still in remarkably good condition.

The oldest mummies in Tarim Basin belong to the Late Bronze Age Qäwrighul culture the site of which was excavated to reveal 42 graves from the time. These are the graves that archeologist James Mallory and sinologist Victor Mair studied at the turn of the 21st century with significant conclusions. They discovered not only elaborate burial customs and funerary rituals but also clear indications of a belief in life after death. The dead, they found, were buried with items likely deemed necessary on their onward journey. Items like ornaments, shoes, blankets, food, weapons, and…ephedra.65 Mallory and Mair think of ephedra as serving both religious as well as medicinal purposes in the Qäwrighul society.

Interestingly, the sites of BMAC and Qäwrighul are only separated by the great Pamir range which is often considered the source of BMAC ephedra. Could the two sites be connected? Scholars say yes and confirm an exchange route between the two and beyond, cutting through the Pamir wall. They call it Inner Asia Mountain Corridor or IAMC—the Silk Road of prehistory. It’s along this route that the BMAC priesthood imported its supply of ephedra from Tarim.

For a while, it was believed that the two populations belonged to the same stock. The most likely scenario seemed that some members of the BMAC communities crossed into the Tarim Basin and formed the Qäwrighul culture, bringing ephedra with them. Then in October 2021, a team of thirty-four experts led by Fan Zhang published their findings after an extensive genomic study of thirteen Tarim individuals. This study revealed that the population of Tarim was indigenous to Tarim and unrelated to both Yamnaya and BMAC gene pools.66 Which lends support to the model that makes Tarim peoples the original users of ephedra and also its exporters to their BMAC neighbors.

But although ephedra enjoys wide consensus, not everyone agrees. An important challenge to its candidature as the exclusive source of the divine potion comes from American Indologist David Frawley. But before we get to him, let’s circle back to Sarianidi for a quick minute.

When Sarianidi said he found ephedra remains in Gonur and Togolok, he also found opium and cannabis in the same pots and in the same ritualistic context. While Gonur pots had both opium and cannabis, the ones in Togolok had only opium.67 Ephedra, of course, was the common denominator. While this doesn’t take away ephedra’s candidature, it does introduce two other plants as substitutes. Likely because ephedra was at least at times, not very easy to come by? This jives well with the idea of it being a Tarim import.

With that out of the way, let’s return to Frawley. In line with Sarianidi’s findings, Frawley too dismisses the notion of ephedra’s exclusivity. While he admits ephedra’s position as the ideal, at least in Iranian rites, he offers sugarcane as a native Indian alternative.68 But not without almost squarely dismissing Wasson’s toadstool first on grounds that mushrooms don’t have leaves, a prerequisite of the Vedic soma plant.69

To further add support to his sugarcane theory, Frawley brings up two words—Śaryaṇāvat and Mūjavant. Śaryaṇāvat comes from the root _śara_ which refers to a type of reed that grows in water and goes by the botanical name _Saccharum sara_. Back in the day, it was considered sacred by the worshippers of Indra. If _Saccharum_ sounds familiar, that’s because it’s the etymological root for _śakkar_ (sugar), and that sugarcane too belongs to the same genus. Śaryaṇāvat refers to the place, a water body to be precise, where śara grew, referenced in 8.64.11.70

As for Mūjavant, the root is _mūnja_ ,71 also a type of reed used as a source of soma in Rigveda. Biologists call it Saccharum bengalense and its habitat includes not just Iran and Afghanistan but also Northeast India and Myanmar. Mūjavant is the place where mūnja grows in Rigveda. If Śaryaṇāvat is a water body, Mūjavant is a mountain as stated in 10.34.1.72 Frawley places this mountain in the Himalayas, as do fellow OIT advocates, but we’ll return to it in a bit to explore an alternative theory.

Long story short, Frawley admits multiple possibilities depending on whatever would’ve been available to the Aryans in their immediate habitat. This seems reasonable, for as we’ve already noted, soma rites in South India use neither ephedra, nor mushrooms, and go for a more readily available _Sarcostemma_. Ephedra sure would have been the original source, the ideal.

But where does that leave us on the Aryan homeland debate? Central Asia if it’s ephedra, India if any of the reeds. We’ll go with ephedra because the findings in Margiana and Tarim make too compelling an argument. Having said that, what if Aryans did originally live in India, used sugarcane, and later switched to ephedra as they moved westward into Afghanistan? That’s a model championed by our old OIT friend Talageri who bases his arguments on Rigveda itself. The most comprehensive discussion of soma is in the 9th book. The whole maṇḍala is devoted to _Soma Pavamāna_ or soma purification.73 If you’d recall that one’s a significantly later composition in Talageri’s chronology, although the word does make an appearance in earlier books as well, 6.2.8 being the earliest occurrence.74

The inference, therefore, is that if soma is discussed at length only so late on the Rigvedic timeline, it must be because the Aryans only got well-acquainted with it after they’d left their Indian confines and ventured into Afghanistan and beyond. That’s the theory Talageri advances.75 Furthermore, he points to 8.64.11 where the potion is said to come from the reeds growing in the vicinity of Suṣomā,76 commonly identified with the Soan River in modern-day Pakistan, which would add further weight to Frawley’s theory and also make soma a thoroughly Indian idea.

While this argument does seem compelling, it does little to explain the ritualistic use of ephedra in Margiana, Tarim, and Iran. Remember, the mummies found in Tarim Basin go back almost 4,000 years, if not more. Is it possible that Aryans originated in India, extracted soma from sugarcane, reached Afghanistan, discovered other cultures using ephedra, switched to ephedra, and splintered off into a Zoroastrian branch that continued with ephedra for its rituals? Yes, but a far simpler model would be where Indo-Iranians, or Proto-Aryans inhabited the fringes of BMAC, got acquainted with ritualistic and recreational use of ephedra, and splintered off between Iranians and Indo-Aryans, the Iranians then continuing with ephedra and Indians switching over to sugarcane and other tropical plants over time. Occam’s razor favors AMT on account of soma. For now.

To add to it, the identity of Mūjavant remains in dispute. While Frawley and Talageri place it in the Himalayas, more precisely Kashmir, Michael Witzel identifies it with a peak towering over its surroundings in the Pamir Highlands—the mighty Muztagh Ata. He invokes etymology in drawing a parallel between Muztagh and Mūjavant. This in itself might make for a weak argument, but there’s yet another piece of evidence tucked away in Avesta. To that end, he refers to the collection of twenty-two hymns in the Younger Avestan language called _Yashts_ , abbreviated as _Yt_. The one Witzel refers to in particular is the thirteenth or _Fravardin_ Yasht. Therein, verse 125 mentions a righteous individual from the Mūza country whose _farvashi_ or spirit is to be held in high regard.77 This, Mūza, he sees as cognates with Rigveda’s Mūjavant, both alluding to the same geography in Muztagh Ata.78 The identification of Mūjavant with Muztagh Ata, along with the continued usage of ephedra in Zoroastrian rituals, reasonably consolidates support for a Central Asian origin of both soma and its ritual.

Also, amateur though he was, we must not lose sight of Wasson’s Third Filter. What are the odds that a process that’s current in pockets of Siberia finds direct mention in Rigveda with zero direct or indirect intercourse between the two cultures? Passing potions through one’s bladder for purification is not that universal an idea. It’s too specific to randomly spring up in two completely unrelated cultures independently. What does seem likely is that the practice first entered Tarim Basin, then Margiana, through trade contacts along the IAMC. Then from Margiana, it made its way into India. While the plant changed with time, the “filter” remained. Because nothing else explains 9.74.4. This model was also echoed in an exhaustive paper on soma published in 2015.79

## Horses

Rigveda is integral to the Hindu culture and horses are integral to Rigveda. So much so that at least two whole hymns, 1.162 and 1.163, are solely dedicated to celebrating their physical and mystical prowess.80 That’s besides several others exalting horse deities, divine chariots, and horse sacrifices. This equestrian theme pervades the entire body of Hindu scripture including the two epics and other post-Vedic works. The beast exploded onto the scene worldwide toward the end of the 13th century BC and was singlehandedly instrumental in a dramatic civilizational churn known as the Bronze Age Collapse. Long story short, it’s hard to overstate the significance of horses in the shaping of human history.

Since horses underscore the Rigvedic way of life so strongly, it naturally follows that their placement would be a subject of tremendous contention. Indigenous horses would mean indigenous Aryans. The Indus Valley Civilization ended in the beginning of the 2nd millennium BC. AMT sees them as pre-Vedic and OIT sees them as Vedic. So to further narrow down the debate, it’s all about whether the Harappans knew horses. If they did, the case for indigenous Aryans becomes compelling enough to at least consider. As one would expect, AMT firmly maintains that there were no horses in IVC. But the notion has strongly been challenged by material evidence unearthed as far back as half a century ago.

Traditional consensus in academic circles has always been on a horse-free IVC. But not always without contest, both frivolous and serious. We’ll, of course, limit our consideration to the latter. It may come as a surprise to many but the first such challenge goes back almost a century. An account of excavations conducted in Mohenjo-daro between 1922 and 1927 was published in 1931. It was edited by none other than Sir John Marshall of the Archeological Survey of India or ASI. In it, was a section on animal remains by Indian anthropologist Biraja Sankar Guha and British naturalist Robert Sewell. And among the artifacts discussed was a partial mandible of an ungulate. This, Sewell identified as _Equus caballus_ , the modern horse. To arrive at this conclusion, he measured the molars and premolars and tallied his findings against two existing tables.81 One was by Swiss zoologist Johann Duerst and pertained to horse remains in a Copper Age village in Turkmenistan called Anau82 (note this name, we’ll be returning to it later). The second was by the Zoological Survey of India and pertained to a modern horse skull in Calcutta’s Indian Museum. The comparisons checked out. If the skull in the museum was a horse, and if the equid in Anau was a horse, then Sewell’s Mohenjo-daro animal was most certainly a horse too.

Seven years later came a rejoinder to the same publication, this time for the 1927–1931 window. It was authored by British archeologist Ernest Mackay, who further corroborated Sewell’s notions with yet another horse figurine. While the animal was missing a tail, obviously broken, the stump at the breakpoint was clearly detached from the rump and pointed upward, reminiscent of a pony’s tail.83 Also missing was a hump, a feature that would’ve easily made it a cow or water buffalo. It had to be a horse, Mackay was convinced.

So if Mohenjo-daro knew horses, likely so did the rest of IVC, which makes a compelling case for indigenous Aryan continuity. Case closed? Not so soon. We’ve still only gotten to 1938 and a lot of Indus has flown since. All we have so far is one partial jawbone and one figurine of which the latter’s identification is, in Mackay’s own words, _purely tentative_.84 We absolutely need more.

Our next stop is 1946 when a paper in the Journal of Near Eastern Studies reported horse remains from Rana Ghundai, a pre-Harappan site near Loralai in Baluchistan.85 These remains, more specifically teeth, were ascertained by the veterinarian to be indistinguishable from those of modern cavalry horses. Of this, the paper seems particularly confident, i.e. definitely a horse, not a pony, not a donkey. But this isn’t all, for not too far from Rana Ghundai is Periano Ghundai where British archeologist Stuart Piggott identified at least one horse in an array of terracotta animals.86 Which makes the case for OIT even stronger, at least on equestrian grounds. 

Moving on, in 1954, a brand new site was discovered in Gujarat, at Lothal. Excavations began in earnest and over five years of relentless digging, a whole Harappa-era city emerged. Among the artifacts found here was a solid alabaster wheel that likely belonged to a cart. Why cart? Because also found here was, you guessed it, a terracotta horse—thick, short tail; unmistakable equine head; no hump.87 Mohenjo-daro to Lothal is more than 400 miles as the crow flies. Surely the two sites knew horses independently and well enough to make figurines depicting them?

Over the following decades, more horse artifacts would show up in various Harappan and even pre-Harappan sites. Listing them all out would be redundant, for the point has already been made. But before we move on to examining these findings more closely, let’s simplify what we have on our hands. Essentially, we can divide these artifacts into two categories—organic remains (bones), and inorganic (figurines, paintings). We won’t examine the latter because prehistoric artwork is never accurate enough to allow an exclusive one-on-one mapping to a real-world subject. Nor do horses stand out as much as, say, giraffes or elephants to allow such a mapping with confidence.

That leaves us with organic remains to validate our conclusions. Someone must examine those dental pieces and confirm that they’re definitely horses and not, say, donkeys or mules. Someone did just that. About five years after the Lothal discovery, German paleontologist Prof. Frederick Zeuner and Indian archeologist Dr. Benapudi Subbarao made a trip to Kutch to study a member of this family native to the region.

Sometime back we had bookmarked the name Anau from Turkmenistan when discussing Sewell’s findings in Mohenjo-daro. Now is a good time to return to it, before we catch up with Zeuner and his friend. The last time we were there, Johann Duerst had identified a modern horse among excavated remains and dated them to more than 4,000 years ago. His argument, besides dental dimensions, was the presence of spur and the placement of vomer incision. Spur, here, is a kind of enamel fold and vomer is the bit of bone that separates the two halves of the nasal cavity.88 This was 1904 and seemed pretty much bulletproof. For almost two decades.

In 1921, another German zoologist Max Hilzheimer came across equid remains in an Ancient Mesopotamian dig at Tell Asmar in Iraq. Upon investigation, he found that neither the vomer nor the spur was exclusive to horses in the region. The placement of vomer incision, for instance, is the same in horses as well as donkeys. As for the spur, while they’re generally absent in donkeys and present in horses, he did note its absence in some oriental horses and its presence in some half-ass specimens in Berlin Zoological Museum. Thus, in the absence of a complete skull, Hilzheimer asserted, it’s impossible to tell a horse and a donkey or a half-ass apart.89

But that’s only the spur and the vomer, we didn’t talk enamel fold. That’s because enamel folds and most other dental features are known to be inadequate in identification of one equid from another. Even if it wasn’t proven as inadequate during Hilzheimer’s time, it would be in ours. A 2017 paper in _International Journal of Osteoarchaeology_ established pretty much just that. A group of nine researchers from universities like Cornell and Cardiff studied equid dental remains from Çatalhöyük to assess the reliability of dental attributes in identification of specific species. They found that enamel folding patterns failed to differentiate between the three equid species under consideration.90

Coming back to Tell Asmar, all this inadequacy of spur, vomer, and enamel folding still fails to conclusively establish that the specimen _definitely_ isn’t a horse. All we’ve established thus far is that it’s not _necessarily_ a horse. To fill that gap, Hilzheimer looked to bones other than those of the skull. Fortunately, limbs were available. Two sets of bones, the metatarsi and the metacarpi, are known to be shaped and aligned differently in each equid species. Which means, those in horses are unlike those in donkeys, which are unlike those in half-asses, and so on. Hilzheimer noted that the metatarsus of Tell Asmar bore a striking similarity to a half-ass or _Equus hemionus hemippus_ specimen (no. 7942) at the Berlin Zoological Museum.91 Also known as Syrian wild ass or Mesopotamian onager, this animal had been hunted for meat and hide since the very dawn of Holocene down to Neo-Assyrian times.

With that insight, let’s revisit Duerst’s Anau horse for a minute. Remember, it’s only dental remains that informed the specimen’s identification as a horse, a notion far from reliable as we’ve already noted in the case of Tell Asmar. Also, the animal in Tell Asmar is also native to Turkmenistan which makes it significantly more likely that what Duerst found was an onager than a horse.

There’s a reason we took this detour out of India. If the “horse” of Anau is really an onager, then Sewell’s conclusions about the Mohenjo-daro mandible are inconclusive, if not straight-up wrong. Once again, all Sewell had was dental remains—no complete skull, no metatarsi, no metacarpi, nothing that could identify the specimen distinctly as a horse and not a donkey or an onager.

Now back to Kutch where we left Prof. Zeuner and Dr. Subbarao to study the local equid. This local equid, interestingly, is also an onager. Locals call it khur, its zoological name is _Equus hemionus khur_. In other words, this animal and the those of Anau and Tell Asmar are subspecies of the same group, _Equus hemionus_. On the other hand, Zeuner found no horses. Which led him to conclude that the equids of Mohenjo-daro could have only been onagers and not horses,92 exactly in line with what we inferred moments ago. But what about the carts? Don’t they require domesticated beasts of burden?

Not necessarily, Zeuner argued, and pointed to onagers used as beasts of traction in Ancient Mesopotamia without domestication. Wild asses or onagers could easily be obtained from the wild as foals and tamed without selective breeding or domestication. This is how elephants are employed even today. He posited a multitude of Assyrian onager depictions to bolster his point.93 Also, while not necessary, onager domestication in prehistoric Near East isn’t an impossibility either, as indicated by depictions on an excavated vase from Khafajeh94. Archeologists place this fragment in the Jamdat Nasr period in Ancient Sumer which makes it at least 5,000 years old. Zeuner’s theory on Mesopotamian onagers would later find resonance in Edward Hyams’s 1972 _Animals in the Service of Man_.95

All this leaves us with three reasonably dependable facts so far:

  1. The equids of IVC were identical to the ones in Turkmenistan, were identical to the ones in Ancient Mesopotamia.

  2. The equid in Mesopotamia (Tell Asmar) is an onager and not a horse.

  3. Kutch, where Lothal lies, is still a natural habitat to onagers, but not to horses.




The inference seems quite clear—Harappans knew wild asses, not horses.

However, a 1972 finding in Surkotada threatened this model once again. While leading a team for the Archeological Survey of India, Jagat Pati Joshi unearthed yet another set of bones that alluded to horses. Interestingly, he found these across all archeological levels, i.e., corresponding to both Period IA which starts in 2100 BC, and Period IC which ends in 1700 BC. The bones’ equestrian identity was confirmed by fellow team member A K Sharma. Harappan horses seemed to be back in play.

Sharma published his findings in 1974 in the official ASI bulletin Puratattva. Unambiguously rooting for Harappan horses, he identified three species in Surkotada’s osteological remains— _Equus caballus_ (modern horse), _Equus asinus_ (donkey), and _Equus hemionus Khur_ (Kutch wild ass or Indian onager). Of these, the caballus was present throughout IA and IC,96 dating them to as far back as the end of the 3rd millennium BC. In the build-up to his conclusion, though, Sharma mentions a series of earlier horse identifications, many of which we have already discussed, e.g., Sewell. One we didn’t is Bhola Nath’s 1962 discovery in Harappa.97 This one’s worth talking about because so far, all our discussions have confined to the south (Lothal, Mohenjo-daro, etc.), Harappa takes us north. A quick glance through Nath’s paper, however, reveals what Sharma forgot to mention—that the horse remains of Harappa dated to the end of the civilization, after the Aryan advent.98 Nath calls it invasion because that was the dominant theory during his time. He also refers to similar findings in Rupar digs which too he places more toward 900 BC than 2000 BC.99

But why do we take Surkotada so seriously when all previous horse findings have only amounted to speculation? Because this time it isn’t mere teeth, it’s limbs as well—two canon bones, one first phalanx, two second phalanx, and two third phalanx. Earlier finds (Lothal, Mohenjo-daro, etc.) were all speculated on dental comparisons the reliability of which Hilzheimer had already established in Tell Asmar. If you recall, Hilzheimer had also proven metapodials or limbs as better identifiers. Well, now we have limbs. Surkotada might just swing things back in OIT’s favor.

The most significant support to Sharma’s horse came from Hungarian archeologist Sándor Bökönyi in 1991.100 He narrowed down to one tooth (no. 2149) and one phalanx (no. 2304) as the only items in the entire Surkotada collection that could be associated with horses. The tooth, he identified as a lower milk premolar, P3 or P4 which, at 39 mm, was too large for any Indian hemionid (half-asses). Similarly, the phalanx measured at least 8 mm longer and 3 mm thicker than the largest khur specimen from the region, clearly indicative of a larger equid, i.e., a horse.101

Bökönyi passed away on a Christmas day three years later. All those years, he was in correspondence with Harvard archeologist Dr. Richard H. Meadow who was entrusted with reviewing Bökönyi thesis for _Harappan Studies_. The two even met in person on the sidelines of an archeozoology summit in Germany in October 1994. Three years later, Meadow published his findings after an exhaustive examination of Surkotada material and all available data.102

First up is the no. 2149. Hilzheimer’s reservations about equid identification on dental grounds notwithstanding, this tooth sample must be reconciled with in light of Bökönyi’s strong inferences. Meadow compared the specimen with counterparts from the Zooarchaeological Laboratory at Harvard and found the crown too high for it to be a milk tooth. In scientific terms, it was too hypsodont to be deciduous. He further studied other parameters such as distal margin, overhang, angle of occlusal, etc., to conclude that it was a permanent molar and not milk premolar as Bökönyi theorized. When this correction was shared with Bökönyi he agreed with it too. But that changes the animal’s identification—the lingual dents on the molar’s surface were more indicative of a donkey or an onager than a horse.103

As for item no. 2304, Meadow found the difference between its dimensions and those of local hemionids too small to matter. Furthermore, he also found this phalanx to be shorter than at least two onager phalanges found in Shahr-i Sokhta back in 1978.104 Which contradicts 2304’s identification as horse on grounds of size. That said, Meadow admits that the Surkotada specimen is the only one in all of IVC that ever came this close to being a horse. Conclusion? No horses in Surkotada.105

Although we intended to limit our discussion to osteology, we must make an exception for one particular seal from Mohenjo-daro and the storm it triggered in the Aryan discourse at the turn of this millennium. This goes back to _The Deciphered Indus Script_ by N S Rajaram and Natwar Jha in 2000. The work’s primary subject, as the name suggests, is epigraphy, but there’s also a section on Harappan horses and the academia’s grand cover-up in the area.

A fierce and unrelenting OIT advocate, Rajaram’s magnum opus promised a significant body blow to the prevailing consensus on the horse discourse. While the academia at large was busy and content with bones, he went for seals. IVC is well-known for its animal seals and Rajaram saw a horse in at least one.

In his book, Rajaram produced an enhanced reproduction of a seal referenced as Mackay 453. This reproduction he strongly likened to a horse, although some would see a deer instead. Rajaram might, however, be on to something because the bushy tail is unmistakably equine. In fact, even onagers and donkeys don’t have such thick bushy tails. If the Harappans depicted a horse in their seals, they certainly knew horses.

Historian and known AMT advocate Steve Farmer decided to investigate. The first point of investigation was Mackay 453, for an idea of how the reproduction compared with the original. And right off the bat, it threw a curveball. For the seal in question is not even an intact piece. It’s broken in a way that the entire front half of the “horse” is missing. Whereas Rajaram’s reproduction is whole. Farmer further informs that Mackay’s seal itself is a later impression. The original was photographed by one Jyrki Lyytikkä for yet another AMT proponent Asko Parpola.

Given that scholarly works like this are never the place for artistic license, it’s hard to not see foul play here. Did Rajaram invent an extrapolation to scaffold his OIT sentiments?

While Farmer has already done an exhaustive breakdown of all the ways Rajaram is wrong, a most obvious argument against Rajaram’s “horse” is its tail. While Rajaram’s reproduction shows a thick bushy appendage, the original seal has a more ropelike appearance with a brush-like end. This is no horse’s tail. A donkey sure seems a better fit, although scholars call it a “unicorn bull.”

Rajaram’s theory stood squarely discredited, with a mountain of rebuttals. So irredeemable was the dismissal that even avowed fellow Harappan-Aryanists like Koenraad Elst distanced themselves from the debate.106 A scholarly defense of the theory never came. And perhaps never will. Dr. Navaratna S. Rajaram passed away in December 2011.

So, the bones are not horses, the figurines are not horses, and the seals are not horses. Can we now conclude that the Harappans didn’t know this animal and that it only came to India with migrating Aryans? Can we now say with confidence that Aryans aren’t autochthonous at least on equestrian grounds? We almost did. Then came French Indologist and member of the hallowed Indian Council of Historical Research Michel Danino. In January 2006, he published a paper on the subject and once again, called into question everything we’d concluded until that point.

Much of the paper speaks of artifacts we’ve already examined and explained, but there’s one piece of osteology we haven’t touched upon—ribs. The modern horse, i.e., _Equus caballus_ , has thirty-six, eighteen on each side. We have twenty-four, twelve on each side. They attach to the sternum or breastbone via soft tissues called costal cartilage. All but a couple. We call them floating ribs. We have two pairs of those, horses have one. Remember this configuration, we’ll need it later. Now back to Danino.

So while we know horses have only eighteen pairs of ribs, a total of thirty-six, a verse in Rigveda seems to speak of one with only thirty-four. 1.162 is one of the two hymns dedicated to horse sacrifice or _aśvamedha_ and offers a breathtakingly gory insight into the procedure. Particularly gory is 1.162.18 where the carcass is cut open with an ax:

     _catustriṃśad vājino devabandhor vaṅkrīr aśvasya svadhitiḥ sam eti.
    acchidrā gātrā vayunā kṛṇota paruṣ-parur anughuṣyā vi śasta._
    “The axe converges on the thirty-four ribs of the prizewinning horse,
    Who has connection with the gods.
    Arrange his legs, undamaged, into patterns,
    And carve them up joint by joint, having called them out in order.” (trans. Jamison)

Thirty-four ribs. This couldn’t be a modern horse, the one alleged to have come from the Steppes with migrating Aryans. What if the Aryans never _brought_ horses? What if they were always here and employed horses that were also always here? No Eurasian Aryan, no Eurasian steeds. This is what Danino proposes.107 The model fits even better if we consider, as Danino suggests, a horse breed native to the subcontinent. One such example is _Equus sivalensis_ , a large equid native to the Himalayan foothills and popularly known as Siwalik horse.

Only problem with Siwalik horse is, it went extinct long before there were humans. The species does have a descendant in _Equus namadicus_ but that too went extinct more than 15,000 years ago. Worth noting is the fact that neither is considered a _true horse_. But that still doesn’t explain the two missing ribs in the horse of Rigveda. Was it really horses they were sacrificing back in the day?

Fortunately enough, the question was already asked long before Danino. And also answered. Among the first was French anthropologist Charles Piétrement who noted the anomaly in his 1870 work on the origins of horse domestication.108 He too, theorized an extinct race of 34-ribbed horses native to the Indian subcontinent first domesticated _in situ_ likely by an indigenous Aryan people.

Naturally, this would remain a conjecture at best until such a time archeologists got their hands on a specimen that fit the model. No such specimen has ever been found. Yet the conjecture needed answering. So, one of the most recognized names in 19th century Indology shot a letter to British biologist Thomas Henry Huxley seeking resolution. The response Max Müller received was published in an 1875 issue of _The Popular Science Monthly_.109

Huxley approached the question from multiple angles, first being linguistic. Right off the bat, he pointed out the absence of articles in Sanskrit. The definite article _the_ , he explains, can profoundly change the meaning of a sentence in English. In this context, “thirty-four ribs” is not the same as “the thirty-four ribs.” The latter implies a total of thirty-four ribs, whereas the former suggests thirty-four, possibly out of more than thirty-four. Subtle, yet crucial. Sanskrit doesn’t have this distinction.110 In agreement with this reasoning, Müller later adds that it’d be rather odd to mention the number if the value were common knowledge. “Cut the ribs” would sound far more intuitive than “cut thirty-four ribs.” Unless it were thirty-four _out of_ , say, thirty-six.111

On the ritualistic side, Huxley argues that the only reason to cut through the ribs could be to access the organs sitting inside the ribcage. If the objective were to avoid mangling the bones as implied in other verses of the hymn, such as 1.162.20, the best way to cut open the cage would be to take the blade to the softest part thereof. That’d be the costal cartilage connecting each rib to the sternum. Once every rib were thus separated from the sternum, the cage could then be laid wide open for easy access to the heart and other organs. And that brings us to the floating ribs we discussed earlier. Since these do not connect with the sternum, there’s no need to cut them. Horses have one pair of those. Which leaves us with thirty-four that do need cutting and two that don’t.112

Huxley illustrates his idea with a rather grisly example of a human autopsy—If a man were to be cut open on the autopsy table, only twenty ribs would be severed from the breastbone which would provide the coroner access to the entire body cavity for further examination. The two pair of floating ribs wouldn’t need cutting, for they’re not joined to the breastbone in the first place. Anyone describing the process, then, would mention the cutting of twenty ribs without necessarily implying that twenty is all that the deceased had.113

On zoological grounds, Huxley squarely dismissed the existence of a 34-ribbed species although he did not discount the possibility entirely.114 And it’s understandable, genetic mutations allow for any anomaly. Six-fingered humans exist, even though that’s not the norm. 38-ribbed horses exist too, even though that too isn’t the norm. As for thirty-four, that’s possible too. Only none has been found so far. Neither extinct, nor extant, suggests Huxley. Thus, the only adequate explanation for the Rigvedic verse seems to be floating ribs.

As for Danino’s _sivalensis_ , its candidature is interesting, given no information on its rib count has ever been published. The species went extinct more than half a million years ago and a complete skeleton is yet to be found. And the same goes for _namadicus_ which went extinct some 14,000 years ago. Interestingly, Danino also proposes Przewalski’s horse as another candidate115 which comes as quite a surprise. Because while this animal does satisfy the rib count, coming as it does with thirty-four instead of thirty-six, it doesn’t quite help OIT. The breed is native not to India but to Central Asia and Mongolia.

Furthermore, thirty-four ribs, while uncommon, are far from a rarity as suggested by both Huxley and Danino. We’ve already seen Przewalski’s, but there’s also the famous Arabian horses. The latter for the most part have only seventeen thoracic vertebrae instead of the usual eighteen. Which means seventeen rib pairs. And yet they’re just as true as any other true horse from the purported Aryan homeland in Central Asia. Therefore, the Rigvedic anatomy need not be an anomaly, after all, although floating ribs still seem a more plausible explanation.

Now an important question worth asking is, how did they even manage to get their chariots into India if they did? These are not mountain-friendly vehicles and horses are not mountain-friendly animals. How could they have made it through the deathly cold of the Hindu Kush? The solution to the puzzle comes from a completely unexpected Baluchistan. Here, cutting through the Toba Kakar range is the gateway of Bolan Pass. On the eastern side of this gateway lie the archeological digs of Pirak and Kachi. What makes this site special is that this is where the earliest horse remains of the Indian subcontinent have been found. Archeologists have dated the finding to 1700 BC. A fact of relevance here is that Baluchistan was far less arid back then than it is today. The region was indeed conducive to horse rearing. Bolan being significantly milder on the weather front meant it offered a better conduit into the subcontinent than its northern counterpart in the Hindu Kush. Indologists like Frits Staal have used this information to propose a model where the Aryans entered India along two separate routes in multiple waves. One came through Kyber and the other through Bolan. In this model, horses came with the latter.116

Staal finds strong scriptural endorsement for his model from Rigveda 7.18.7 which names a tribe of Bhalānas along with others who are “led hither” by Indra, the “companion of Arya.117” He draws an etymological line between the Bhalāna and Bolan to conclude that this must be the route the eponymous tribe would have taken.118 Parpola, too, agrees with this etymology and extrapolates it back to BMAC.119

All in all, zero evidence currently supports horses in either the Indus Civilization or elsewhere in the subcontinent. The animal came to India from Central Asia, and not through the Hindu Kush as earlier thought, but through the Toba Kakar along a more southerly route. Without making any conclusive statement on Aryan origins on the basis of this finding, let’s move on to chariots.

## Chariots

A most common misconception in non-scholarly circles on either side of the aisle is that only chariots may have spoked wheels and only chariots may be drawn by horses. At least in India, bullock carts with spoked wheels have been ubiquitous for centuries. And horses have drawn carts in rural Europe for just as long. So, before we even get to chariots, let’s first distinguish them from carts.

Chariots are generally defined as light, two-wheeled contraptions meant to transport people, especially in wars. These were the primary mode of battlefield locomotion in late Bronze Age and early Iron Age warfare. Carts, on the other hand, have been around for far longer. These are typically heavier, four-wheeled, and used primarily for transporting goods rather than people. The key difference here is in weight. Goods like farm produce, for instance, don’t warrant speed. They can be transported slowly. But speed becomes immensely crucial in wars. Which is why chariots ought to be lightweight. One way to make them lighter is by reducing the wheel count, hence two instead of four. Another way is to replace solid wheels with spoked wheels. This is how chariots first emerged and revolutionized warfare. Of course, spokes did get ported to cartwheels over time because why not?

With the preliminary out of the way, we can finally discuss the _why_ of chariots in the Aryan context. For the longest time, AMT has held that chariots, like domesticated horses, first emerged in the Eurasian Steppe and over time brought to India and other parts of the world by migrating pastoralists, Aryans in our case. This mainstream scholarship has often come under scrutiny in light of newer findings in various Harappan digs. If Harappans had chariots, it’d lend fresh weight to OIT, because it’d indigenize the single most important aspect of locomotion in all of Hindu scripture.

Solid wheels have been excavated from Harappan sites since the earliest days of archeology. As have terracotta models of solid-wheeled carts drawn by bulls or other bovids. But those aren’t relevant to our discussion. We want spokes. Do we have any? Popular AMT position is in the negative. However, Danino injures that notion with some surprising pieces of material evidence. He speaks of terracotta wheels from sites like Rakhigarhi, Bhirrana, Banawali, and many more that show clear signs of spokes.120 Not real spokes, mind you, but painted on an otherwise solid surface. Even if just painted, these spokes serve well enough to revive the otherwise buried possibility of the real deal. If the Harappans knew spokes, they most certainly knew chariots. But these are no new findings. Danino merely refers to discoveries made almost a century ago. The Bhirrana specimen, for instance, goes back over 80 years.

One of the oldest finds comes from Harappa itself. Archeologist Madho Sarup Vats published pictures of his discoveries in 1940; these included a wheel with painted spokes. But here’s the thing—He reported it as a pedestal, not wheel. And this was no subjective opinion based on speculation, there’s a reason behind his interpretation—other parts of the purported pedestal. He noted that a piece that looked like a pedestal’s stem and yet another that looked like its top fit quite well with the disk-shaped piece. This could only mean that the latter was not a wheel but a pedestal’s circular base.121 Vats’ inference was also informed by the presence of a large number of broken vases and goblets that would’ve likely sat on the purported pedestal back in the day. Think pieces of a jigsaw puzzle fitting together just as expected.

II, by Madho Sarup Vats, Government of India Press, 1940.]

Two years later, Ernest Mackay discovered a bunch of toy carts in Chanhu-daro, a Harappan dig not too far from the better known Mohenjo-daro. In broken parts, of course. Reporting it in the 2019 _Purātattva_ , University of Calcutta archeologist Krishnendu Das quoted Mackay as calling a wheel in this collection, a “prototype of a spoked wheel.”122 But a look at what Mackay actually said betrays a clear misunderstanding. The specific artifact Das refers to is item 20 from plate 58. Indeed it’s a wheel. And indeed Mackay refers to wheel perforations serving as the “prototype of a wheel.” But the latter is said about modern cartwheels in rural Sindh where wheels are still carved out to make them lighter, and reinforced with iron bands.123 Mackay’s wheel is a tripartite model with no spokes by any stretch of the imagination.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!hzrB!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb538ca8c-f8cd-445a-b9bb-2f9beff76015_800x502.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!hzrB!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb538ca8c-f8cd-445a-b9bb-2f9beff76015_800x502.jpeg)**Fig. 8** : Toy carts excavated by Mackay. #20 is a tripartite wheel with no hint of spokes, painted or otherwise. [courtesy E. J. H. Mackay in “Plate LVIII. Toys: Harappā Culture,” _Chanhu-daro Excavations: 1935–36_ , by E. J. H. Mackay, American Oriental Society, 1943.]

On the other hand, the same collection also carries, as #12, a toy ram with wheels sporting painted spokes. Since Mackay’s photographs aren’t clear enough, Das points to a better copy published several years later by American archeologist and IVC specialist Jonathan Mark Kenoyer.124 This image, does show clear hints of what could only be spokes, much like on Vats’ pedestal base. Except, this time, it was no pedestal. Could this finally be the smoking gun?

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!SZfe!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fefcab0bc-2d44-49a5-b0d8-21d36d7c65f5_415x335.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!SZfe!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fefcab0bc-2d44-49a5-b0d8-21d36d7c65f5_415x335.png)**Fig. 9** : Toy carts excavated by Mackay. #20 is a tripartite wheel with no hint of spokes, painted or otherwise. [courtesy E. J. H. Mackay in “Cat. no. 161,” _Ancient Cities of the Indus Valley Civilization_ , by Jonathan Mark Kenoyer, Oxford University Press, 1998.]

It could. But the strokes need not represent real spokes. They might just as well be decorative artwork. Had the creator intended for them to be spokes, it isn’t really difficult to carve out real spokes in wet clay. Also, every aspect of art isn’t necessarily an accurate representation of reality. Take the crisscross lines on the ram, for instance. Rams do not come with hatch lines. Nor do they come with wheels. Keyoner himself agrees with the possibility of such painted “spokes” being mere decorations although when he does that, it’s in the context of another wheel excavated from Kot Diji.125

Another specimen Das refers to in support of Harappan spokes comes from Kuntasi in Gujarat. This one was identified and proposed by the archeologist late Madhukar Keshav Dhavalikar.126 But the “wheel” is much larger than even the cart it’s purportedly meant to pull, and is concave on the outer, i.e., painted side. This isn’t how wheels are supposed to be and Keyoner thinks it’s more likely to be some kind of a lid instead.127 In a similar vein, Keyoner also rejects other specimens from Lothal and Banawali.128

The only specimen Keyoner admits as “convincing” is from Rakhigarhi129 which, interestingly, looks identical to Vats’ pedestal base—same raised hub, same painted strokes, and same dimensions. Both Das130 and Danino131 concur on its identification as a spoked wheel. But if the wheel from Harappa wasn’t a wheel, one ought to wonder how this one must be a wheel either.

Having said that, it’d still be rather premature to conclude that spokes definitely didn’t exist in IVC. The evidence so far does not favor spokes as strongly as we’d like, but they do not confidently dismiss them either. Shikaripura Ranganatha Rao, the archeologist famous for having discovered Lothal, published a report on spoked wheels from the Mature Harappan period in the 2006 issue of Purātattva where he strongly sided with the possibility. The paper focused on artifacts from Bhirrana in Haryana. One of the wheels, he noted, had two sets of spokes painted on, one clockwise, the other counterclockwise. In a criss-cross fashion. He recognized the pattern as the spokes on many modern bicycles.132 Interestingly, however, while Rao generously sides with spoked wheels from Mature Harappan sites in the Ghaggar-Hakra basin (Haryana and its vicinities), he also notes a conspicuous absence of such artifacts on the Indus side.133 Long story short, so far spokes in Harappan society has remained a matter of subjective hypothesis rather than concrete corroboration. The only spokes we’ve ever had are painted or etched in solid disks. Representative of physical spokes? Or decorative artwork? Opinions stand divided.

Now that we’ve established that spokes may or may not have been a Harappan feature (mostly not), it’s time we circle back to the big chariot misconception we started off with—that they do not have a monopoly over spokes at all. All chariots have spokes, but not all spoked wheels belong to chariots. Lightness is crucial for chriots, but not undesirable for carts either. Therefore, even if the spoked wheels did establish their presence in IVC beyond doubt, which they haven’t so far, they won’t necessarily establish chariots.

And this brings us to Daimabad where occupation was discovered across five chronological phases, the last one spanning four centuries through 1000 BC, also called the Jorwe culture. Among a vast array of Chalcolithic artifacts found in this dig is a miniature bronze “chariot” yoked to a pair of draughts. This is perhaps the only such creation in metal from a Harappan-era site which makes it a mighty significant find.

The piece is a swift-moving lightweight chariot not different from those used in Late Bronze Age warfare in horse cultures such as Egypt and Mesopotamia. The contraption depicts an extremely barebones payload, with room for a single, unseated individual and no more. The reason mainstream scholarship refuses to admit it as evidence for autochthonous Aryans is, once again, the absence of horses. The pair of draughts yoked to the chariot are both humped animals, most obviously oxen.

Archeologist and ethnologist Erwin Neumayer, however, disagrees and claims that the yoke seems to be originally configured for horses, even if eventually repurposed for oxen. The central pole, for instance, is too long for oxen but perfect for horses. That’s because the two animals are driven differently. Horses are whipped, oxen are tugged at by the tail and poked in the behind. In the latter case, the charioteer must be close to the animal, hence necessitating a shorter pole.134 But the absence of horses still blocks a confident conclusion. The chariot itself has been dated to the Jorwe culture, i.e. between 1400 and 1000 BC. Which means, even if Neumayer’s conjecture holds water, and it does, it still fails to argue against AMT because, by that time, the Aryans would have already been in India anyway. So the chariot could easily be an Aryan idea borrowed by the Jorwe people. At the same time, one mustn’t ignore Daimabad’s location. How an Aryan idea reached a remote Maharashtra, remote even for the Harappans, at a time when they were just starting to urbanize the upper Gangetic plains, is a question that remains open even if we side with AMT.

But that chariot isn’t all, Neumayer also shines a light back on spokes by introducing us to an array of those in prehistoric rock art across sites in Madhya Pradesh and even Karnataka, such as Chibbar Nulla, Chatur Bhoj Nath Nulla, Gelpur, and Bhanpura–Katiria Kund. While he admits it’s impossible to identify the animals as horses, given the extremely high level of abstraction, the wheels remain unmistakably spoked.135 Unfortunately, it’s also impossible to date these paintings. Could these be horse-drawn chariots from “pre-Aryan” times? Yes. Could they be proto-chariots drawn by equids other than horses, such as onagers? Also yes. If anything, the latter still remains a far likelier scenario in the absence of more convincing artifacts. Physical chariots, however, may never be found given our subcontinent’s tropical character which doesn’t jive well with wooden remains.

Or so it seemed until a startling discovery in 2018. Some forty miles from the national capital of India is the Late Harappan site of Sinauli. The site, located in the Ganga-Yamuna interfluve or _doab_ , came to light when in the early years of the 21st century a group of farmers stumbled upon human skeletons and potsherds while working the fields. The serendipitous finding was immediately reported to the ASI which began excavations toward the end of 2005. This was the first phase that lasted a year and unearthed a number of post-Harappan burials along with a suite of grave goods including weaponry. A second phase was conducted in the summer of 2018 and this is the one we’re interested in.

Sensation was created when the 2018 dig revealed, among other things, three coffin burials. Of these, one coffin came with two full-sized chariots and another with one. This was the first time we found evidence of not only chariots but also weapons in Harappa. Hitherto, prevailing scholarship had imagined the Indus Civilization as a war-free community. The very reason Aryan Invasion Theory fell out of favor was the absence of any kind of weaponry in all of IVC. Sinauli challenged this notion with not only chariots but also swords, bows, and arrows. There’s a lot to unpack here.

For starters, there’s the chariots. It’s Sanjay Kumar Manjul, the man leading the excavations, who first identified them as such. The reason was the number of wheels, besides the configuration of the axle and yoke. Chariots, given their need for speed, almost always sport a single pair. Carts, on the other hand, can and often have two. Exciting though the idea sounds, there’s one big problem with Manjul’s proposition—spokes. Every single one of the six Sinauli wheels is a solid, unspoked disk. Remember, while carts may or may not have spokes, chariots must. Witzel was the first to point this out for Sinauli. Parpola seconded it. The ossified wheel remains do indicate impressions that seem to radiate from the hub out to the outer rim. But those, Parpola found to be decorative pieces of copper stuck upon otherwise solid wheels rather than spokes carved into hollow ones.136

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!wnG7!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb2e756f2-8ca6-494a-85d8-d95b107a0161_628x473.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!wnG7!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb2e756f2-8ca6-494a-85d8-d95b107a0161_628x473.png)**Fig. 10** : The impressions on Sinauli wheels are decorative copper triangles rather than real, functional spokes. [courtesy Vijay Kumar in “Fig. No. 09. Closure view of the Wheel of the Chariot,” _Indian Journal of Archeology_ , vol. 3, no. 2, edited by Vijay Kumar, American Oriental Society, 2018, p. 742.]

The absence of horses further complicates the case for chariots, although to be fair, no other animal has been found either. Even the animal depictions in found in Sinauli artworks are lacking in horses, whereas bulls abound.137

While enough evidence exists to affirm that the “chariots” of Sinauli were, in fact, carts pulled not by equids but by bovids, there’s still a crucial missing piece in the puzzle. And that’s the burial itself. What we have in Sinauli is unlike anything found anywhere in the entire Indus Civilization. The Harappans did sometimes bury their dead, but they cremated far more often. We know this because not nearly as many prehistoric graveyards have been found so far as we’d expect. And even when they buried, it wasn’t cart or chariot burial for sure. We’ve already noted the anomaly regarding weapons. Clearly, the inhabitants of Sinauli were different from the inhabitants of IVC. Who were they?

Parpola suggests Aryans. He likens Sinauli cart burials to the kurgans of the third-millennium Yamnaya burials where grave goods typically included chariots and horses. But in the absence of horses at Sinauli, Parpola proposes these Aryans as first-wave immigrants who rode not horse-drawn chariots but bull-drawn carts. Which would mean that not only did Aryans come in multiple successive waves, but they started out even before the first chariot with spoked wheels emerged in Sintashta.138 If this theory checks out, it completely upends the paradigm while still lending tremendous credence to AMT. So far, no competing theory exists for Sinauli. The final tie-breaker can only come with a proper DNA study, something that’s still awaited as of this writing.

Long story short, we do not have enough to confidently associate chariots with any pre-Aryan society on the subcontinent. There are conjectures, some good ones even, but still far from compelling enough to draw a conclusion. Horses stand unproven. Spokes stand unproven outside of painted strokes which are more likely to be decorative than functional representations as explained before. And the star attraction of this debate, Sinauli comes temptingly close, yet fails to establish autochthonous chariotry. Even if it didn’t, dating would be crucial. Any artifact dated to within 4,000 years would be hard to conclude as pre-migration. For now, chariots, like horses, soma, genetics, and to an extent, linguistics and scripture, fail to support OIT with meaningful confidence.

## Hydrology

Ganga is the single most important river in all of Hinduism. But this wasn’t always the case. Back in the times of Rigveda, that position belonged to another river by the name of Sarasvatī. The name finds mention in reference to a water body as early as 6.52.6139 and subsequently in eight out of nine remaining books. Three whole hymns, 6.61,140 7.95, and 7.96.141 exclusively celebrate Sarasvatī. The Ganges, on the other hand, finds just two mentions, in 6.46.31142 and 10.75.5.143 Clearly, Sarasvatī once trumped the Ganges as the prima donna of Hindu rivers. In post-Vedic times, however, the river seems to have disappeared. Naturally, its course and location have captured Hindu imagination for thousands of years and are now subjects of wild speculations. And for a very understandable reason. Given the river’s primacy in the very first Hindu canon, its location would be a reasonably good indicator of the ultimate Aryan homeland. Just like the first soma plant. So let’s locate Sarasvatī.

Among the earliest theories on Sarasvatī’s location is one by the 19th-century German orientalist Christian Lassen who named the elusive river as ancient India’s western boundary, a line separating the noble ones who followed Vedic tenets from the barbarians who didn’t.144 The Aryans, in his model, came from a distant land and spent some time in Kabulistan on the banks of the Kabul River, before moving eastward to settle along the Sarasvatī. Further describing the river’s course, he speaks of it as originating in the Sivalik, flowing past Sirmaur, in the general direction of Indus, then disappearing into the sands.145 There’s no river today called Sarasvatī (although there _is_ one called Sarsutī). But there is one that fits Lassen’s description—Ghaggar. This river later joins the now moribund Hakra channel to become the Ghaggar-Hakra system.

To arrive at his conclusions, Lassen points to a reference in the Zoroastrian scripture, more specifically Vendidad 1.19, to a land of seven rivers or _hapta hindava_ created by Ahura Mazda. This land is subsequently made hot, diseased, and inhospitable by his adversary Angra Mainyu.146 The seven rivers are interpreted by Lassen as Indus, Sutlej, and the five rivers of Punjab.147

If this theory holds, it’s a meaningful blow to AMT despite Lassen’s own AMT leanings because it places Sarasvatī well within the subcontinent. If Rigveda was composed in India, and the most important river it mentions is Indian, then it’s reasonable to assume the composers, ergo early Aryans, were very much native to India.

Five years later came Max Müller with his seminal 50-volume collection, _The Sacred Books of the East_ , the biggest undertaking of its kind in the history of oriental scholarship. In it, he argued that Sarasvatī dried up only in the post-Vedic period148 referring to Rigveda 7.95.2 that clearly speaks of the river as originating in the mountains and draining into the ocean.149 He further added references to post-Vedic texts like Baudhāyana sūtras and Vashistha Dharmasutra to establish the river as a mostly dried up body forming the western frontier of the Aryan country or Āryāvarta.150 In more modern terms, Müller noted Sarasvatī as a river that was once comparable to Sutlej and formed the “iron gate” between Punjab and the rest of India.151 Once again, no other body of water fits the description better than Ghaggar-Hakra. This too coming from a known AMT (then, AIT) champion.

British physician and Indologist Charles Frederick Oldham added his nod to Müller’s hypothesis in 1893 when he referred to the ancient fortress of Sarsutī close to a city that still bears a corrupted form of its name as Sirsa, and linked it to the elusive river. The fortress, incidentally, stands on the confluence of a rather unremarkable rivulet that bears its name and Ghaggar.152 In 10.75.5, Rigveda lists out five rivers as recipients of homage. In this list, Sarasvatī falls between Yamuna and Sutudri (Sutlej).153 This may or may not be representative of a real-world order of occurrence; to Oldham it was.154 The most obvious piece of evidence that Oldham advanced was the complete absence of the name Ghaggar in both Vedic and post-Vedic literature.155 This could only mean that the river they’re referring to as situated between Yamuna and Sutlej is none other than Ghaggar.

Three compelling pieces of evidence, all from scriptures, and we’re not in the twentieth century yet. Also, amusingly, all from advocates of non-autochthonous Aryans. The case for Ghaggar-Hakra seems sufficiently compelling.

The twentieth century opened with yet another salvo against a foreign Sarasvatī with the noted Sanskritist Arthur Anthony Macdonell reinforcing the candidacy of Ghaggar. His reinforcement came from Yajurveda’s description of the Aryan geography. The text describes Madhyadeśa, the Aryan country, as split between Kurukshetra and Panchala. The former was demarcated by the Ganga-Yamuna interfluve to its east and Satluj to its west. It’s western fringes were washed by minor rivers Dṛṣadvatī and Sarasvatī. Macdonell added to this the etymology and associated local legends of Sirsa to present his case for Ghaggar.156

In 1917, Sir Aurel Stein of Britain concluded an exhaustive survey of the entire Ghaggar-Hakra course through Haryana and onward into Rajasthan, and published his findings in _The Geographical Journal_. He too, like Oldham, found meaning in the Rigvedic order of river names where Sarasvatī is listed between Sutlej and Yamuna.157 As for his survey, Stein notes that the extended riverbed through Bikaner is too wide for the amount of seasonal water it carries today, hinting at a mighty river several times the size of today’s Ghaggar that once ran through it eventually dropping into a larger water body.158 In his model, Ghaggar was once, for a brief window, fed by a stray branch of the mighty Sutlej which accorded it enough water to earn reverence as a mighty civilizational river. This is when Rigveda noted it as Sarasvatī. As the window closed and Sutlej changed course, Sarasvatī reduced to a minor seasonal rivulet, eventually dying in the desert before reaching its destination.159 How it came to be renamed Ghaggar or Hakra in later times, remains unanswered.

So far, all theories and conjectures have come from AMT/AIT quarters. And they have all pointed to Ghaggar-Hakra. As the twentieth century drew to a close, voices from the other side of the aisle started weighing in too. The first major affirmation comes from David Frawley, whom we’ve already met before, and renowned computer scientist and OIT ideologue Subhash Kak. In 1995, the two coauthored a work with German Indologist and Yoga scholar Georg Feuerstein. They not only corroborated the river’s indigenous provenance but also hypothesized a model where the Harappans and Aryans are one and the same.

Attributed to Kak’s father (noted Kashmiri author Ram Nath Kak), this model locates the earliest Aryans in the bountiful watersheds of Indus and Sarasvatī as the Indus Valley Civilization, which is why they prefer the term Indus-Sarasvatī Civilization. Those days, Sarasvatī rose in the Himalayan foothills, cut through Haryana and Rajasthan, and poured into the sea at Kutch, having received much perennial water from Sutlej along the way. Then a geological catastrophe turned Sutlej away from Sarasvatī and toward Indus.160 This created two very different problems in two different parts of the civilization. The Indus swelled with all the additional water from Sutlej and flooded its basin. The Sarasvatī, on the other hand, shrank after the loss of input from Sutlej. Both became unfit for habitation. some migrated southward into the Deccan, others went east to eventually settle in the Gangetic plains in Uttar Pradesh.161 Do note that Talageri rejects this eastward migration into UP in favor of a UP homeland and a migration in the opposite direction.

To further highlight Sarasvatī’s primacy in the civilizational framework, Kak and Frawley insist that many more IVC sites lie buried under the Thar than along the Indus River.162 This way they successfully hyphenate the civilization with Sarasvatī which is already assumed to have dried up in Rajasthan. Their source for this information is a 1992 paper by archeologist Virendra Nath Misra which claims as many as two-thirds of all Harappan sites to be along the purported Sarasvatī River with only 4% along the Indus.163

Between anthropologist Gregory Possehl in 2002164 and geologist Khadg Singh Valdiya in 2017,165 as many as half a dozen major names from India and elsewhere endorsed the position with, at best, minor variations in details.

Despite this overwhelming support for Ghaggar’s bid, the question remains far from settled. For starters, why did Sarasvatī even vanish? Or, more appropriately, why only Sarasvatī? Every river listed in the scripture flows unabated, even flooding its catchment every now and then, to this day. Ganga, Yamuna, Sutlej, Beas, Indus, none show any sign of drying up any time soon. And yet, only the mightiest of them all did. Something doesn’t add up. If a river like Nile can run through a thousand miles of arid desert and yet manage to flood its banks every year for thousands of years, so should Sarasvatī. Sure, there was a cataclysmic event that disrupted the river’s geology, but why only this one and not any other. We will examine the proposed event more closely but first, back to the scripture.

In 7.95.2, Rigveda describes Sarasvatī as originating high in the mountains and emptying into a _samudra_.166 Now this is an interesting word. Today, we translate it as sea. But in original Sanskrit, the word is a compound of _sam-_ (together) and _udán_ (wave), i.e., a rendezvous of waves or waters. In other words, a confluence of any kind. Two or more rivers, a river and the sea, a river and a lake, everything fits the bill. Of note here is the nature of river’s origin. It’s evident that Rigveda’s Sarasvatī was a prominent, perennial channel, on the lines of Indus and Yamuna. Such a river cannot depend on seasonal showers for its payload. It must be glacial. Such a consistent source of high-volume meltwater could never come from ice-free foothills. So we have, as per this verse, a prominent river that rises very high in the mountains and empties into a large water body, either offshore or inland. While Ghaggar feeding off Sutlej makes sense, the scripture is rather unambiguous on the mountain part.

Ghaggar’s bid gets further complicated in a 2004 _Current Science_ paper that approaches the problem from a geochemical angle. But before we try to understand how, let’s get some basics in order. Geologists love carbon. But they love two other elements even more—strontium and neodymium. Both elements exist in nature as isotopes, strontium as Sr-86 thru Sr-88, and neodymium as Nd-143 and Nd-144. There are many more but these are the most abundant in nature. What makes them particularly attractive to scientists is the fact that every region has its own combination of these isotopes, the distribution is far from uniform. Which means two rock or sediment samples from two different regions would always feature different proportions of strontium and neodymium isotopes. Unless they were connected by, say, a river.

This is what the 2004 paper studied. Sediment samples from the purported Ghaggar paleochannel in the desert were compared with those collected from the Himalayas and Himalayan rivers.167 And it was found that Thar sediments and Ghaggar alluvium shared noticeable affinity in isotopic composition, proving that the paleochannel indeed belongs to Ghaggar.168 The two samples also seemed to share a non-glacial sub-Himalayan origin, which immediately distances Ghaggar from its glacial counterparts like Sutlej and Yamuna, thus militating against the hypothesis that Yamuna and Indus fed Ghaggar before some cataclysm diverted Sutlej toward Indus and Yamuna toward Ganga. To summarize all of it, the paper offers two alternatives. Either Ghaggar wasn’t Sarasvatī, or Sarasvatī wasn’t Himalayan.169 Because Ghaggar, going by isotopic composition of its sediments, was never a glacial river, nor was ever fed by one. Since Rigveda stipulates a mountainous origin, Ghaggar already becomes a weak candidate.

But there happens to be another that just seems to fit the bill and, as some would argue, even better. Unlike the ephemeral Sarsutī and Ghaggar, this one remains permanently active. It rises way up in the Kūh-e Bābā extension of the Hindu Kush and rushes through more than 700 miles of Perso-Afghan territory, gathering waters from five tributaries along the way, before finally forking into two branches, one emptying into Lake Hāmūn and the other vanishing into the Sistan marshlands. The lake is the largest freshwater body in the region which makes it a decent fit for Rigveda’s _samudra_.

Among the things that make a strong case for Helmand is its etymology which comes from the Zoroastrian scripture. Vendidad 1.13 and 1.14 name _Harahvaiti_ and _Haetumant_ as Ahura Mazda’s tenth and eleventh realms, respectively.170 While the former seems cognates with Sarasvatī, the latter seems cognates with Helmand. Linguistically, Afghanistan does seem to have something going for it. Ghaggar doesn’t even have such an etymological connect. But this still doesn’t help Helmand much, as the two names occur as two separate realms in Vendidad. Sarasvatī of Rigveda maps with Harahvaiti of Avesta, but Harahvaiti does not exactly map with Helmand, Haetumant does. So India’s “where is Sarasvatī?” becomes Afghanistan’s “where is Harahvaiti?”

A general consensus exists on Harahvaiti’s identification with the Arghandab River which also rises in the Hindu Kush and empties into Helmand.171 Some scholars, however, disagree and identify it as Dṛṣadvatī instead. The latter position isn’t without reason, though. Helmand receives waters from five tributaries—Arghandab, Argastan, Kajrud, Terin, and Tarnak. Coincidentally, the exact same number is attributed to Sarasvatī in Yajurveda.

This is no insignificant confusion because if left unresolved, it at least partially jeopardize Helmand’s bid. Helmand’s phonetic affinity to Haetumant and Sarasvatī’s to Harahvaiti cannot be dismissed. In which case, it’d seem that the identification switched between the main river and its tributary at some point in time. Sounds far-fetched, but can it happen? At least one example from Central Asia warrants attention in this context—the Oxus or Amu Darya River. The Hindu scripture refers to it as _Vakṣu_ , although the name only occurs in Puranic literature and not Vedic. The phonetic affinity is a dead giveaway. However, Oxus also has a tributary, Vakhsh, which seems to better correspond to Vakṣu. Here too, the name seems to have transferred from a main river to a tributary. If it could happen to Oxus, it could, at least theoretically, also happen to Helmand. Besides, at least on two occasions, 5.53.9172 and 10.75.6,173 Rigveda mentions _Kubhā_ , which is generally identified with the Kabul River. If the composers knew Kabul, it only follows that they likely also knew Helmand. As an aside, these verses also mention names like Sarayu and Gomati which somewhat muddies the water. But with the establishment of the name switch, Helmand’s bid stands marginally stronger than before.

At this point, it ought to be clarified that seven is the number of rivers in the scriptural continent, _Saptasindhu_ in Rigveda and _Hapta Hendu_ in Avesta, and five is the number of tributaries for the main river, Sarasvatī in Rigveda and Harahvaiti in Avesta. It also ought to be clarified that the Avestan references to Helmand and Arghandab is not without reason. The Helmand basin is widely understood to be one of the earliest centers of the Zoroastrian faith, Ancient Greeks noted Balkh as the faith’s original homeland by the name of _Zariaspa_.174 With that, let’s return to India.

In 1979, a paper published in _The Geographical Journal_ attempted to approach the problem from a different vantage point in hopes of finding a confident answer. It took a bird’s eyeview—literally. A series of satellite images of the Indian subcontinent were taken in 1972 by NASA’s Landsat program. The team behind this paper analyzed images pertaining to the western Indian subcontinent and noted clear indications of a paleochannel from Suratgarh in northern Rajasthan to Fort Abbas in Pakistan’s Bahawalnagar175. Field investigation revealed that wells along this channel held water better than those further away and that vegetation along the channel was visibly richer than in adjoining areas.176 This brought the spotlight back to Ghaggar-Hakra.

But the paper doesn’t just stop with findings, it also offers possible reasons for the river’s demise. Techtonic movements alter landscapes all the time, especially in regions as dynamic as the Himalayas and the Rann. So the authors do not rule out such geological upheavals as the cause of the river’s demise. An example they offer for illustration is of Allah Bund, one of the most dramatic topographical changes in recent times. This was the result of a violent M8 that struck Gujarat in 1819. Such was the force that it raised a large tract of land about twenty feet high,177 effectively damming the local Puran River, the dam eventually getting named _Allah Bund_ or God’s dam.

That’s a spectacular amount of sheer force gone into effecting a modest twenty-foot elevation. Now Indus Delta, on average, runs 75 feet deep.178 If Sarasvatī were even remotely comparable in dimensions and volume, damming it off the Indian Ocean would warrant a force far bigger than the earthquake of 1819. Larger the force, the less frequent its occurrence. An impact of this magnitude could only have last occurred in the Quaternary period, says the 1979 paper.179 That’d be more than two million years. Surely Rigveda wasn’t composed that long ago. The paper, however, also points out that a relatively smaller hit even way up in the Himalayas could have far-reaching impacts on the river’s volume and that damming on the scale of Allah Bund may not be all that necessary. But even something of that reduced magnitude isn’t as common as we think. One paper argues against an event of even that scale in the last 10,000 years.180 This still predates Rigveda.

To conclude, yes, there’s a strong likelihood that Ghaggar-Hakra was once a far more prominent body of water than it is now. Satellite imagery proves this. But was it the Sarasvatī of Rigveda? Isotopes say not likely. Tectonic history says not likely. Etymology says not likely. And Rigveda swings wildly between not likely and very likely.

It’s also quite likely that Sarasvatī referred to different rivers in different periods owing to the name’s sacred connotations. Since rivers cannot be transported, a migrating community would simply do the next best thing—apply its name to the most prominent river in their new habitat. So, it’s possible that either Harahvaiti or Haetumant was the original Sarasvatī, and when the Aryans settled in Haryana, they applied the name to the only major river they could find in the region. Even today, so many locally local water bodies are named Ganga by Hindu communities transplanted to regions with no access to the original, the Ganga Talao of Mauritius being one of several examples.

## Paleoastronomy

This is one of the less-trodden paths in the study of Aryan origins. But sufficient scholarship exists, nevertheless, to make it worthy of serious consideration. Mankind has observed stars and drawn interpretations since the very dawn of civilization. Rigveda has a large number of verses that speak of celestial objects and their relative arrangement at various times of the year. Since everything in the universe is in constant motion, how the sky looks today isn’t how it did, say, a thousand years ago. It’s also possible, using sophisticated calculations and computer software, to extrapolate each object’s position at any arbitrary point in the past or future. Thus, an investigation into what the Vedic sky looked like should offer a reasonable idea of when the text was composed. We have already dated the IVC down to its individual phases, using radiocarbon and other technologies. Therefore, if the Rigveda is proven to have been composed before or during the IVC window, it’d make a decent case for Aryan indigeneity. Therefore, the astronomical investigation is in fact more about dating the Vedas, than about directly locating its composers.

Before we dive in, it’s necessary to familiarize ourselves with some essential concepts and caveats. The first caveat is that for the purposes of this discussion, the line between astronomy and astrology stands blurred. Our model, for instance, is geocentric because that’s what observations show. So, “the Sun moves” actually means “the Sun appears to move.” Similarly, “that constellation is on the equator” actually means “that constellation is aligned with the equator.” Also, our model imagines the space as a two-dimensional dome with all celestial bodies stuck to its inner surface. Which means a statement like “the Sun is in Virgo” actually means “the Sun appears to be in front of Virgo.”

Two important terms we’ll be using a lot when locating celestial objects are declination and right ascension. Declination is the object’s angular distance from the celestial equator, 0° being aligned with the equator and 90° and -90°, with North and South Poles respectively. Just like latitudes. Right ascension is the distance of the body eastward from the vernal equinox measured in hours and minutes. Think celestial longitude. As for vernal equinox, it’s the point in space where the Sun crosses the celestial equator from south to north. This point occurs around March 20 every year and marks the beginning of spring in the Northern Hemisphere, hence also spring equinox. When we say the vernal equinox is in a particular constellation, it means the Sun is spatially aligned with that constellation on the day of vernal equinox.

The last thing we’re left with is precession. We know that the Earth spins around an axis with a 23.5° tilt. But this axis, while still maintaining the angle, isn’t stationary either. Just like a spinning top, it wobbles. And the wobble causes it to trace a circle in the clockwise, i.e. westward direction, once in roughly 26,000 years, i.e. approximately 50.3" a year or 1° every 72 years. This movement is called precession and is responsible for changes in the constellation vernal equinox falls in. Which makes it the single most important piece in paleoastronomy.

Speaking of constellations, or asterisms, there are two kinds in the Vedic system— _raśi_ and _nakṣatra_. A constellation that appears at the exact points of sunset or sunrise right after sunset or before sunrise is said to be a solar constellation or zodiac, raśi in the Hindu system. A constellation that’s similarly visible as visited by the Moon is called a lunar constellation or mansion, nakṣatra in the Hindu system.

And with that, we should be well-equipped to follow the astronomical arguments put forth by the experts in the field. The first time anyone considered astronomy as a tool for dating the Vedas was in 1895 when mathematician and astronomer Sankar Balkrishna Dikshit published a short paper in _The Indian Antiquary_.181 His paper was about a verse in a later Vedic text called Śatapatha Brahmana. He’d later expound his proposition more comprehensively in a 1969 book on the subject.182

The Second _Kāṇḍa_ or book of Śatapatha Brahmana (there are fourteen in all) opens with a discussion on the appropriate nakṣatra for a householder’s first sacerdotal fires. The first three verses recommend Pleiades or _Kṛttikā_. What this means is that the fires should be consecrated when the Moon is in this asterism, i.e. appears in front thereof. Each verse offers a unique reason—per 2.1.2.1,183 it’s because Agni is Kṛttikā’s presiding deity (every nakṣatra has one); per 2.1.2.2 because it has the most stars; and per 2.1.2.3 because Kṛttikā never deviates from the east. This last one is of particular significance here.

Kṛttikā is the most prominent asterism in the night sky and, therefore, one of the earliest known to, and exploited for navigation by, mankind. It has seven prominent stars, although some cultures count only six. In Vedic mythology, these are seven ladies married to the seven _ṛṣis_ (sages) of the _Saptarṣi_ constellation (the Big Dipper). Now since the text claims that the Seven Sisters (stars of Kṛttikā) never deviate from the East, Dikshit reasons, they must sit right on the celestial equator, i.e. their declination must be 0°.184 Another way to state the same is that their azimuth as observed from the Northern Hemisphere is 90°; this value measures the angular distance between the celestial North Pole and the object in question. As an aside, _deviate_ here means deviate from the east-west line.

Every star system has a main star that represents it in calculations since the entire set cannot be at the same spot all at once. This representative, typically but not always the brightest, is called the group’s _yogatārā_ or junction star. Thus, to say Kṛttikā is on the equator is to say that its yogatārā, likely Alcyone or η Tauri, is aligned with the celestial equator. This isn’t the case today; the star is 24 degrees off, thanks to the Earth’s precession. But going back in time at 50" a year, Dikshit arrives at a zero-degree declination around 3000 BC,185 pushing Śatapatha Brahmana and consequently Rigveda further back than consensus by over 1,800 years. This would make the first Veda older than even the Early Harappa Period. A compelling case for OIT emerges here.

But what if the declination was still close enough to zero around 500 BC, the date ascribed to Śatapatha Brahmana by mainstream scholarship? While zero would mean true east, a value larger but by a reasonably small margin would still mean east enough. This is what Witzel thinks in his 1999 paper on the subject. In this regard, one must note that the verse in question does not qualify “east” as true or exact:

     _etā ha vai prācyai diśo na cyavante.
    sarvāṇi ha vā anyāni nakṣatrāṇi prācyai diśaścyavante tatprācyāmevāsyaitaddiśyāhitau bhavatastasmātkṛttikāsvādadhīta._
    “And again, they do not move away from the eastern quarter, whilst the other asterisms do move from the eastern quarter. Thus his (two fires) are established in the eastern quarter: for this reason he may set up his fires under the Kṛttikās.” (trans. Eggeling)

The expression _prācyai diśo_ is better translated as “from the eastern quarter,” which admits anything that’s in the general eastern direction even if somewhat northeast or southeast. The “true east” interpretation is surprisingly recent, i.e. less than 700 years, says Witzel.186 Support for this position comes from the fact that Vedic texts do not have names for intermediate directions like northeast, southwest, etc. Even in Śatapatha Brahmana, the word _antaradeśa_ (intermediate region) is used for spaces between adjacent cardinal points. It’d then follow that each cardinal direction swept 45 degrees on either side of the line.187 Hence, when a star is said to “not leave the eastern quarter,” an accommodation of up to forty-five degrees is implicit.

Witzel further notes that while Kṛttikā’s azimuth at vernal equinox was 90[°](https://www.degreesymbol.net/) in 2900 BC, it was still well above 77[°](https://www.degreesymbol.net/) in 500 BC. Which means a deviation of less than 13[°](https://www.degreesymbol.net/).188 Could this be “east enough” to the unaided eyes? Admitting the Vedic notion of cardinal “quarters,” it’d certainly seem so. Consequently, 3000 BC seems no more reasonable than 500 BC for the text, at least on astronomical grounds.

At any rate, the text in question knows iron, Rigveda doesn’t. For example, 13.2.2.16, prescribes iron knives for lesser slaughters, as against gold for horses.189 Rigveda refers to metals in general, mostly copper and bronze, as _ayas_. It’s only with Chandogya Upanishad that _Kṛṣṇāyas_ makes its debut as a special kind of metal, i.e. iron. And Iron Age on the subcontinent is generally dated to 1200 BC, so Śatapatha Brahmana couldn’t be dated earlier than that anyway. Either way, the balance, once again, swings in favor of AMT.

Returning to astronomy, a Harappan artifact has often been cited in an attempt to identify Vedic astronomy with the Indus Civilization. Found in Mohenjo-daro, M-1186 bears what could be the oldest representation of the Seven Sisters of the Pleiades anywhere on the subcontinent,190 clearly indicating that the Harappans not only recognized it but rather placed a great deal of importance on it. While the idea seems tempting at first crack, we must understand that this is among the most readily noticeable clusters in the night sky and almost every major civilization has worked it into its mythology independently since as early as the first human settlement. The Harappans knowing the Pleiades doesn’t mean they were Aryans any more than the Aztecs knowing them means they were Japanese. The former knew the cluster as _Tiānquiztli_ and the latter as _Subaru_ , we don’t even know what the Harappans called it.

Having said that, it is entirely plausible that the myth of the sisters being wet nurses to Rudra’s son Kārtikeya emerged among the Harappans and then entered Aryan folklore who already had another myth where the seven sisters of the Pleiades were wives to the seven sages of the Big Dipper. The fact that the seven figures on the seal are all female only makes the possibility stronger. Myths do accrete like that when cultures collide.

In sum, 3000 BC, give or take, is when Kṛttikā rose in the east; true east, that is, zero declination. This was noticed and recorded as a useful navigational legend. Committed to memory, the information passed down the generations as traditional wisdom. Over time, the asterism deviated a bit from true east, but not enough to remodel the tradition. By the first millennium BC, as the hymns began to be composed, knowledge of Kṛttikā’s locus entered the corpus. The memory from 2,500 years ago prevailed. This is what Witzel proposes,191 although consensus remains elusive as autochthonists disagree vehemently on grounds that ŚB 2.1.2.3 is in the present tense.192

We can still attempt to break the stalemate if we step outside the subcontinent for a minute. More than a hundred years ago, archeologists digging up Ancient Mesopotamian sites stumbled upon a tablet that would prove to be the most comprehensive treatise on Babylonian astronomy to date. They named it MUL.APIN. In 2019, Assyriologists Hermann Hunger and John Steele published a transcription (not the first) along with detailed commentary. Turns out, the Babylonians had a mighty solid grasp on stars and constellations and were doing sophisticated astronomy more than 3,000 years ago. And the Pleiades enjoyed a special prominence in their system, just as it does in almost every other including ours. So prominent, they called it _MUL.MUL_ ;193 literally, just “star star.” In many places, the inscription also refers to it as the _Old Man and the Stars_. Of particular significance to us here is line 2.i.70,194 “The Old Man and the Stars stand where the East wind rises.” Interestingly, the expression East wind here refers not to true east but a whole quarter between northeast and southeast.195 Also interestingly, this tablet has been dated to 1000 BC. While still older than the purported date for Śatapatha Brahmana, it’s clear that directions were pretty much approximate back in the day and east meant not just east but the whole sweep between northeast and southeast. Not only the Pleiades in the east but the Ursa Major or Saptarṣi—MAR.GID.DA or the _Wagon_ to the Babylonians—in the north is also attested by the tablet, in line with the Brahmana.196 The transcription agrees with an earlier interpretation by American historian David Pingree,197 so it shouldn’t be entirely unreasonable to take it as sound until proven otherwise.

At this point, we have three competing views on the Vedic positioning of Kṛttikā:

  1. A memory carried over from a long lost time elsewhere, ultimately committed to text in India (Witzel),

  2. A novelty adopted from indigenous Harappans and ultimately committed to text by immigrating Aryans (Parpola), and

  3. Knowledge acquired natively through observation and committed to text _in situ_ by indigenous Aryan-Harappans (Dikshit).




It all depends on how we choose to read the Brahmana’s positioning of Kṛttikā. To add to the conversation, some of its stars have names indicative of rain— _Abhrayantī_ (bearer of clouds), _Meghayantī_ (bearer of rain clouds), _Varṣayantī_ (bearer of rain).198 This etymology will soon prove consequential. For now, let’s talk about the Sun’s north-south motion, involving two turning points over the course of twelve months. These turning points are called solstices—winter solstice when it’s south to north, and summer solstice when north to south. The midpoints between the two are equinoxes, vernal or spring going from winter to summer and autumnal or fall from summer to winter. In Vedic times, the two turning points were of immense significance. They were days of major feasts— _Viṣuvant_ in summer and _Mahāvrata_ in winter.

The sacerdotal significance of the two points is laid out in Kauṣītaki Brahmana where 19.3 places the winter solstice feast or Mahāvrata in the new moon of _Māgha_ and the summer solstice feast or Viṣuvant six months thenceforth while recommending the day after the _amāvāsyā_ or new moon of Māgha for the consecration of the sacrificial year or _Gavāmayana_ (lit. the course of the cow).199 But immediately after, the same passage prescribes, for the same consecration, the day after the new moon of _Caitra_ which coincides with the vernal equinox, instead.200

Interpreting this disparity in a 1985 paper for _The Indian Antiquary_ (the same edition that carried Dikshit’s aforementioned), German Indologist George Thibaut suggested that while the original practice did involve a solstitial start, it shifted three months out of phase to an equinoctial start by the time of the Brahmana’s composition.201 Thibaut also interpreted with evidence the “new moon of Māgha” as the new moon before the full moon in Maghā, in consonance with Jyotiṣa Vedāṅga itself, a direct corollary of which places the winter solstice in _Dhaniṣṭhā_ (Delphini), a situation that does not correspond to Dikshit’s 3000 BC or even close. In fact, Jyotiṣa Vedāṅga itself has already been dated to within the 12th century BC.202 Thus, we can note that in the Brahmana period, two years existed at once—a civil year that started in winter, and a liturgical year that started in spring.

And now we can return to Kṛttikā and its rain connection. This was indicated by Parpola in a 1994 work on Indus scripts. Since both Vedāṅga and Brahmana are six months out of step with the “original” calendar, it follows that Mahāvrata too would move six months ahead of the winter solstice to the autumnal equinox, coinciding with the end of India’s wet season. Incidentally, that’s also when the full moon happens to be in Kṛttikā, something the ancients must have noted as a useful marker for agricultural purposes. Parpola sees this as a strong rain connection for the asterism made stronger by the fact that at least three of its members have rain-related names as we just noted earlier.203

Further building upon Parpola’s proposition, Witzel adds that since it’s only the Punjab-Haryana-Delhi belt and not Sindh that experiences a wet season ending around the autumnal equinox, a Harappan provenance for the Brahmana makes little sense204. Thus, for all intents and purposes, the Pleiades argument fails to confidently support an Indus origin for the Vedas, despite those Harappan seals bearing the Seven Sisters.

This still fails to close the debate, for in 1991, autochthonist K. D. Abhyankar pushed _Purva Phālguṇī_ as evidence for a “pre-migration” Rigveda. He posited that the moon was full in this nakṣatra on the winter solstice day in the Brahmana period, an arrangement that doesn’t exist today. If true, this would push the scripture back to 3000 BC, or beyond. A handy concept here is that of ecliptic longitude or λ. This value represents a celestial body’s angular distance from the vernal equinox along the ecliptic. The value progresses from 0° at vernal equinox through 90° at summer solstice, 180° at autumnal equinox, and 270° at winter solstice; in other words, a sweep of roughly one degree a day to complete the entire circle in a year. If this sounds like right ascension expressed as degrees instead of hours, it’s because it is. As of 1950, the Sun’s λ was calculated to be 340° during the Purva Phālguṇī _pūrṇimā_ or full moon while the nakṣatra itself was at 160°.205 For the two events to align, the Sun would have to be at 270° at the time of the said full moon, a gap of 70°. Extrapolating with a 72° annual precession the alignment’s epoch comes to 5,040 years ago or a little before 3000 BC.

In a 1998 paper, Abhyankar also points to the bonfire ritual on the eve of Holi, a spring festival, as evidence of winter. He even invokes analogous celebrations from other cultures, such as Europe’s pre-Christian tradition of the yule log as additional evidence. Communal fires make perfect sense as protection against the winter solstice chill.206 But all this, of course, is predicated upon there being, in the first place, scriptural evidence of such an alignment. In other words, do texts from the period indicate a Purva Phālguṇī full moon on the winter solstice, i.e. the start of the civil year? Turns out, they do. ŚB 6.2.2.18, for instance, names Phālguṇī pūrṇimā the “mouth of the year” and the night after, the first of the year.207 Tāṇḍya Brahmana too says the same in 5.9.8.208 Perhaps there are other texts too that agree, but the point has been made, so we move on.

The idea was first floated long before Abhyankar by none other than Bal Gangadhar Tilak who authored not one but two books dating the Vedas. It’s in an 1893 work that he pointed to the aforementioned Brahmanas and suggested that the year started with Phālguṇī pūrṇimā.209

So far so good, but we’re yet to establish that it also coincided with the winter solstice. Given that the winter solstice started the civil year, it’s tempting to conclude that anything else that did, such as Phālguṇī pūrṇimā, must also fall on the same date. The Brahmanas do not have to state the obvious. But what if it isn’t a single calendrical cycle we’re talking about? We have already seen two different calendars, three months out of phase. What if there’s a third?

Any investigation into the matter warrants a good handle on the concept of seasons or _ṛtus_ in the Brahmana context. Today, the Hindu framework recognizes six. In its formative Brahmana period, it only did three, namely wet, cold, and warm. Their Sanskrit names are not important for this discussion. These seasons divided the year into three four-month sections called _cāturmāsyas_. ŚB 1.6.3.36 is one of the earliest verses in the text to mention the word. It defines cāturmāsya as special votive offerings made to gods at the beginning of each season, three in a year.210 How the months distribute, however, remains a subject of contention among scholars. Indologist Herman Jacobi, a supporter of Tilak’s model, proposed the following starting points211:

  * **Wet season** : Summer solstice (_c_. June 20)

  * **Cold season** : One month from autumnal equinox (_c_. October 21)

  * **Warm season** : Two months from winter solstice (_c_. February 22)




In contrast, Thibaut proposes a slightly earlier start212:

  * **Wet season** : Early June

  * **Cold season** : Early October

  * **Warm season** : Early February




His model is predicated more on practical observations than strict arithmetics. Monsoon showers, for instance, do not wait until the later part of June in much of North India. Both Thibaut and Jacobi agree that the wet season is the primary marker, for the rains are far easier to peg as temporal signposts than temperature change which is a more gradual, less dramatic phenomenon in comparison.213 Thus, in both their models, the annual cycle of seasons opens closer to summer than to winter.

The three oblations that inaugurate the seasons are _Vaiśvadeva_ , _Varuṇapraghāsa_ , and _Sākamedha_. Only the first is of our concern here. Eggeling notes in his introduction to ŚB 2.5.1 that Vaiśvadeva was scheduled on the full moon of Phālguṇa. He also indicates that the seasons are listed in the order of spring, rainy, and autumn.214 If this order means anything, Phālguṇī pūrṇimā automatically becomes the starting point of the seasonal cycle, i.e. year. At any rate, at least the first part of this note—Vaiśvadeva’s association with the Phālguṇī full moon—is unambiguously borne out in 2.6.3.13.215 Thibaut notes this as a most organic reason for Phālguṇī pūrṇimā being called the “mouth of the year” in Brahmana texts. It started the year’s first cāturmāsya or four-month window.216

As for winter solstice, Kauṣītaki Brahmana already clarifies that its original placement was in the new moon before Māgha’s full moon. And we know Phālguṇa comes right after Māgha, separating the two full moons by a month. Add to it the half month between the new moon in question and Māgha’s full moon, and we’re left with a whole month and a half between the solstice and the pūrṇimā in question.217 In short, the Phālguṇī pūrṇimā of the Brahmana period fails to sustain the 3000 BC proposition.

Speaking of 3000 BC, there is another Taurean object that makes its case as demonstrated by Abhyankar in his aforementioned 1998 work. _Rohiṇī_ , a Taurean nakṣatra with the red giant Aldebaran or α Tauri as its yogatārā, appears to chase after Kṛttikā across the night sky. It commands significance in pretty much every astrological system across the world. Abhyankar’s argument is that the vernal equinox was in this group around 3000 BC.218 This is borne out by the same computation that we used for Phālguṇī above. In 1950, the nakṣatra was at a 69° on the ecliptic.219 And ŚB 11.1.1.7 places it in conjunction with the new moon or amāvāsyā of _Vaiśākha_.220 Now Vaiśākha comes three months after Māgha, which obviously means its amāvāsyā also comes three months after that of Māgha. We have already established that at one time, the latter coincided with the winter solstice, a situation where the Sun’s λ is 270°. As the value changes by one degree a day, the shift comes to 30° a month or 90° from Māgha new moon to Vaiśākha new moon. That makes it 270**°** \+ 90**°** or 360°, i.e. 0°. Which means Vaiśākha amāvāsyā during the time of Śatapatha Brahmana should be at 0° or vernal equinox and not 69°. Accounting for a precession of 1° every 72 years, we get 4,968 years or 3000 BC as proposed by Abhyankar.

He also locates Rohiṇī on the famous Paśupati seal from Mohenjo-daro221 in an attempt to establish continuity between the Harappans and the Aryans, but that is more of a subjective conjecture than anything scientific. For instance, he sees the elephant as a metaphor for Ganesha when nothing suggests that the Harappans saw it as such. He also sees an elephant’s trunk in _Vṛścika_ or Scorpio’s torso although, again, there’s nothing to suggest that the Harappans also imagined the pattern as such.

But even ignoring the seal, the calculations stand. A Rohiṇī equinox still sits at 3000 BC, making the Brahmanas 5,000 years old if not more. Any argument against OIT ought to account for this calendrical anomaly.

But wait a minute, haven’t we also established the 3000 BC vernal in the vicinity of Kṛttikā before (v.s.)? Subhash Kak, too, pegs its zero declination at 2950 BC in a 2003 paper,222 although going by its current λ, its zero-longitude only occurs around 2300 BC. The same equinox cannot occupy two different nakṣatra at once. Obviously, something is off. What if Kṛttikā and Rohiṇī aren’t two separate groups in the first place? Of course, that’s absurd, but asterism identification is more of an observational tradition than a hard universal standard, especially when considered across thousands of years. An undated paper by one Avtar Krishen Kaul attempts to make a case for this absurdity.223 Let’s examine.

Kṛttikā’s yogatārā η Tauri sits on the ecliptic longitude of 57° today, give or take.224 In 3000 BC, it was at a very different 350°, almost ten degrees to the vernal equinox. The star also sits some seven degrees from the edge of Taurus which means the Sun enters Taurus about seven days before it conjuncts with η Tauri. Now Pleiades spans only 110 arcminutes or 1.8 degrees225 and, with the exception of η Tauri, has no star with a magnitude of 3.0 or below. The lower the magnitude, the brighter the star. Naturally then, according to Kaul, it makes sense for the cluster identified as Kṛttikā in Vedic times to have included other prominent clusters as well. We’ve already seen ŚB describe Kṛttikā as the most starry asterism.226

As an aside, the vernal equinox is also referred to as the first point or cusp of Aries, i.e. _Meṣa_. Hindu astrology calls it _Meṣa Saṅkrānti_ because that’s the rāśi the Sun is said to enter during the equinox. This, however, is no longer the case thanks to precession and the event now happens in Pisces or _Mīna_ instead. The shift is calculated to have happened as early as 57 BC.227 Thus, the vernal equinox being called the first point of Aries is more of a tradition than a reflection of current reality. Now back to Kṛttikā.

All astrological references in both Vedic and post-Vedic texts name Kṛttikā as the “first nakṣatra,” a strong indication of it being coincidental with the vernal equinox or Meṣa Saṅkrānti. Therefore, if the earliest date of reckoning is taken as 3000 BC and the latest at around 500 BC, the time of Vishnu Purana, we get 2,500 years of Meṣa Saṅkrānti falling in Kṛttikā.228 The precession during that period comes to about 34.7°. Given that Taurus spans more than 37 degrees and Pleiades a mere 1.8, the Sun would have long left the Pleiades but still remained within Taurus the whole time.229

And yet, no Veda, no Brahmana explicitly mentions Rohiṇī in conjunction with Meṣa Saṅkrānti.230 Sure, ŚB 11.1.1.7 places in it the Vaiśākha pūrṇimā which does indicate the equinox, but it doesn’t mention the latter (v.s.). Add to it the consideration that none of the groups in the neighborhood has stars brighter than those of Rohiṇī, with the exception of Alcyone which is considered part of Kṛttikā. Putting all this together, the implication that the name Kṛttikā probably included neighboring asterisms of Rohiṇī and Bharaṇī and not just Pleiades becomes less contestable.231

At this point, it’s difficult to confidently pick between Kṛttikā and Rohiṇī as the asterism of the 3000 BC vernal equinox, for both have compelling computational arguments in their favor. Which is why a position that obviates this choice becomes a more tenable one. Vedic astronomy is a highly sophisticated, extremely complex domain developed over several millennia, which puts it beyond the realm of a single study, however exhaustive. So much so that one meticulous investigation led Tilak to the conclusion that Aryans originated in the Arctic and Golwalkar to another than the North Pole was once in India. Bizarre as the conclusions might be, neither of the two can be doubted in his scholarship and noetic integrity.

What we can nevertheless infer for now is that more arguments favor a non-Harappan, “post-migration” Rigveda with an observational tradition going back to at least 3000 BC than they do otherwise. More because even though ŚB 11.1.1.7 seems hard to reinterpret in Kṛttikā’s favor and Kaul’s hypothesis involves a rather controversial presumption, arguments like Kṛttikā’s east not being true east, Brahmana Holi not being solstitial, Kṛttikā’s association with the wet season, Vaiśvadeva’s chronological primacy, winter solstice’s Dhaniṣṭhā placement, and Jyotiṣa Vedāṅga’s late second-millennium dating, stand.

We’re still left with several other topics of contention, most notably the dating of Mahashivratri and the placement of _ekāṣṭaka_ , but they all come down to the computation of nakṣatras, to wit: of Kṛttikā and Rohiṇī. Thus, everything depends on our choice between these two and whether we take the latter as more than just Pleiades.

## Current Scholarly Consensus

After the last ice thawed some 11,000 years ago, give or take, much of mankind had its first-ever rendezvous with sustained fair weather. And fair weather is good for farming. So man invented agriculture and at least in two pockets in what we call Near East, the hunter-gatherer became farmer and prepared the ground for history’s first civilization. These pockets were Anatolia and Iran. As the food supply grew, so did the population and its need for room. By about 7000 BC, some Anatolian farmers entered Europe and some Iranian farmers entered the Indian subcontinent. Practically simultaneously. This is how agriculture came to Europe. And this is how agriculture came to India. But this second bit may or may not be entirely accurate as many have argued that farming emerged in India independently and that the Iranians who came in 9,000 years ago were more likely hunter-gatherers than agriculturists.

Be that as it may, the Iranian genes were now here. And they were here to stay. Started with small farming communities like Mehrgarh, then coalesced into the full-blown urban behemoth called the Indus Valley Civilization over the following millennia. The IVC gene pool was likely a thorough mix of Iranians and indigenes. Likely, because to date, we haven’t been able to get our hands on any Indus gene from the period. What we do have though, is reasonably well-preserved genetic remains from the fringes of the Civilization where the Harappans would have lived in contact with their non-Harappan neighbors.

In paleogenetics, we name genetic materials for easier reference. The one we just spoke of, we called the Indus Periphery Cline or IPC. Similarly, the main components that made up the indigenous, non-Iranian side of this pool are called Andamanese Hunter-Gatherer (AHG) and West Siberian Hunter-Gatherer (WSHG). How and when a Siberian component entered our subcontinent is outside the jurisdiction of this discussion. So far, we’re still in the pre-2000 BC zone. Events of our interest would happen _after_ 2000 BC.

But we’ll rewind to 3000 BC for this post-2000 BC story. The first leg of our story began in the Near East. Now it’s time for the second leg and it begins in the Eurasian Steppes, someplace north of the Caspian and Black Seas, between the Dniester and Volga Rivers. The people who lived here were neither farmers nor hunter-gatherers. They were pastoralists. They owned livestock, domesticated horses, and buried their dead in pits covered with mounds of earth called kurgans. We call them Yamnaya and their way of life, the Pit Grave or Ochre Grave culture.

Just as two sets of Near East agriculturists migrated westward into Europe and eastward into the Indian subcontinent back in 7000 BC, two sets of Yamnaya pastoralists migrated westward into Europe and westward through Kazakhstan around 3300 BC, give or take (all dates here are approximate with a good few centuries of wiggle room). by 3000 BC, they’d hit a wall called the Altai Mountains and turned southward. This new trajectory took them along the Inner Asia Mountain Corridor and brought them into the northernmost reaches of the Indus periphery zone in the Swat Valley. This was around 2000 BC.

Here, Yamnaya genes admixed with IPC along with some more genes from the mid-to-late Central Steppe populace and formed the Steppe Cline. From there on, in the centuries that followed, this Steppe Cline continued to mix with IPC, acquiring a more Indian character. Meanwhile, IPC in other areas also continued to mix with more indigenous gene pools, notably the Ancestral Ancient South Indian or AASI. The IPC–Steppe admixture produced the Ancestral North Indian or ANI clade whereas the IPC–AASI admixture produced Ancestral South Indian or ASI.

This was only one of several branches. Another major branch went toward Iran instead and entered the subcontinent, not through Khyber Pass but through a less treacherous Bolan Pass into Baluchistan, a region that was greener back then than it is today. This is the branch that brought domesticated horses and chariots to India.

Roughly between 1000 BC and 100 AD, ASI and ANI mixed in varying degrees to form the ethnic fabric of India as seen today. Thus, all Indian groups today, barring a tiny handful such as the Andamanese Onge people, bear a mix of ANI and ASI ancestry. Broadly speaking, the proportion of ANI is higher in the north than in the south.

This is where the consensus seems to stand as of today. Among academic circles anyway. But outside the ivory towers of high academics, things continue to be bitterly contested. The Steppe Cline, by the way, is what scholars associate with the Aryan or Indo-Aryan identity. Of course, Hindu nationalists reject the idea in light of both ethnoreligious sentiments as well as new 21st-century research. As of today, though, as we’ve seen, a growing mountain of evidence stands behind a non-subcontinent Aryan homeland on every count there is.

But while OIT enjoys little evidentiary support, it does enjoy overwhelming ideological patronage. Where does this patronage come from? There’s of course a political side to it, but let’s examine the scholarly side. We have already looked into the scholarship in earlier sections. Now let’s look at the scholars. The key intellectual influencers and their motivations to stand their ground in the face of irrefutable data, who they are, and what incentivizes them.

## The Seven Sages of OIT

While OIT itself goes back almost a century, we shall confine ourselves to the seven most influential names in contemporary discourse. Interestingly, almost every single one of these is either an autodidact or qualified in an area completely unrelated to the subject at hand. While this does not in and of itself negate their scholarship, it’s important to read them in juxtaposition to the names they’re attempting to controvert with that scholarship. We take a cursory glance at both camps to establish their credentials, purely in the context of the Aryan debate.

The first name, in no order whatsoever, is that of Michel Danino. A visiting professor of social sciences and humanities at IIT Gandhinagar as of this writing, Danino seems well-placed to discuss a slice of Indian prehistory; history, after all, is part of humanities. Interestingly, though, his academics do not appear compatible with his domain. He has a degree from École supérieure d'électricité, Supélec for short, one of France’s premier institutions of technical studies until its merger with École Centrale Paris in 2015. As the school’s name suggests, Danino’s degree is in electronics and electrical engineering, a domain that enjoys little overlap with his purported scholarship. His theories on indigenous Aryans have widely been panned as based on an appeal to Hindu nationalism and selective appropriation of Sri Aurobindo’s speculations on the subject. Danino, after all, did spend time in Pondicherry in his youth which is when he pivoted to Indology. In 2015, he was appointed to the Indian Council of Historical Research. Two years later came a Padma Shri. Of note it the fact that both happened when the government belonged to a party that’s consistently positioned itself as a champion of saffron nationalism and Aryan nativism. Danino’s intellectual objectivity, thus, becomes particularly suspect.

Next up is Shrikant Talageri who has been extremely prolific in his indigenous Aryan evangelism. A Chitrapur Saraswat Brahmin from Mumbai, Talageri is arguably the most aggressive of the lot and has been writing for Hindutva outfits like _Swarajyamag_ and _OpIndia_ for years now. Besides frequently appearing on independent shows and podcasts with an openly Hindutva pitch. Problem is, his degree, albeit from a prominent institution like Sydenham, is in commerce. Everything he brings to the table in the field of Aryan origins is a product of non-academic investigation. Talageri’s area of expertise is comparative linguistics, but there’s not one paper to his name on the subject in any peer-reviewed publication. The mention of his caste in this profile, by the way, is also by design. Talageri himself makes caste central to his position on the Aryan question, openly calling his challengers casteist because the foreign origin theory, in his own words, “linked them and their illustrious ancestors with the colonial rulers and gave them a sense of superiority distinguishing them from other lesser Indians.”232

Navaratna Rajaram is the oldest of the lot and the only one who’s unfortunately no longer among us. Rajaram was a highly erudite academic who taught at various American universities for several decades before returning to India and passing away in 2019. He had a PhD from the illustrious Indiana University. In Mathematics. Even before his American stint, his career was in applied engineering with little to do with history, archeology, linguistics, or epigraphy. A bigger problem with Rajaram is his intellectual dishonesty which became publicly known after he published an “enhanced” computer image of a Harappan seal showing a horse that was later proven to be fake. We have already discussed this incident and the seal in question before so won’t dwell on it here. So indefensible was the act that even allies like David Frawley distanced themselves. Even discounting this incident as a stray lapse of judgment, Rajaram’s complete lack of domain credentials remains problematic.

David Frawley is an American Indologist with some truly exotic degrees to his name. For starters, there’s this Doctor of Oriental Medicine that he obtained via distance learning from one International Institute of Chinese Medicine in Santa Fe. The school, by the way, no longer exists as financial irregularities forced it to shut down in 2003. As of this writing, Frawley is the sole instructor at his school for Vedic studies in the same city. As for his knowledge of the Vedas—after all, he teaches the subject—he calls himself an autodidact. Which is impressive, no doubt, for self-learning isn’t in and of itself a bad thing. In 2015, Frawley too landed a Padma award for his contributions to Indic thought. Needless to say, this award demands to be viewed in the context of the ideology in power at the time and his own hostility against what both he and Hindu nationalists see as “Western agenda.” Just as the elevation of Marxist historians through the 60s and the 70s ought to be viewed in the context of Nehru’s own socialist ideals.

Subhash Kak attended both NIT Srinagar and IIT Delhi and has to his name an illustrious career that includes names like Bell Labs, TIFR, Louisiana State University, and Oklahoma State University, among others. As the names suggest, his academics are confined entirely to STEM with zero spillover into any area of humanities, much less history. Computer Science dominates both his academic as well as professional credentials. In the field of history and Aryan studies, we can pretty much call him an “autodidact.” Following in the examples of Frawley and Danino, Kak also landed a Padma Shri in 2019, again, by a government whose ideology Kak’s scholarship aligns with in no uncertain terms.

Abhijit Chavda is perhaps the most mysterious of the lot. The gentleman comes from a mechanical engineering background and has degrees from the Coimbatore Institute of Technology and one Laxmi Chand Institute of Technology. While no college seems to exist by that name, it could be a misspelling of Lakhmi Chand, a name that does exist. At any rate, there is no information on Dr. Chavda’s doctorate although he happily goes by that title. Nor is there any information on his credentials as a theoretical physicist, a profession every bio of his lists, not that it would help place him in the domain fit to discuss Aryan homeland. Interestingly, he’s the hardest to find anything on—no Wikipedia entry, no university page, no publicly acclaimed profile, although there does exist an IMDb page, something few academics and scholars can boast of. The page, of course, offers nothing in the way of a work or academic profile. The only public portal where one learns of his qualifications is a website called Celebrity Novel233. Which, in turn, references his LinkedIn as source. Dr. Chavda remains an articulate mystery. Again, an autodidact, a theme that seems to be common across all OIT enthusiasts.

And finally, we come to Koenraad Elst. With a PhD in Asian Studies from the Catholic University of Leuven or Katholieke Universiteit Leuven, Elst seems to be the only name on our list, and perhaps the entire crop of OIT advocates, who brings relevant academic credentials to the table. Elst threw himself into the subcontinent’s ideological debate fairly early in his life and even spent a good number of years studying Indology at the Benaras Hindu University. While his academics seem perfectly aligned with his scholarship, it’s not the entirety of his background. His affinity with extreme Hindu nationalism is no accident of scholarship. There exists a very fertile common ground between the two, a common ground that promises precious academic teeth to Hindutva hardliners and immense sociopolitical approval to Elst. That common ground is Islamophobia. Elst has had an interesting past with extreme right-wing activism of his own in his home country and is more than open about his Islamophobic leanings. Of course, his work includes titles like “The Problem with Secularism” and “Forever Ayodhya.”

So, these are the Seven Sages of OIT, the source of much of Hindutva scholarship. These, by no means, are the only ones though. We also have the likes of Raj Vedam, P. N. Oak, Nilesh Oak, and Sanjeev Sanyal, among many others.

The third pattern one can’t help but notice is that every name, both Indian and otherwise, has a background in unrelated fields. Sanyal is an economist with degrees in commerce, philosophy, economics, and politics. P. N. Oak was into law with a degree in the field and literally ran an outfit called _Institute for Rewriting Indian History_. Nilesh Oak has degrees in chemical engineering and business administration. While being from unrelated domains and being rewarded by a tendentious regime do not negate scholarship, they certainly add an amount of motivation to the proponent’s conclusions. One ought to cultivate a measure of skepticism that non-scholarly prejudices _could_ color their scholarly output, not excluding mine. Let’s now take a look at who they’re up against.

Among the most reviled is Michael Witzel. What does he bring to the table? A degree in Indology from an institute of both vintage and eminence, for starters. Then there’s the PhD in Indology from one of Germany’s premier institutions dating back to the eighteenth century. On the research front, Witzel has an enviable body of work in the field of Indian prehistory and Vedic philology that spans over four decades and over two hundred peer-reviewed papers. He has taught Vedic Sanskrit at places like Harvard and translated at least five whole mandalas of Rigveda as of this writing. Without exaggeration, when it comes to Vedic linguistics and Sanskritic philology, Witzel remains one of the final words. By the way, a common point of attack on him from the OIT quarters is his Jewish identity. Whether that has anything to do with his scholarship and pedagogical positions remains to be seen.

David Reich is next. Also a subject of antisemitic attacks like Witzel, Reich is to paleogenomics what Witzel is to linguistics. His first degree was in physics but the next was in zoology. Today, he teaches genetics at Harvard Medical and has, over the years, added an impressive corpus of research to his name with more than a hundred papers in various peer-reviewed journals. Reich played a pivotal role in landmark endeavors such as the sequencing of the first complete human genome and the discovery of interbreeding among various human species. In the Indian context, he’s been working on the subcontinent’s paleogenomics for more than a decade now. In short, Reich has a rock-solid résumé packed with relevant academics and peer-reviewed research behind his conclusions. The peer-reviewed part is crucial, not just because it adds credibility from researchers at large but, as we’ve already noted before (_q.v._), he has worked even with AMT-skeptics like Thangaraj and Singh who went on to agree with Reich’s findings.

There’s a reason mainstream is mainstream. For a piece of scholarship to enter mainstream discourse, it must meet certain quality stipulations. Quality that can only come from years upon years of original research and academics that substantiate it.

A question one might and must ask at this point is, are degrees infallible? Of course, not. One could be highly erudite, highly articulate, highly accomplished, and still produce bad scholarship. This could be due either to research impediments (as was the case with Tilak), or to ideological motivations (the case with Irfan Habib). In fact, ideological motivations have a long history of polluting scholarship. If anything, accomplished historiographers are _more_ susceptible to such corruptions simply because being at the heart of the academia, they’re the ones who stand to lose and gain the most from such corruptions. In some cases, this may be downright malicious too. The academia is a whole other stratosphere of political slugfest.

A related question is, does alignment with power necessarily establish corruption? Again, of course, not. When a work is rewarded by the government of the day, the worst it can mean is that the conclusions of the work align with the ideology of that government. It does not have to also mean that the conclusions are wrong. Those are two separate things. Having said that, yes, being rewarded by the ideologues in power can serve as _one_ legitimate reason for closer scrutiny.

The problem with OIT scholarship is not that they are autodidacts or that they are favored by a very indicative ideology. The problem also isn’t that they are demonstrably wrong. The problem with them is that they _are_ motivated by a very specific outcome. That’s not research. Research is about studying and accepting whatever conclusions that study throws up. They already have an outcome in mind. And all they do is fabricate, cherry-pick, or misinterpret their studies and those of others to somehow, anyhow prove _that_ chosen outcome right. That’s less than intellectually honest. They are not incompetent. They are remarkably skilled in what they do. Just with the wrong motivations.

In short, to a layperson with no time to personally go down the rabbit hole and examine every piece of evidence there is, it’s a toss-up between scholars who have institutionally studied and contributed to Indology for decades and ideologues who have studied, say, mechanical engineering and worked with, say, Lockheed Martin. One is always free to trust a chemical engineer over a qualified Sanskrit philologist in a debate on Vedic linguistics. Or a qualified paleogeneticist over a “self-taught” history communicator (which, again, includes me) in a debate on Aryan haplogroups. But scholarship generally favors credentials.
