# The Politics of Sufism

*How Popular Shrines Scripted the Rise and Fall of Three Islamic Empires*

Sufism as an esoteric creed of extreme devotional rigor is not quite the novelty to Islam that it’s often mistaken as. The practice has a history that goes back all the way to the days of the Umayyads whose indulgence alienated a large body of puritan Muslims who sought a less hedonistic path to salvation. Never mind the resentment over the locus of authority no longer being in _Ahl al-Bayt_ or Muhammad’s household. This primordial Sufism sought to bypass the Caliph in its quest for salvation. Much like Protestantism would seek to bypass the Papacy in the Middle Ages. But let’s start from the beginning to understand the chronology.

## The Political Backdrop

Muhammad’s death in 632 AD set off a chain reaction that would take Islam through three caliphates, three civil wars or _fitnas_ , and a large number of big-ticket assassinations. At first his companions established Islam’s first theocratic polity, the Rashidun Caliphate. This only lasted three decades before running into problems and folding. At this point, Islam experienced its first “theopolitical” schism. One faction followed Muhammad’s cousin and son-in-law Ali, and the other followed Muʿawiya, the governor of Syria and a Rashidun loyalist. Ali’s camp would later develop into Shiʿa and the mainstream into Sunni. But for now, the difference was less doctrinal and more political.

Muʿawiya continued the caliphal mainstream as a new Umayyad Caliphate whereas Ali’s successors established a separate Imamate of its own. In the latter sequence Ali was followed by his eldest, Hasan. But Hasan got killed shortly thereafter and was followed by younger brother Husayn. This one lasted a decade. After Husayn, the Alid line also underwent a minor split in leadership. While the mainstream Shiʿa polity continued with his son al-Sajjad, a splinter group separated under Ali’s third son Muhammad ibn al-Hanafiyya. This was the Kaysani Imamate.

The Umayyads, meanwhile, had embarked upon a territorial blitzkrieg and conquered vast swathes from Spain in the west to Sindh in the east and Transoxiana in the north to Egypt in the south. This brought a large number of non-Arabs into the Islamic fold. A distinct class of non-Arab Muslims or _mawālis_ emerged. For the first time in Islam’s history, there were more non-Arabs than Arabs. But the Caliphate remained Arab and this was problematic. While the mawālis played a crucial role in the empire’s expansion, they were often excluded from positions of power and subjected to discriminatory policies. In most newly conquered non-Arab lands, Arabs and mawālis were barred from intermingling. Meanwhile, there was a simmering discontent over nepotism. A large section of Muslims was growing disenchanted with the caliphate as the leadership became a _de facto_ hereditary position. We will return to this later. Let’s catch up with the Alids first.

After al-Hanafiyya died in 700 AD, the Kaysani leadership went to his son Abu Hashim. Disenchanted by the growing politicization of Islam, Abu Hashim preached a more puritanical doctrine with a special focus on austerity and frugality. He is widely acknowledged as the world’s first “Sufi.” The name Hashim is an allusion to Banu Hashim, the tribe he belonged to, via his grandafther Ali. Banu Hashim was a subset of Muhammad’s Banu Quraysh and Kaysani was a subset of Banu Hashim. Even today, Jordan’s royal family claim descent from this bloodline, hence the appellation Hashemite.

Abu Hashim passed away in 716 AD but before doing so, he named a distant cousin Muhammad ibn Ali ibn Abdallah or Muhammad al-Imam as his successor. This may or may not have been a rumor but that’s immaterial, for the succession went as claimed anyway. Muhammad al-Imam became the new Kaysani Imam and the head of the Banu Hashim clan. Drawing upon his great-grandfather Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib, al-Imam and his descendants are also called Abbasids.

After al-Imam, the Kaysani leadership went to his eldest, al-Saffah. This was a particularly turbulent time in the already violent Muslim world, for the very next year it imploded into its Third Fitna or civil war between two Umayyad factions. Remember the discontent over the Caliphate’s nepotism among Arab Muslims? This was its culmination. The immediate trigger was Caliph al-Walid II’s ouster and execution by a rival aspirant. Such was the upheaval in Damascus that the capital had to be moved to Harran in Turkey. But the proverbial Rubicon had already been crossed. The conflict quickly escalated to attract even non-Umayyad adversaries, most notably the Alids, the Kharijites, and the Hashimiyya. It lasted three years and by the time the dust settled, the Umayyads were a dying shadow of their former glory. But had the dust really settled?

## The Age of the Abbasids

Far from Damascus and Harran, a different kind of unrest brew in Khorasan. Today split between Afghanistan and northeastern Iran among others, this region had a very different culture from the rest of the Arab world. While Arabs and mawālis were prohibited from intermingling elsewhere, they did so freely here. Without the apartheid, Arab settlers grew increasingly Persianized in their cultural and ethnic makeup here. This was also a region with strong Shiʿa influence thanks to its close proximity to Karbala, the site of a pivotal Alid massacre. Consequently, in these parts the Sunni-Shiʿa political divide was virtually nonexistent. Therefore, when the Umayyads lost their moral, military, and political capital in the west, the already resentful east saw opportunity.

This resentment culminated, almost immediately after the Fitna, into a new civil war that would ultimately end the Umayyads. This was led by none other than al-Saffah and his brother al-Mansur. The unrest lasted three years and is called the Abbasid Revolution. Al-Saffah rallied support on the plank of restoring Islam’s authority back in Muhammad’s household via Ali’s bloodline. The waning Umayyad power along with the loose caliphal control over these lands meant the Abbasids were destined for victory. Which finally arrived in 750 AD. The Umayyad run had folded less than a century since its first Caliph.

The Abbasid Caliphate was now in session. Its seat was in Kufa, Iraq. With the exception of a brief four-year window during the civil war, the Umayyad seat had always been in Damascus. With the Abbasids there was a clear shift, the power center had moved east and was for the first time in a non-Arab city. This was Sufism’s first affair with power. The world’s first Sufi was, to cut a long story short, the ideological progenitor of what would go on to become Islam’s most influential Caliphate. Although it must be noted that restoration of Islam to its original form and the Caliphate to Muhammad’s bloodline was all just a political ploy. Once in power, the Abbasids not only extinguished the very Imamate that seeded its authority, but also descended into the same hedonistic and nepotistic abyss that sank their Umayyad predecessors. And yet, they lasted longer than both Rashidun and Umayyad put together. How? And what role does political Sufism has to play in it?

## Emergence of Shrine Cults

With the Abbasids, came a visible shift in the ethnic constitution of the Islamic administration. While the Arabs dominated the cultural, political, and doctrinal spheres in both earlier Caliphates, it’s the non-Arab Persianate gene-pool that started gaining ground in the new regime. Kufa, the new capital, was in the thick of Persian country which meant an increasing amount of mawāli infiltration into the Caliphate’s cultural character. Within a couple of decades, Caliph al-Mansur founded a new capital not too far from Kufa and named it Baghdad. This move was accompanied by a general knowledge renaissance in the Islamic world that we now call the Golden Age of Islam.

A major driver for this renaissance was the influx of a whole new race into the Islamic mix—the Turks. Oftentimes these were either non-Muslims, such as Buddhists, Nestorians, Zoroastrians, Jews, etc., or second or third generation Muslims. They brought to the capital a vast body of scientific and philosophical work unknown to the Arab world of the time. The Caliph lent enthusiastic support to this knowledge transfer and a large number of Greek, Indian, and Persian works, many of them long considered lost even in their own lands, were translated into Arabic. With this influx, also came a new theological injection. A new kind of Sufism started making its way into Arab lands.

While this paints a rather pleasant picture of prosperity, consensus, and peace, discontent simmered in the background. Remember, al-Saffa’s promise of restoring the Caliphate to the Alids upon completion of the Umayyad overthrow? Of course, that never happened, and the new Caliphate just ended up being another hereditary line not different from that of Muʿawiya before. Under these circumstances, it became crucial for the Alids to build shrines of their sacred figures—mostly members of Muhammad’s and Ali’s households—for both veneration and commemoration. These shrines slowly grew into Shiʿa power centers in their own capacity and served to maintain a close-knit community around the veneration of Alid elites. This was the beginning of the Islamic shrine cult, and it spanned the entire duration of the Abbasid Golden Age, that is a hundred years from 750 AD thru 850 AD.

Although the popular notion defines the Golden Age as the period between the eighth and the thirteenth centuries, i.e. from the start of al-Mansur’s Caliphate thru the Mongol invasion of Baghdad, the window was, to be honest, far smaller. We will see why but first a little more on the shrine cult. The same conquest of Transoxiana that brought Turkic scholars to Baghdad also brought a new wave of Sufi theologians from places like Bukhara and Samarkand. As they crystallized their position in the Islamic world as doctrinal authorities, they started building up huge followings of their own. When these figures died, they too had commemorative shrines built by their followers. These entombed saints would become extremely consequential to the Abbasid power struggles in times to come. The initial reaction was to destroy these shrines in order to quell any possibility of an Alid uprising but over time these shrines became more popular among lay believers than the Calip himself. Destroying shrines would no longer prove wise. Now back to the end of the Golden Age.

In the truest sense of the name, the knowledge renaissance pretty much ended with the ninth century Anarchy at Samarra that followed Caliph al-Mutawakkil’s assassination by a Turkic guard. This didn’t happen in a vacuum. In 833 AD, al-Muʿtasim became the eighth Caliph of the Abbasid empire. His reign saw a curious shift in political and military traditions. Wary of Arabs who were increasingly sympathizing with the Alids and growing hostile to the Abbasid rule, al-Muʿtasim started a new policy of hiring Turkic mercenaries into his army and even personal security. This proved fatal as very soon, Turks outnumbered Arabs in the Abbasid power circles and grew ambitious. The period of strong Caliphs had, for all intents and purposes, ended. To further distance himself from untrustworthy Arabs, he even moved capital from Baghdad to Samarra. The situation continued to deteriorate before finally culminating in al-Mutawakkil’s assassination thirty years later.

The Anarchy at Samara lasted ten years and five Caliphs. Under al-Muʿtadid, the sixteenth Caliph, Samarra was finally abandoned, and the capital moved back to Baghdad. But the decline could never be arrested. The Arabs would never return to the position of influence and authority they once held. Barely half a century after the move to Baghdad, a new force came from the east and dealt yet another body blow to the Abbasid authority—the Buyids. This was a particularly humiliating defeat, since the Buyids were not only Turks but also Shiʿa. It was the first time a Shiʿa polity had defeated a Sunni caliphate. Although the doctrinal divide between the two sects didn’t exist yet, a clear political divide did. But the Buyids did not replace the Abbasids. Despite their takeover of Baghdad, they retained the Abbasid Caliph on the throne as a vassal. Why?

## Power of a Paper Caliph

The Buyids were a Turkic force and like all Turkic forces in the region, lacked a royal lineage. In cultures across the world, lineage has been a major source of legitimacy to kings. A Turkic warlord could never be the Caliph because he was neither Muhammad’s family, nor an Arab. And lording over an overwhelmingly Muslim population without this fundamental element was a tricky affair. So, they needed to manufacture this legitimacy via alternate means. They could not acquire a lineage, but they could acquire the Caliph’s blessings. In other words, the Caliph could legitimize the warlord’s authority through a set of rituals to inject him with a piece of caliphal sovereignty. The first such ritual was to add the warlord’s name alongside that of the Caliph in Friday sermons. The symbolism of this ritual was immense. This was then supported by a second ritual where the Caliph would wear a special robe, take it off, and offer it to the warlord who would then put it on and appear in public. This way he would become an extension of the Caliph’s physical body and a legitimate leader of his Muslim subjects. Therefore, even a powerless Caliph was an indispensable element of Turkic sovereignty. Just like the Pope in the Holy Roman Empire.

Meanwhile, being Shiʿa, the Buyids engaged heavily in the Alid shrine cult already thriving in the Muslim world. Sandwiched between two Shiʿa forces, the Sunni Abbasids could only watch helplessly as the enshrined Imam and the enthroned Amir became partners in politics rather than adversaries in influence. The same shrines that were being destroyed by the Abbasids before were now being actively patronized by the Buyids. The shrine cult too underwent a change of its own. While the most popular shrines among the laity so far were those that venerated prominent members of Muhammad’s family or Imams, it was now those that venerated Sufi saints and Persianate theologians. The spotlight had shifted from Arab elites to Turkic saints, probably a result of changing demographics and Turkic political dominance.

In the middle of the eleventh century, after a successful ninety-year run, the Buyids were replaced by yet another Turkic polity, the Seljuks.1 But whereas the Buyids were Shiʿa, the Seljuks were Sunni. The latter, in fact, positioned themselves as the Sunni crusaders who saved the Caliphate—and by extension, Islam—from the clutches of Shiʿa hegemony.2 This change almost immediately reflected in the Abbasid-Alid equation in the region. For starters, the Sunni-Shiʿa divide that was merely political thus far, now became doctrinal. The Shiʿa doctrine was no longer a fringe sect of the Sunni mainstream but an independent body of Islam in its own right. This split changed the regime’s relationship with the shrine cult. Veneration of Alid figures could no longer continue in light of this new development. At the same time, the patronage of shrines could not stop either, given the immense cultural and religious sway they still held among lay believers. So, what’s the solution?

## Shiʿa, but Not Shiʿa

The Seljuks and other Sunni groups who conquered territories with prominent Alid shrines encountered a difficult situation. These sites held significant political capital, requiring patronage to maintain public favor. The conquering rulers recognized this necessity. However, as Sunni, overt displays of veneration risked them slipping into the Shiʿa fold, a less than desirable outcome. The solution involved a nuanced approach, incorporating elements that defied Shiʿa taboos, exemplified best by the Ayyubids in Syria.

There, Saladin’s son patronized an Alid shrine but strategically incorporated elements that diverged from Shiʿa practices. Notably, inscriptions offering prayers for Muhammad’s Companions or _Sahabah_ were added to these shrines. Such prayers were nonexistent in Shiʿi traditions. In extreme cases, the Companions were even cursed as part of Shiʿi rituals.3 With these subtle inclusions, Sunni elites could acknowledge the popular Alid saint without aligning with a specific sectarian movement. This approach allowed for pragmatic accommodation of religious sentiment within the bounds of Sunni orthodoxy.

This model proved remarkably adaptable. Beyond the Ayyubid example in Syria, a similar approach emerged in the distant northeastern Iranian city of Mashhad. This pilgrimage center housed the shrine of the eighth Shiʿi Imam, Reza Shah. Notably, inscriptions extolling Muhammad’s Companions were incorporated during the reign of ʿAlaʾ al-Din Muhammad, a powerful Central Asian warlord ultimately defeated by Genghis Khan. As the Khwarazmshah or ruler of the Khwarazmian Empire he was a contemporary and a bitter adversary of the Abbasid Caliph al-Nasir. He undertook a large-scale reconstruction of the Mashhad shrine during a period of heightened political competition with the Caliph. On his part, al-Nasir too followed Saladin’s example and had several Alid mausoleums inscribed with invocations to the Companions.4

The previously discussed dynamic between Amirs and Caliphs played out similarly across Turkic rulers in the region. The Shah, craved recognition from the Caliph, who himself lacked political authority of any kind. But al-Nasir was keen on changing this equation and restore the Caliphate to its former glory.5 To that end, he withheld acknowledgement of the Shah’s sovereignty, despite the latter’s victories in Iran. The simmering tension that followed escalated when the Shah marched on Baghdad in response. Only the onset of winter and the looming Mongol threat under Genghis Khan deterred him from potentially ending the Abbasid Caliphate.

While this confrontation is well documented, less so is the Shah’s subsequent move that ritualistically declared al-Nasir dead and installed an Alid as a rival Caliph. The latter’s name was proclaimed during Friday prayers throughout the Khwarazmian country, briefly establishing a Shiʿi counter-caliphate. This intense political competition may have influenced the Khwarazmshah’s decision to renovate the Imam Reza shrine. Obviously, the appointed Alid “Caliph” lacked the legitimacy of the established Abbasid line. True authority in the region resided with Imam Reza, whose shrine had grown in importance since as early as the ninth century.

The Khwarazmshah’s patronage of Mashhad served to secure his own legitimacy by leveraging the enshrined saint’s influence, rather than seeking validation from the Abbasid Caliph. The inclusion of inscriptions praising the Companions on the Imam's shrine further underscored this strategy. It demonstrated respect for Ali’s lineage without fully committing to the Shiʿi ideology. In essence, the Khwarazmshah mirrored the Ayyubid strategy in Mashhad, appropriating Alid shrines for political gain without converting to Shiʿa Islam. This approach was prevalent in the early thirteenth century.

This new development necessitated a corresponding response from the Abbasids to ensure their continued relevance. Caliph al-Nasir astutely recognized this exigency and implemented a strategic adaptation. He embraced Alid symbolism and cultivated an association with the _Futuwwa_ , a Sufi fraternity that fostered a sense of community and loyalty among his subjects. While al-Nasir’s specific initiatives related to the Futuwwa brotherhood did not endure the eventual Mongol invasion, the fundamental significance of saintly shrines as centers of religious devotion and political legitimacy persisted.

## The Sultan of Khorasan

The Caliphate prestige encountered its second major setback in the winter of 1258 when Baghdad was irrecoverably sacked by the Mongols. If 945 AD was humiliating because they lost to a Shiʿa power, 1258 was humiliating because they lost to a pagan power. The man who led the sack was none other than Genghis Khan’s grandson, Hulagu. This episode marked a paradigm shift in the royalty’s relationship with the Caliph. As pagan, the new sovereign or _Ilkhan_ did not feel the need for caliphal approval that his Muslim predecessors did. This was a major shift. The Buyids had retained the Caliph as a figurehead because they drew legitimacy from him. The new Mongol regime didn’t need such a legitimacy as they already had a powerful Chinggisid lineage to boast of. As Sunnis, the Abbasids offered no value proposition in this new world order. Which is why the Caliphate moved to Cairo for survival. Even after the dynasty’s eventual conversion to Islam, the need for caliphal validation remained nonexistent. But why?

The Mongols conquered a territory where the Shiʿi cult was ascendant and saint shrines had already eclipsed the Caliph as a source of doctrinal and political authority. That’s why even after Hulegu’s great-grandson Ghazan converted to Islam (for political reasons, of course) and felt the need for Muslim validation, he understood that it could only come from dead saints in Khorasan rather than the living Caliph in Cairo. So, he instituted a novelty called _dar al-siyada_ or “house of Prophet’s kin” in a bid to attach Alid charisma to his Chinggisid lineage. The first dar al-siyada was a hospice built as an annex to the Reza Shah shrine to provide for Muhammad’s descendants or Sayyids. Over the years, several more were built throughout the Ilkhanate, each attached to a popular saint shrine. This measure firmly established the Ilkhan as the royal guardian of Sufi interests in the realm. It is, therefore, no coincidence that during the Timurid period, Imam Reza would be referred to as the “Sultan of Khorasan,” in recognition of his spiritual dominion over the region.

With this historical context in mind, we can explore two specific cases that illustrate the complex relationship between rulers and shrines in the late Timurid and early Safavid eras. The first case involves the destruction of the Naqshbandi poet Jami’s shrine in Herat by the Safavids, a dynasty known for establishing Shiʿism as the state religion. The second examines the pilgrimage undertaken by the Timurid ruler Humayun to the Alid shrines in Ardabil and Mashhad.

## The First Shiʿa Empire

The notion of shared sovereignty between the throne and the shrine in the wake of Mongol invasion created a fertile environment for the emergence of new Sufi orders especially in the eastern fringes of the Ilkhanate. One such order or _tariqa_ was established shortly after the invasion, by a Kurdish mystic from Ardabil named Safi-ad-Din. Shortly after his death, another order was founded by one Bahaʾal-Din Naqshband in Bukhara. With enthusiastic royal patronage many such orders emerged and flourished throughout the Islamic world. The king and the saint were in a mutually beneficial partnership and the most pronounced expression of this partnership was the pivoting of the Safavid Sufi order into empire-building in 1501 with Shah Ismail I. The Ilkhanate, by the way, had already ended by the time of Safi-ad-Din’s death.

This pivot had an interesting outcome. First of all, keeping in mind the political contingencies of the time, the Sufi order had to be infused with the crucial Alid charisma. To that end, Safi-ad-Din was retroactively given a Qurayshi bloodline tracing his genealogy to Ali. With that, all Safavid shrines were now automatically Alid shrines, making its dynasts a legitimate Islamic authority. As is well known, it’s under the Safavids that Iran became a Shiʿa theocracy for the first time. The Safavid ancestors were no longer mere Sufis now, they were Imams. By the time of Ismail’s successor Tahmasp, the empire had swallowed Mashhad too. With Ardabil as the first dynastic shrine on the other side of the Caspian, Mashhad now became the second.

The political nature of the State’s involvement in shrine affairs was particularly evident in its unprecedented assumption of direct administrative control over the Imam Reza shrine. Pre-Safavid rulers offered patronage through endowments, but the Safavids took to directly governing the shrine instead.

But this aggressive imposition of Shiʿism came with its own complications. Remember the tradition of inscribing prayers to Muhammad’s Companions, or Rashidun, on Alid shrines that was started by the post-Buyid Sunni rulers? This was done by the Sunni ruler to enlist Alid loyalties without personally converting to Shiʿa Islam. It had served well for centuries and even the Mongol invasion did not disturb the practice. But now, there was a problem. Shiʿa practitioners hated the Companions. It was historical, it was ritual. Some Shiʿa creeds even cursed the Rashidun as part of their religious practice. The openly Shiʿa Safavid Shah had little reason to continue his Sunni predecessors’ political correctness. There was now no need to maintain those inscriptions on the shrines. Nor was there any need to proscribe the ritualistic cursing of the Companions.

At the receiving end of this upheaval was the Naqshbandi order that had emerged in Bukhara shortly after Safavid order in Ardabil. In the fifteenth century there lived in Afghanistan a Naqshbandi poet named Nur ad-Din Jami. Upon his death, he was buried in Herat where a shrine to him was built under Sunni patronage. Just as Safi-ad-Din was given a lineage connecting him with Ali, Bahaʾal-Din Naqshband was given one that connected him to Abu Bakr, a Companion. Naturally, Jami’s shrine carried copious inscriptions venerating Abu Bakr. This became a problem when Herat came under Safavid rule.

The sole eyewitness account of this situation comes to us from a minor Herati poet named Zayn al-Din Mahmud Vasefi. Herat was then under a Sunni Uzbek ruler named Muhammad Shaybani. If this name rings a bell, that’s because he’s the one who gave Babur the chase all the way across the Hindu Kush and into India. In 1510, Ismail Shah snagged Herat from Muhammad Shaybani and added it to the Safavid domain. To formally announce the annexation, a victory proclamation or _fatahnama_ had to be read. To get a local to do it, a generous reward was announced. Someone from the town took it up and proceeded to read the proclamation. Unfortunately, he ended up losing his life instead.

He had not the slightest idea that the language of the proclamation involved cursing the Companions. As a Sunni, he was hesitant to go that far. As hesitation turned into refusal, Safavid soldiers proceeded to hack him to death in full public view. Still not done, the entire Herati population was then marched to the shrine of Jami under the threat of death and forced to watch as the structure was torched. The message was clear, convert to Shiʿism or die. Herat was no longer to have a single Sunni within its walls. Vasefi recounts many such anecdotes of Sunni persecution at the hands of their new Safavid overlords, one involving even himself.

On the surface, it’s easy to see why the Shah went so hard on the poet’s shrine. The shrine represented the Naqshbandi order, and the Naqshbandi order represented a Rashidun bloodline. To a Shiʿa fanatic, this was reason enough. But it went deeper. When the Safavids took Herat, it wasn’t exactly from Shaybani. Because the battle where Ismail faced off against and killed Shaybani took place more than 250 miles to the north in Merv, Turkmenistan. When the Safavids entered Herat, the city was already without a king. One could argue, here, that given Jami’s intimate ideological proximity to the Timurids and later, Uzbeks, destruction of his tomb symbolized the ideological subjugation of the eastern realms. To the Safavids, the saint essentially stood in for the city’s missing sovereign.

Shaybani had ruled Herat only for three years. Before him, the city had served as a Timurid capital. With the Safavid capture the Timurids had lost all hopes of ever recovering their lost capital. The destruction of Jami’s shrine was also a body blow to the Timurid prestige. Such a blow that Babur does not even mention the event in his biography. Nor does Humayun who actually visited the site of the torched shrine during his exile from India. The only account that tells us anything about Jami’s fate is that of Vasefi.

## Ajmer As India’s Mashhad

Shaybani Khan’s death came as a welcome relief to Babur who had endured an exhausting conflict with the Uzbek warlord for the better part of his adult life. The Shah even reunited him with his sister who had been earlier abducted and practically held hostage by Shaybani. Ultimately, however, Babur was destined to settle in India because even after reconquering his homeland with no Shaybani around, he failed to hold it. In India, he only got to rule for four years before passing away. Humayun succeeded him but even he could only manage ten years before being chased out by an Afghan chieftain from Bihar.

In exile, Humayun headed west, his first sojourn being Herat, the city his father had held before Shaybani. Here, he visited the burned-down shrine of Jami, among others, seeking the dead saints’ intercession. Of course, he makes no mention of Jami’s shrine in his autobiography for obvious reasons. Kings were not fond of recalling humiliating episodes in their chronicles those days. From here, Humayun wrote to Safavid Shah Tahmasp seeking permission to visit the shrines at Mashhad and Ardabil. Accounts say, he spent more than a month in Mashhad meditating and praying at the imperial shrine. He only left after the Shah sent him summons for an audience in the capital. While leaving, Humayun hung his bow at the gates as a votive offering to Shah Reza.

Humayun’s veneration of a Shiʿa saint despite his own Sunni allegiance might seem odd but, again, this too had a clear political objective. He badly needed to speak with the Shah and the only way to improve his chances of landing an audience was by displaying his submission to the imperial Safavid shrines. At the royal court, Humayun was made to wear a Safavid headgear or _Qizilbash_ to symbolize his submission to the Shah as his disciple in keeping with the Sufi tradition. Remember, the Safavids were also a Sufi order. But why did Humayun have to do all this? Because he needed two things from Iran—refuge and military support. The latter was still nowhere in sight, though.

It’s at this point that he decided to make a trip to Ardabil to pay homage to the Safavid patriarch, Shaikh Safi-ad-Din. Here, Humayun sought intercession from not only Safi-ad-Din but also Tahmasp’s father Ismail I. This worked, for when he returned to the capital, the Shah committed to Humayun a contingent of twelve thousand soldiers. The Shah could not possibly let his father’s legacy be tarnished as the saint who wouldn’t answer prayers. This was the power of dead saints. At its heart, this was not very different from Ismail desecrating Jami’s shrine due to the saint being seen as a symbolic sovereign of Herat. That’s how intertwined a Sufi’s life and legacy was with those of a king.

This merger of political and spiritual roles of Sufism meant all shrines were fair game as political targets during raids and wars. Even a shrine like that of Imam Reza did not escape this fate. The Uzbeks made several attempts on Khorasan, the most notable one being about half a century after Shaybani Khan’s death. During this period, the shrine at Mashhad became a prime target as a symbol of Safavid sovereignty. While Reza Shah himself enjoyed universal reverence, including that of Sunni Uzbeks, the shrine was still a Safavid icon and hence fair game for plunder. The invading army took away all the gold and jewels from the building, desecrated the tombs of Safavid kings, and slaughtered all caretakers. Only Imam Reza was spared. The idea was not to destroy and loot for the sake of destroying and looting, but to sever the Safavid lineage from its appropriated Alid charisma. Now back to India.

Humayun finally succeeded in reclaiming his lost throne in 1555 after fifteen years of exile but didn’t live long enough to enjoy the success. After him came Akbar who was keen on replicating the shrine model that seemed to work so well for the Safavids. He needed an Indian equivalent to Mashhad, a saint shrine that could serve as a spiritual wellspring of sovereignty to him like Imam Reza did to the Safavids. He picked Ajmer.

The Chishti Sufi order is among the oldest in the world, dating back to pre-Buyid times. But the Abbasid disintegration and the subsequent rise of Shiʿa forces, most notably the Safavids, forced many Sunni Sufis out of Khorasan. Some of them came to India, Muinuddin Chishti among them. Traditionally his arrival is placed at the end of the twelfth century or even early thirteenth. Ajmer is his final resting place. For a long time, his shrine was an insignificant mud affair with few visitors since the region had few Muslim inhabitants. But as the Chishti order grew in influence and more Muslims gentrified Ajmer, the shrine too grew in popularity. In time, even Hindus started visiting the grave for divine intervention.

As the shrine grew in influence, it began to attract political attention too. Both Hindu and Muslim kings scrambled to patronize the dead Sufi in an attempt to win over a devoted demography. One historical account speaks of Jodhpur’s Rajput ruler annexing Ajmer and turning the shrine into a Hindu temple with figures of Hindu deities installed inside on his orders. Later it was reclaimed for the Muslims by a Sultan from Gujarat. In other words, royalties of all kinds fought to appropriate the Sufi’s spiritual aura for political ends.

This scramble ended once Akbar took over. He named Muinuddin Chishti the patron saint of his dynasty and assumed the right to appoint the shrine’s caretakers. Exactly as Ismail I had done in Mashhad. But there was one big difference. While the Safavids coopted the shrines at Mashhad and Ardabil as imperial resting places, the Mughals did not. The shrine at Ajmer would remain exclusive to the Chishti saint. No king was buried there. Here, kings always preferred to be buried in grand mausoleums built exclusively for them. Later, Akbar would in fact start placing himself even above the Sufi in spiritual significance, which would have its own set of problems as new militant kind of Sufis would emerge to steer Islam back to its puritanical roots.

## Sufis in the Ottoman Realm

Six years after the Seljuks took Anatolia from the Byzantines after the Battle of Manzikert, a splinter group seceded from the Seljuk mainline to form the Sultanate of Rum. Rum because Anatolia those days was literally East Rome and its conquest by the Turks was seen as the conquest of Rome itself. The Mongol invasion in the thirteenth century, however, greatly weakened the Sultanate and placed it on the path of steady political decay. By the fourteenth century, it had disintegrated into various Anatolian principalities called _beyliks_. One such principality was that of Osman, an Oghuz Turk based in Bursa. A confusing power struggle that lasted half a century led to the emergence of Osman as the most successful beylik of all. He united all other principalities into a monolith under him that we now call the Ottoman Empire. Together with the Safavids and Indian Timurids or Mughals, they form the gunpowder trinity.

Although the Safavids had taken over as the most powerful force in the Middle East, there remained a caliphal vacuum in the region due to their Shiʿa persuasion. The Abbasids existed but only as token Caliphs based in a far-from-action Cairo. The Ottomans wanted to change that and restore the significance of the Caliphate. Especially after the conquest of Constantinople, they felt every claim to the title as the conquerors of Rome itself. That’s why just as the Safavids took Afghanistan, the Ottomans took Egypt in the early years of the sixteenth century. With this conquest, they extinguished the last remaining remnants of the Abbasid Caliphate by forcing the Caliph to ritually transfer his authority to the Sultan. Sultan Selim I, thus, became the first Ottoman Caliph.

Anatolia had been a fertile Sufi playground ever since the Islamic conquest of Transoxiana. The famous Bactrian Sufi Rumi had brought the Sogdian whirl to the region as early as the thirteenth century and even established the Mevlevi order (named after Mevlana, another name of Rumi) best known for its adaptation of Sogdian whirl as the “whirling dervish.” In fact, when the Ottomans took over Anatolia, they inherited two major shrines already in existence, one of Haji Bektash Veli and the other of Seyyid Battal Gazi. The latter was not even a Sufi but a legendary folk hero who had died fighting the Byzantines several centuries earlier. The Ottomans felt the same political compulsion to patronize Sufi traditions that their counterparts in Iran and India did. But there was a difference.

Unlike the shrines in Safavid and Mughal countries, the one in Turkey were built lavishly with no pretense of modesty. Here, saint shrines looked less like Sufi mausoleums and more like grand imperial palaces in Istanbul or Edirne. Also, unlike the other two, the Ottomans did not directly patronize these shrines. They just built them up and left them to be managed by their vassals. Only in situations of active rebellion would they interfere in shrine affairs as punishment to the revolting vassal. In one such case, a shrine was shut down to punish the vassal in control of the structure. Thus, saint shrines in the Ottoman realm became a most powerful political tool that helped the Sultan maintain his control over his tributaries.

There were, however, instances where the regime departed from this norm and took to actively patronizing a shrine. Such instances typically involved major conquests. So, when a new territory was annexed to the empire, the Sultan would build a new shrine to a popular figure in the region and establish him as a venerated saint. One such example is the shrine to Abu Ayyub al-Ansari that was built by Mehmed II after his conquest of Constantinople. Another example is the Salimiyya Takiyya in Damascus. Built as a shrine to the Andalusian mystic ibn Arabi by Selim I upon his conquest of Egypt and Syria in 1517, the structure is considered the first Ottoman building in Syria. This is when the Caliphate shifted from Abbasids to Ottomans. About two decades later, Suleiman the Magnificent effortlessly took Baghdad from the Safavids and rebuilt Mazār Ghous, the mausoleum of Abdul Qadir Gilani. Rebuilt because Shah Ismail I had destroyed it earlier as part of his anti-Sunni campaign. Gilani was founder of the Qadiriyya Sufi order which had a soft corner for the Companions.

It’s worth noting that not all Ottoman-supported shrines were of Sufi saints. Many, like Seyyid Gazi and Abu Ayyub were not Sufis but warriors. But the shrines accorded them the same Sufi-like aura of spiritual authority which then passed on to the Ottoman Caliph by association.

The political nature of the Ottoman shrine cult becomes most apparent in the case of Rumi. His shrine in Konya predates the Ottomans by a couple of decades. Unlike the shrines of Haji Bektash and Seyyid Gazi, this one did not belong to a vassal, so enjoyed direct patronage from the Ottomans who developed it as a political counterweight to the other two. In order to understand the need for this counterweight, we need to understand the institution of Janissaries.

Under Murad I, a system of elite infantry unit was instituted to support the many military campaigns of the nascent empire. This unit was made up of boys captured as spoils of war in newly conquered Christian territories. These boys were taken away from their Christian families, converted to Islam, and trained to be soldiers. As their initiation to Islam, the Bektashi Sufi order of Haji Bektash was chosen. There was a reason for this. The Bektashi order was a far more moderate ideology as compared to the puritanical Sunni mainstream. For new converts, it offered the path of least friction. Haji Bektash had expressly developed his doctrine to be inclusive to both Byzantine natives and Turkoman imports.6 Thus, he became the Janissaries’ patron saint, and his shrine became their Mecca.

By the sixteenth century, however, the Janissary influence had started growing beyond the Sultan’s control. The trend only grew stronger over the following centuries until they became a parallel authority barely subservient to the Sultan. Sometimes, they would even participate in palace intrigues and interfering in succession plans. It’s to arrest this rising Janissary clout that the Sultans started positioning Rumi as a doctrinal counterweight to Bektash. In other words, Rumi and Bektash ended up as the unofficial representatives of Ottoman sovereignty and Janissary autonomy respectively. The dead saints were, once again, active participants in real-world political intrigues.

## A Jihadi Reinvention

By the eighteenth century, the age of empires had practically come to an end. The first to go down were the Ottomans who entered a series of ruinous conflicts with a European confederacy called the Holy League in 1683. This situation, collectively known as the Great Turkish War or the War of the Holy League, lasted about sixteen years and cost the Ottomans almost all its possessions in Europe, including Hungary, Transylvania, and the Balkans. The 1699 Treaty of Karlowitz heralded not just the end of the conflict but also the Ottoman Caliphate’s first major defeat. From this point on, the empire would remain in a state of steady decline until the final nail in 1924.

Next up were the Mughals. Although they maintained a token presence until as late as 1857, they had already begun to lose relevance more than a century earlier. It started with the death of Aurangzeb in 1707, although some might argue a much earlier date attributing the decline to his catastrophic Deccan campaigns. Aurangzeb left behind a troubled empire, perpetually embroiled in wars of succession and fatal foreign invasions. Delhi was first sacked by Iran’s Nadir Shah within three decades of Aurangzeb and then raided multiple times by Afghan and Maratha forces, reducing the Mughal regime to a tiny enclave within Shahjahanabad.

Finally, it was the turn of the Safavids. This time, the adversary was none other than Russia’s Peter the Great who invaded Iran in 1722 to expand his influence in the Caspian Sea and Caucasus regions. The war lasted a little over a year and resulted in a humiliating defeat for the Safavids. The Treaty of Saint Petersburg symbolized not just the end of this war but also the end of Safavid authority. The empire would ultimately disintegrate into several smaller kingdoms and cease to exist in 1736.

With the decline of the three so-called “gunpowder empires,” the age of shrine cult also came to an end. Without imperial patronage, most shrines withered away into political oblivion and lost all doctrinal influence they once held. Increasingly, a new genre of Islamic thought began to emerge and actively militate against established Sufi eclecticism. Ideas that once enjoyed political patronage, now came to be seen as heretical and unislamic. This was also Sufi, but different. Thinkers like Shah Waliullah Dehlavi in India and ʿAbd al-Wahhab campaigned for the restoration of Islam to its Shariʿa core without concessions. Dehlavi was a Naqshbandi Sufi himself while Wahhab was a Naqshbandi-influenced Hanbali.
