# Oldest Language in India

*An Attempt to Settle the Long-Standing Debate on India’s Linguistic Heritage*

- date: 2023-11-28
- audience: only_paid
- url: https://www.schandillia.com/p/oldest-language-in-india
- slug: oldest-language-in-india

---

The debate over the Indian subcontinent’s oldest language is complex, confusing, and, to make matters worse, politically loaded, with no easy answer. This region boasts of an enormous linguistic diversity, second only to the tiny Polynesian nation of Papua New Guinea. The number of languages spoken here ranges from 400-odd to almost twice as many, depending on who we ask. With extraordinarily touchy speakers. So touchy, an entire nation broke free purely on linguistic grounds less than three generations ago. In India, this touchiness expresses itself in the perpetual high-octane debate over a “national language.” As of this writing, India has none.

A debate on national language is, expectedly, a debate on language primacy. And a debate on language primacy is, just as expectedly, a debate on linguistic vintage. The older the language the more its preeminence. Ultimately, it’s a clash of chauvinism with surprisingly few contenders. Of the several hundred languages spoken in India, only two have their horns locked in this clash—Sanskrit and Tamil. This article hopes to navigate this hotly, at times violently, contested terrain and return an answer backed by as much scholarship as possible.

## Definition

The prerequisite to even opening a debate on language antiquity is a logical definition of language itself. Unfortunately, there’s none. Hindi and Urdu are different languages but Bhojpuri is a mere dialect of Hindi. Modern English is a descendant of Old English and yet speakers of the two would barely, if at all, comprehend each other. Clearly, mutual intelligibility doesn’t always dictate terms which makes defining language a tricky affair. Oftentimes, the line between language and dialect is political rather than linguistic.

Just as we cannot tell the exact year _Homo erectus_ evolved into _Homo sapiens_ , we cannot tell the exact moment, say, Proto-Dravidian evolved into Tamil. And just as we are not the same species as our _erectus_ ancestors despite the ancestry, should we also say Modern Tamil is not the same language as Proto-Dravidian, despite the ancestry? Organic languages, after all, evolve just like us even if on a much smaller scale. Or should we treat them differently because the smaller scale makes it harder to locate the point where the ancestral language ends and its modern descendant starts? The smaller scale means even a slight subjectivity in placing that point would mean a significant swing in the language’s vintage. And subjectivity is integral to an exercise of this nature because that point is in many ways, quite arbitrary. Think ship of Theseus. English is only five hundred years old if we only consider Modern English, but a millennium older if we consider Old English. But the average English speaker of today will struggle as much with _Die Zeit_ as with _Beowulf_. One definition makes it a very young language, the other makes it older than French. As we can see, definition is what the whole verdict hinges on. Since any alternative is as arbitrary as all others, for the purpose of this conversation we’ll define a language continuous only to the extent the average modern speaker could comprehend, even if with some difficulty. The moment it becomes incomprehensible, we note it as a departure from an earlier language. Thus, for our purposes, Chaucer’s English is English, but Beowulf’s English is not. 

Another consideration, and a big one, is the current status of the language. It’s both futile and unfair to pit a language spoken today against one that went extinct centuries ago. Even the debate is over the oldest “continuously spoken” language. But things again get tricky when we consider languages that remain in the purgatory of the liturgy. These are neither alive nor dead. They’re used, but only in liturgical circles. An example is Latin. Or Avestan. Nobody, outside of some linguists and enthusiasts, speaks or even comprehends them. Only the high priests of Christianity understand Latin and only a tiny subset of the global Christian base that attends the Mass in Latin speaks it. Closer home, one such example is Sanskrit. The language is far from dead. But it’s no lingua franca either. Comparing it with Tamil might seem unfair because it’s dying, and also fair because it’s dying, not dead. We’ll go with the latter.

In sum, in our context, a language **a)** starts at a point beyond which its form becomes unintelligible to the average current speaker, and **b)** is alive even if not lingua franca, so long it’s in currency anywhere including the liturgy. These will be the guiding caveats for this investigation. We shall start with Tamil, then examine Sanskrit, and then will see if there are other possible candidates that aren’t even in the race. Tamil ought to be the starting point because it’s the language that stakes the most aggressive claim. An endeavor of this kind must eschew all sociopolitical prejudices and blindly follow peer-reviewed scholarship to whatever truth it leads to. Which is what we’ll do.

## Tamil

Tamil is a member of the Dravidian family, ultimately tracing its roots back to a reconstructed Proto-Dravidian root more than 6,000 years ago. It’s often hypothesized that this Proto tongue was amongst the languages spoken in the Indus Valley Civilization, another member being a mysterious “language X.” This already accords the language a formidable heritage of continuous usage, something hard for most languages to match not just in India but the world.

But this circles back to our question, when exactly did Tamil start? Proto-Dravidian is certainly not Tamil any more than Proto-Germanic is English. While the answer to that question is not an easy one, a good starting point is to look into the oldest dateable text in the language. The older a language, the older its earliest compositions; in the absence of other parameters, this should be a reasonable measure of antiquity. And the oldest work in Tamil is _Tolkāppiyam_ , a collection of three books on Tamil grammar, morphology, and prosody. At least that’s what remains of it. The name is a portmanteau of _tol_ , meaning ancient, and _kāppiyam_ , meaning book. Tolkāppiyam isn’t exactly an easy work to date, but attempts have been made over the years.

Before we go deeper into Tolkāppiyam, however, let’s investigate something more material. That’d be some kind of an inscription that has been dated with reasonable confidence. But do keep a tab on Tolkāppiyam, we’ll return to it shortly.

Tamil is written in a form of Brahmi, the mother script of almost all Indian writing. For long, Tamil Brahmi has been understood to be a post-Ashokan development, i.e. appearing after 300 BC, give or take. In the spring of 2013, however, a paper in _Prāgdhārā_ reported a specimen that pushed it to almost 500 BC. Some 75 miles from Coimbatore, lies the archeological dig of Porunthal. This is where the specimen was found—a four-legged jar, among other things, containing about five pounds of paddy inside. It was a part of grave goods in a burial chamber. On it was an inscription in Tamil Brahmi. At first, there was little consensus on the jar’s date but later mass spectrometric studies on the paddy sample returned a date of 490 BC.1 This was a revolutionary finding, for no deciphered script has ever been found to predate this sample. The Harappan script, once deciphered, would push our horizon back by another couple of millennia. But for now, Tamil Brahmi seems to take the cake with a 2,500-year vintage.

For the longest time, it has been understood that Brahmi came to the south with the spread of Jainism and Buddhism, which didn’t happen before Ashoka. This makes the findings at Porunthal particularly groundbreaking. Now, Tamil Brahmi stands significantly older than Ashoka or anything else in the north. Porunthal got an additional boost when pots with similar inscriptions were unearthed in Adichanallur, another archeological site sixty miles north of Kanyakumari.

A burial urn discovered at this site was curiously found to have inscriptions on the inside, rather than outside. Only seven letters were tentatively identified and assigned a date of 500 BC, even older than Porunthal. The conclusion, however, comes from preliminary thermo-luminescence dating, a less reliable method as compared to radiocarbon.2 Again, these are not the oldest inscriptions in India, these are just the ones we’ve successfully deciphered. Undeciphered inscriptions go back several millennia but are not relevant to this conversation.

And now we can return to Tolkāppiyam. The reason Tolkāppiyam matters despite Tamil writing already being the oldest in the subcontinent is that language isn’t born when it’s written down, it’s born when it’s first spoken. People have spoken, sung, and composed in their mother tongue for centuries and millennia before they learned or even thought to write it down. Could Tolkāppiyam push Tamil further back in time?

A useful orthographical concept to understand here is that of _puḷḷi_. Ever noticed how words and names almost always seem to end in an _/a/_ sound in Tamil and other South Indian languages? Ram becomes Rama, Ravan becomes Ravana, and so on. There’s a reason for that. Every South Indian consonant, Tamil in our context, comes preloaded with an _/a/_ sound to make it complete. Think of it as a default vowel; a consonant without a vowel is meaningless and cannot stand. So either give it one, or it assumes an _/a/_. But what if you really wished to say Ram and not Rama? How do you indicate that the final consonant should be read without the inherent _/a/_ sound? In other words, how do you strip a consonant of its preloaded vowel? That’s where puḷḷi comes in. It’s a diacritical dot above the consonant meant to be stripped of its vowel.

This wasn’t always the case.

For a long time, Tamil consonants came with no inherent vowel, an anomaly in the region. The _/a/_ sound had to be indicated with special vowel marks just like any other. But this, as we just noted, was an anomaly in the region. So with time, Tamil came under the influence of its neighbors and started treating its consonants as non-basic, i.e. with implied _/a/_. This is when puḷḷi was born, to imply that the consonant was basic with no vowel sound after it. Iravatham Mahadevan, a now-deceased Indian epigraphist and the final word on Tamil Brahmi, dated this switch from basic consonant to puḷḷi consonant to the Arikamedu period, i.e. in the first century AD.3

But what does this have to do with Tolkāppiyam? Only that the work mentions puḷḷi as a marker of basic consonants. The first of Tolkāppiyam’s three _atikārams_ or books discusses phonology and phonetics. The first _iyal_ or chapter of this book discusses orthography. It’s here that puḷḷi makes its debut, in 1.1.15, as an unambiguous marker of consonants.4 Japanese Indologist Takanobu Takahashi interprets this as Tolkāppiyam being no older than the first century AD,5 a most natural conclusion in light of Mahadevan’s dating of the dot. Mahadevan himself posited this theory seeing the dating of Arikamedu artifacts as infallible. The reason we keep bringing up Arikamedu is its prominence as a Roman trading outpost about two millennia ago. Potsherds found here bear peculiar graffiti in both Tamil and Prakrit vernacular. These are by far the first Tamil Brahmi specimen to feature the dot, notes Mahadevan, an undeniable departure from the inscriptions found in caves from earlier times.6 Thus, any composition explicitly discussing such a diacritic must belong to that period at best.

But before we settle on the first century AD, let’s see if the date can still be pushed any further. Renowned 20th-century Tamil philologist Saravanapperumal Vaiyapuri Pillai notes Tolkāppiyam’s treatment of menstruation in 3.4.11317 as a direct inspiration from Manusmriti’s treatment of the same in 3.46 and 47,8 even agreeing on the exact number of days, i.e. twelve. Since Manu lived in the second century AD, it follows that so did Tolkāppiyam’s authors.9 But things don’t stop even here.

Kamil Zvelebil, a Czech philologist with a special focus on Dravidian linguistic heritage, points to a Panini connection in the two verses 2.5.643 and 64410 which prescribe four parts of speech, in line with Panini’s prescription in Sanskrit.11 Since Panini has been dated to roughly 4th century BC, some assign that date to Tolkāppiyam as well. While tempting, one must understand that similarities with Panini do not necessarily establish contemporaneity with Panini. It might as well mean that grammatical rules set by Panini influenced the ones in Tolkāppiyam.

Since Panini isn’t much help here, we should turn to later scribes to further narrow down our placement.Pillai suggests Patanjali, a grammarian who lived at least 200 years after Panini and composed a seminal commentary on _Aṣṭādhyāyī_ called _Mahābhāṣya_. In 2.9.902,12 Tolkāppiyam posits four kinds of compound words or _samāsas_. This, Pillai confirms, is a direct loan from Mahābhāṣya,13 which comfortably places the work in the second century BC. And these are just a couple of examples. Pillai identifies a whole array of such loans from both Patanjali and Panini.

A seemingly strong argument often made in favor of Tolkāppiyam’s pre-Paninian antiquity involves the word _Aintiram_. The word is cognates with Sanskrit _Aindra_ , one of the eleven schools of Sanskrit grammar, and perhaps the most preeminent. The school is said to come from Indra himself and predates Panini. Naturally, mastery of this grammar would have been a subject of great prestige among grammarians and scholars. Tolkāppiyar, the purported author of Tolkāppiyam, seems to have enjoyed that prestige. An introduction by one Panambaran refers to Tolkāppiyam as “full of Aintiram” or in accordance with the Aindra system.14 This was first pointed out by a 19th-century Dravidian and Sanskrit philologist from Britain named Arthur Burnell. Many scholars since have interpreted this as Tolkāppiyam being coeval with Panini, or possibly even older.

Problem with such an interpretation is, an older concept can be and often is referenced in a newer work, but a newer concept cannot be referenced in an older work. In other words, it’s possible for a 300 AD work to borrow from a 400 BC Panini, but it’s not possible for a 400 BC work to borrow from a 200 AD Manu. At any rate, the puḷḷi already places a ceiling of first century AD on Tolkāppiyam’s vintage. Having said that, the possibility of there being multiple authors offers an interesting picture. Multiple authors would mean different parts being composed t different points in time, likely over generations. This would easily help reconcile the antiquity of the puḷḷi reference with the relative recency of the Patanjali and Manu loans.

Zvelebil builds further upon the multiple-author premise and proposes Tolkāppiyar as the main figure, the man who composed the core and bulk of the work, with later accretions credited to various unknown authors over centuries. In this model, he dates the earliest nucleus of Tolkāppiyam to no older than 150 BC15 and attributes its creation to Jain influences. The reason he dismisses the Aindra hypothesis is that the term was not even in currency during Panini’s time.16 The school was, the term wasn’t. It finds no mention in Aṣṭādhyāyī where the word used instead is _Prancah_.17 Norwegian Indologist Christian Lassen further asserts that the name doesn’t show up in any textual record from the pre-Christian era and only in later Buddhist writings do we find its mention as a divine body of grammar “suppressed by Panini.18” The first time Aindra shows up as a reference to a school of Sanskrit grammar is fairly recent—in the works of the late 14th-century Vedic scholar named Sāyaṇa.19

Long story short, the oldest known Tamil composition does not go beyond 200 BC. Granted it’s only the oldest known and not the oldest, for it’s quite likely that other works from earlier times are either lost forever or awaiting discovery. But we go by what we have as of now. And what we have as of now, doesn’t allow us to go beyond 200 BC. Even if we were to be infinitely generous and make Tolkāppiyar coeval with Panini, which would be purely speculative and not grounded in evidence, we can only stretch it another 300 years. The oldest inscription also takes us to 500 BC at best. Could Tamil have existed before that point? It could, but we have nothing to prove that. No inscription, no composition, no reference in a later composition to anything that would have only been known to people from prehistory. In sum, Tamil is, at best, 2,500 years old. Also, how far is Tolkāppiyam’s Tamil intelligible, if at all comprehensible, to a modern speaker, is something only a native speaker can tell.

## Sanskrit

As we’ve already noted a great deal of Sanskrit influence in Tolkāppiyam, it’s reasonable to assume that it predates at least the first Tamil composition, if not Tamil itself. Question is, does it predate Tamil? We know Sanskrit had a grammar long before Tamil did. But that doesn’t in and of itself establish Sanskrit’s vintage over Tamil. While the oldest Tamil inscription on record is from the fifth century BC, the one for Sanskrit only goes back to the first. It’s a stone inscription found about eight miles north of Chittorgarh in Rajasthan and comes in a total of seven lines in three incomplete fragments. Archeologists have named it the Hathibada inscription.20

On the higher end, German Indologist and paleographer Johann Georg Bühler places the script in the Mauryan period, around the fourth century BC.21 Of course, writing has a somewhat older history in northern India, the oldest being Ashoka’s rock edicts. But those were all in Prakrit and Greek. Hathibada takes the cake for Sanskrit. Other experts, however, offer a less remote first century BC instead.22 Looks like written Sanskrit is hard to push beyond Ashoka, even at our generous best. Which clearly makes it younger than written Tamil.

But it doesn’t need a philologist to reckon that a language is always older than its script. Therefore, a more reliable metric to compare would be compositions. Tamil’s composition, as we’ve already established, does not go beyond 200 BC. The inscription at Adichanallur adds another three centuries and pushes it to 500 BC. But that’s about it. Nothing beyond that, at least nothing that we’ve discovered so far. Sanskrit, on the other hand, offers one of the oldest textual compositions known to mankind, in the shape of Rigveda. Even the most conservative of estimates do not place it later than 1200 BC. Others go as far back as 1700 BC. So there’s little doubt that Sanskrit as a language predates Tamil. But the Sanskrit of the Vedas is not exactly the Sanskrit of the Puranas.

Roughly speaking, we have two Sanskrits—Vedic and Classical. The dividing line between the two is traditionally placed around 700–600 BC. Classical Sanskrit developed as a simplification of phonology and syntax and much Dravidian influence. The retroflex _/i/_ sounds that existed in Vedic Sanskrit, for instance, disappeared in Classical. Some case endings changed too. But were these changes big enough to cause unintelligibility? Probably not. But even if we discount Vedic Sanskrit, which we should not, Sanskrit Puranas still predate Tamil pottery inscriptions.

So just between Sanskrit and Tamil, we should now be well-equipped to resolve the dispute on two counts:

Which of the two is older? Sanskrit.

And which of the two is older in written form? Tamil.

Again, reiterating here that a language is not its script and the dating of an inscription has no bearing on the dating of its language. As for intelligibility, we’re comparing Vedic Sanskrit to Old Tamil, the language of Tolkāppiyam. The gap is a whole millennium, if not more.

Should we include the reconstructed Proto-Dravidian in our reckoning? Ideally not, given its absolute unintelligibility with a modern speaker. But should we consider anyway, we’d also have to do the same for Sanskrit and go back to Proto-Indo-Iranian. Or Proto-Indo-European? Where do we draw the line and on what grounds? It can only be arbitrary. Should we still choose to go down that road, Proto-Dravidian goes back 6,000 years at best. PIE, on the other hand, beats it by two whole millennia.23 The comparison remains meaningless though, not just because they’re nothing like their modern descendants but also because they’re both mere reconstructions. The debate is over the oldest “continuously used” language.

But in the land of the world’s second-highest linguistic diversity and over 400 languages on record, it’s reductive to confine the debate to just two. Just because a language isn’t in the race, does not mean it wouldn’t qualify, or even win. Let’s look at some eligible challengers that have maintained surprising vintages without the clamor of a contest.

## Iruḷa

High in the Nilgiri Mountains, far from the mainstream, lives a tribal community of some 200,000 known as _Iruḷar_ (lit. dark people). Iruḷa is their language. Like most tribal tongues, Iruḷa does not have a script of its own and hence uses the one for Tamil. Many count it as a dialect of Tamil although the jury remains out on that one. It does belong to the Dravidian family, though.

Zvelebil, an authority on Dravidian linguistics, has reasons to see Iruḷa as an independent tongue rather than a Tamil dialect. He posits that the language started off along with Tamil as a single South-Dravidian unit. Then by 300 BC, as Proto-Tamil began taking shape, Iruḷa split off as a dialectical variant from the mainstream. This was before the Sangam Era.24

As almost a quarter of a million still speak the language, we should be safe to count Iruḷa as a living language with a tradition of continuous use since its split from Old Tamil almost 2,500 years ago. Does it have a script of its own? No. Does it have a literary tradition? No. But, is it a thriving language with an independent existence of its own? Without question. Since Iruḷa and Tamil split from a common ancestral branch well before Tamil as we would comprehend at all today took form, it should be fair to assign Iruḷa the same vintage as Tamil. In which case, although still younger than Sanskrit, it takes away Tamil’s position as the only Dravidian language of its antiquity. Once again, this entirely depends on whether we consider Iruḷa a language or a dialect. If we do, calling Iruḷa younger than Tamil would be akin to calling Spanish younger than Portuguese. Both split from a common Vulgar Latin ancestor.

There is a number of morphemic properties that make Iruḷa a distinct language within the Dravidian family yet independent of Tamil, according to Zvelebil. Several phonological properties that were lost in Tamil, were retained in Iruḷa. The language also features several phonological innovations unique to itself, further setting it apart from Tamil.25 One example is its vowel system. Both Sanskrit and Dravidian languages largely feature five basic vowel sounds in long and short forms. Iruḷa features four more, with corresponding long and short variants. Zvelebil calls them central or centralized vowels. These do not exist in any other Dravidian or Sanskritic language.26 He also offers a number of morphophonemic artifacts that characterize Iruḷa but not Tamil.

Zvelebil, by the way, isn’t alone in seeing Iruḷa independently of Tamil. French linguist Gérard Diffloth also calls it “a close relative of Tamil” but not Tamil.27 All said and done, all of this also hinges on the mutual intelligibility of the two tongues. If the average Tamil speaker and the average Iruḷa speaker can understand each other with little difficulty, many would be inclined to call Iruḷa a Tamil dialect despite its lineage. And it won’t be entirely dismissable either, for the line between language and dialect is often a matter of great subjectivity.

## Tulu

Tulu is a relative latecomer to the writing scene. The oldest we have on record is an inscription from the 12th century that was discovered only four years ago as of this writing.28 To further weaken its case, the language also lacks a long-standing literary tradition along the lines of Tamil. And yet, none of this allows us to dismiss its claim to antiquity. As we’ve noted multiple times, a language is born long before it’s written down. In Tulu’s case, this ought to be particularly emphasized, for it was spoken for over a millennium before anyone thought to etch it in stone.

Tulu’s classification within the Dravidian ambit has been a source of considerable disagreement and deliberation. Noted Dravidian linguist Bhadriraju Krishnamurti, who sadly passed away in 2012, counted it in Central Dravidian, which would place a significant Abstand between itself and other prominent Dravidian tongues like Tamil, Malayalam, Telugu, and even Kannada. A tentative nod to this position came from American linguist Murray Emeneau who based his take on Tulu’s past tense conjugations which exhibit little affinity to other members of the South Dravidian family.29 Another Dravidian linguist, also no longer with us, Prakya Subrahmanyam disagreed and placed it in South Dravidian instead, bringing it closer to Tamil, Kannada, and Malayalam. Interestingly, however, both find common ground in their dismissal of Tulu as a mere dialect of Tamil or other Dravidian heavyweights. Krishnamurti finds reasons to see Tulu as an offshoot of the reconstructed Proto-Central-Dravidian tongue, a sister tongue of Proto-South-Dravidian.30 Similarly, Subrahmanyam sees it as a descendant of Proto-South-Dravidian.31 If Tulu branched off the mainstream before the formation of Tamil, Kannada, and Malayalam (Telugu is a whole other subfamily), it already acquires a chronological primacy over its more prominent siblings.

A more concrete chronology in this regard comes from Soviet Indologist Mikhail Sergeevich Andronov, who spent over two decades studying Indian linguistics with a special focus on Dravidian languages. He posited that the Proto-Dravidian ancestor began disintegrating as early as 6,000 years ago when certain communities in what’s today Baluchistan spun off their dialect as an independent tongue called Brahui. Which, by the way, would make Brahui the oldest Dravidian language by far, but we won’t consider it for this discussion as it’s no longer an Indian language. Andronov further places the Tulu spinoff in the vicinity of sixth century BC.32 This already makes it older than Old Tamil which he places after 400 BC.33

Further evidence, albeit weaker than those already discussed, of Tulu’s independence and antiquity is furnished by Tamil literature itself. Tulu country or Tulu Nadu, although not a political entity, finds ample mention in Sangam corpus as a largely autonomous entity. It formed one of twelve such territories within the overarching Tamilakam ambit, says Tulu Nadu archeologist P. Gururaja Bhatt in a self-published work from 1975.34 The territory also features in a popular Malayalam charter myth that involves the Hindu deity Parashuram reclaiming land from the ocean to settle Brahmins from the north, Tulu Nadu being the name for the west-coast stretch from Gokarna in Karnataka down to Kannapuram in Kerala.35 Weak argument because in the remainder of his work, Bhatt vehemently challenges the notion of Sangam-era Tulu Nadu being geographically identifiable with the Tulu country of today.

To conclude, there’s ample evidence to establish that Tulu speakers have been noted as a separate community since the earliest of Sangam years and that the language retains more Proto-Dravidian artifacts unchanged than any other Dravidian descendant with the exception of Brahui. If we accept Andronov’s 600 BC, Tulu becomes the seniormost Dravidian tongue in India, older than Tamil, yet younger than Sanskrit.

## Conclusion

As said before, everything depends on where we draw the line between language and dialect. With that overarching caveat, we can conclude that the question has a straightforward answer and several conditional answers. Straightforward answers are attractive, but they come at the cost of nuance. And nuance is not really dispensable, however inconvenient to the argument at hand.

The straightforward answer would, of course, be Sanskrit. But then comes the nuance. It’s no doubt a living language in the sense that it’s still used and still understood. But it’s also moribund with virtually no native speaker outside of sacerdotal professions. There are ten times as many speakers of Esperanto as there are of Sanskrit. Technically alive, practically dead? Be that as it may, it remains the oldest tongue on the subcontinent and one of the oldest on the planet on all counts.

But what about Tamil? Certainly not nearly as old as Sanskrit, it fails to take the spot even within its own family. Discounting Brahui as no longer Indian, Tulu takes that crown, probably sharing it with lesser recognized tongues like Iruḷa. Both Iruḷa and Tulu are in continuous use and the only areas they lag behind is in literary tradition where Tamil scores over all Dravidian tongues. Tulu even had a script of its own but switched to Malayalam and Kannada scripts over time for some reason. Iruḷa never had any and borrowed from Tamil. Even Kannada emerged before the Old Tamil phase. In sum, while Tamil brings to the table a rich heritage of cultural and literary traditions and enjoys more currency in terms of native speakers today than any of its Dravidian brethren, its antiquity remains evidently overrated. The only way it can be and is assigned a vintage of more than 2,500 years is to include Proto-Dravidian in its history. If we did that, there’s no reason not to assign Proto-Indo-European’s span to Sanskrit. As we’ve noted already, doing that would accord Sanskrit a 2,000-year headstart over Tamil. Of course, that would be a silly comparison for the simple fact that neither PD nor PIE would be intelligible to us today any more than they would be to a European or a South American.

But what if we Proto-Dravidian were older than what Andronov proposed? A study was conducted in 2012 to find the most enduring relationships between the seven major language families of the world. This included both Proto-Dravidian and Proto-Indo-European. The most remarkable part about this story was its conclusion that at least two language families emerged before PIE. One is Proto-Kartvelian which is ancestral to Georgian, and the other is Proto-Dravidian. The latter was pegged around 12,000 years ago, give or take a couple of millennia.36 That makes it a good 2,000 years older than PIE even at its lower end. Although it still doesn’t affect the Tamil-Sanskrit debate, it’s interesting to note how far the different roots go in time. As an amusing aside, the study also proposes a Central Asian origin for Dravidians, a spectacularly radical proposition that we won’t get into here.

The timeline still remained far from settled, however, and there was yet another study six years later in 2018. This was a Bayesian phylogenetic research specifically on Dravidian lexicons with the objective of better understanding the relationships among its descendants. This study placed the Proto-Dravidian family at 2500 BC, a good 1,500 years later than what even Andronov had proposed before.37 Additionally, this study also reaffirmed Tulu’s chronological preeminence in concluding that it was the first language to branch off the South I mainstream.38

As of this writing, the 2018 study seems to be the most recent. Can future research upend these findings? They can, indeed. But for now, this is where scholarly consensus stands. So to circle back to our question, here are some quick one-word answers:

Oldest language? Nobody knows.

Oldest language still in use? Sanskrit.

Oldest language still lingua franca? Tulu (along with Iruḷa and many other obscure tribal tongues).

Oldest writing? Nobody knows.

Oldest writing that’s been deciphered? Tamil.

Oldest literature? Sanskrit.

Oldest Dravidian language? Brahui.

Oldest Dravidian language in India? Tulu.

Oldest Dravidian literature? Tamil.
