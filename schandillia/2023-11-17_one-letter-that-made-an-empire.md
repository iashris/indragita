# One Letter that Made an Empire

*The First Brexit and the Virgin Queen’s ‘Friend Request’ to Akbar*

Sometime in the spring of 1583, an Englishman named John Newbery arrived in India with a letter from Elizabeth I to an Indian ruler. What happened of him after, is a tad fuzzy but it’s assumed he was robbed and killed on his way back. Newbery wasn’t alone. His expedition carried two merchants named Ralph Fitch and John Eldred, a jeweler named William Leeds, and a painter named James Story. Nor was he the first Englishman in India. That credit goes to one Father Thomas Stevens of Wiltshire. Rev. Stevens was a Jesuit preacher who had recently settled in Goa and authored _Dovtrina Christam_ , the first such work in Konkani. So what’s an English priest doing in a Portuguese colony and why is the English monarch writing letters to India?

## The First Brexit

For answers, we’ll rewind to 1534, the year of the first “Brexit.” The man on the throne is Henry VIII, Elizabeth I’s father. Much of Europe was then the Holy Roman Empire, an entity that was, as they say, neither Holy nor Roman. And this entity was under the House of Habsburg, an Austrian dynasty. The ruler of this house was Charles V, whose aunt Catherine of Aragon was Henry VIII’s wife. In other words, the ruler of Britain was uncle to the ruler of Europe. And Henry VIII wanted an annulment. Today he would’ve sought divorce but that was not a concept back then. Nor was polygamy. So should you fancy yourself a new wife, the only way was to either wait for the old one to die or secure an annulment. For Henry VIII, that new woman was Catherine’s lady-in-waiting Mary Boleyn. A major reason for his fancy was also his desperate need for a son, something Catherine couldn’t beget. Actually, she did, but none survived past infancy. So, an annulment was as much a personal necessity as it was political. But the Pope wouldn’t permit that. So the king came up with a solution—leave the Pope.

Before acting on impulse, however, a final effort was made to convince the Pope in the form of a letter threatening secession should the Vatican continue to refuse the annulment. This exceptionally large piece of parchment with silk ribbons and dozens of wax seals was hand-delivered by Henry’s noblemen to Pope Clement VII in 1530. The threat failed to work and the annulment remained unapproved. The king waited a good three years for the approval before unilaterally announcing the end of his marriage. The following year, while still a devout Catholic, Henry VIII entitled himself the “Protector and Supreme Head of the English Church and Clergy” and announced his departure from Vatican’s suzereinty. The Pope expectedly responded with excommunication, thus completing history’s first “Brexit.” Unfortunately, Boleyn too failed to deliver him a son that survived and ended up losing her crown. And her head. It’s Jane, his third, who finally bore him the coveted heir, but died in labor. The king went on to have three more wives, two of them Catherines. Again, the kingdom still remained Catholic, just not under the Pope. That’d start to change with Henry’s son and successor.

Edward VI, in partnership with the Archbishop of Canterbury, launched a vigorous reformation campaign and introduced many Protestant traditions into the new church. But he died after a quick six years, still a teenager, and was succeeded by Mary I who lasted even less—just five years. Mary I was Henry’s only child with Catherine of Aragon and being a girl, was initially ineligible for the throne. She was a devout Catholic and is best known for reversing the whole English Reformation and undoing everything done by her two predecessors. By the way, that’s the infamous “Bloody Mary.”

We can clearly see her motivations given how her father had treated her Catholic mother. Also, add to it her marriage to a very Catholic Philip II of Spain. Then in 1558, she succumbed to influenza after short but eventful five years on the throne. And now comes Elizabeth I, her younger step-sister, Henry’s daughter with Anne Boleyn.

## The Second Brexit

Elizabeth ascended to the throne because she was the only surviving sibling to Mary who died childless. The two sisters never liked each other. Elizabeth had rooted for her father, Mary for her mother. So it should only be expected that the new queen would endeavor to bring back the Reformation. Which is exactly what she did.

This is when the Church of England was born, an entity that leaned more Protestant than Catholic and was entirely outside of Rome’s liturgical jurisdiction. This would eventually earn Elizabeth an excommunication, just as it had her father. Those days, politics and religion were so closely intertwined, excommunication directly translated into political and commercial isolation. This second Brexit came at a cost. Britain got completely isolated from Catholic Europe and the latter ceased all business dealings with it. In other words, Europe placed “sanctions” on Britain. The economy began to suffer. This was Rome’s clout. And it was tough as Elizabeth I had inherited a financially wrecked kingdom. Her father had left England under nearly half a million pounds of debt. The queen had to quickly scout for new mercantile opportunities to keep her country from sinking.

In 1570, the Pope finally issued her excommunication (Papal equivalent of a _fatwa_ , if you will) which worsened her position as it included not just a political boycott but a call to her dethronement and made a Spanish invasion imminent. Catherine’s ghost was back to haunt her dead tormentor’s legacy. Elizabeth needed a lot of money, very quickly.

That’s when opportunity appeared in the shape of a letter from Turkey. It was the spring of 1579 and the missive was written by Murad III who had ascended to the Ottoman throne barely five years ago. Through this letter, the new Sultan had offered new trading opportunities to Elizabeth’s merchants in his realm. This was uncharted territory for England but having arrived at such a crucial juncture, the letter was more than a windfall to the flailing monarch. The queen responded promptly and kickstarted a correspondence that would last no fewer than seventeen years and help Britain avoid insolvency. Many letters were exchanged between the two monarchs. Elizabeth wrote to him about how she and Murad both had a common adversary in Catholicism. She reminded him of the Crusades and even drew his attention to the myriad parallels between Protestantism and Islam. The two struck quite a chord. Exotic gifts were soon being exchanged and the Sultan’s wife almost became a sister to Elizabeth. The two even exchanged embassies and many Englishmen wound up in Constantinople.

While all was going well, this relationship complicated another—Russia. England and Russia had been trying to work out a deal for decades now. Problem is, Turkey and Russia didn’t like each other much. This animosity went back centuries and would endure. Those days, Russia was under an extraordinarily violent tyrant, Ivan the Terrible. And the biggest pain in his neck was an equally violent people that inhabited Crimea—the Tatars. The latter were known for mounting brutal raids on Moscow and being remarkably hard to repel.

To understand England’s involvement with Russia, let’s roll back to the summer of 1571. That’s when the Khan of Crimea Devlet I Giray invaded Russia with a joint Turco-Tatar army. Ivan sought help from Elizabeth, who was already in less-than-optimal financial health at the time, only a little over a year into her excommunication. It’s under these circumstances that Anthony Jenkins, Elizabeth’s emissary in Moscow returned to England for some time. He returned with not only a renewed trade deal but also accounts of Tatar atrocities upon the Russian people. And a slave—a Tatar girl captured in the aftermath of the Russo-Crimean War. Jenkins brought her as a gift for his queen.

The girl’s name was Aura Soltana and they later recorded her as Ipolitan the Tartarian. In documented history, this girl is the first Muslim in England. Jenkins had bought her from the Russians for, in his words, “a loaf of bread worth sixpence in England.” Brought a Muslim, Aura was quickly baptized on the queen’s orders and taken in as a member of the royal court. The girl would never return home.

Over time, the Tartarian received countless gifts from the queen, each more precious than the last. There were shoes, silk gowns, damask cloaks, fur coats, gold dolls, jewelry, and lots more. Although captured as a slave in Russia, she was living it up in England. While life was now set for Aura, it was only getting complicated for her queen. Elizabeth’s mercantile dependence on an embattled Ivan was problematic. Freshly excommunicated, she needed a quick bailout. And then came 1579. And Murad’s letter.

Two years after the first letter, the queen granted charter to a new business entity with the unimaginative name of the Turkey Company, sometimes also known as the Levant Company. The following year, the new enterprise launched a voyage to a distant land that was already enriching the Portuguese and still appeared to have room for many more. It was the ultimate Orient. Actually, there were two such destinations but we’ll only talk about one for now, India. This voyage was headed by three London merchants named John Newbery, Ralph Fitch, and John Eldred. Also tagging along were jeweler William Leedes and painter James Story. The boat was named Tyger and it carried a very special payload, besides all the gifts and sundry—a letter from the queen herself. It was addressed to an Indian king she barely knew anything about and Newbery was to hand-deliver it to him.

While India was still uncharted territory to the British and Indian kings rather unknown, Elizabeth did have a vague understanding. She knew of this place called Cambay that served as the entrepôt to the Indian subcontinent. This is where all European merchants flocked. Of course, she’d also heard of Goa but it was a Portuguese stronghold and being Catholic, off-limits and downright dangerous to her men. So she was using the voyage to scout new opportunities in this land of abundance. The letter to this near-mythical “king of Cambay” was a diplomatic maneuver, a cold call for friendship as well as new mercantile opportunities. If it worked out, England would become unstoppable. Elizabeth wanted to do everything in her capacity to ensure that. The letter was as polite as it gets.

There were two letters, the other one for China. Even hedged bets ought to be hedged. Anyway, so the expedition first sailed to Tripoli, then onward to Aleppo. From there, it was an overland trip to Ormuz—today’s Hormuz—on the Persian Gulf. The ship reached India under particularly unfortunate circumstances. As we just learned that Goa was extremely risky to anyone with a British standard and had to be avoided at all costs. Well, as fate would have it, this is exactly where Newbery’s boat wound up. You do not want to be a Protestant prisoner in Catholic lands in the Middle Ages. Especially when your queen has just been excommunicated by the Pope. This was a rather precarious position.

And this is where a fellow Englishman comes in. Remember Rev. Stevens, the priest from Wiltshire? He stepped in for their rescue and hired James Story, the painter, to paint the churches in Goa. Story took a native wife, opened a store, and never left Goa. The remaining three sneaked out of Goa while on bail and traveled all over the Deccan in pursuit of trading opportunities. Three and not four, because John Eldred had already abandoned the expedition while in Aleppo. So now it was just Newbery, Fitch, and Leeds. Their accounts of Masulipatnam were the reason later English merchants would pick it for their inaugural trading outposts on the subcontinent. Their second discovery was that there was no such thing as a “king of Cambay.” Cambay—today known as Khambat—was just part of a much larger entity, larger than even Britain.

The Mughal capital in those days was a citadel near Agra called Fatehpur Sikri and its ruler was Akbar. Here, Leeds the jeweler was offered a job by Akbar himself and he decided to settle down for good. The other two chose to hand over the letter and return home. Unfortunately, only Fitch made it back as Newbery lost his way, his possessions, and his life to the bandits in Sindh.

So, was the letter worth the unfortunate adventure? Did it secure Elizabeth the hedge she so desperately needed? Hard to say because few records exist from the time, but we do know that before the turn of the century, Elizabeth had signed yet another royal charter to one Governor and Company of Merchants of London trading into the East Indies, later renamed the East India Company.

While the queen herself died, as did Akbar, before the Company set up its first Indian factory in Machilipatnam, she did manage to turn around her country’s fortunes. What she inherited as a 300,000-pound debt, was now well on its way to becoming humanity’s biggest empire ever. Within four years of the East India Company’s foray into India, Sir Thomas Roe was in the Mughal court seeking Jahangir’s patronage for a second factory in Surat. From here on, although Jahangir did not grant his patronage then, India’s fate was sealed.

The letter Newbery carried was recorded well within the queen’s lifetime by an Oxford preacher named Richard Hakluyt. The original text might be hard to read today given its unrecognizable English, but the “typos” are practically a work of art. One must note that this was more than a hundred years before Samuel Johnson’s dictionary of the English language. Until that point, there was no standard spelling and you spelled words as you sounded them. A dictionary did exist during Elizabeth’s time—A Northwood headmaster named Richard Mulcaster published one the year before her letter to India—but it only listed 8,000 words, and even those with spellings unrecognizable to the modern reader. Back then, the language of prestige was still Latin.

So this is the story of how one letter from Turkey saved Britain from bankruptcy and another to India catapulted Britain into empirehood. And all this because a king wanted to get rid of his wife. Practically, the most expensive annulment in the history of annulments.
