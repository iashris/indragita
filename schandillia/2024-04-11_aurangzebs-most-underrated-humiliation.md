# Aurangzeb’s Most Underrated Humiliation

*Lachit Borphukan and the Ahoms’ Last Stand in Saraighat*

“Gauhati and Kamarupa do not belong to the Mughals. It was through mere chance that they fell into the hands of the Mughals for a few seasons. Now God has given them back to us. When He pleases to give them back to the brother-sovereign, the Mughal emperor, he will then get Gauhati, and not before that.” Thus read the Ahom emissary’s missive1 to the Rajput king Ram Singh, encamped in Sualkuchi, a scant twenty miles from Guwahati across the Brahmaputra. Ram Singh, charged with securing Lower Assam for his suzerain, Aurangzeb, had threatened swift retribution should the Ahoms refuse submission—a mere hour’s battle, he had boasted, would see Guwahati fall. Adding further insult to injury, he even proffered implements of war, should the Ahoms be lacking in those.

In response, the Ahom general not only conveyed the utmost improbability of capitulation, but also added that his men were prepared to fight till the last drop of their blood. As for the humiliating offer, he added that the Rajput “has come over a long-distance undergoing fatigue in his journey, and the provisions may be inadequate for his own purpose. Our Majesty the Heavenly King has nothing unavailable to him. If the Rajput Raja falls short of materials, let him ask me and I shall try to oblige him.” This war of words was part of a far more protracted psychological operation the likes of which are rare to find in medieval Indian history. The Mughals were eager to conquer and the Ahoms hoped to wear them out with delays. In response to the general’s taunt, a Rajput servant named Devakinandan brought him a bag of poppy seeds.2 “This is how numerous we are,” was the implication. To which the general dispatched Ahom servants Nim and Ramcharan with a tube filled with sand. “This is the characteristic of our soldiers. They’re as numerous and as dissoluble as this sand.”3

But why this hammy exchange instead of a direct, full-frontal encounter? Was this cowardice? We will return to this question but first let’s understand where it all began and why Ram Singh even came to Assam against a fellow Hindu. The story begins years before any of its lead characters assume their roles—before Ram Singh, before Aurangzeb, and before this Ahom general.

## The Origins

Thirteenth century is an important milestone in the subcontinent’s millennia-old history of demographic upheavals. In that century, two extraordinary change agents entered the scene, one from the West seeking conquest and the other from the East seeking refuge. The former came first. Although Islam had been in Sindh and around for nearly half a millennium, it’s only with the Turks (not to be confused with inhabitants of modern Turkey) at the dawn of the thirteenth century that it became an established political reality as the Delhi Sultanate. Less than two decades later, an Austroasiatic Tai community made its way into the Brahmaputra valley to establish a new homeland. This later became the Ahom polity.

While the Sultanate eventually perished and got supplanted by the Mughals, the Ahoms continued to grow in both territory and prominence. By the time of Shah Jahan, they were in possession of the entire Brahmaputra valley from the Manasa River in the West to the Hills of Sadiya in the East. They called it Assam. Laterally, the territory split into two at Kaliabor, the eastern half being Lower Assam and the western, Upper. Guwahati sat in the former. The Mughal–Ahom rivalry goes back to 1615 when Jahangir was still on the throne. This one resulted in a humiliating Mughal defeat.

The state of hostility would eventually culminate in yet another conflict two decades later when the Mughal throne had passed to Shah Jahan. This ended in the famous Treaty of Asurar Ali and, for the first time, heralded peace between the two adversaries. At a territorial compromise, of course. The treaty was signed by _Faujdar_ Allah Yar Khan for the Mughals and Momai Tamuli Borbarua—remember this name, we’ll need it later—for the Ahoms. Essentially, the Ahoms surrendered the western half of Lower Assam, from the Manasa River to and including Guwahati to the Mughals in exchange for recognition of their suzerainty over the rest of Assam and resumption of trade and commerce between the two kingdoms. For Shah Jahan, this was a major win given he’d just blown a fortune on building the Taj in the face of a catastrophic famine in the Deccan and Assam offered more than enough resources—minerals, elephants—to make up for the depletion. Skirmishes continued, but a large-scale encounter was no longer an immediate threat. His son would change that.

Aurangzeb’s violent ascension to the Mughal throne opened a new chapter of conquest and destruction in the subcontinent’s history. His ambitions placed him on a collision course with every major polity outside the Mughal domain, most notably the ones in the Deccan. This is what helped the rise of the Marathas, a formidable new Hindu force that quickly grew to be a major thorn in Aurangzeb’s side. While the Mughals under his watch alienated most of their allies, especially the Rajputs, they still managed to retain some. Among them was Jai Singh of Amber who singlehandedly earned Aurangzeb much of his successes in the Deccan, including the big one at Bijapur. But his most landmark feat was the subjugation of Shivaji in 1665.4 The following year, in an unfortunate chain of events the Maratha chief landed in Mughal captivity. As we all know, however, not for very long. Unfortunately, it’s Jai Singh’s son and successor Ram Singh who was held responsible for Shivaji’s escape.5

While Aurangzeb’s Deccan campaigns are well known, not as much is known of his campaigns in the northeast. The last time we were in the Ahom country, the Mughals had acquired Guwahati and everything west of it. The city served as the seat of the Mughal commander Allah Yar Khan. Shah Jahan’s illness in 1656, disrupted this. His infirmity triggered a violent war of succession that included even fratricide. In this mess, the Ahom ruler Sutamla, or Jayadhwaj Singha, saw a tempting window of opportunity to reclaim all that was lost in the treaty of 1639. Within a few quick years, the Ahom army took back not only Guwahati but the entire Kamrup territory to the west, all the way to the Manasa River. To add further insult, the nearby Hindu kingdom of Koch Behar also declared independence from Mughal suzerainty.6

This is what Aurangzeb wanted to undo upon ascension. But he knew the Ahom were not an easy adversary. Besides elephants and seasoned guerrilla warriors, they also had geography on their side. The thick rainforests of Assam and the watery ferocity of the Brahmaputra offered stiff resistance to any alien cavalry typically best suited for the plains. Also, he was already stretched thin all over the subcontinent, especially in the Deccan. So, he assigned Bengal’s governor Mir Jumla to the task.

## Mir Jumla’s Assam Blitzkrieg

The expedition started in the first quarter of 1662 with a promising speed. Koch Behar was easy to fall. Just as easy was Kamrup, the western Ahom territory. The Muslims were now back in possession of Guwahati. The expedition proved easier than Jumla expected. This was due less to his own military prowess and more to the internal fractures within Ahom ranks. The king had just appointed a new Phukan or military leader for Lower Assam who proved extremely unpopular among his peers on account of his caste and lineage.7 Many nobles refused to cooperate with the new leader which, in turn, weakened the Ahom resistance. This helped Mir Jumla cut his way through every Ahom line of defense with remarkable ease and swiftness. All the way to Kaliabor, the marker separating Lower Assam from the heartland.

This alarmed the Ahom nobility. Thus far, it had been all Lower Assam, the bastion of the new Phukan. But if Kaliabor fell, it would mean an immediate threat to Upper Assam too. The internal fractures could be dealt with later, Upper Assam had to be saved now. So, the Ahoms decided to rise above their issues and mount a united defense against the Mughals in Kaliabor. But it was too late. The Mughal morale was already at a peak and the Ahom defense fell apart in no time. Barely a dozen miles away in Samdhara, the Ahoms didn’t even bother defending and just fled preemptively.8 The Mughals were now in possession of entire Lower Assam. But Jumla’s brief was to take all of it. Aurangzeb wanted the whole Ahom country annexed. So he continued his eastward march. The campaign from this point on proved just as easy as it had thus far, marked by Ahom desertions, defections, and feeble defenses.

By the middle of March, Garhgaon had fallen. With the capital gone, the Ahom king fled into the mountains of Namrup to the east, far out of Mughal reach.9 With this, for all intents and purposes, ended the half a millennium old Ahom rule. What didn’t, however, was the resistance. Seeing the Muslims in the capital hit the Ahoms differently. They realized the sheer scale of what they faced and the insignificance of their internal grievances next to it. For the first time, the threat was existential, and that injected a fresh dose of nationalism into their ranks. The Mughals must be ousted, whatever it took. The only way forward was to go guerrilla.

This state of affairs lasted another six months, both sides desperate to get back home. The Ahoms longed to return to their cities and the Mughals longed to return to Delhi. In January 1663, the two sides finally came to an agreement and signed a deal. Through this treaty, the Mughals demanded two Ahom princesses for the imperial harem, including the only child of the king himself. In addition, they also demanded a war indemnity of 300,000 rupees and ninety war elephants, along with an annual tribute of twenty elephants. All of this in exchange for just Upper Assam and part of Lower Assam between Guwahati and Kaliabor.10 The terms were both humiliating and heartbreaking, but the Ahoms had no alternative. The deal was signed and the demands were delivered. Mir Jumla stationed one Rashid Khan in Guwahati to govern Lower Assam and left. To add further insult, he took with him over ten thousand Ahom men and women as spoils of war. While some went voluntarily, a vast majority were captives. Jayadhwaj Singha came down with an illness and passed away less than nine months later. He is still often remembered as the _Bhagania Raja_ or the deserter king.11

Unfortunately, though, Jumla himself did not last long enough to enjoy his triumph. He contracted malaria on his way back from Kaliabor and passed away even before reaching Dhaka.

## The Ahoms Bounce Back

After Jayadhwaj came his cousin Chakradhwaj. The new king was keen on reversing his predecessor’s losses at the earliest. This wasn’t just a matter of prestige but also pragmatism. The treaty with the Mughals had reduced a sovereign nation to Mughal vassalitude. The annual tribute was too steep and the Ahoms faced prospects of yet another invasion over non-payments.12 Chakradhwaj Singha wanted to be better prepared this time.

As the first order of business upon his coronation, alliances were struck with neighboring Hindu kingdoms of Cooch Behar, Cachar, and Jayantia. The idea was to ready a formidable Hindu confederacy before formally announcing severance of all ties with Delhi. Neighbors’ allegiances secured, the king now turned to his own army. While he was eager to declare war right away, Prime Minister Atan Buragohain urged restrain.13 The Ahom economy was just not ready for such an expensive undertaking yet. Nor was the kingdom militarily prepared. So, work began in earnest to rejuvenate both economy and military.

New parcels were assigned to paddy cultivation under royal supervision to ensure uninterrupted food supply. On the military side, new war vessels were commissioned to fortify the navy and _paiks_ (conscripts) were put through a rigorous training under the king’s personal supervision. Metalworkers forged arrows, swords, and other war implements round the clock, while stonemasons toiled to build new fortifications in record time. A number of organizational reforms were introduced to improve the army’s efficiency. To make all of this any consequential though, the Ahoms also needed a strong military general—one who was not just a sound strategist but also a shrewd diplomat.

And that brings us to Momai Tamuli Borbarua, the general who had signed the treaty in 1639. Rising from nothingness to the very top of the Ahom military pyramid, Momai had amassed a variety of accomplishments and firsts in Assam’s history. He is responsible for the most defining reform in the Ahom military organization, its rank system.14 He introduced the idea of economic autonomy to the Ahom household and popularized weaving as a domestic chore. As a result of this initiative, every Ahom household became self-sufficient in its daily necessities. To this day, weaving remains an occupational mainstay in large parts of Assam thanks to him.15 There couldn’t be a more desirable leader for the Ahom army.

But Momai was already dead. So, the spotlight fell upon his son who was said to have inherited much of his father’s sense of loyalty. Right now, he was the head of the kingdom’s police force as its _Barua_. Keen on elevating him to the helm of the army, the king summoned him for a two-factor audition. The first was a simple question as to how the Ahom army should defend against a potential Mughal invasion. The Barua’s answer was a confident reassurance that on the king’s command, the kingdom could easily present enough gallant men to take on an enemy of any might.16 The Mughals too were, after all, mere mortals. The second test was more practical in nature where a courtier was assigned to insult the Barua by snatching his headdress as he bowed to the king. The latter just wanted to see how the candidate reacted to an attack on his esteem. The Barua passed this test as well.17 Pleased, the king elevated him to the top of his army as well as the governorship of all Ahom possessions in Lower Assam. His new title was Borphukan. From now on, he’d be known to both friends and foes as Lachit Borphukan.

## Ram Singh’s Fall from Grace

Aurangzeb, meanwhile, had just started making new allies and consequently new adversaries in the Deccan. An emergent Maratha polity was giving the Bijapur Sultanate a lot of grief. Unable to counter these incursions on her own, the Begum sought help from Delhi and Aurangzeb obliged. This was the beginning of the disastrous Mughal interface with the Maratha chieftain, Shivaji. With both military and resource superiority, Aurangzeb prevailed and by the summer of 1665, Shivaji was a Mughal tributary. The following year, however, things took an ugly turn when Shivaji ended up in Agra as a Mughal hostage. How this happened doesn’t concern this story, so we’ll skip it here. The man entrusted with Shivaji’s captivity was none other than the Mirza Jai Singh and his son Ram Singh.

The fact that Jai Singh actively lobbied for the fellow Hindu’s release at the Mughal court18 did not do him any favors. Because after only three months in captivity, Shivaji escaped. While Aurangzeb was expectedly furious, he also placed the entirety of the blame on the Rajputs, thanks to Jai Singh’s overt sympathies with the Maratha. While the father didn’t live long to face the consequences, passing away only months later, the son found himself in a very difficult spot. Luckily, the fall from grace did not lead to charges of treason typically punishable by death. But it sure affected his political career. The emperor wasn’t going to let go of the debacle for sure.

It’s against this backdrop that news came from Assam. The king had died, and the new sovereign was preparing to throw out the Treaty of Asurar Ali. This would mean a loss of all Mughal holdings in the northeast. But the Mughal army wasn’t the best with mountain warfare. And any humiliation in Assam could affect Mughal morale elsewhere. The Deccan campaigns were yet to begin. So, Aurangzeb came up with an idea. He assigned the job to Ram Singh—a punishment campaign to Assam, if you will. The brief was to attack, defeat, and annex the entire Ahom country into the Mughal realm. If he lost and died fighting, it’d be a punishment for helping Shivaji escape. If he won, it’d be atonement. Preparations began in earnest.

## The Legend of the Muslim Hero

The man governing Guwahati for the Mughals those days was Syed Firoz Khan. While he had proven quite capable of defending his capital against any Ahom onslaught, he counted heavily on Mughal reinforcements. The closest he could get those from was Dhaka, more than two weeks away on the finest of horses. This gave his adversaries a distinct edge. Another potent Ahom weapon was, of course, their element of surprise. And that’s what they were about to press into service in their quest for Guwahati.

On August 20, 1667, two Ahom detachments started from Garhgaon and sailed down the Brahmaputra to Kaliabor, the frontier town between Upper and Lower Assam.19 From there, it was just about a hundred miles to Guwahati. News reached Firoz Khan. He knew he was on his own, no reinforcement could be expected within the available window. The Ahoms started with the fort of Bansbari, which was an easy score,20 and then blitzed their way through the northern bank of the Brahmaputra, one fort after another. In practically no time, they were at the river preparing to invade the city. This was going to be a tricky affair.

This was Firoz Khan’s home base and the garrison of Itakhuli was on top of a hill which accorded them a crucial vantage point. Whoever held this fort, held Guwahati. Naturally, defending it was as critical to the Mughals as taking it was to the Ahoms. To inject morale into his ranks, the leader of the charge is said to have announced that he’d be the bondsman to the soldier who succeeded in taking the fort of Itakhuli.21 Morale boosted, the army hatched a plan. Again, it was the element of surprise that came in handy, and it was here that one man emerged as the unlikely hero of the night. Who this hero was remains unknown outside of legends. Let’s first learn the legend before we get to why it even is one.

The Ahoms were largely a Hindu force but they did employ some Muslims too. Just as the Mughals employed some Rajputs. One such Muslim was a man named Ismail Siddique. He was hired by the late king Chakradhwaj Singha after rumors of him fighting off a tiger singlehandedly reached the capital. This rumor, along with his appointment as a _hazarika_ (commander of a thousand paiks), had earned him the name history would record him by—Bagh Hazarika. Now back to Itakhuli.

The fort had to be taken, but the Ahoms had neither the numbers, nor the vantage. So they had to think out of the proverbial box. Bagh Hazarika offered to lead an advance party right into the fort. This sounded suicidal on the surface, but the man had a plan. While the Mughal soldiers were on constant vigil, as a Muslim himself, Hazarika recognized one small window of opportunity, a crack in this otherwise ironclad vigil. The mandatory _namâz-e-Fajr_ or dawn prayer. Muslims are obligated to pray five times a day. This is one of those. The prayer must be performed by all without exception and strictly in twilight right before sunrise. The whole ritual takes no more than fifteen minutes. That’s the only window Hazarika could exploit.

Around midnight, Hazarika and a few soldiers crossed the river and lay in wait at a blind spot just outside the walls. As the window approached, they stealthily climbed up the walls and poured water into the cannons. All cannons thus rendered useless, they returned to their base. The following morning, the Mughals led a preemptive charge into the Ahom base with their cannons, blissfully unaware of what happened earlier. The cannons failed to fire and the Ahoms scored an easy victory. Itakhuli fell by midnight. The Mughals were subsequently chased all the way to the Manasa River, bringing once again the entirety of Assam back to Ahom rule.

While the cannons being rendered ineffective stands historically attested as is the existence of Muslims in the Ahom army, Bagh Hazarika still remains less so. Surya Kumar Bhuyan, the authority on Ahom history, does not mention this character in any of his works on Assam. The only place he does is a book on Mughals titled _Annals of the Delhi Badshahate_ which came out in 1947. In this work, he names Bagh Hazarika as a “military genius partly responsible for the success of Lachit Borphukan’s operations against Ram Singha.” His source of this information is, by his own admission, an alleged descendent named Maulavi Mufizuddin Ahmed Hazarika.22 The latter tells us about Bagh Hazarika in an Assamese-language poetic work called _Jnan Malini_. Beyond that, there is nothing. Just folklore. In sum, the only source of there ever being a Bagh Hazarika is a nineteenth-century Maulavi who claimed to be his descendent and hearsay. Be that as it may, Guwahati had been reclaimed.

The reoccupation of Lower Assam was indeed a remarkable feat and deserved all the pride and celebration that comes with triumphs of this scale. But the Ahoms knew there was no time to celebrate. Holding is far harder than capturing. A retaliation was an inevitability. The Ahoms must prepare for a much bigger war. Aurangzeb was not kind to take territorial losses in his stride. And the Rajputs were yet to make an appearance. Remember Ram Singh? He was already on his way to Assam.

## Prelude to the War

Both Mughals and Rajputs were alien to the Brahmaputra terrain, the Ahoms had a clear superiority. As we’ve noted, the only reason the Mughals had managed to prevail earlier was the internal fractures within Ahom ranks. To add to it, the natives also boasted of naval superiority, a crucial component should the battle shift to the waters. A good way to improve their odds was to strengthen their fortification. Atan Buragohain was tasked with not only reinforcing existing forts but also building a series of new ones around Guwahati.

Atan pulled all stops and is said to have completed the task in record time. The legend doing the rounds was that these forts were built by Vishwakarma himself.23 Armed paiks were stationed every thirteen-odd feet on the ridges and hilltops and every nine feet on the ramparts.24 Lachit Borphukan, the general, stationed himself at Itakhuli on the south bank and from there, made routine trips around Guwahati to inspect preparedness. Atan Buragohain similarly based himself on the north bank in the fort of Lathia. The newly repossessed Kamrup was further broken down into smaller administrative units for efficient taxation and supervision.25 Guwahati was virtually impregnable now.

For all his confidence, however, Lachit was more than cognizant of his adversary’s raw military might. The Mughal army came with no fewer than twenty-one Rajput generals, thirty thousand infantrymen, and fifteen thousand Koch archers who knew Assam just as well as the Ahoms.26 And if that weren’t enough, there were almost twenty thousand fine Turkish cavalrymen27 and a thousand war hounds.28 There were reinforcements from Bengal, Bihar, and even Kamarup. Besides hardware and manpower, Ram Singh also came armed with spiritual arsenal. He carried blessings of no less than the Sikh Guru Teg Bahadur who even accompanied the contingent to Assam.29 “Guru Nanak will assist thee, and thou shall conquer Kamrup,” he said to Ram Singh.30 This was no paranoia. Assam was called the “graveyard of generals” in Mughal traditions,31 and for a very good reason. For every win in this terrain so far had proven Pyrrhic to the invaders. Here, malaria was a bigger killer than any army. Recall how Mir Jumla died.

By February 1669, the Rajput–Mughal combine was already in Assam. The first encounter at the frontier garrison of Rangamati proved to be an easy one. While the Ahoms had around a hundred thousand paiks,32 they were notably weak on cavalry. To make things worse, the further away from Guwahati, the weaker their defense. This is what made Rangamati such an easy win for the Rajputs. In sum, the Ahoms didn’t stand a chance. But the general wasn’t going down without a fight.

What the Ahoms lacked in numbers and horses, they made up for in strategy. A full-frontal standoff would be futile. So, the general planned to go guerrilla. The first step was to anyhow lure the invaders into the warzone of Guwahati where the Ahoms were at their strongest and had geography on their side.33 Lachit appointed three commanders to the task. These commanders were sent to Rangamati and from there, they were to feign retreat. As they sailed upstream back toward Guwahati, the enemy forces followed.34 Ram Singh didn’t suspect anything and just read this as the enemy’s inability to mount any resistance, likely out of fear.35

After about six days of sailing, the three commanders camped at a place called Sualkuchi. Ram Singha followed, exactly as expected. But just before, the Rajputs got there, one of the commanders left, leaving behind the other two to be captured. This too was part of the plan. When Ram Singha reached, he found the two asleep and held them as prisoners.36 To him, these were handy assets and could be used as messengers with the Ahoms. It’s through them that he made the offer of a one-hour duel to Lachit Borphukan,37 the response to which we saw at the beginning of this story.

Guerrilla was the Ahoms’ forte. Lachit had also announced that any deserter would be beheaded, so that took care of loyalty. The closer the Rajputs drew to Guwahati, the easier it got for the Ahoms to hit. The thick jungles of Assam were breeding grounds for all kinds of spooky folklore and paranormal stories. Hauntings and ghost sightings were common. Putting this element of mystique to good use, Lachit ordered his men to hide in the woods and only emerge late in the night. Every night, these faceless guerrillas would sneak into the Mughal encampment, inflict serious damage, and swiftly retreat into the forests. To many Mughal and Rajput soldiers, these were nocturnal man-eating _rakshasas_ or demons.38 Ram Singh understood the trickery and accused the Ahoms of fighting the dishonorable war. He called the guerrilla tactics “thieves’ affair.”39 To which, the general responded that lions too fought in the dark.40

Frustrated by this protraction, Ram Singh enquired the Ahom envoys in his court about the men leading the Assamese charge. The envoys are said to have quipped thus: “Not to speak of yourself, even the Padshah of Delhi will be unable to vanquish them in war.”41 To reconfirm the threat, Ram Singh turned to his own envoy who responded in the affirmative and listed out three names as “wonderfully capable commanders presenting a rare combination of beauty, accomplishment, valor, and wisdom.”42 He listed out three names. Among them was Lachit Borphukan.43

It was Lachit’s idea to stretch out the situation indefinitely and just continue inflicting countless papercuts upon the enemy in the interim. As they were not very good at cavalry fighting, sneak-attacks were the Ahoms’ only pragmatic bet. There did exist one area, however, where they enjoyed a decisive superiority. The waters. With centuries of experience on the mighty Brahmaputra, the Ahoms were virtually unbeatable at naval warfare. The Mughals, not so much. The Rajputs, not at all. So, until such a time, the enemy could be corralled into a naval encounter, guerrilla was the only way forward.

But Ram Singh wasn’t the only one frustrated by Lachit’s vacillation. Word also reached the Ahom court at Garhgaon and didn’t make Chakradhwaj Singha very happy. He ordered Lachit to go on the offensive right away or face deadly consequences.44 Lachit knew this would be suicidal, but the king’s word was God’s word. He prepared for war.

## Battle of Alaboi

By now, the Mughals had advanced to a large open plain near Alaboi Hill, just across the Brahmaputra from Guwahati. If hills and jungles are for guerrillas, plains are for horses. Ram Singh could play out his cavalry to its full potential here. The plains were skirted by Brahmaputra on one side and a minor Sessa River on the other.45 Despite its the disastrous implications, Lachit ordered the offensive.

To make the defeat even more humiliating, Ram Singh had placed at the lead of his force, a female warrior named Madanavati.46 This way, the Ahoms would have not just a defeat but a dishonorable defeat, at the hands of a woman. After an initial setback with heavy casualties, however, the Ahoms mounted a surprising response. Assamese fighters emerged like ghosts from trenches and successfully routed the much larger Mughal army.47 Madanavati died fighting and Mir Nawab, the Mughal general and counterpart to Ram Singh (Aurangzeb had a policy of planting a Muslim general when the charge was led by a Hindu general, especially against a Hindu adversary), ended up in Ahom captivity.48

But the euphoria proved premature and just as the Ahoms retreated in victory, Ram Singh ordered a fresh charge. This was catastrophic for Lachit’s men, more than ten thousand of whom lost their lives.49 The Battle of Alaboi had been lost. The loss of lives devastated the Borphukan who lamented every loss as the loss of an Ahom pillar. The Ahom army was no standing army. It was a militia. Every Ahom youth was trained for and drafted for war. These were farmers, carpenters, teachers, and all kinds of civil contributors to the Ahom society. Thus, every death on the battlefield was a loss of not just a soldier but a contributor to the Ahom economy.

## Battle of Saraighat

Reinvigorated by the triumph at Alaboi, Ram Singh now intensified his demands for Guwahati’s surrender. The latest win had given him a position of immense superiority. But Lachit refused to budge. So, the Rajput decided to move in for the final kill. A charge on Guwahati.

This meant crossing the Brahmaputra because Alaboi and Guwahati were on opposite sides of the river. The planning had to be immaculate because neither the Mughals nor the Rajputs could risk entering a battle in the waters, least of all Brahmaputra. Lachit Borphukan was stationed at Itakhuli. From there through Kamakhya Hill, for about five miles along the Brahmaputra, stretched a sandbank named Andharubali.50 On this sandbank stood a defensive wall. Espionage reports informed Ram Singh of a breach in this wall not far from Guwahati. So, a plan was hatched to ferry a detachment of the Rajput–Mughal cavalry across the river in boats and then enter Guwahati through the reported breach.51 Meanwhile, Chakradhwaj Singha died. Barely eight months after the Alaboi disaster. He was succeeded by younger brother Udayaditya Singha.

Lachit Borphukan was not inherently a warmonger. He was just loyal. To his king. Therefore, every time the Mughals asked him to surrender, his response was, to paraphrase, “not unless our king says.”52 This made Ram Singh’s job a tad easier after Chakradhwaj Singha’s death. That’s because his successor did not inherit his predecessor’s enthusiasm for war. The army was also at its low point in terms of morale after the setback at Alaboi. All this in mind, the new king grew more conducive to an amicable solution. This practically meant surrendering Guwahati. Loyal to his king, Lachit didn’t disagree.53 The Swargdeo’s word is God’s word.

But there was one voice of dissent—Atan Buragohain.54 He had personally overseen the construction of all major Ahom fortifications along the Brahmaputra in anticipation of Mughal incursions and had completed the job in record time. He was one of the three names read out to Ram Singh when he’d sought information on the leaders of the Ahom charge. He opposed the Raja’s peace overtures saying the Mughals could not be trusted with peace and that any such treaty would render meaningless the sacrifice of tens of thousands of Ahom soldiers who went down fighting for every inch of Ahom territory. This worked. The king ordered Lachit to prepare for another war.

Back in enemy camps, Ram Singh received news from Amber. Annoyed by the slow progress in Assam, Aurangzeb had started venting it out on the Rajput king’s son, Krishna Singh.55 Aurangzeb also threatened to force-convert him into Islam.56 This was relayed to Ram Singh by the women of his household, his wife and mother. They also implored him to abandon Assam because attacking and desecrating a Hindu territory where cows, _vaishnavas_ , and Brahmins lived in peace, would only bring misfortune.57 They also reminded him of Mir Jumla, who had earlier tried to conquer Assam and died soon thereafter.58 In short, nobody who ever attacked Assam lived very long. Bound by his oath of loyalty to the Mughal emperor, however, Ram Singh remained in Assam. The plan that was hatched earlier, was now in execution. Mughal boats prepared to cross the Brahmaputra for the final charge on Guwahati.

Lachit Borphukan, though ready for the war, suddenly fell very ill in his fort at Itakhuli.59 The Ahom morale was already at its lowest. The breach in Andharubali sank it further. Although the breach was quickly repaired, the Ahoms knew they were up against an unstoppable force. Lachit’s sickness further deprived them with leadership and the futility of war grew more pronounced by the hour. Desertions followed. Many Ahom soldiers started leaving the fort in boats, sailing upstream to safer havens away from Guwahati.60 Some even offered to take Lachit along, for he was in no form to face an army. He couldn’t even get off his bed. But he refused. “I am going to die on this spot, and I never think of abandoning my charge,” he said.61 That’s when word came from Churamani, his chief astrologer.

Astrologers were a crucial aspect of the Ahom polity. Their word was always final. No war was fought without a greenlight from astrology. Everything must be done when the stars aligned in favor. Every minister, every general, every noble had a personal astrologer to advise him in these matters. Churamani was Lachit Borphukan’s. And he told Lachit that it was the most auspicious window for a counterstrike.62 This meant, Ahoms were guaranteed a victory if they attacked now. Such was the faith in astrology that Lachit ordered to be carried to his soldiers. Four _bhuyans_ or helpers carried the cot, with Lachit in it, out of the sick room.63 The general intended to lead from the front despite his illness.

Held by two helpers, the Borphukan emerged from his gatehouse, boarded a boat, and sailed right into the heart of his navy formation. As the soldiers saw their leader climb down the steps despite his sickness, they entered a whole new phase of animation and vigor.64 Lachit met the Mughal fleet near a place called Saraighat, with the rest of Ahom navy in tow. Their naval superiority over the Mughals and the Rajputs was about to bear fruit. A fierce battle ensued, and the Mughal fleet was routed.65 To make the win more decisive and lasting, the Ahom navy continued the chase overland and didn’t stop until it had driven the Mughals completely out of Assam.

The Ahoms were now in possession of all of Assam, including Kamrup. The campaign would prove to be Aurangzeb’s last. Save for a brief three-year window from 1679 through 1682 where Lachit’s younger brother and successor Laluksola Borphukan abandoned Guwahati, the Mughals would never enter Assam again. Lachit Borphukan, unfortunately, succumbed to his illness shortly after the win at Saraighat.

Such was the Ahom resilience under Lachit and Atan that even centuries after the events, a historian said, “The Assamese were to the Moslems what the Numidians and Mauritanians were to the Old Romans— _genus insuperbile bello_.”66 That’s Latin for “the unconquerable race.” Today, Lachit Borphukan is a regional hero and celebrated even by the National Defense Academy in the form of a Lachit Borphukan Gold Medal awarded to the best cadet every year.
