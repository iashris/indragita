# The Man Who Mugged Aurangzeb

*A Grand Mughal Heist and the World’s First International Manhunt*

Aurangzeb inherited a deeply troubled empire at a deeply troubled time. When he took the throne, the East India Company was already in India, albeit more as merchants than as imperialists. But none of this could make a dent in the Mughal treasury which kept growing unaffected and unabated. They say India accounted for a fourth of the world’s GDP when he inaugurated his reign. Of course, none of this meant a whole lot in terms of general prosperity, for the bulk of this wealth was confined to the Mughal royalty and hence subject to its whims.

It’s true the Mughal treasury lost much of its contents to Aurangzeb’s myriad military campaigns. But even that left him enough to place him among the richest of his time. Remember, his great-grandfather’s net worth is still mentioned as “incalculable.” At least to an extent, Aurangzeb made up for his territorial splurges with personal frugality. Man is said to be the single most spartan member of arguably the single most profligate dynasty in the subcontinent’s history. The story of Mughal wealth had been a matter of legend. Already before Aurangzeb, the East India Company had nearly two dozen factories in India and two major ports, Surat and Madras. A third addition would come in 1661 when a newly-coronated King Charles II married Catherine of Braganza (in Portugal) and received Bombay in dowry.

Aurangzeb was already two years into his reign when the Company acquired Bombay. Within a decade of this acquisition, Charles II granted the Company right to acquire colonies and exploit them for profits. It now graduated from commerce to imperialism. This elevation invested the Company with a new kind of power. And with power comes new ambitions. Part of this ambition was to score trading privileges with the territories beyond the coast, deeper into the subcontinent. It wanted to do business with the entirety of the Mughal Empire. So it spent the following twenty-odd years building diplomatic inroads to that end. The goal was Bengal. The Mughal Empire was the world’s richest entity at the time and Bengal was its moneybag, its richest _subah_ or province. Having lost to the Portuguese in the spice game, the British saw fortune in textiles. Bengal had been producing and exporting some of the finest muslin known to mankind since Roman times. Besides, it had a great harbor and a tremendous, untapped market. It’s not that the Company was new to Bengal. A trading outpost here went back to 1612 when Jahangir ruled the Empire. But there were also others—the Danes, the Dutch, the French, and even the Portuguese. The British sought monopoly. The kind it enjoyed in Madras.

Unfortunately, however, negotiations didn’t go through and Bengal remained elusive. All options exhausted, the Company decided to take it up on the battlefield, a bold gambit which, if successful, could make the British insanely rich insanely quickly. Before long, the conflict had escalated into a full-blown war between James II and Aurangzeb, much of it at sea. The four-year episode, known as Child’s War (after Sir Josiah Child, leader of the English side), seemed to end in 1688 in a British victory. The English had scored an ace by capturing a bunch of Mughal ships headed to Mecca for the annual Hajj pilgrimage. To make matters worse, an entire fleet was dispatched to blockade Bombay and Surat. This is where Aurangzeb finally relented and agreed to negotiate. Things could have ended there, deals could have been signed, and everybody could have gone home happy. But, the British decided to pull out of the negotiations at the last minute and mounted fresh attacks on the Mughals. With no sign of a deal, Aurangzeb ordered all Company properties confiscated and their men thrown out of India. Essentially, the end of the East India Company’s affairs in India. With all else gone, the British were now left with just Calcutta and Bombay. Even this was not secure. In 1689, a Mughal commander laid siege to Bombay with the help of an Ethiopian fleet manned by Moplah Muslims. On Aurangzeb’s orders, of course.

The siege lasted a year and broke the Company’s back. When the city started running out of food in 1690, it sued for and secured peace with the Mughals. In exchange for what would today be well over a billion dollars, in damages. The Company verged on bankruptcy thanks to the outrageously expensive Child’s War. Nevertheless, peace was finally here and they could at least start rebuilding. And this is where we introduce the other character of this story.

## Henry Avery

In a sleepy little fishing village near Plymouth, a man named John Avery ran a small grocery store where he sold, among other things, alcohol. Being a prominent watering hole right by the water, the village being on an estuary, Avery’s business flourished. Shortly after Aurangzeb took the crown, John Avery took a wife. And had a boy. They named him Henry. John and his wife weren’t educated, but they wanted better for the child. Unfortunately, things wouldn’t pan out exactly as planned. The boy never learned to read or write. That said, Henry did develop a different set of skills that’d prove far more useful to his career than literacy. In the rough company of sailors and boatswains that routinely gathered at his family store for drinks, the kid learned to read the compass. And to swear.

When he was about six, the boy begged a boatswain to smuggle him aboard his ship, and the latter obliged. The captain was initially appalled but eventually acquiesced and let him be. The ship was headed to America, a five-day voyage. At the destination, the boy was given to a merchant. The merchant tried putting young Henry through school but failed as miserably as John had before. After three years of trying to civilize him, the merchant put the kid back on a ship to Plymouth. By the time Henry reached home, John was long dead of heartbreak. The mother followed soon. Henry Avery was now officially an orphan. Still too young to look out for himself, the boy now went under the guardianship of one Mr. Lightfoot.

Even this arrangement, however, didn’t last as Mr. Lightfoot was a merchant and merchants often suffer losses. Mr. Lightfoot suffered one too many and went bankrupt. Henry was once again on his own. So he went back to doing the only thing he was any good at—sailing. Petty pilfering was his source of livelihood for now. This would change, but there’s time. The boy is still too young. Toward the end of his teens, Avery boarded a ship named Duke. With this, his life was to enter a new chapter.

Duke was a 24-gun, 100-men privateer. Those days, Spain was engaged with France in what we call the Nine Years’ War. For a change, England was an ally. Along with Sweden, Scotland, the Netherlands, and the Holy Roman Empire. This coalition, creatively called the Grand Alliance, heavily relied on English ships. Ships put together at Bristol were routinely rented out to Spain and other allies for naval use. Duke was one such ship and it was commanded by a chronic alcoholic named Captain Gibson. Also out on rent was a companion of Duke named, of course, Duchess.

Once in Spain’s possession, Duke became Charles II, in honor of the reigning Spanish monarch at the time. Avery was on board this ship. In other words, Henry Avery would now fight for Spain. But that’s not exactly how things panned out. Wars are uncertain times. At least were then. Avery wasn’t new to this life though. The boy had worked with the Royal Navy for years now. Even before the Nine Years’ War, Avery is said to have been part of the campaign against Algiers. Earlier in the Nine Years’ War itself, he once took a large French convoy off Brest. Therefore, as evident, Avery’s tryst with ocean warfare was no novelty. What was, was a stint under a direct, non-English command. Hard to say if he was excited about this new assignment but working with the Spaniards would’ve been quite an addition to the résumé.

But a sudden change of plan came along when Captain Gibson was recalled to England. With his ship, of course. We don’t know why. Avery was on board. So the ship, along with its crew of about a hundred, and Avery, turned around and headed back to where it was born, Bristol. Now, “drunk sailors” isn’t an archetype for nothing. Gibson loved his alcohol and was known for drinking beyond his faculties and passing out dead every night. That night was no different. Avery saw opportunity. In fact, he already had the day before. Consequently, he had already hashed out a plan with his confidantes not only on _his_ ship but also on the other, the Duchess. That night, Avery’s co-conspirators from the companion ship hopped into a longboat and oared their way to Duke.

One of them asked Avery, “Is your drunken boatswain on board?”

“All is fate,” replied Avery. This was code for “all clear.”

Assured the captain and his crew were out drunk, about twenty conspirators climbed into Avery’s ship and started tossing drunk sailors overboard.

By the time it was all over, Henry Avery was the captain of a ship now headed to Madagascar instead of Bristol and Captain Gibson was in a longboat headed to, most likely, Bristol. Oh, and it was no longer Charles II. The new name was Fancy. Henry Avery was no longer a naval officer. Or a regular citizen. He was now a pirate.

At Madagascar, the pirate gang came upon its first bounty, an abandoned sloop. A sloop is a sailing boat with a single mast, popular with small merchants and lesser pirates in those days. A closer investigation yielded that the sloop was on the run from New England. The crew had fled the boat because they thought Avery’s men were sent after them. Having taken them in his confidence, Avery made the first addition to his nascent naval possession. From ship, he’d now graduated to fleet. The group’s first shot at piracy came only after it sailed back west, into the Caribbean. But first, it scored two more sloops. These sloops were found off the coast of La Désirade in the French West Indies. The fleet now had four ships and about eighty-five men. Primed for action.

Action came in Barbados. Here, Avery’s men looted an English merchant vessel—their first official act of piracy. This loot added to their inventory clothes, money, a dozen guns, almost as many barrels of gunpowder, ten cakes of beef, and five men. Not bad for a first hit. But nothing compared to what was to come later. Meanwhile, the Atlantic exercise wasn’t done yet. Avery’s next score was in Dominica where he met with six stranded Englishmen and added them to his crew. These men had been stripped of their ships and belongings by a French sloop and left to die. Avery offered them a new life. Of piracy, obviously.

Now Avery turned to the east. Atlantic was great but that time of the year, the Indian Ocean was a different kind of temptation. It offered the kind of bounty an Atlantic pirate could barely begin to imagine. What happens here depends on who one trusts.

## Ganj-i-Sawaʾi

Let’s return to India now and pick up from where we left off. We know that much of the subcontinent was then under Aurangzeb and that the East India Company was a commercial entity confined to but a few factories along the Indian coast. We’ve also seen how the Child’s War ended badly for the Company and resulted in Aurangzeb nearly wiping them off the subcontinent. Being Muslim, the Mughals were big on _Hajj_ and a trip was made to Mecca every year. These trips were typically done in convoys of ships with a flagship carrying one or more members of the royal family. Charity is a big part of Hajj rituals and Mughals were extravagant. Unaffected by Aurangzeb’s own borderline-fanatical austerities, the Mughal Hajj entourage would carry with it immense wealth in bullion and gems for charity. One such convoy carried the emperor’s own daughter. The year was 1695. Having performed the Hajj, it was on its way to Surat.

Henry Avery and his tiny fleet led by Fancy were already in Arabian waters when the Mughal convoy left Mecca. Avery knew this would be a hard raid to pull off, but if he did, he’d make history, for Mughal convoys were reputed to be the richest in the world. But more than wealth, it was clout. A successful heist on a Mughal convoy would cement Avery’s name in the Pirates’ Hall of Fame if there were any.

It was August. Earlier, in spring, Avery had already issued an open letter to all British seamen inviting them to join him and promising no harm to any British or Dutch ship that might come his way, provided they weren’t hostile. Fancy was in the Bab-el-Mandeb strait between Yemen and Ethiopia. This strait links the Red Sea with the Gulf of Aden and was considered one of the most target-rich environments in the Indian Ocean. And every pirate worth his rum knew this. Here, Avery met five more pirates, all bigger names than himself, and the six agreed to form a grand confederacy to take on the Mughal convoy. Among the six big names was someone Avery himself idolized and aspired to emulate. His name was Thomas Tew.

Tew had only recently acquired fresh limelight after a successful raid on an Indian dhow in the Red Sea. The raid, worth well over $100,000 in seventeenth-century money, was a superlative of its time. Avery didn’t know he was about to top that record. Nor did Tew.

After lying in ambush for days, Avery’s men finally spotted a sail on the horizon. It looked Dutch. Not bad. Must be loaded with spices from India. But as they prepared for the raid, the flag looked different. Closer still. Definitely not Dutch. It was Mughal—gargantuan convoy of over two dozen ships armed to the teeth. The six men between them had a flotilla of six flagships, several sloops, and well over 400 men. Still dwarfed by what they hoped to take down. What they’d spotted was the enormous Mughal flagship, Ganj-i-Sawaʾi, which alone had eighty cannons. Then there was its 600-ton escort, Fateh Muhammed. The pirate coalition chased the Mughal convoy all night. Somewhere along the way, Tew entered a fight with one of the convoy’s ships and died. A setback, but the pursuit continued.

The pursuit lasted about a week during which Avery’s men took down the escort, Fateh Muhammed. An eye-watering £60,000 was looted from this ship, but the real prize was Ganj-i-Sawaʾi. And that still remained elusive.

After a persistent chase and much crossfire, Fancy eventually drew right next to the grand Mughal flagship. What followed over the next few hours was intense. Besides the cannons, Ganj-i-Sawaʾi’s 400 men were armed with muskets and trained for action. After an enormous loss of limbs and lives, Avery finally prevailed. The mammoth was down. Avery’s plunder came to, by some estimates, over five million rupees in gold coins. Along with money, both in coins and jewels, he also got his hands on the centerpiece of the whole convoy, the Mughal emperor’s daughter. While other women in the convoy were brutalized in all ways imaginable, this girl wasn’t. Instead, Avery took her for himself and made it to Madagascar where he married her, had a son, and then dumped her. This account comes from Khafi Khan, a contemporary whose _Muntakhab al-lubāb_ pegs the loot at “fifty-two lacs of rupees in gold coins” and speaks of women throwing themselves overboard to “save their honor.”

Remember we said Avery’s story in the Indian Ocean depends on who one trusts? Well, there are two accounts, besides Khafi Khan’s, of course. One by Avery himself and the other by John Connor. The account so far is from John Connor. Avery’s own account is significantly different. According to him, it was not Aurangzeb’s daughter but his granddaughter, not Hajj but a marriage party, and not in the Arabian Sea but in the Bay of Bengal. A very different story. Going by Avery, the girl was betrothed to a Burmese prince, the son of the king of Pegu. Pegu of Burma is Bago of Myanmar today. Avery ambushed the convoy near Calcutta and looted the ship while respectfully setting the women free. Also, no bloodshed in this account. Although it’s hard to be absolutely certain of the finer details, most contemporaries and historians disagree with Avery’s account. Either way, one thing that remains a constant is the sheer scale of plunder. To date, the largest.

Most of Avery’s men got in share more money than they hoped to make in entire lifetimes of piracy. A heist of this size was a matter of immense pride for Avery. And of immense embarrassment for Aurangzeb. As expected, the news didn’t make him very happy. The livid emperor revived his earlier order to oust the East India Company from Indian shores. All English merchants and settlers in Surat were promptly arrested and held hostage until the culprit were found and punished for his audacity. The Company was already in bad shape. Fresh out of the ruinous Child’s War, it could not afford to lose trading opportunities in India once again. Aurangzeb had to be appeased. And the only way to do that was by dragging Henry Avery out of whatever hole he was inhabiting, and hanging him until death.

To that end, the Company announced a bounty of five hundred pounds on Avery’s head. Within weeks, it was doubled for added motivation. This was history’s first international manhunt, a good two centuries before the Interpol and its Red Corner notices.

## The Disappearance

While Connor says Avery escaped to Madagascar, another source offers a different ending to the story. In his book, _The Pirates’ Pact_ , Douglas Burgess says Avery and his men made it all the way to the New World. With or without the princess, isn’t known. About six months after the plunder, an English merchant named Henry Bridgeman dropped anchor in the Bahamas. Here, he met with Sir Nicholas Trott, the local governor, and handed him a letter from the Crown. The letter instructed Trott to offer the bearer some shore time.

In return for shelter for himself and his hundred-odd men, Bridgeman offered the governor over eight hundred pounds in pieces of eight. This was a deal Trott couldn’t refuse. Not only the letter but also the money. So he relented. Bridgeman and his men made Nassau home. But there’s something Trott didn’t know at the time. Henry Bridgeman was Henry Avery.

This account, of course, differs from Connor’s Madagascar story, an account also supported by Khafi Khan, the only primary source from India. After about a year of relative peace and utter boredom, Avery and his men finally had some semblance of adventure when word of his bounty reached the Caribbean. Trott had by now already come to suspect Bridgeman’s bona fides but chose to keep quiet. When the Royal Navy finally got hold of a solid tip, a warrant of Avery’s arrest was promptly issued to Nassau. Driven to the wall, Trott had no choice but to give up Avery. Before doing that, though, he didn’t forget to tip Avery off. With a 24-hour notice, Avery escaped.

Before the escape, he dropped all kinds of misleading breadcrumbs. Different people were told different stories of his planned trips. Thus, when the authorities interrogated the locals, they failed to put together a coherent trail to pursue. And that’s the last the world ever saw or heard of Henry Avery. All that remained was a trail of conjectures and rumors, none more reliable than the rest. Some claimed Ireland, others Devon. Some said he died wealthy, others said penniless. Truth is, no one knows.

As for Aurangzeb, he had to contend with a little over half a million pounds in reparations and a public execution of five of Avery’s men. The emperor didn’t live long enough to see the end of the manhunt anyway, dying in 1707—sick, and on the edge of bankruptcy.
