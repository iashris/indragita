# Proto-Indo-European: A PIE in the Sky?

*An Objective Look at Paleolinguistics’ Most Controversial, Most Polarizing Hypothesis*

- audience: only_paid

The Proto-Indo-European language (PIE) is a concept central to historical linguistics, proposed as the common ancestor of a vast group of languages spoken across Europe and Asia, including Sanskrit, Greek, Latin, and Hindi. Reconstructed through comparative analysis rather than attested in written records, PIE has long been a subject of academic inquiry. Yet, it faces increasing scrutiny, particularly from scholars in India, where some question its existence as a historical reality. This skepticism frames PIE as a speculative construct—possibly a product of imagination rather than evidence—prompting debate about its validity. This article examines that debate, exploring the linguistic and archaeological data behind PIE to assess its status as a hypothesized language.

The roots of this scrutiny trace back to 1786, when Sir William Jones, a British judge in colonial India, noted striking similarities between Sanskrit, Greek, and Latin during an address to the Asiatic Society of Bengal. He suggested these languages descended from a shared source, a claim that birthed the Indo-European language family hypothesis and, eventually, PIE. For two centuries, linguists have built on this idea, reconstructing PIE’s sounds, words, and grammar from its presumed daughters. However, the absence of direct evidence—no inscriptions or texts in PIE—fuels doubt. Critics ask whether these similarities could arise from contact, borrowing, or independent development rather than a single ancestor.

In India, skepticism often carries additional weight. Sanskrit, enshrined in ancient texts like the Rig Veda, holds a revered status as a cornerstone of cultural and linguistic heritage. For some Indian scholars, the notion that Sanskrit derives from a reconstructed PIE—often linked to a distant steppe origin—challenges its primacy. This perspective is compounded by historical context: Jones’s work emerged under British colonial rule, raising concerns that PIE might reflect a Western agenda to reframe India’s linguistic past. Such doubts align with broader critiques questioning whether PIE is a testable hypothesis or an unprovable abstraction.

The debate hinges on fundamental questions. If PIE existed, what evidence supports it? How do linguists reconstruct a language never recorded? Can shared features among Indo-European languages only be explained by a common source, or are alternative explanations viable? Beyond linguistics, does archaeology provide corroboration, or does it complicate the picture? These questions are not merely academic—they touch on identity, history, and the reliability of scientific methods applied to the past.

This article hopes to settle the debate to the best possible extent using all available evidence. We’ll start with the theory’s conception, then trace its development over the years, going through all the changes and new ideas along the way. Also, along the way, we’ll get a quick tutorial on all the linguistic ideas of relevance, such as phonetic terms and concepts like Grimm’s Law. Then we will endeavor to get a clear picture of all the arguments against it and those for it, before finally drawing conclusions. In keeping with the tradition of objectivity here, equal space will be accorded to voices from either side of the argument.

This structure aims to provide a comprehensive evaluation. Each section builds on the last, grounding the discussion in established scholarship while remaining open to critique. The goal is not to prejudge PIE’s existence but to interrogate the evidence—linguistic patterns, sound laws, artifacts—systematically. Whether PIE emerges as a concrete language, or a contested hypothesis depends on how this evidence holds up under scrutiny.

## Historical Linguistics Basics

Thomas Stephens, an English Jesuit missionary, arrived in India in 1579, marking one of the earliest European encounters with Indian languages. As the first Englishman to set foot in India, he wrote from Goa in a letter to his brother in Paris, commenting on the structural similarities between Indian languages and Greek and Latin. Stephens’ observations, though not deeply linguistic, hinted at a connection that would later spark broader inquiry. His work laid an early foundation for what would become the study of the Indo-European language family, though it would take centuries for these ideas to fully develop.

Six years later, Florentine merchant Filippo Sassetti, stationed in Cochin, echoed Stephens’ observations in a letter, noting that Sanskrit shared features with Italian. Sassetti described what he called “Sanscruta” as “pleasant and euphonious” and pointed to lexical similarities, such as numerals like “six, seven, eight, and nine,” which he found strikingly similar to Italian. His remarks, while insightful, lacked the depth of formal linguistic analysis. Over the following century, European engagement with Sanskrit grew through missionaries and travelers. Dutchman Abraham Roger published Indian texts in Amsterdam between 1673 and 1677, and Henricus van Rheede tot Drakenstein’s _Hortus Malabaricus_ (1678–1693) included Sanskrit material. Yet these efforts focused more on cultural documentation than linguistic study. Ancient Greeks like Megasthenes (circa 302–291 BC), as well as later travelers such as Arrian and Strabo, had written about India’s culture but showed little interest in its languages. Roman merchants, whose coins and artifacts appeared in Pompeii, and Venetian traveler Marco Polo in the late thirteenth century, similarly overlooked Sanskrit’s linguistic significance.

It wasn’t until 1786 that Sir William Jones, a British judge in India, formally hypothesized a common ancestor for Sanskrit, Greek, and Latin. Jones didn’t name this ancestor but observed their shared grammar and vocabulary, suggesting they “sprung from some common source, which, perhaps, no longer exists.” At the time, it was already accepted that Spanish, Portuguese, Italian, and French had spawned from a common ancestor in Latin. Jones just extrapolated this further to include German, Greek, and Sanskrit into a much larger Indo-European family. His address to the Asiatic Society of Bengal marked a turning point, igniting curiosity about language relationships.

The term “Indo-European” was coined in 1814 by English polymath Thomas Young in an article in the _Quarterly Review_. Meaning “common to India and Europe,” it referred to both the language group and the peoples associated with it. Linguist William Dwight Whitney, in his 1875 work _The Life and Growth of Language_ , credited Franz Bopp with popularizing the term. By the early nineteenth century, this curiosity had crystallized into the formal discipline of historical linguistics. German scholars like Bopp and Jacob Grimm began systematically comparing languages to uncover their histories. Bopp’s 1816 work, _On the Conjugational System of Sanskrit in Comparison with Greek, Latin, Persian, and Germanic_ , linked verb forms across these languages, while Grimm’s identification of sound shifts, later termed Grimm’s Law, revealed evolutionary patterns in Germanic tongues. This marked the birth of historical linguistics, a field dedicated to tracing language evolution over time, distinct from descriptive linguistics’ focus on modern forms.

Historical linguistics aimed to reconstruct ancestral languages, such as Proto-Indo-European (PIE), the hypothesized root of the Indo-European family. PIE, assuming it existed, was spoken by a nonliterate people more than 4,500 years ago leaving no written records. Yet in 1868, August Schleicher crafted a story, _Avis akvasas ka_ or “The Sheep and the Horses,” in this reconstructed tongue. Later revisions, such as Herman Hirt’s 1939 version _Owis ek’woses-kwe_ and Winfred Lehmann and Ladislav Zgusta’s 1979 update _Owis ekwoskwe_ , refined its phonology. These reconstructions amazed many but also sparked suspicion. Without texts, how accurate could they be? Archaeologists, accustomed to tangible evidence, often dismissed such “linguistic prehistory” as speculative. This skepticism was compounded by a lack of interdisciplinary understanding. Linguistics was rarely taught in archaeology programs, and archaeology was absent from linguistics curricula, creating a divide deepened by dense jargon on both sides.

The comparative method became the cornerstone of historical linguistics, enabling scholars to analyze words in “daughter languages” like Sanskrit _pitṛ́_ , Greek _patēr_ , and Latin _pater_ to hypothesize, for example, PIE’s _ph₂tḗr_. This method assumed that languages evolved predictably, allowing linguists to reverse-engineer changes and reconstruct PIE’s features. Schleicher’s fable and terms like _kʷékʷlos_ (wheel) demonstrated its plausibility, while the Neogrammarians’ assertion of exceptionless sound changes further strengthened the approach. Spanning Europe and Asia, PIE defined the Indo-European family, with shared traits like the verb root _h₁es-_ (to be) defying explanation by chance or borrowing.

Despite early European encounters with Sanskrit, it was the systematic work of 19th-century linguists that established PIE as a cornerstone of historical linguistics. Figures like Jim Mallory, who bridged linguistics and archaeology, helped illuminate how language evolution could reveal the past. Yet debates over PIE’s historical reality persist.

Now that we have in place a decent snapshot of PIE’s history, we are almost equipped with all we need to start dissecting its inner workings. Almost, because a prerequisite to understanding how language evolves or corrupts over time is the understanding of the laws that govern sound shifts in the context of languages that do exist right now. Different people can pronounce the same words differently even if they’re both native speakers. These variations deepen and diverge over time, giving us what we call dialects. And they’re not random. There’s method to this madness. In the following section, let’s try to understand this “method.”

## Sound Shifts

Here’s a sentence in Old English, also known as Anglo-Saxon:

_Cóm on wanre niht scríðan sceadu-genga._

Can you guess what it might mean? Of course, an accurate translation is virtually impossible to the untrained eyes, but the sentence is still English. Here’s the same sentence in Modern English:

“In the colorless night came slinking the shadow-wanderer.”

This is from the most celebrated English epic, Beowulf and the original dates back to more than a thousand years ago. See the evolution? In just a millennium, we have a language change beyond recognition. A more literal word-by-word translation might help:

“Come on dark night glide shadow-walker.”

In a little over a thousand years, _Cóm_ became “come,” _niht_ became “night,” and _sceadu_ became “shadow.” Almost as if the same words got corrupted in pronunciation. This corruption is called sound shift. Of course, not all languages change to the same degree as English did. Some like Icelandic change much slower. A number of factors inform this rate—number of speakers, intensity of foreign contacts, geographical distribution, etc.

A most well-known example of such a shift is the one from /k/ to /s/ before /e/ and /i/. This shift isn’t without reason. The /k/ sound is made with the back of the tongue touching the back of the palate, we call it a back consonant. The exact opposite happens with a front consonant like /s/ where the tip of the tongue approaches the alveolar ridge instead. In the case of vowels, similarly, the qualification is a function of where the highest point of the tongue lies during articulation. In the case of /a/, /o/, and /u/, it’s further back in the mouth, which is why we call them back vowels. In /e/ and /i/, it’s more toward the front, hence front vowels.

Pronunciations tend to move from higher complexity to lower. The tongue is lazy, it dislikes too much movement. This tends to simplify articulation over time. In this context, the shift is from a position of more tongue movements, back and forth, to fewer. Take /ke/, for instance. We start with a back consonant and end with a front vowel. Not ideal. Much easier to simplify it to something where the consonant and the vowel are both frontal, something like /se/. /ko/ is fine, though, since here both consonant and vowel are back. This is how Latin _centum_ /kɛntəm/ corrupted to Spanish _ciento_ /sjento/, Italian _cento_ /tʃɛnto/, and so on. And this is also why Latin _costa_ /kosta/ remained a relatively unmolested _côte_ /kot/ in French and completely unchanged in Spanish and Italian. This simple line of reasoning helps us not only trace a direct etymological line from Latin to its various Romance descendants but also test it in the real world, since all languages in question are alive and understood. Linguistics is full of patterns like these. This one is often referred to as the _centum-satem_ phenomenon. In phonetics, the more technical term is assimilation.

A further extrapolation of this pattern was observed and propounded by a man named Jacob Grimm of the Brothers Grimm fame in 1822. Jacob Grimm is best known for fairy tales like _Hansel and Gretel_ and _Cinderella_. The collection he and his brother Wilhelm published under the title of _Grimms’ Fairy Tales_ has entertained children all over the world for more than two centuries now. But there’s another side to him that isn’t as well known in popular conscience—his seminal contribution to the discipline of linguistics. A comfortable familiarity with his work is crucial to an understanding and appreciation of the process behind PIE. Let’s do just that in the following section.

## Grimm’s Law

How people say words changes with both distance and culture. When these changes are small, it’s dialects. When they’re big, it’s languages. These changes are not random. Take Latin _pater_ , for instance, and its English equivalent _father_. Almost as if it’s the same word pronounced differently. We know Latin came before English. So we can safely assume that over time, the /p/ of pater corrupted to /f/ to become father as we know it. Of course, something changed with the /t/ too. But why? Was it a chance mispronunciation? Perhaps, but a similar pattern can be found in _pisces_ becoming _fish_ , _ped_ becoming _foot_ , and several others. It’s a pattern. Grimm was the first to notice it, study it, expand on it, and articulate it as what we now call Grimm’s Law. What we discussed is just one of the several patterns the law explains, a thorough understanding of which is crucial to a better appreciation for linguistics in general and PIE in particular.

At the heart of Grimm’s Law are three sound shifts that govern much of linguistic evolution in the Indo-European language family:

  1. Stop to fricative,

  2. Voiced to voiceless, and

  3. Aspirated to unaspirated.




A “stop” is a consonant sound where you totally block the air in your mouth for a moment before letting it go if a vowel comes next—think of sounds like /p/, /b/, /t/, /d/, /k/, and /g/. Try saying _pat_ out loud—your lips snap shut for the /p/, holding the air back, then pop open to release it. That’s a stop. Now compare that to a “fricative,” where the air isn’t stopped but squeezed through a narrow gap, making a hissy or buzzy sound—like /f/, /v/, /s/, /z/, /θ/, or /ʃ/. Say _fish_ —the /f/ lets air slip past your teeth and lips with a soft whoosh.

Next up is “voiced.” A voiced sound is when your throat vibrates—like your vocal cords are humming along. Put your fingers on your throat and say /b/—you’ll feel a little buzz. Now try /p/—no buzz, just air. So /b/ is _voiced_ (it’s got that throat hum), and /p/ is _voiceless_ (quiet in the throat).

And finally, there’s “aspiration.” An aspirated sound comes with a little puff of air—say _pin_ , and you’ll feel a breathy burst after the /pʰ/. Now say spin—the /p/ is still there, but it’s less airy, no big puff. Here, the /p/ is unaspirated. In old languages like Sanskrit or Greek, they loved aspirated stops (like /pʰ/, /dʰ/, /gʰ/) with that extra breath. English has no voiced sound that’s also aspirated, such as /bʰ/, /dʰ/, or /gʰ/.

Pronunciation is all about wind in the mouth. Stop it and you have a stop, add a hum and you have a voiced consonant, release it with a puff and you have aspiration. Stops require arresting a body of air, fricative allows for it to move smoothly. This drives the first shift where the stop /p/ in Latin _pater_ turns into /f/, its fricative counterpart, in English _father_. or where the stop /p/ in _pisces_ becomes its fricative counterpart /f/ in _fish_. Sharp sounds, this way, get softer with time. But not all stops undergo this change. Only voiceless stops. So a /p/ becomes a /f/, but a /b/ doesn’t become a /v/.

Along similar lines, comes the second shift, voiced to voiceless. Making that hum in the throat requires efforts. So there’s a tendency to skip it altogether. That’s how we get a voiceless /t/ from its voiced counterpart /d/ in the shift from Latin _duo_ to English _two_. Again, there’s a caveat. Not all voiced consonants undergo this change, only voiced stops. They become voiceless stops. So a /b/ becomes a /p/, but a /v/ does not become a /f/. 

The loss of aspiration, Grimm’s third shift, also demonstrates a similar tendency to minimize efforts in articulation. To not aspirate is easier than to aspirate. Which is why Sanskrit _bhrāta_ sounds so similar to English _brother_. And which is also how we know that Sanskrit is older than English, because the shift is more organic in the aspirated-to-unaspirated direction, that is /bʰ/ > /b/, than in the opposite. This again, only applies to voiced aspirates and not voiceless.

In simpler terms, sharp sounds lose their sharpness, buzzy sounds lose their buzz, and breathy sounds lose their breath. It’s a “law” because these are not just a few chance occurrences but a whole pattern observed across thousands of examples. And this is just one of several such laws that come together to give the final picture.

If we understand how languages evolve over time, we should also be able to reconstruct their earlier forms by reversing those changes. This approach mirrors the successful methods used in biological evolution, where ancestral forms are inferred from modern species. With a good enough corpus of such patterns, we should be able to reconstruct a current language’s lost ancestor or a current dialect’s archaic form with reasonable accuracy. But how do we determine the accuracy of a register thus reconstructed? Isn’t it all mere speculation? Enter Ferdinand de Saussure and his theory of laryngeals.

## A Lost Consonant

Consider the English noun “song.” This noun has a corresponding verb, “sing.” This verb, in turn, has two other inflected forms depending on the tense, “sang” and “sung.” Just changing one vowel changes the word. And these are all related words, not just random picks. Almost as if a single consonantal template, _s-ng_ in this case, were used with multiple plug-and-play vowels for the desired effect. In linguistics, this is called an _ablaut_. A systematic vowel alternation within related words that changes meaning or grammatical function. The feature was first codified by Panini, the earliest known grammarian in all of Indo-European language family, more than 2,500 years ago.

Three kinds of ablauts have been registered in the entire Indo-European family. We call them grades, depending on the vowel—full grade, zero grade, and lengthened grade. To understand, let’s take three Greek examples, _pélomai_ (to become), _pólos_ (axis), and _épleto_ (became). The template here is _p-l_. With an _e_ , we get the full-grade or e-grade pélomai. With _o_ , we get the lengthened-grade or o-grade pólos, and with no vowel, we get the zero-grade épleto.

However, not all roots follow this neatly. Some show unexpected long vowels or unusual shifts, which are difficult to explain using traditional ablaut theory alone. A 22-year-old Swiss linguist noted this anomaly in 1879 and proposed what we now call the Laryngeal Theory. His name was Ferdinand de Saussure.

De Saussure theorized that these irregularities came from some mysterious PIE consonants that went extinct before modern PIE daughters were born. He suggested that these consonants, which he believed were articulated in the larynx and thus termed “laryngeals,” influenced surrounding vowels in ways that left lasting traces, even after the consonants themselves disappeared. Three such laryngeals were hypothesized in his paper. We now designate them _h₁_ , _h₂_ , and _h₃_. The hypothesis offered a groundbreaking explanation for the vocalic shifts that traditional ablaut theory alone could not fully account for.

For instance, the presence of _h₂_ was thought to color an adjacent _*e_ into an _*a_ , while _h₃_ could turn it into an _*o_ , and _h₁_ might leave it unchanged but affect vowel length. When these consonants dropped out of pronunciation in the evolution of PIE into its daughter languages, their vocalic effects lingered, creating patterns that defied the neat framework of full, zero, and lengthened grades. This hypothesis not only accounted for the anomalies in Greek forms like pélomai, pólos, and épleto, but also shed light on similar irregularities in Sanskrit and other branches, laying the foundation for a deeper understanding of PIE phonology that continues to shape linguistic scholarship today.

For a more relatable illustration, take the English word “night.” Back in Anglo-Saxon days, the word was _niht_ , pronounced with the same /x/ sound that we still find in German _nacht_ or Scottish _loch_. The vowel was a simple /i/. In Middle English, the word morphed to _nyght_ but retained the same pronunciation. Today, we have “night” with a diphthongal /aɪ/ instead of /i/ for vowel, thanks to the Great Vowel Shift of sixteenth century. Now, imagine a couple of centuries from now, a new spelling upgrade decides to drop the silent “gh” and spell the word as “nit.” Such spelling changes happen all the time. Remember when Webster decided to drop the unnecessary “u” from words like “colour” and “favour”? So now you’re spelling the word as “nit” but still pronouncing it as /naɪt/. A future linguist then might wonder why a seemingly straightforward vowel is being pronounced as a diphthong. As if, some invisible consonant has altered the character of the “i” in “nit,” and this consonant never existed in words like “bit” and “sit.” What could that invisible consonant be? A study of similar words like “might,” “bright,” and others, could then reveal a pattern. This is somewhat how the idea of laryngeal works.

But isn’t this a convenient invention to prove PIE rather than a discovery borne out by evidence? Who’s to tell these consonants really existed in PIE if PIE itself is an unattested construct? We need something more empirical. Regrettably, de Saussure could never validate his hypothesis with concrete proof during his lifetime. But less than a decade after his passing, a startling breakthrough sent ripples through the field of historical linguistics, offering unexpected support for his ideas.

## Discovery of Hittite

Linguists and archaeologists had already been studying the Anatolian language family for almost half a century when de Saussure published his controversial paper. At the time, this was considered an isolated family with no relationship to the rest of the Indo-European tree. In the last quarter of the century, a collection of cuneiform tablets was discovered from the town of Amarna in Upper Egypt. Initially sold in the local antiquities market, the tablets eventually came in the possession of researchers who identified them as letters from Canaan and other neighboring kingdoms to the Pharaoh. They named this collection Amarna Letters.

In 1902, Norwegian linguist Jørgen Alexander Knudtzon published an argument that the language of at least two of these letters shared morphological affinities with the PIE family. A few years later, these observations found further support when German orientalist Hugo Winckler discovered a number of tablets in the same language in Turkey. Czech orientalist Bedřich Hrozný studied these artifacts and affirmed them to be Indo-European in nature in a 1915 paper. This was Hittite, today understood to be the oldest descendant of the PIE ancestor. As the language was further deciphered, a stunning discovery was made—the laryngeals. Two were identified. The more ubiquitous of the two was h₂, and it was found in the exact same spots, between the exact same kind of vowels, as was predicted by de Saussure’s hypothesis. Traces of h₃ were found too. This was the first time a linguistic speculation, albeit educated, was demonstrated to be valid by a real-world illustration. The extrapolation was already logical, now it was empirical too.

These laryngeals do not exist in any surviving Indo-European language. Which is precisely why experts have suggested that the Anatolian branch was the first to split from the PIE ancestor. Well, there’s other reasons too, but this one’s the strongest. If we have such smoking-gun corroboration for an extinct ancestor, why is there so much discomfort accepting it? Why are so many, both inside and outside academic circles, find it hard to admit even in the face of mounting evidence? The theory is logical, demonstrable, and demands the fewest of assumptions. Hittite isn’t the only corroboration, as we have seen, there’s patterns to be seen even in languages that are very much alive. Then what’s the problem? Of course, it’s political. Let’s examine each objection individually and weigh it against available evidence to see if it holds water.

## PIE Homeland

Although not part of this conversation, the homeland argument forms the ultimate leitmotif of all objections to the PIE hypothesis. When Subhash Kak published a blog post on his objections to the idea in 2018, the homeland argument was his opening salvo. He challenged the traditional view which holds that the homeland of this ancient language was located somewhere in Europe, north of the Caucasus Mountains. This theory assumes that PIE was spoken thousands of years ago, well before the earliest historical records. Kak argues that new evidence calls this traditional understanding into question.

The first part of Kak’s argument revolves around the dating of the earliest records of any Indo-European language, which are found in Sanskrit. The Vedic texts, including the Rigveda, are considered the oldest known examples of the Indo-European family. These texts are conservatively dated to around 2000 BC. However, Kak notes that recent research into the astronomical references in the Vedic literature suggests these texts could be much older, possibly even dating back to 2500 BC or earlier. These astronomical calculations have only recently been properly understood, which strengthens the argument for an even older Vedic culture.

In addition to the Vedic texts, Kak also discusses the Sarasvati River, which is highly significant in the Rigveda. The river is described as flowing from the mountains to the sea, symbolizing an important cultural and geographical feature. Archaeological evidence indicates that the Sarasvati River dried up around 2000 BC, which would align with the time frame of the Vedic texts. If the Vedas were composed when the river was still flowing, then the language and culture described in these texts must be at least as old as the river itself. This contradicts the idea that Indo-European languages arrived in India much later from an outside source, particularly from the northern regions.

Kak also highlights the continuity of culture and traditions in ancient India. If Indo-European languages had come from an external homeland, there should be signs of cultural disruption or replacement. Instead, the evidence shows a deep continuity in the arts, traditions, and customs. This suggests that the development of the Vedic culture was organic, rather than the result of a recent migration from another region.

The most significant part of Kak’s argument is based on recent genetic research. Scientists studied the DNA of 45 ancient human remains from the Caucasus region, dating from around 2500 BC to 1200 BC. The genetic analysis revealed that the people in this area primarily moved from south to north, contradicting the earlier theory that Indo-European languages spread from north to south. This finding suggests that the Indo-European languages may have originated south of the Caucasus, and their spread into Europe was the result of migrations from the south.

Kak argues that if the Indo-European languages in Europe are much younger than previously assumed and their origin lies south of the Caucasus, then the entire concept of PIE is flawed. There would be no need for a hypothesized parent language, and the idea of a unified PIE-speaking community becomes untenable. Instead of piecing together an ancient language from hundreds of years before the first records, it may be more accurate to see the Indo-European languages as emerging over time in different regions, without the need for a single, unified origin.

While Kak raises interesting challenges here, his arguments contain several fundamental misconceptions that conflict with established evidence. Let’s start with his dating of Sanskrit. Which ties into his dating of the Rigveda. The astronomical dating method alluded to here remains highly controversial and far from gaining mainstream consensus. And not without reason. Two main issues hurt his Sarasvati argument—identity of the river and its role in Rigveda’s dating. Part of this argument hinges on the identification of Sarasvati with the Ghaggar-Hakra River. This is something that hasn’t been established yet. In fact, identifying Sarasvati with this river system comes with problems of both hydrology as well as pedology. Another problem with this argument is that it doesn’t hold up even if Sarasvati _were_ identified with Ghaggar-Hakra. Sarasvati being in Haryana does not necessarily preclude a later composition of the Rigveda. A far more detailed discussion on the subject can be found in an older article on Aryans.

## Independent Origin

The alternative Kak proposes, as do some even if not all Indian nationalists, to PIE is the idea that all Indo-European languages originated independently without the need for a common ancestor. The lexical and phonological similarities could be explained away by trade-driven cross-pollination. In other words, convergence and not descent. Which makes sense because that’s how we have a large corpus of Sanskrit words in Dravidian languages which have an entirely non-Sanskritic lineage. Same with Persian and Arabic which, despite their disjoint ancestries, share a number of words and quirks thanks to centuries of coexistence. Convergence is also how we get language composites called creoles and pidgins. But with PIE, it isn’t that simple.

The idea of PIE hinges not merely on lexical or phonological similarities but a whole host of syntactical similarities too. Convergence can only account for vocabulary or at best pronunciation, not grammar. PIE has been imagined as a highly inflected fusional language with complex systems of declension and conjugation. These features are preserved in varying degrees across Indo-European languages. For example, PIE’s case system (nominative, accusative, etc.) and verbal conjugations are mirrored in Sanskrit, Greek, Latin, and other Indo-European languages. The retention of such intricate grammatical structures across diverse branches cannot be plausibly attributed to convergence.

Distance too makes such cross-pollination extremely unlikely. For languages as far apart as Irish, Latin, and Sanskrit to develop a similar case structure, they must have been spoken in territories with shared borders. But they weren’t. There’s zero geographical congruence among the three. Take for instance, the word “father.” The word in Sanskrit is _pitṛ́_ and in Latin, _pater_. It’s quite possible for pitṛ́ to enter Europe through mercantile contacts and evolve into pater, or vice versa. By the way, there’s zero evidence of such contacts between India and Europe in the Bronze or even Iron Age. Let’s not bother with the question of the original Latin word for father before the advent of pitṛ́ in this example. So pitṛ́ becomes pater. Now consider the following statement:

“I visit father.”

Here, I am the subject, and father is the object, with the verb “visit” separating the two. This is how we say it in Sanskrit:

_Ahaṁ pitaraṁ milāmi._

And this is how we do it in Latin:

_Ego patrem visito._

People borrow words, not inflections or any other aspect of grammar. How did the word for father end up getting the same case-ending in two unrelated languages? _pitaraṁ_ and _patrem_ —what are the odds? In fact, why must there even be cases in the first place? Not all languages have them. But all Indo-European languages do. The simplest explanation remains that of a common origin.

Additionally, computational phylogenetic analyses estimate that Indo-European languages diverged from a single root around 8100 years ago, further supporting the notion of a unified proto language.12 To add to it the existence of other well-documented language families (e.g., Uralic, Afro-Asiatic) reinforces the validity of genealogical relationships among languages. These families exhibit similar patterns of systematic sound correspondences and shared grammatical features as IE languages.3 Rejecting PIE would require rejecting all language family classifications—a position untenable given the overwhelming evidence for genealogical relationships among global languages. In sum, while convergence between languages does occur, it cannot account for the depth and systematicity of the relationships within the Indo-European family.

## Impossible Endeavor

Many archaeologists, and even some linguists, do not believe it is possible to reliably reconstruct any portion of the PIE lexicon and do not accept the reconstructed vocabulary as real. This skepticism removes a valuable tool for tracing PIE origins. Some argue PIE was merely a linguistic hypothesis never actually spoken. But that’s just not true. Linguistic reconstruction isn’t arbitrary guesswork. It relies on the comparative method, which identifies systematic sound correspondences across related languages, and internal reconstruction, which analyzes patterns within a single language to infer earlier forms.

Besides semantics and phonology, there’s a whole cultural dimension to this reconstruction. The PIE lexicon isn’t a collection of isolated words. It reflects a coherent cultural context. Reconstructed terms related to agriculture (_h₂érk̑trom_ , “plow”), animal husbandry (_h₁éḱwos_ , “horse”), and social organization (_ph₂tḗr_ , “father”) paint a picture of PIE speakers as pastoralists and early agriculturalists. These cultural implications align with anthropological evidence suggesting PIE origins in the Pontic-Caspian steppe region during the late Neolithic and Bronze Age.

Archaeological findings often corroborate linguistic reconstructions, providing independent evidence for the material culture and lifestyle of PIE speakers. The reconstructed PIE vocabulary for wheeled vehicles (_kwekwlo-_) is consistent with the archaeological record showing the emergence of wheeled transport in the Pontic-Caspian steppe around 4000 BC4 and chariots around 2000 BC.5 David Anthony’s work highlights how archaeological evidence supports the linguistic reconstruction of PIE culture, enhancing the credibility of both disciplines.

Further support for this linguistic dispersal also comes from archaeogenetics. Ancient DNA from Yamnaya graves in the Pontic-Caspian steppe shows genetic markers that later appear in the rest of Europe, coinciding with the proposed dispersal of IE languages.6 This genetic evidence provides independent support for the linguistic and archaeological reconstructions of PIE origins and migrations.

A real language lies behind reconstructed PIE, just as a real language lies behind any dictionary. A dictionary contains words that appeared at different times, yet it represents a real language with a history. At least 1.500 unique PIE roots have been reconstructed, forming the basis for a much larger vocabulary. While borrowing is a factor affecting specific reconstructed roots, it does not invalidate the usefulness of a large, systematically reconstructed vocabulary. The internal coherence of reconstructed PIE, with regular patterns in grammar and phonology, indicates that it was a real language, not merely a theoretical construct. Even if the reconstructed PIE lexicon is seen as a hypothesis, it’s a testable one as we’ve already seen in the case of Hittite. New linguistic, archaeological, or genetic discoveries can either support or challenge specific reconstructions.

## Was It Sanskrit?

The Indocentric theory posits that PIE originated in the Indian subcontinent, specifically with the Indus Valley Civilization (IVC), and that Sanskrit served as the original proto language. After all, who’s to say Sanskrit evolved out of some obscure European tongue and not the other way around? How do we, for instance, know that pitṛ́ and pater both evolved from a speculated *ph₂tḗr? Why couldn’t pater have evolved from pitṛ́ instead, obviating the need for a speculative proto vocabulary altogether? Occam’s razor says that the simplest solution, the one that warrants the fewest assumptions, is often the right solution. So why can’t Sanskrit be that PIE ancestor?

While the proposition seems logically sound at first, it quickly falls apart in the face of the very concepts we have discussed so far. Let’s start with the centum-satem split. We know that voiceless stops tend toward voiceless fricatives, i.e. from a place of more effort to that of less effort, as per Grimm’s Law. An extension thereof is the “palatovelar shift” which suggests that sounds made with the tongue against the back of the palate, such as /*ḱ/, /*ǵ/, etc., eventually morph into simpler corresponding sounds, either plain velars, like /k/, /g/, etc., or plain sibilants, such as /s/, /z/, etc. This shift has a name in linguistics—Weise’s law. The movement, again, is from complex to simple. It’s this rule that led to the centum-satem split where some PIE descendants articulate the word for hundred with a /k/ sound (Greek _ekató_) and others with an /s/ sound (Avestan _satam_).

This is how we know Sanskrit could not be the proto language. The flow is from palatovelar to velar or sibilant. Sanskrit being a satem register is clearly in the latter group. A sibilant is extremely unlikely to evolve into a velar if existing languages and their phonological relationships are anything to go by. Remember, Latin with its centum (hard /k/ sound) came _before_ Spanish with its ciento (soft /s/ sound), and not the other way around. That’s also how we know that Kaiser with the hard /k/ became Tsar and Caesar with a soft /ts/ and a soft /s/ respectively, and not the other way around.

Another piece that hurts Sanskrit’s case is the laryngeals. We know that these consonants, three to be precise, existed in the ancestor but disappeared over time. Only Hittite shows remnants of two, as we’ve seen already. But Sanskrit, like all other descendants currently in use, there’s none. Of course, there’s also a whole body of genetic, archaeological, and anthropological arguments that further weaken the case for Sanskrit, but we won’t repeat them here. The article on Aryan homeland discusses them in great detail. The Indocentric theory, while appealing to some, lacks robust support from linguistic, archaeological, and genetic evidence. The weight of evidence favors the Pontic-Caspian steppe as the PIE homeland, with migrations spreading Indo-European languages across Eurasia. While Sanskrit is undoubtedly an important ancient language, it is not the proto language from which all other Indo-European languages descend. The convergence of evidence supports the mainstream theory of PIE origins and dispersal.

## South to North?

The most interesting of Kak’s arguments against PIE is that the theory is “dead” because the speakers of this language moved from the south of the Caucasus to its north, and not the other way around, as early as 2500 BC. Since Pontic-Caspian steppe lies to the north of the Caucasus, and the speakers arrived there from the south, PIE must have a southern origin. But since this movement occurred around 2500 BC and the oldest of European languages have only been dated to 2000 BC, then any proposed timeframe for the existence and development of PIE becomes too short to account for the linguistic diversity we observe today. In other words, there wouldn’t be enough time for a single parent language to evolve into the various distinct Indo-European languages found across Europe and Asia.

Great argument, but it works on two fundamental assumptions—that PIE originated in the south of the Caucasus in 2500 BC, and that its European descendants appeared already by 2000 BC. The movement bit comes from a 2018 preprint7 that later got peer-reviewed and published in a 2019 issue of _Nature_.8

There are, however, some serious issues with Kak’s reading of these papers. For starters, the paper does not assert that the speakers of the primordial PIE tongue originated in the south of the Caucasus. In fact, it starts off with the assertion that the Caucasus acted not as a barrier but as a bridge to population movements. The paper further clarifies, in page 11, that the Yamnaya gene pool received the majority of its composition from the Chalcolithic steppe populations and a good 16% from the farmers of the Globular Amphora culture of Central Europe.9 The paper does not support a direct southern origin (i.e., south of the Greater Caucasus Range) for the Yamnaya. Instead, it indicates that the steppe ancestry profile originated in the steppe zone north of the Caucasus, with contributions from Caucasian Hunter Gatherer or CHG ancestry likely filtering in from the south over time.

What the paper does suggest is a subtle gene flow from the south (Anatolian farmer population) into later steppe groups, including some Yamnaya individuals, around 20 generations (approximately 560 years) before 3100-3000 BC.10 Reading this as a southern origin for PIE is a considerable overreach on Kak’s part.

The peer-reviewed version from 2019 further crystallizes our understanding with the unambiguous assertion that while the genetic similarity between the groups to the north of the Caucasus and the those to its south prove human movement across the mountains in the Bronze Age, there is still a genetic separation between the populations of the steppe and the Caucasus.11

Having said that, the Chalcolithic gene flow from the south is enough to entertain the possibility that the original PIE homeland could lie to the south of the mountains. This model could also explain the early divergence of the Anatolian languages such as Hittite. This is what the 2019 paper suggests in page 10, which seems to support Kak’s conclusions.12 But the same page, the same paragraph, also adds that this southern hypothesis must contend with the substantial genetic and archaeological evidence tying the later spread of PIE branches to the steppe populations north of the Caucasus. The well-documented steppe ancestry in European and Indian populations, coupled with the timing of pastoralist expansions during the Late Middle Bronze Age, suggests that even if PIE originated in the south of the Caucasus, its westward and eastward dispersal could not have happened without significant mediation through the Pontic-Caspian steppe, complicating a purely southern origin model.13 But there’s more if we step outside this paper.

A more recent paper by a team of Japanese researchers, from 2022, takes the PIE homeland back to the Pontic-Caspian steppe as initially thought. It divides the development of the primordial tongue into an early phase characterized by the Sredny Stog Culture before 3500 BC, and a later phase characterized by the Yamnaya Culture after 3500 BC.

The Sredny Stog Culture saw hunter-gatherers in the steppe region east of the Dnieper River transition into a semi-nomadic, agro-pastoral society after contact with the Cucuteni-Trypillia culture around 5200 BC.14 Adopting rudimentary farming and cattle herding, they marked the genesis of PIE languages with terms like “harrow” and “wheat.”15 This semi-sedentary culture matured the steppe economy, built social networks, and linked with “Old Europe,” setting the stage for further evolution.16

The Yamnaya Culture represented evolution into a fully nomadic paradigm, especially with the introduction of the horse and related innovations like wheel and wagon.17 The economy shifted from semi-agrarian to commercial with animal husbandry taking the centerstage. This is the phase that saw PIE expand to distant lands in all directions, including toward Europe and Central Asia and beyond.18

In page 47, this paper reaffirms the earlier paper’s findings of a south-to-north movement in the Chalcolithic Age, but not in the same context. We know the steppe people tamed the horse in the second millennium BC which helped large-scale migrations. But how did they build wagons if they did not invent the wheel? Where did wheels come from then? Mesopotamia? It has long been known that wheel was developed in the Fertile Crescent. But how did it reach the steppe? This is what the paper answers in page 47. The period between 3700 BC and 3500 BC was a period of intense trade between the steppe nomads in the north and the Mesopotamian cities in the south.19 The Caucasians were animal herders and were known for their cattle and sheep. The Mesopotamians had wheel and farm equipment. A trade was inevitable. The farmers received wool from the nomads and the nomads received the wheel from the farmers.20 The Caucasians in question are the people of the Maykop culture that thrived in the southern foothills of the Caucasus. While distinct from the Yamnaya of the north, the Maykop people engaged in much trade across the Caucasus as well, which is how the Yamnaya came in possession of the wheel.

In sum, Kak’s assertions that a south-to-north movement obviates the existence of a primordial PIE tongue more than 5,000 years ago does not hold water. Even if it were to establish a PIE homeland in southern Caucasus, which it anyway doesn’t.

## Conclusion

The debate over Proto-Indo-European (PIE) as a historical language reveals a tension between rigorous scholarship and skepticism rooted in cultural and political concerns. Linguistic evidence, from the comparative method to sound laws like Grimm’s Law and the laryngeal theory, supports PIE as a real, spoken ancestor of the Indo-European family. The discovery of Hittite, with its laryngeals matching Ferdinand de Saussure’s predictions, provides empirical weight to these reconstructions, while systematic patterns—such as the centum-satem split and shared grammatical structures—defy explanations like convergence or independent origin. Archaeological and genetic data further bolster this view, linking PIE to the Pontic-Caspian steppe around 4500–3500 BC, with migrations spreading its daughters across Eurasia. Terms like _*kwekwlo-_ align with material evidence of steppe innovations, painting a coherent picture of PIE speakers as pastoralists and early agriculturalists.

Critics, particularly from India, challenge PIE’s validity, questioning its steppe origin and suggesting alternatives like a Sanskrit-centric model or no unified ancestor at all. These arguments, while raising valid points about colonial biases and dating uncertainties, falter under scrutiny. The Rigveda’s timeline, even if pushed to 2000 BC, aligns with PIE’s dispersal, not its origin, and genetic studies contradict a south-to-north linguistic flow overturning the steppe hypothesis. Sanskrit, as a satem language lacking laryngeals, cannot be the proto-language; it’s a descendant, not the source. Convergence fails to explain deep grammatical parallels across vast distances, leaving descent from a common root as the simplest, most evidence-backed conclusion.

PIE remains a hypothesis, but one grounded in testable, interdisciplinary evidence. It’s not an unprovable abstraction but a window into prehistory, refined by each new discovery. While debate persists, the weight of linguistic, archaeological, and genetic data affirms PIE’s status as a once-living language, bridging ancient cultures across continents.
