# The Kautilya Conundrum

*A Deep Dive into the Most Contested Legacy in India’s Political History*

---

Arthashastra must be one of the most polarizing, most influential treatises on statecraft ever produced by man. More than its contents, what makes it so polarizing is its author. Variously called Chankya, Kautilya, and Vishnugupta, this individual’s identity and existence has been fiercely contested in both academic and political circles for years now. As for his identity, the whole resentment boils down to his caste. Historically, Buddhism and Hinduism have been two religions. While things were fuzzy and lines blurry through antiquity, with even a bit of a shared pantheon, the separation has only grown rigid with time, and almost throughout the common era, the two have remained pretty much distinct. In the modern era, however, thanks to Ambedkar, a peculiar new equation has taken shape, and this is unique to India. Buddhism has now become associated with a certain section of Hindus called _Dalits_. Thus, for all practical purposes, Hindu vs. Buddhist has become analogous to Brahmin vs. Dalit. But what does that have to do with Chanakya?

In Buddhist tradition, some Indian rulers hold a special esteem for their enthusiastic patronage of the creed. Among them are the likes of Kanishka and Menander, both non-Indian. But the one that arguably holds the highest esteem remains an Indian—Ashoka. The reasons are too obvious for us to even explore here. Ashoka belonged to a dynasty of rulers called the Mauryas. Now this is where the seeds of the controversy lie. It’s unanimously agreed that Ashoka converted to Buddhism. Before or after Kalinga is a conversation we won’t get into here but convert he did. Then the question is, from what? Some claim the Mauryas were Shudra or what we now include among Dalits. Others claim they were Kshatriya. Again, that’s not a debate we’re going to settle here, this is just to lay out the context. The Dalits want them to be Dalit, the non-Dalits want them to be Kshatriya, it’s pure identitarianism at play. Be that as it may, the founder of the dynasty was Chandragupta, and he is said to be a protégé of Chanakya. And traditions identify Chanakya as a Brahmin. This is where the controversy truly takes its final form.

If Chanakya was a Brahmin who mentored the founder of the Mauryans, it’d indirectly make the single most celebrated king of the Buddhist tradition the product of a Brahmin’s tutelage. Of course, that’s unacceptable to Dalit-Buddhist ideologues because it makes a Brahmin seem superior and more influential than a Buddhist dynasty. Therefore, it’s in their interest to refute his existence altogether and present Chandragupta as more of a self-starter. Conversely, caste Hindus see it as cultural capitulation and refuse to deny what to them is an irrefutable historical reality just to appease Dalit sentiments. But historical truth is indifferent to this ideological battle. What is it? Was Chanakya really a modern Brahmin invention to establish ideological leg-up over Buddhists? Or did he really exist, and his denialism is just a product of political bitterness? That’s what we’ll explore here.

## Jain Sources

Ironically, some of the most unambiguous, longstanding corroborations of Chanakya’s existence come not from Hindu sources but from Buddhist and Jain canons. All major sources present Chanakya as a Brahmin kingmaker, with Buddhist or Jain leanings depending on the account in question, who identified Chandragupta and groomed him for kingship, then used him to topple the ruling Nanda dynasty to avenge an old insult. Chandragupta once placed on the Magadhan throne, Chanakya would go on to serve as the royal advisor to both him and his son Bindusara.

The latest such story comes from a twelfth century Sanskrit Jain poem called _Parishishtaparvan_ or _Sthaviravalicharitra_. We will discuss the Buddhist sources a little later. Parishishtaparvan is a work of history that covers Magadha through the Haryankas, the Nandas, and finally the Mauryas. The reason Hemachandra wrote this extensively about Magadha is because the Mauryans weren’t exclusively Hindu or Buddhist, there were some Jains too. Ashoka’s grandson Samprati is one example. Even Chandragupta is said to have ultimately renounced his worldly affairs and turned to Jainism in the lead-up to his death in Digambara traditions. Therefore, Jain chroniclers had good reasons to write about this dynasty. And given his integral association with its founder, Chanakya is bound to find mentions therein.

So what does Hemachandra say? That Chanakya was born to a Brahmin named Chanin—also a devout Jain—in a village in the Golla district called Chanaka, which is how he gets his name.1 Scholars unanimously locate this district in South India which makes Chanakya a South Indian native. This account also makes Chanakya a practicing Jain, but those are unimportant details in this conversation. The only detail of import is that Chanakya lived.

Parishishtaparvan itself forms part of a much larger treatise called _Triṣaṣṭi-Śālākāpuruṣacarita_ , or “The Lives of the Sixty-three Illustrious People.” But since this work comes more than 1,500 years after Chanakya, it’s difficult to accept it as historically accurate. One counter to that, albeit not sufficient, comes from Hermann Jacobi, the man who translated Hemachandra’s work into English. In a footnote in his translation, he claims that Hemachandra himself sources the story of Chanakya’s birth from a much older Svetambara work called _Avashyakaniryukti_.2 Although there isn’t unanimity on its authorship, many accept it to be the Jain sage Bhadrabahu whom conventional dating places in the second half of the fourth century BC. If accurate, this would lend a great deal of credence to Chanakya’s existence if not all else claimed about him in Hemachandra’s account. But given the mist over Avashyakaniryukti’s authorship, we can at best treat Hemachandra as one piece of the dossier rather than evidence in and of itself.

Before we end this section, there’s one more Jain source worth a mention. _Nitikavyamrita_ is a Sanskrit treatise on ethics and statecraft authored by the tenth century Digambar monk Somadeva Suri. In this work, Chanakya makes a fleeting appearance in the “Discourse on Envoys” section where the author speaks of how a king ought to conduct before foreign envoys and also protect himself from a potential act of malice. To ensure the latter, says the author, the king must always have his warriors about him whenever meeting envoys from foreign lands. As an illustration of why this is crucial, Somadeva speaks of Chanakya having used a female envoy with poison to murder the Nanda king. We already know Chanakya had a beef with this king owing to an old insult, so the story checks out on that front. But the problem again remains the same—it’s not primary. Chanakya lived, if he did, more than a thousand years before this work. In fact, Somadeva himself presents Chanakya as a character from oral traditions, i.e. hearsay. He does not mention any primary source, no inscription, no text from Mauryan times, no concrete chain of narration. He just says _śruyate hi kila_ , meaning “it is heard that.”3 Therefore, in isolation, this too doesn’t work. But it can absolutely join the dossier as a small tertiary corroboration in support of Chanakya’s existence.

## Vikramaditya V

About a hundred years after Somadeva lived Vikramaditya V, a Chalukya dynast from the Western or Kalyani line. Interestingly, although originally from the Pratihara country, more specifically Kannauj, Somadeva spent his later years in a Chalukya domain based in Vemulavada. Which was conquered by and annexed into the more powerful Kalyani realm shortly after Somadeva’s death.

Vikramaditya V was the third of his line and reigned for about seven unremarkable years. Nothing of note happened during this period. But it left inscriptions one of which is of relevance to this story. This one was found on a slab in a medieval temple in Karnataka’s Dharwad district by the English orientalist Lionel Barnett, who later reproduced, translated, and published his finding in Epigraphia Indica.4 The set consists of forty-six verses in total of which only the last few are in Sanskrit while the rest are all in Halegannada or Old Kannada.

The edict is dated 1010 AD and signed Maruloja who probably made it as a panegyric to his king. While the whole text is a praise for Vikramaditya’s and his bloodline’s greatness, of particular importance are lines 14 thru 24 where a large number of similes have been invoked to glorify the king’s superhuman personality— _Brihaspati_ to the _Kaliyuga_ , _garuda_ to the serpents, lion to the elephants, and so on. Among these similes is one that invokes Chanakya. But as Kautilya, not Chanakya. Kautilya and Chanakya are names of the same individual, Vishnugupta, although there’s a dispute on that too but we’ll deal with it later. The exact metaphor, which appears in line 16, says, “Kautilya to the crafty,”5 implying Vikramaditya’s superlative competence in statesmanship and tact.

Whether or not Chanakya truly existed, this inscription clearly establishes that at least in the tenth century, a character by that name was well recognized, either through oral traditions or scripture, as the ultimate word on governance and statecraft. Ultimate enough to be used as a metaphor. This isn’t the only inscription that names Chanakya. There are two more, both from centuries before Vikramaditya. But before we discuss them, a word on Banabhatta.

## Kadambari of Banabhatta

Harsha was a Hindu king that ruled the entirety of the Gangetic domain in the first half of the seventh century. He’s often called the last Hindu emperor in the Indian subcontinent. Banabhatta was his imperial poet and dramatist. Best known for his biography of Harsha, _Harshacharita_ , Banabhatta also authored much of a romantic work titled _Kadambari_ , generally held as one of the world’s first novels. Much of, because he died before he could finish it. Which his son did eventually. This way, the work is traditionally divided into two sections—Purvabhaga (section authored by Bana) and Uttarabhaga (section added by his son). All his works including Kadambari were in the Sanskrit language. We’re only concerned with Kadambari here. Because this is where Chanakya makes an appearance.

Here too, as in Vikramaditya’s edict, the name is Kautilya. What makes Kadambari special in the context at hand is that it’s the only text that’s expressly critical of Kautilya. The way he wrote about Kautilya, he clearly must have been a socialist of his time, fiercely contemptuous of Kautilya’s Machiavellian ways with both wealth and governance. This is what he writes according to a note by English civil servant John Faithfull Fleet in a 1915 translation of Arthashastra:67

    Is there anything that is righteous for those for whom the science of Kauṭilya, merciless in its precepts, rich in cruelty, is an authority; whose teachers are priests habitually hard-hearted with practice of witchcraft; to whom ministers, always inclined to deceive others, are councilors; whose desire is always for the goddess of wealth that has been cast away by thousands of kings; who are devoted to the application of destructive sciences; and to whom brothers, affectionate with natural cordial love, are fit victims to be murdered?

To paraphrase, he questions the morality of anyone who follows the precepts of Arthashastra, which he calls cruel and merciless. Those who follow Kautilya’s principles, in Bana’s views, are greedy, deceitful, destructive, and practitioners of black magic. So unabashed is he in his criticism that he ends the snippet with a murderous dog whistle against those people. This text can be seen as representative of the society Bana inhabited and the prevalence therein of the extreme contempt for Kautilya and, likely, Brahminism, for it’s highly unlikely a court poet would have written something this bold without imperial patronage.

This 1915 translation is something we’ll return to later in this article. It’s one of the strongest pieces of evidence in support of Chanakya and his work. Just put a pin on it for now as we loop back to Somadeva.

## Brihatkatha

More than a century before Bana lived Gunadhya. He lived in the Satvahana country in what’s today Maharashtra and is best known for his writing. He wrote an epic that is now lost to time and for it he’s often compared with Valmiki. This epic is titled _Brihatkatha_ or “the Great Narrative.” It is understood that Gunadhya was familiar with Chanakya and mentioned him in his work. Which makes Brihatkatha a very special piece of work because if true, it’d be the only work to do so in a non-mainstream language. While all other texts in our list are in languages like Sanskrit, Pali, Old Kannada, etc., this one is in a most esoteric Prakrit called Paishachi. _Pishacha_ was a class of demonic beings in Indic traditions, associated with cannibalism and general malevolence. Paishachi was their vernacular. Outside of mythology, the language was probably spoken by a fringe community outside the Hindu-Buddhist-Jain fold. It was considered exotic even in medieval times when poets like Dandin called it Bhutabhasha or “ghost language.”

So Gunadhya mentioned Chanakya in Brihatkatha. But Brihatkatha is nowhere to be found, it’s lost to time. Then how do we know it was ever written in the first place, let alone it mentioned Chanakya? Through references in later works. Several poets from the early medieval period have attested to this work in their writings. This includes Bana’s Kadambari and Dandin’s Kavyadarsha. But these are mere references, we need the whole text. Fortunately for us, two adaptations exist, both in Sanskrit. This is serendipitous because Paishachi is not a language we understand well today, it went extinct more than half a millennium ago. Both works date to the middle of the first millennium, at most a century apart, and both were authored by Shaivite scholars from Kashmir— _Kathasaritsagara_ by Somadeva (not Somadeva Suri of the Nitivakyamrita fame) and _Brihatkathamanjari_ by Kshemendra.

Kathasaritsagara is a collection of didactic legends meant to educate young students on ethics and character. One of the stories talks about a man named Sakatala who served the Nanda king as his minister. In this legend, the Nanda king has died naturally, and his body usurped by a malicious yogic. Aware of this mischief, Sakatala recruits a Brahmin named Chanakya to destroy this fake king, also referred to in the story as Yogananda.8

Kshemendra’s Brihatkathamanjari is the other adaptation of Gunadhya’s work where Chanakya makes an appearance.9 In both cases, it should be noted, the name of choice is Chanakya instead of Kautilya. In sum, if Chanakya is legendary, it’d seem the legend is at least as old as the third century, for that’s the most recent dating we have for Gunadhya, the earliest being first century BC. But it goes even further. For now, let’s explore yet another inscription.

## The Gudnapur Edict

In the spring of 1971, a partially buried pillar was discovered in the Gudnapur village of Karnataka. The site lies some sixty miles from the coastal town of Gokarna. Upon inspection, the pillar was found to bear inscriptions from the fifth century when the region was part of the Kadamba country. The edict was commissioned by King Ravivarman who reigned between 465 and 500 AD. The twenty-foot column lay on its side for decades before it was finally propped upright in 2015. A total of twenty-seven lines are inscribed on its face. These lines represent a poetry section and a prose section. The poetry section comprises thirty verses and takes up the first sixteen lines. The remaining lines cover the prose.

Much of the poetry section is a panegyrical genealogy of the Kadamba dynasty, a common feature of most such inscriptions from the time. The lineage as described here starts with a Brahmin named Virasharman, presented to us as the progenitor of the Kadamba line. The verses that follow discuss his successors, _viz_. Bandhushena, Mayuravarman, Kangavarman, and the rest. In verse 12, the name Mrigeshavarman makes an appearance. This is none other than Ravivarman’s father. Far more verses have been dedicated to this individual than any other. Verse 18 speaks of his political acumen and, as a testimony to his competence, tells us that the king had mastered not one but two apex treatises on statecraft—one by Subandhu and the other by Vishnugupta.10

This is the first time in our list that the name Vishnugupta makes an appearance. We will discuss the confusion and controversy around the three names later but for now, let’s just assume that Vishnugupta is Kautilya is Chanakya.

## Vishnu Purana

One of the eighteen _Mahapuranas_ mentions Chanakya by name. Dated to roughly around the fifth century, the Vishnu Purana, as the name suggests, centers around Vishnu as its primary object of devotion. Like all other Puranas, Vishnu Purana also chronicles the story of Magadha starting with the first Nanda ruler, Mahapadma.

In this work, Mahapadma is “predicted” to be born of a Shudra mother and an enemy of the Kshatriya class. In other words, the Nandas are a race of avaricious Shudra kings who would go on to rule the entire world with an absolute authority. He would have eight sons who, along with their progeny, would carry on the Nanda rule for a hundred years before the last nine would ultimately be rooted out by a Brahmin named Kautilya.11

Some historians have taken immense liberty with the dating of this work and have gone as far back as seventh century BC which would instantly establish Chanakya’s veracity without any further investigation. Unfortunately, though, that position does not hold up against closer scrutiny and any dating that places it in the pre-common era is now outside of scholarly consensus with only Ramchandra Dikshitar and Vincent Smith holding that view.12 We shall not get down that rabbit hole here because our objective is Chanakya, but anyone interested should consider reading the text itself. The clues are right there in the names of kings and kingdoms in its verses.

## Mahavamsha

Our next fifth century reference comes not from Hindu or secular sources but Buddhist. And not from India but Sri Lanka. A Buddhist monk named Mahanama composed an epic poem covering the history of the island right from its legendary origins to its second millennium conquest by the Cholas—the _Mahavamsha_. While not part of the Buddhist canon and not entirely factual on all counts, the Pali epic remains an important work in Buddhist and Lankan historiography held in high scholarly esteem to this day. Much of what we know of Sri Lanka’s antiquity comes from references in this work that were later confirmed by archeological and epigraphical findings. Therefore, anything that finds a mention here is significantly likely to be a historical reality, especially if corroborated by other sources.

Since Mahavamsha starts with Gautama Buddha himself and covers the Mauryan dynasty in meticulous details, it’s only reasonable to expect that it would cover Chanakya too. Although a mention of Chanakya here would not in and of itself constitute evidence of his existence, a lack thereof would certainly raise legitimate questions. Fortunately for Chanakya proponents, the mention exists, even if fleeting.

The name appears in the corrupted Pali form, Chanakka.13 Here, he’s a Brahmin who discovered Chandragupta and anointed him the ruler of _Jambudipa_ after having Dhanananda, the ninth and final Nanda dynast, slain.14 The story is in consonance with the prevailing mainstream narrative of Chanakya’s revenge. But, as we just noted, in and of itself the chronicle does not constitute irrefutable evidence. We need to examine the author’s sources. And that’s where we run into a problem. Mahavamsha is understood to be a recension of a slightly older work _Dipavamsha_. By slightly we mean about a century. And it does not mention Chanakya. It mentions Chandragupta—as Pali Chandagutta—but not Chanakya.

If Chanakya was so integral to Chandragupta’s story, shouldn’t he find at least a fleeting mention in a work that tells that story? Good question, but not without answer. Dipavamsa is a very niche chronicle. While Mahavamsa expands the narrative well beyond Dipavamsa’s scope, the latter confines itself to the history of Buddhism in Sri Lanka with some very light references to the goings-on in India and only where absolutely relevant to the context. Now, Chanakya was not a Buddhist himself, nor did he patronize Buddhism, so he wasn’t deemed necessary to the narrative. Chandragupta, on the other hand, finds mention but only as an ancestor of Ashoka who ruled for twenty-four years.15 We also cannot conclude the nonexistence of Chanakya based on Dipavamsa because the work does not mention the Nandas either, and we know for sure that they existed.

Having said that, the fact that Mahavamsa is the only Buddhist work to mention Chanakya by name remains a point of relevance to our investigation. Even though Dipavamsa has good reasons to exclude the man, the question of where the author of Mahavamsa learned of Chanakya remains unanswered. Also, even with these chronicles, we’re still more than seven centuries away from the man in question, so the search for primary corroboration continues.

## The Minister’s Signet

Still in the middle of the first millennium, we meet Vishakhadatta, a Sanskrit playwright whose exact year and place aren’t known with certainty. Most scholars place him in the court of Chandragupta II, the fifth and one of the most successful Gupta rulers known to us. Vishakhadatta is best known for two works, both political dramas. One of them is the famous _Mudrarakshasa_ or “the Minister’s signet.”

While largely a work of fiction with a decent number of creative liberties, Mudrarakshasa is held to bear a reasonable affinity to historical reality. The play narrates the story of Chandragupta and the lead-up to his coronation. Given the spotlight it throws on Chandragupta, it’s only natural that it’d also mention Chanakya. And sure it does, albeit as Kautilya. In fact, Chanakya appears in the very first act of the play16 and is listed as one of the key characters in the work’s _dramatis personae_. The role is prominent and positive.

Mudrarakshasa may not serve as an independent confirmation of Chanakya’s existence but can still join the dossier as one of the several smaller pieces in the puzzle. Depending on who we believe, the work can be dated to as far back as fourth century, but not more. On the other side of the spectrum, we get eighth century, which might be a stretch if we were to consider Vishakhadatta a contemporary of Chandragupta II or Vikramaditya. Be that as it may, this still establishes the presence of Chanakya as a kingmaker in the Indian psyche during late antiquity if not more. Whether this presence was as a historical figure or legendary, remains to be ascertained.

## The Essence of Statecraft

Somewhere between the third and seventh centuries emerged a guidebook on statesmanship and governance titled _Nitisara_ or the “Essence of Statecraft.” If this sounds reminiscent of Arthashastra, it’s probably because the work is based on Arthashastra. In other words, not only is Nitisara in the same genre as Arthashastra, it’s a derivative of Arthashastra. And that’s not something Nitisara’s author disagrees with. An ode to Chanakya appears in the very introduction to the work where he’s introduced as a man of letters, well versed in the Vedas, who came from an illustrious family of Brahmins, who rooted out the Nandas, and who placed Chandragupta on the throne.17 But instead of Chanakya or Kautilya, the name given is Vishnugupta.

Nitisara has a curious dating despite scholars placing it with confidence between the third and seventh centuries because its author Kamandaka—who also goes by Kamandaki or Kamandakeya—makes an appearance in a work as old as the Mahabharata. In Shantiparva, a collection of verses relates an interaction between a Bharata king named Angaristha and sage Kamandaka.18 The conversation is about vice and its consequences. If this Kamandaka is the author of Nitisara, as unanimously accepted by scholars like K. P. Jayaswal, we have a dating problem on our hand. We know for sure that the epics predate the common era by several centuries. Was Kamandaka, then, a contemporary of Chanakya?

Not necessarily. According to scholars like Winternitz, the epic was a work in flux and while the core could have been composed as early as the fourth century BC, it’s entirely possible that it acquired its final form more than half a millennium later in the third or even fourth century AD.19 Winternitz offers a whole host of points backing his position which we won’t go into here, but we do know for a fact that the Mahabharata we know today consists of the main body along with accretions from late antiquity. A most striking illustration comes from the section in Adi Parva that speaks of Kali Yuga and the arrival of Kalki which is understood to be a Gupta-era interpolation.20

Therefore, it’s entirely possible for the Kamandaka reference to be a later addition to the epic. Even then, however, Nitisara remains a solid attestation to Chanakya’s existence in public conscience as early as the third century if not earlier. Even though we’re still falling short of a primary source.

## Bajaur Collection

A four-hour drive from Peshawar, not far from the Afghan border, is the village of Miakili or Mian Killi. Somewhere in its vicinity stand the ruins of an ancient Buddhist monastery known among the locals as _mahal_. Toward the end of the twentieth century, someone made a discovery in these ruins. It was a bunch of folded birch bark scrolls with inscriptions in an extinct script once common in the region—Kharosthi. Before Ashoka invented his Brahmi, this right-to-left system was the script of choice for all Indic languages in northern India, both Sanskrit and vernaculars.

These scrolls are collectively called the Bajaur collection, after the district of the discovery spot. Experts have dated the artifact to between the first and second centuries AD, making it the oldest in our discussion so far. So what do these scrolls bear? A number of Buddhist and non-Buddhist works. These were probably copies made by the monks or scribes at the aforementioned monastery, a common academic practice those days in the absence of printing press and paper. Among the texts included in this set, on the reverse of fragment 9, is Chanakya’s Arthashastra.21

The Bajaur Arthashastra is a rare example of Sanskrit writing in the Kharosthi script. Sanskrit was rarely written down before the common era and even when it was, rarely ever in Kharosthi. What also adds to this specimen’s value is the fact that Kharosthi was only later modified to accommodate Sanskrit, just as Nastaliq was modified to accommodate Persian and later Urdu. This specimen is not in this modified Kharosthi, but in an older form. Which makes it the arguably the only specimen of its kind. With Bajaur Collection, we are now firmly and indisputably in the pre-medieval territory. Chanakya died in the third century BC. We are now just four centuries away.

## Panchatantra

Panchatantra is an all-too-familiar collection of didactic fables assorted into five books which is how it gets its name. Storytelling was a common pedagogical tool used in ancient India and these were composed to teach a bunch of princes who were averse to learning and education. The original composition has been lost to time, but recensions exist in almost all major languages in the world including Chinese, Persian, Greek, and several others. What we have today is back-translation from these recensions.

Panchatantra is said to be a dumbed-down version of Arthashastra aimed at kids and young adults who are not yet intellectually ready for the complexities of Chanakya’s work. The author, Vishnusharma, makes it clear in just as many words right in the introductory passage when he thanks Manu, Chanakya, and others, and confirms that his five _tantras_ are a distillation of knowledge acquired from studying the entirety of Arthashastra.22

Now dating the Panchatantra remains a tricky affair. While scholars like Patrick Olivelle place it confidently in the fourth century AD,23 others like Johannes Hertel go all the way back to 200 BC, adding that even at this point, “many of the stories were already ancient.”24 Now, whether we agree with 300 AD or with 200 BC, what remains outside dispute is Panchatantra’s clear corroboration of Chanakya. Is it sufficient, though? Alone, not at all. But as part of the entire set of evidence we have assembled so far? It just might approach that point. But let’s explore further because we are yet to see anything remotely primary.

## Sanchi Inscriptions

About six miles southwest of Vidisha—Bhilsa in older records—is Sanchi where on top of a hill stands the famous stupa understood to have been commissioned by Ashoka himself. What makes this a rich source of knowledge is the large volume of inscriptions on its many columns. These inscriptions record the myriad gifts and donations received by the monastery toward its upkeep over the years. Through these inscriptions we learn that such donations came not just from imperial sources but also common laypersons, such as mendicants, monks, students, teachers, and merchants. And one of them reads thus:

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _Arahakasa bhichhuno chanakaya dánam_

This records a donation from a mendicant monk named Chanakya. If we take the inscription to have been made at the time of Ashoka, when the structure was built, or even not too long thereafter, it will give us a reference to Chanakya that is as close to primary as it gets. The man is understood to have died in 283 BC, give or take. Ashoka reigned between 268 and 232 BC. Even though the reign doesn’t share any overlap with Chanakya’s life, the two men must have known each other because Ashoka was way past his teens when the preceptor died. All of this assuming, of course, that the dates hold as given.

Thus, the inscription at Sanchi establishes, at the very least, knowledge of a figure called Chanakya during Ashoka’s time. We could end the conversation here, but things are less than straightforward even now. The inscription at hand translates thus:

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
    Gift of _arhaka_ , the mendicant monk of Chanaká

Which names Chanakya as the donor, serving our theory that a man named thus was known during Ashokan times. But it can also be translated as:

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
    Gift of _Arhaka_ , the mendicant monk, and of Naká

The _ch_ in the inscription translates into “and” if rendered as an independent word. In which case, we are left with no Chanakya. Alexander Cunningham, the man who discovered and studied the inscriptions, leaves us both translations without establishing a preference. One could argue that it should be easy to ascertain by just noting word breaks in the original inscription, but the problem is there isn’t any. Back in the day, there was no concept of spaces between words. Or any punctuation for that matter. Everything was a single string of characters divided only into lines and nothing else. Interpretation was easy for the readers of the time, for they were fluent in context. For us, things are a tad more complicated and much of the interpretation is educated guesswork.

Another argument often advanced against Chanakya is his complete absence in the entire Ashokan corpus. No major or minor edict commissioned by the king mentions Chanakya by any name. If Chanakya was so integral to his dynasty’s roots and lived through the emperor’s entire childhood, shouldn’t he find at least one mention anywhere between Gandhara and Pataliputra? This sounds like a reasonable argument, but a counter could be that Chanakya being instrumental in the death of Ashoka’s mother as per legends, might not have enjoyed much of the emperor’s grace. Ashoka’s Buddhist proclivities could also have made him distance his legacy from a that of a Brahmin statesman. Putting the two together—Chanakya’s hand in the mother’s death and his Brahmin identity—one could see sufficient meaning in Ashoka’s irreverence for Chanakya.

## Arthashastra

Speaking of primary sources, we have only one—the writings of the man himself. Although not necessarily and not always, the existence of a work can be a good indicator of the existence of its author. But for the longest time, there was no Arthashastra outside of references in secondary and tertiary works from the late antiquity onward. No manuscript was found, neither of the original, nor of a recension. It was something everybody talked about from a multigenerational memory going back to a long-lost era. Naturally, then, questions on its existence persisted.

Until 1904, when a manuscript finally emerged. It was on a set of palm leaves brought to Dr. R. Shamasastry of the Mysore Government Oriental Library by unnamed monk from Tanjore. The text was in a form of Brahmi associated with South India, the Grantha script, and the language was Sanskrit with a vocabulary that wasn’t very familiar to the dictionaries of the time. Palm leaves have a shelf life of between three and seven centuries depending on the weather.25 That’s why copies are made periodically for preservation. The copy Shamasastry had could not be older than that. However, this was the first time we had a complete manuscript on hand making it possible to look for clues of its authorship and origin right in the source. At any rate, what we’re looking for isn’t a contemporary manuscript because nothing was being written down until after Ashoka, his edicts being the first written specimens on the Indian subcontinent. We may still find clues in the manuscript at hand with a careful examination of language, semantics, vocabulary, and context.

First, right off the bat, we come across 1.1.1 which states that the work in question is a distillation of several earlier works in the genre. Which establishes that Kautilya, assuming he existed, was less a creator and more the compiler, by his own admission. The compilation, however, grew so influential that it eclipsed all its predecessors in the manuscript tradition. Which is why whatever copies we have of Arthashastra today are of the Kautilyan recension and not its predecessors. From this point on, the work went through several minor redactions over the centuries.

One thing Arthashastra makes abundantly clear is that its author is Kautilya. Several of its chapters end with a note naming Kautilya as the author. Interestingly, the last chapter names Vishnugupta. This is the source of the notion that the two names belong to the same individual. One, however, may still wonder why drop two names in the same work anyway. To which there’s no easy answer. One theory attributes the anomaly to Shastric recensions, revisions made during the early centuries of the common era. In this theory, Kautilya is the name of the scribe who made the recension and Vishnugupta the original author, making them two distinct individuals separated by centuries. But this is just one theory.

Another is that the name was Vishnugupta, which is why it only appears in the closing note. In this view, Kautilya isn’t a name, but either an epithet ascribed to Vishnugupta in a Shastric recension or his _gotra_ or lineage.

A bigger question posed by deniers is the inclination of the author addressing himself in the third person. He calls the chapters “Kautilya’s chapters,” not “my chapters.” This leads one to suspect that the text was written by someone else at a later date and ascribed to Chanakya, Kautilya, or Vishnugupta for political reasons. In other words, the character was invented in medieval India. But that argument, while sound on the surface, does not hold in light of a more comprehensive investigation. The practice of identifying oneself in one’s own work in the third person was not exactly uncommon in Ancient India, case in point being Patanjali who references himself by the epithet Gonardiya in his seminal _Mahabhashya_.26 Patanjali too was a Mauryan-era writer albeit posterior to Chanakya’s purported dating. But none of this conclusively places the Arthashastra, and by extension Chanakya, in the fourth century BC. We need more.

That more comes from the treatise’ monetary system. We know money changes over time. Even in the modern era, we’ve gone from annas to paise and from mohurs to rupees. The definition too changes. If we examine the currency used in Arthashastra, we should be able to place it on the timeline with reasonable confidence. We find this information in 2.19, the chapter on weights and measures. It lists as currency things like _masha_ , _karsha_ , _dharaṇa_ , and organic materials like mustard and rice seeds.27 We know this coinage, _karsha_ in particular was also current in the mid-first century BC because the names appear in Panini’s _Ashtadhyayi_ too.28 But we see a change in Patanjali’s work.

By the time Mahabhashya came out, Magadha had added a new set of coins to its monetary system. In addition to karsha and masha, we also find dinar now. This information comes to us from the recensions of Katyayana as quoted in Devanabhatta’s _Smriti Chandrika_.29 Dinar, which is still a currency in many Arab countries, comes from Latin _denarius_ , which makes it a foreign injection into our land. While Romans never came to India, the Greeks did. The currency is understood to be a Bactrian influence because Bactria was the closest Greek domain to the Mauryan country at the time, especially the Punjab region.

But even this comes with problems. Patanjali himself never mentions dinar. Nor is there sign thereof in Katyayan’s _Vārttikakāra_. And Smriti Chandrika is a post-thirteenth century work. So there’s no way of telling if Devana Bhatta’s attribution of dinara to Katyayana really holds water. What we do know for sure is that Arthashastra is not aware of dinar, ergo, it was composed before the currency entered India. Then the question is, when would that be? Even if we dismiss the possibility of dinar being in circulation in Punjab during late Mauryan times through Bactrian influence, we do have a date we cannot dismiss—the arrival of the Kushans. They were the first to introduce gold coinage to the subcontinent’s currency (even though something called _suvarna masha_ was known even to Chanakya) and they called it dinar. This also sits well with denarius because under them India had a prosperous trade relation with Rome. Thus, denarius could have directly entered India without the need for a Greek intermediary and get repurposed as dinar. That the Kushans melted and restruck Roman coins for Indian circulation is a well-attested fact.30 And Vima Kadphises, the ruler who introduced dinar into India, reigned between 113 and 127 AD, which makes second century AD the _terminus ante quem_ for Arthashastra and Chanakya.

Furthermore, the denomination of masha had changed too. While it was one-sixteenth of a karsha during Panini, it had reduced to one-twentieth during Patanjali.31 Which further brings the lower limit to well before the common era and places Arthashastra between Panini and Patanjali. Panini is fifth century BC, give or take, Patanjali is second century BC. This tracks with the conventional placement of Kautilya in or around the third century BC. Had Chanakya lived and composed in the common era, he certainly would have known and mentioned the dinar. On the flip side, Chanakya mentions suvarna masha, a kind of gold currency, in Arthashastra which would bring him back to Kushan times. So, there are two possibilities:

Either Chanakya lived in the Kushan era, i.e. in the second century AD, or the Kushans were not the first to introduce gold coinage to India. The latter seems more probable and is easy to reconcile with in light of the brisk Roman trade. Gold was certainly a precious metal and even if present as currency, might not have been in much circulation before the Kushans. With the advent of the Kushans, Roman trade would have made gold more commonplace in routine circulation. This way we can keep Chanakya in the third century BC without violating any caveat.

Lastly, many also point to a complete absence of Chanakya in Greek records as evidence against him. That can easily be addressed with the fact that the only Greek whose visit to India overlapped with Chanakya was Megasthenes. He made extensive noted during his visit which he compiled as _Indica_. It’s true that this work does not mention Chanakya but it’s also true that a large part of this work is lost to us and what we currently have at our disposal is only a small fragment. To conclude anything off such a small subset would be incorrect.

In sum, there isn’t a whole lot of evidence to support Chanakya’s non-existence. At the same time, there isn’t a whole lot to confidently place him at one specific spot on the timeline either. What we have is an assortment of small, mostly secondary and tertiary corroborations that when pieced together give a less ambiguous picture. This picture places Chanakya and his work with reasonable certainty in the Mauryan era. To further conclude, the evidence against Chanakya is demonstrably weaker in comparison to that in his favor.
