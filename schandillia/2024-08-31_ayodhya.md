# Where Is Valmiki’s Ayodhya?

*A Scriptural, Archeological, Architectural, and Epigraphical Inquiry*

Sprawling megapolis, well-laid arterial network, royal highways, multistorey edifices, huge arched gateways, gem-studded mansions, lush gardens, neatly lined markets, deep moats, impregnable fortification, plentiful farms—this is how Ramayana describes Ayodhya, the birthplace of not only Rama but India’s first epic.1 The word megapolis is not used lightly here. The city, in Ami Ganatra’s picturesque elaboration, was nearly five times the size of today’s New York.2 Of course, enormous would be a gross understatement, but a far more remarkable takeaway is that an agglomeration of this size was at once beautiful and peaceful, superlative on both counts. A more utopian city has not existed in all of human history. You tend to prosper when you’re at peace. Strife does the opposite. And peace endures when a city cannot be invaded, as testified by the very etymology of this city. Ayodhya is literally the negation of _yodhya_ , Sanskrit for “conquerable.” This inspires awe. And questions.

A most immediate question once the awe tempers is, is it true? Unfortunately, that’s an extremely charged question and connotations can range from harmless skepticism to ideological violence, from prudish ignorance to flat-out Hinduphobia. So, let’s ask the same question a little differently. Ramayana is held as practically primordial, going as far back as 1500 BC by some reckoning. There are those who go even further, but this is the most commonly accepted dating. We won’t go into the scientific and archeological veracity of such a dating, least of all in matters of faith such as this one. But we can now rephrase our question with this vintage in mind. Is it possible for the city described in _Ayodhya Kanda_ to have existed in modern-day Uttar Pradesh 3,500 years ago?

Related to this question is another. We know one of Daśaratha’s wives was Kaikeyi, who came from the kingdom of Kekeya, a polity that lay between modern-day Punjab and ancient Gandhara. In fact, the Ramayana itself locates it bordered by the Beas River to the east and Gandhara to the west. And Kosala, as we all know, was in central Uttar Pradesh. And this raises the question, how probable was a marital alliance across such vast distances in those days? In other words, could a king this deep in the Gangetic heartland think of marrying a princess from a place as far as modern-day Afghanistan-Pakistan, skipping neighbors like Videha, Kashi, Magadha, Panchala, and even Kuru? How did they communicate?

These are no new questions. They had already been asked more than forty years ago by eminent subject matter experts like archeologist Hasmukh Dhirajlal Sankalia. These are not popular questions for obvious reasons, but they stand, nonetheless. This essay does not expect to answer them, but it does hope to get somewhere charting the course taken by giants like Sankalia, Lal, Cunningham, and others. To distill everything that we discussed so far into a single, portable problem statement, is the Ayodhya of today the Ayodhya of Ramayana?

Indian historiography is a hotly contested space, both ideologically and politically charged. It’s no secret that there are two broad camps with two conflicting objectives—Hindutva and Marxist. Calling one more eminent than the other, tempting as it may seem, is a grossly inaccurate oversimplification. They just come with different endgames, that’s all. This difference does not affect their scholarship, it only affects how they deploy that scholarship and to what end. 

The choice of Sankhalia is of interest and merits some explanation. Right off the bat, it’s the fact that he was the first to ask these questions, but more than that, Sankhalia is held in high regard by all with no ideological ascriptions tarnishing his legacy. If anything, it’s said he harbored “a strong sympathy and desire to ‘prove’ the existence of traditional accounts” or Ramayana,3 and yet was objective enough to ask difficult questions. This makes him a worthy participant in any conversation on Ramayana. He comes with no political baggage. And also, as “father of post-Independence archeology,” he has much of immense value to offer. But we’re not listening to Sankhalia alone. In a tradition we’ve consistently maintained since inception, we _will_ listen to experts with political baggage too. We will listen to Marxist historians, and we will listen to Hindutva historians. This will be a long discussion.

Although common knowledge, some key tenets warrant mentioning before we proceed with our study. These will help contextualize the discussion and also place events in the right order. First up, Ramayana is about Rama. It’s also about many other things but it’s primarily Rama. He came from a long line of Kshtriya kings called Ikṣvāku. The dynasty ruled over Kosala, one of the sixteen Aryan polities that emerged during the Late Vedic period in what we now call the second urbanization of the subcontinent. Ayodhya was its capital. The events described in the epic took place during this window, _i.e._ the Late Vedic period. In other words, Ramayana came after the Vedas. Traditionally, it’s understood to predate the events of the other epic, the Mahabharata. Everything we discuss henceforth builds on top of these fundamental, let’s say, axioms.

## The Legend of Śunaḥśepa

One of the Ikṣvāku kings was Harishchandra. The king lists given in the Ramayana place him roughly in the middle. This is a name of significance to our subject, for it finds a mention not only in the epic but also in a work that belongs to the Vedic corpus. Two works, in fact. The first is the _Aitareya Brahmana_ , where Harishchandra is the protagonist of the legend of Śunaḥśepa. So, before all else, let’s lay out the legend.

Harishchandra had a hundred wives but no son, so he prayed to Varuna for one. Varuna granted the wish on the condition that the king would sacrifice the son to him at a later date. The son thus born was named Rohita, but when Varuna demanded the sacrifice, Harishchandra dithered and delayed it. As Rohita grew up and the inevitable couldn’t be stalled anymore, he refused to be sacrificed and fled to the forest, leaving his father afflicted with an illness by Varuna. During his sixth year of wandering, Rohita met a destitute Brahmin named Ajigarta and purchased his middle son, Śunaḥśepa, to be sacrificed in his stead. Much drama followed as the priests refused to perform the sacrifice and the victim’s own father agreed to do the job for a price, but ultimately a divine intervention managed to rescue the boy. Sage Vishvamitra then adopted Śunaḥśepa, renamed him Devarata, and exiled his own sons who refused to acknowledge Devarata as their elder brother to the fringes of Aryavarta. Tribes like Andhras and Dasyus descend from these outcasts.

This is what comes down to us from the Aitareya Brahmana.4 The same story also appears in _Śankhayana Śrauta Sutra_ , another Late Vedic work roughly placed around 600 BC. In both sources, when Harishchandra came down with the illness, purportedly a curse from Varuna for reneging on his deal, Rohita returned to the village to see his sick father, although he soon left again this time to scout for a sacrificial proxy. This is what interests us. He did not return to a sprawling megapolis but a village.5 The word used is _grāma_.6 We know Harishchandra was an Ikṣvāku scion, and we know that the Ikṣvākus ruled over Kosala. We also know that Ayodhya was their capital. Putting it all together, we get an Ayodhya which is starkly different from the city described in Ayodhya Kanda.

The legend of Śunaḥśepa also appears in the _Bala Kanda_ of Ramayana, but with some crucial changes.7 Here, instead of Harishchandra, it’s Ambarisha,8 also an Ikṣvāku king but several generations senior to Harishchandra. And there is no son involved. Instead, it’s the king himself who sets out to find a proxy for his horse that was stolen by Indra just before an _Aśvamedha_ sacrifice.9 Most importantly, in this version, Ayodhya is mentioned by name.10

Although the complete legend first appears in Aitareya Brahmana, it’s possible that the lore was in circulation since the earliest of Aryan days. Śunaḥśepa finds a mention in a work as primeval as the Rigveda itself. As many as six hymns pertain to this character in the first mandala, which is why they’re also collectively referred to as the Śunaḥśepa hymns.11 But these hymns do not tell the whole story. They only tell us the prayers he chanted to the various Vedic deities starting with Prajapati and ending with Usha. While this may not conclusively establish the Vedic vintage of the legend, it would still not be wrong to assume that Śunaḥśepa was a Rigvedic character who was appropriated in a Later Vedic legend that involved Harishchandra and his son. Later, this same legend got incorporated into the epic with minor modifications.

The conclusion we can draw with this much information is that the Ikṣvāku clan existed in the Late Vedic period. And so did Ayodhya. What we can also draw from the Brahmana and the Sutra is that Ayodhya was a rural entity at the time, a far cry from the bustling metropolis of Ramayana. This is exactly what Dr. Bimala Churn Law does in his _Historical Geography of Ancient India_.12 Now, if we take Late Vedic as coeval with the second urbanization, _i.e._ around 600 BC, the epic must have been composed significantly after that since by its time, Ayodhya had grown from rural to metropolitan. Either that, or the Metropolitan capital of Ayodhya Kanda was a product of Valmiki’s imagination. But not everyone agrees.

Among the skeptics is orientalist Frederick Eden Pargiter. In the footnote of a paper published in the Journal of the Royal Asiatic Society in 1917, Pargiter calls into question the literal reading of the word grāma in both Late Vedic works.13 According to him, this usage could be a quirk of Brahminical lexicon since both Aitareya Brahmana and Śankhayana Śrauta Sutra were composed by Brahmins.14 These composers likely lived in the woods, far removed from human settlements. If so, the only kind of human settlement they’d be familiar with are villages.15 A kshatriya, on the other hand, might have referred to Ayodhya as a city since they’d be familiar with both villages as well as cities. In Pargiter’s views, therefore, the only thing this establishes is that the authors of the Brahmana and the Sutra knew nothing about towns or cities.16 If this holds water, we’re back to an Ayodhya in the Late Vedic that’s very much a bustling megapolis but referred to as a village in contemporary works because the Brahmins writing about it live in the forest and either know little about cities or make no distinction between villages and cities. Do note that we’re still in the second urbanization phase at this point, so the concept of cities would be alien to most Aryans, especially those living away from human activities.

So, what do we have now? Nothing except a Late Vedic Ayodhya that is likely a small, unremarkable village, but almost as likely a megacity with multistorey buildings and well-laid arterial roads. In short, back to where we started. But it must have been a village at some point? Every city starts as a village. So, when was it for Ayodhya if it was already a city in the Late Vedic? Obviously, sometime before 600 BC? The only way to settle this matter is to see how other Vedic works see Ayodhya.

## Status in Vedic Works

While reading the legend of Śunaḥśepa, we are so focused on Śunaḥśepa and Harishchandra that we entirely forget something crucial—that neither Aitareya Brahmana nor Śankhayana Śrauta Sutra mention Ayodhya by name. They just say grāma and we assume it’s Ayodhya because that’s where Harishchandra, the Ikṣvāku king is. A city of Ayodhya’s stature finding no mention by name in a legend centered around one of its most prominent rulers, is not easy to disregard. But what about the rest of the Vedic corpus?

In 1912, the English Sanskrit scholar Arthur Anthony Macdonell published a list of all the nouns that appear in all four Vedas as the two-volume _Vedic Index of Names and Subjects_. This is an incredibly useful work for anyone researching the four Vedas, almost indispensable. Any person, place, or entity named in any of the four Vedas must appear on this index. If it isn’t here, it isn’t in the Vedas. Interestingly, Ayodhya finds no mention in either volume. The closest it comes is _Ayudha_ , which it picks from the Rigveda’s battle hymn as a broad term for war implements.17 Clearly, Ayodhya was not significant enough, either socially or doctrinally, to be spoken of at all in any of the four _Samhitas_. There’s an exception that we’ll talk about later.

It must be noted that Kosala was one of the last regions occupied by the Aryans. Therefore, it’s natural that its capital would have risen to prominence only later, at least much later than the early Vedic period. Kosala’s neighbors were Kashi and Videha. The first time the three names appear together is in Śankhayana Śrauta Sutra 16.29.6–11.18 Here, a single priest is mentioned as representing the kings of all three polities, suggesting that these entities were not only contiguous but also in close contact both culturally and politically. Further reinforcing the intimacy, the section also uses the term _Nigustha_ for the peoples of all three territories. The _Śatapatha Brahmana_ even hints at a political alliance between the three against the rival Kuru-Panchala alliance, forming an “east vs. west” situation dividing northern India into two axes of power.19 In fact, the Index even confirms that the name Kosala is missing in Rigveda and other members of the early Vedic tradition, and that it only makes its first appearance in the Brahmana period with Śatapatha.20

This much should be enough to conclude that Kosala, Videha, and Kashi were some of the last Aryan polities. That Ayodhya was too insignificant to feature in Vedic works until the beginning of the Brahmana period. That even when Śankhayana Śrauta Sutra mentions Kosala as part of an eastern confederacy, it makes no reference to Ayodhya by name. And that the city isn’t significant enough to warrant a named mention even in the Ikṣvāku reference in Aitareya Brahmana. Thus, the city described in the epic, unless it were a result of Valmiki’s artistic license, a possibility that cannot and should not be ruled out, could not have existed in either Early or Late Vedic Age. But what if Kosala had a different capital during its early days and only moved to Ayodhya later? Not likely since even that capital should have then found mention in the Vedic Index. None exist. According to Dr. Law, Buddhist traditions too hold Ayodhya as the first capital of Kosala and Saketa the second.21

## Status in Non-Vedic Works

Since we’re on the subject of Ayodhya’s status, it wouldn’t hurt to also touch upon the other epic, the Mahabharata. As one of the original sixteen _janapadas_ , Kosala finds ample presence in this work, but not as a single entity. In the Mahabharata, there are five Kosalas—Eastern Kosala, Northern Kosala, Vriahadvala, Central Kosala, and Southern Kosala. All of these participated in the Kurukshetra War, siding with the Kauravas. Interestingly, however, there’s no mention of Ayodhya in the entire story, according to early nineteenth century Pali scholar Rhys Davids.22 In his _Buddhist India_ , he confirms not one, but three cities named _Ayojjha_ , Pali for Ayodhya, in Buddhist works.23

What all of this indicates is that Ayodhya was an insignificant village in Vedic times, and it was an insignificant village or multiple insignificant villages in Buddha’s times. Davids uses the term _unimportant_.24 If we place Buddha where traditions and mainstream scholarship do, we get sixth century BC as the _terminus post quem_ for Valmiki’s metropolitan Ayodhya. But before we freeze this, there’s much else remaining to contend with. Ayodhya also figures in Jain traditions.

The city is noted as the birthplace of at least five out of twenty-four spiritual leaders or _tirthankaras_ , making it one of the important nodes on the Jain pilgrim circuit.25 Among the five tirthankaras born in Ayodhya is the very first, Ṛṣabhadeva. In Jain canon, he is himself an Ikṣvāku scion. If true, Ayodhya’s lower limit comes to the fourth century BC. However, no Jain chronicle or scripture elaborates on Ayodhya’s physical characteristics. If it truly were how Valmiki described it, it should have also enjoyed similar adulations in Jain works as well. A city of such prominence and preeminence could not go unnoticed and unremarked upon. Which only further strengthens the position that Ayodhya was not a major city and certainly not the megapolis of Valmiki’s vision.

## The Śunga Inscription

The Mauryan dynasty came to an end toward the last quarter of the second century with the death of Brihadratha Maurya who left behind a large but crumbling empire with a strongly Śramaṇic character. By most accounts, especially the Puranas, he was assassinated by his general Puṣpaka or Puṣpamitra, better known as Puṣyamitra Śunga. While the affiliations of the final few Mauryans isn’t well documented, the dynasty can be understood to be predominantly Buddhist or Jain since Aśoka. With Puṣyamitra, this changed. He became the first Hindu sovereign of Magadha in several centuries. But usurping the throne, especially after having murdered a reigning monarch, is not a very popular move. Usurpers are typically not accepted as legitimate rulers.

To remedy this, Puṣyamitra performed the ancient rite of Aśvamedha. The ceremony had traditionally solemnized and legitimized monarchs since the earliest of Vedic times and after a long hiatus, it was being revived and mainstreamed by a Hindu ruler. Nothing could legitimize his kingship better. Needless to say, the ceremony had to be announced to the world for maximum impact. Everybody should know that the Śunga Empire was now in session. What better way to accomplish that than to follow Aśoka’s lead, _i.e._ inscriptions?

We don’t know how many inscriptions were made but one was curiously placed in Ayodhya. On a flat stone slab, the artifact still sits at the entrance of a _samādhi_ about a mile from modern-day Ayodhya.26 What makes this piece of epigraphy special is the fact that it’s in Sanskrit. One of the oldest such works. Until that point, the preferred language was Pali or any of the other vernaculars collectively called Prakrit. Some Aśokan edicts even bore Greek and Aramaic. But never Sanskrit. With the Ayodhya inscription, that changed. In this inscription, Puṣyamitra identifies himself as _kо̄sal-ādhipēna_ or the “Lord of Kosala,” and announces not one but two horse sacrifices.27

Although this inscription comes centuries after Valmiki’s purported period, it still offers something of value to us. It tells us that at least by the second century AD, Ayodhya had come to be seen as the administrative seat of Kosala and located almost exactly where the modern city of Ayodhya stands. If that’s where Ayodhya has stood for 1,800 years today, it’s not unlikely that that’s where it would have stood for 800 years then. We could end the debate here but we’re still in the realm of conjecture here. And that’s where archeology comes in.

## Archeology

In the context of Ayodhya’s archeology, a bigger name than Braj Basi Lal does not exist. He carried out excavations not too far from the famous Asharfi Bhawan in the spring of 1975.28 This small-scale dig yielded a startling result more on which in a bit. The topmost layers threw up some coins from the second century AD, marking a _terminus ante quem_ for continuous occupation.29 Deeper still were, as should be expected, artifacts from the Śunga and Mauryan eras.30 In case it isn’t already obvious, archeological finds are always buried in descending order of chronology—the deeper the find, the older its origins. In the case of Ayodhya, the Śunga artifacts were above Mauryan artifacts, themselves above specimens identified as Northern Black Polished Ware or NBPW. Below NBPW were found what Lal identified as a derivative of Painted Gray Ware or PGW.31

Now, the NBPW is an Iron Age artifact traditionally associated with the second urbanization and the rise of Magadha as an empire, between the seventh and fifth centuries BC. It succeeded the PGW culture which spanned the half millennium between the twelfth and sixth centuries BC. PGW is generally associated with the rise of the Kuru-Panchala kingdom, the first prominent polity of the Vedic Age. As for the specimens found in the lowermost Ayodhya layer, Lal places them between PGW and NBPW, an intermediary step if you will. Needless to say, this places a ceiling on the earliest dating of the region that is Ayodhya today, and that ceiling would be eighth century BC.32 In other words, if Valmiki’s Ayodhya was the same as today’s Ayodhya, it could not be older than 800 BC.

And now we come to the startling implication of this find. Lal also dug Hastinapur, the place traditionally associated with the Kuru-Panchala polities and the epic of Mahabharata. There, he found clearly identifiable PGW sherds on the lowest levels. Since the oldest artifacts in the Ayodhya dig were identified as later than PGW, it naturally follows that Ayodhya was gentrified later than Hastinapur. Lal notes this and concludes that the events of Ramayana must have occurred _after_ the events of Mahabharata,33 which goes straight against the conventional understanding of the timeline. Lal wasn’t the first to find PGW artifacts in Ayodhya either. Nearly a decade before him, Sankalia too had paid a visit to Ayodhya and picked up a potsherd which Lal later identified as a late PGW artifact. Of course, this fits well with his post-Mahabharata model. But Sankhalia hesitated to draw that conclusion. The good news is that this confusion has zero bearing on the subject at hand, so we need not pursue it further.

So, Ayodhya was settled around 800 BC. What does that mean to our inquiry? The Brahmanas already indicate a village as the seat of the Ikṣvāku monarchy around 600 BC. And Lal’s excavations give us nothing more than potsherds and terracotta figurines. No sign of an urban sprawl, like we have in Harappa and Mohenjo-daro. No paved roads and no multistorey buildings. It’s clear that Valmiki’s imagination does not match what lies buried under modern-day Ayodhya. But before we rush to draw any conclusion, Lal reminds us that it’s only one dig at one site.

Ayodhya is dotted with a number of mounds, locally called _tila_ , named after prominent personalities from the epic. Lal sees immense value in excavating these mounds, none of which have been so far. A large-scale excavation could yield something like Harappa, who knows. But it is extremely unlikely to ever happen given the political and communal sensitivity of most archeologically rich spots in the city. We might never find out. But what we have so far indicates nothing even remotely like Valmiki’s grand vision.

Having said that, Lal was—he passed away in 2022—firmly of the view that Ramayana is a historical account and that Valmiki’s Ayodhya did exist and stood right where modern Ayodhya does today. At this point, it would only be fair to bring in someone who disagrees, Sankalia. It’s also fair because Sankalia too, like Lal, was an archeologist. And a scholar of Sanskrit. While he did not carry out much excavation in Ayodhya, he did build a formidable case against Ayodhya’s conventional placement. Not from archeology but the epic itself.

For what it’s worth, although not of much significance to the question at hand, latest excavations by B. R. Mani led ASI have pushed Ayodhya’s timeline even further to seventeenth century BC.34 That’s comfortably in the Ochre Colored Pottery or OCP realm, a dramatic shift from Lal’s PGW. However, not much is known of this find since its report isn’t public yet.35 Anyway, the shift has little bearing on the location of Valmiki’s Ayodhya, for we’re looking for a highly sophisticated urban center whereas both OCP and PGW are subsistence cultures.

## Bharata’s Journey

Ramayana is literally Rama’s _áyana_ or journey. And that’s what blindsides us to another equally important but far less highlighted journey, the one undertaken by Bharata and Shatrughna. For a clearer understanding of Ayodhya’s true character and location, that’s the journey we need to study. We already know that Bharata’s mother Kaikeyi is from the far western kingdom of Kekeya which is ruled by her father Aśvapati. Besides Kekeyi, Aśvapati also has a son named Yudhajit whom we first meet in Ayodhya Kanda 1.1–2. Here, Yudhajit has arrived in Ayodhya at the behest of his father to fetch his nephew Bharata.36 While Bharata’s journey is key to locating Ayodhya, it’s also crucial to the broader story of Ramayana, for it sets the stage for Bharata’s absence during key events involving Rama’s exile. So, let’s tag along and see where it leads.

The verses in the first _sarga_ or chapter offer no details about the journey or even the destination city. They simply mention that the half-brothers left for Kekeya with Bharata’s uncle. The city’s name first appears in chapter sixty-two where Sage Vasiṣṭha dispatches envoys to Kekeya’s capital, Rājagṛha, to bring the boys back for an emergency.37 And what’s the emergency? Rama’s exile. Unable to reconcile with the prospects of separation from his son for such a long time, Daśaratha falls critically ill, while the kingdom grows anxious for an heir. Bharata is expected to fill in for Ram but he’s in faraway Kekeya, which is why Vasiṣṭha sends for him. What we have in 2.62 is just the first part of the trip we’ll examine, the one undertaken by the envoys to Kekeya. The return journey from Kekeya to Ayodhya comprises the second and is covered in a later chapter.

Sankalia suggests closely following both parts in order to locate Ayodhya with better precision. We will follow his lead. The first part begins in 2.62.10 with the crossing of the Ganges at Hastinapur, then the passage first through Pañcāladeśa or Pañcāla, then Kurujangala.38 From there the envoys make their way through the city of Kulinga, then the Bahlika country before crossing over to the other side of the Sudama Mountain.39 Next, they pass by the Vipāśā or Beas River and reach Śālmali,40 the penultimate milestone before the final arrival at Girivraja,41 another name for Rājagṛha.

There’s a lot to unpack here, but first a few words on Girivraja. Alexander Cunningham locates it near Lahore,42 which makes it more than 800 miles from Ayodhya. While Valmiki identifies it as the capital of Kekeya in the west, the name also rings a bell in the east. It’s a name we first hear in the Mahabharata as the capital of Jarasandha, the Magadhan king who is slayed by Krishna. Today, the site forms part of the historic city of Rajgir in Bihar. Rajgir too is a corruption of Rājagṛha. Since Ramayana is understood to predate Mahabharata, a logical conclusion would be that the names originally applied to the western city and only later repurposed as the capital of Bṛhadratha’s Magadha. Sankalia calls this a common case of toponymic transference.43 Which means this may or may not be the only such case.

On this point, Sankalia also reminds us that Ayodhya finds no mention in any Vedic text. Not in the Samhitas, not in the Brahmanas. Curiously though, neither does Hastinapur.44 But Kekeya does. In Śatapatha Brahmana, Aśvapati Kekeya is mentioned in high regard as someone with the complete knowledge of God.45 The same is also repeated in _Chandogya Upanishad_.46 The implication is clear—Kekeya existed since the earliest of Vedic times and so did its capital Rājagṛha or Girivraja, the names that would later be repurposed as Magadha’s capital. This toponymic transference is important and will come in handy later.

Now let’s look at other names from the trail. First up is Pañcāla which sat roughly on the Ganga-Yamuna interfluve and corresponds to modern day districts like Bareilly, Budaun, Farrukhabad, and others in central Uttar Pradesh. Like Hastinapur and Girivraja, this too is a name reminiscent of the Mahabharata. Nothing stands out with these names, for both Hastinapur and Pañcāla are fairly close to Kosala as placed on the map. The stop after Pañcāla does not raise concerns either. Kurujangala is the western part of the Kuru country and corresponds to modern-day Rohtak, Hansi, and Hissar in Haryana. The Kuru country was divided into a total of three sections, and this was one of them. The other two were Kururashtra and Kurukshetra. In the Mahabharata, Kurujangala was ruled from its capital in Indraprastha or Delhi.

The next two milestones, however, seem odd—Kulinga and Bahlika. Kulinga rings a bell with its close affinity to Kalinga. If so, it instantly breaks our route. Kalinga, of Aśoka fame, is unanimously identified with Orissa. A road from Ayodhya to Kekeya has no reason to pass through Orissa. Could that mean Ayodhya of Valmiki wasn’t the Ayodhya of Uttar Pradesh? Probably, but we need much more than this. The Kulinga–Kalinga affinity, for now, is at best a conjecture.

Things are further complicated by the name that comes after—Bahlika. This might not ring a bell as readily as Kulinga, but it does bear familiarity. Bahlika has been known since Mahabharata times and applies both to a person and a place. In later sources, the place is known as Balkh, the name most _should_ be familiar with today. And Balkh is in the far northwest of Afghanistan. Again, no reason for a road from Ayodhya to Lahore, to ever touch Balkh. So, what are two dramatically off-circuit sites doing on a trip that does not involve side-quests?

The penultimate stop is no less mysterious either. All we know of Śālmali is from the Puranic literature where it’s named as an island. _Viṣṇu Purāṇa_ lists it as one of the seven primordial continents—Jambu, Plaksna, Śālmali, Kuśa, Karuñca, Śāka, and Puṣkara.47 If Jambudvipa was all of India as known at the time, was Śālmali outside the subcontinent? Again, why would a trip from Ayodhya to Kekeya ever leave Jambudvipa? One could call this a later development, given the Puranas came much later than the epics. But the pelagic model, along with the name Śālmali, is also noted in the Mahabharata.48 At least one scholar identifies Śālmali with Chaldea or Assyria,49 probably inspired by the Assyrian king Shalmaneser III who lived in the ninth century BC. Of course, this is not a widely shared view. At any rate, as described in the Mahabharata and the Puranas has no business in the journey essayed in Ayodhya Kanda.

Now let’s look at Bharata’s return trip, from Rājagṛha to Ayodhya. This time, the journey begins with the crossing of two rivers, one of them Śatadru.50 Next up is a river called Śilas51 which may or may not be the one mentioned as Sillas by Megasthenes in his _Indika_.52 The Sillas River, however, remains unidentified to this day. After this crossing is a forest and then the Yamuna River.53 Next comes Kulinga, then Jambuprastha, and then the village of Varutha.54 After the village comes a forest of _sāla_ trees.55 This is important, let’s put a pin on it. A couple of villages more and the entourage eventually crosses the Kapivati River at Lauhitya.56 Thereafter, there are a couple of river crossings across the Gomati before arrival at Kalinganagara.57 From Kalinganagara, there’s another crossing through a sāla forest before finally arriving at the destination.58

As evident, the route this time is very different from the last. For starters, as Sankalia points out, there seem to be a lot more pit stops in the return journey than the one undertaken by the envoys. Also, the stops seem far more rural in nature than before. The envoys went through major kingdoms and capitals like Hastinapur, Pañcala, Kurujangala, Kulinga, and Bahlika, whereas Bharata’s entourage traveled almost entirely from one village to another with a large number of rivers in between. This peculiarity is of import, but more on it a bit later. For now, let’s examine the villages.

The one thing that springs right up at us is that the return trip lists both Kulinga and Kalinganagara as two different places likely separated by vast distances. This solves our Kulinga conundrum where we confused it with Orissa’s Kalinga. But also presents us with a new one. Kalinganagara is highly unlikely to be different from Kalinga. Kulinga is also Sanskrit for sparrow, so it’s likely the place was just named after the bird to indicate its abundance. But no such interpretation available for Kalinganagara. It has to be Kalinga. In which case, the question remains unchanged—why Orissa on a trip from Pakistan to Uttar Pradesh?

But just before the arrival at Kalinganagara, the entourage crosses the Gomati River. We know for sure that this can’t be right. Gomati as we know it runs through Lucknow, a long way away from Orissa. Is it possible that the name is the result of, in Sankalia’s words, a toponymic transference? He supports his argument with references to the Vedic corpus.59 In the river hymns of the tenth mandala, the Rigveda identifies Gomati as a tributary of Indus.60 Later, the name was repurposed for another river in Kurukshetra.61 Sankalia therefore believes, it shouldn’t be unlikely that the same was done with other rivers as they were discovered by the Aryans on their eastward march. Thus, there could be multiple Gomatis. Kalinganagara still remains a pain point.

Another river that stumps us is Kapivati that was crossed at a place called Lohitya. Anyone from modern-day Assam would identify Lohitya with Assam. Before the Brahminical polity of Kamarup, there was a pre-Brahmin Lauhitya Kingdom. Both Kapivati and Lauhitya have traditionally applied to the Brahmaputra. The river is still called Luit in Assamese. This again complicates Bharata’s route which is supposedly taking him to Ayodhya from the west.

As an aside, by the way, the Sarayu River too is listed among the western tributaries of Indus in Rigveda 5.53.962 and then again invoked alongside Indus and Sarasvati in 10.64.9,63 positioning it firmly as a far western water body. And yet the Sarayu we know today is in the Gangetic heartland far removed from its Vedic roots. It’s quite possible for names to transfer over to newer entities with newer explorations. It happens all the time.

And now we come to the pin we dropped on the sāla forests. Sāla or _Shorea robusta_ is a tropical tree that’s both evergreen and deciduous depending on the precipitation. This tree, interestingly, grows only in Bihar, Bengal, Orissa, Assam, and parts of Madhya Pradesh. Outside of India, it’s also at home in Myanmar, Nepal, and Bangladesh. To further narrow it down, as Sankhalia points out, the core habitat remains confined to Jharkhand, western Orissa, West Bengal, and Chhattisgarh.64 If this holds, it should explain the presence of sāla forests around Kalinganagara. In which case, it would be more reasonable for Valmiki’s Ayodhya to be closer to Orissa than for Kalinga and its sāla habitat to be in Uttar Pradesh. Be that as it may, Bharata could not have entered Ayodhya from the west if he was coming from Kalinganagara and its surrounding sāla woodland. He could only have entered from the east.

We are still left with many contentions. What about Sutlej and Beas, for instance? On their way to Kekeya, the envoys had passed by or crossed the Vipāśā or Beas. But on his way back, Bharata crossed the Śatadru or Sutlej instead. This is problematic. Because if one were to travel from modern-day Ayodhya to modern-day Lahore or thereabouts, it would be impossible to avoid the Sutlej. Beas could be avoided if one took a more southerly route—because it drains into the Sutlej way up north—but not Sutlej. The only way to explain this is by invoking the good old topographical transference of names. Sutlej is identified as Śutudrī in the Rigveda, twice over.65 So, the earliest of Aryans were familiar with the river. They must be since they had to cross it to venture further east. In Ramayana, the name becomes Śatadru. So how do we even know that Śatadru and Śutudrī refer to the exact same river? There is no clear, unbroken etymological line between the two names. Could they be completely different rivers?

## Architectural Clues

Valmiki’s Ayodhya was a picture of architectural grandeur. We have already learned of its well-laid boulevards, its impregnable fortification, its lush gardens, and its prosperous markets. What catches attention here is the description of Ayodhya’s buildings. Sankalia notes some as high as eight stories.66 That’s impressive even for modern times. When the Home Insurance Building came up in Chicago in 1885, it instantly earned a spot in history as the world’s first skyscraper.67 And it stood only ten stories. The Ayodhya we’re talking about predates this by more than two thousand years. How did they build so tall? What materials were used? The Home Insurance Building used a steel weight-bearing frame. Did the folks in Ayodhya have access to that kind of material? Being in the Iron Age, they certainly knew steel.

We know that the Indus Civilization built with sunbaked mud bricks and many of its homes were multistoried, but none rose as high as eight stories. The oldest mud-brick high-rises still standing are in Yemen’s Shibam. So far as we know, these buildings don’t use metal frames and are entirely made of sunbaked bricks. But Shibam only goes back a little over a millennium and a half and its impressive high-rises, half a millennium. As for Ayodhya’s buildings, Valmiki doesn’t tell us much. The Critical Edition says nothing about the kind of bricks they used. Similarly, there’s nothing on roofing either. Was it thatched? Or tiled? Sankalia presumes some kind of a solid terrace from where residents could and did watch the various royal processions in the streets below.68

While the moat isn’t hard to explain with a perennial river at hand, nor is the arterial road network which was already an established feature of Harappan cities, the multistory construction made purely of sunbaked bricks isn’t. In cities like Mohenjo-daro such construction would make sense given the relatively hot and dry climate. But in tropical regions like the Gangetic Plains, which are subject to heavy rains and sustained flooding, mud-brick high-rises would be quite problematic.69 Tall edifices could be an inspiration from grand temples with high ś _ikhara_ , but no such structure has so far been found to be older than the sixth century AD.70 Not anywhere on the Indian subcontinent. That’s a notable argument against the veracity of Valmiki’s Ayodhya.

But what about stupas? Ayodhya is well-recognized as a key Buddhist center right since the days of Gautama Buddha. Those could rise as high as fifty feet, roughly equivalent to four or five stories as we measure them today. We could admit Ayodhya’s eight stories as harmless artistic license. But we’re not talking about stupas as inspiration behind Valmiki’s edifices. We’re talking about stupas depicting multistorey buildings in their motifs. The stupa at Bharhut in Madhya Pradesh, for instance, bears a number of friezes and reliefs that depict at least two stories complete with arched windows and balconies. The same can be seen in the stupa at Sanchi as well. If the edifices could exist on friezes, they could also exist in reality?

The oldest of stupas, including those at Bharhut and Sanchi are generally placed within the tenure of Aśoka. Therefore, purely in the context of architectural innovations, Valmiki’s Ayodhya can be granted a _terminus ad quem_ of third century BC. Which means, the buildings at Ayodhya were likely built with stones and not bricks. Stone can withstand rains, mud bricks can’t. All this, _if_ we grant that the reliefs on stupa friezes represent reality of the time _and_ that reality existed in Ayodhya. That, in and of itself, is a big _if_ considering nothing indicative has yet been found in excavations in or around Ayodhya.

To reiterate, Ayodhya did not have an urban character in the middle of the first millennium BC, given its identification as a village in Aitareya Brahmana and Śankhayana Śrauta Sutra. Also do recall that neither of the two mention Ayodhya by name. Which leaves us with a window of three centuries for the city’s rise from a PGW settlement to a sprawling metropolitan agglomerate with multistorey stone buildings. But can we be certain of that?

This is where Sankalia points to another blink-and-miss clue hidden in Valmiki’s work. That clue is Kauśalya’s residence. The news of Rama’s imminent exile caused her much anguish, and she fainted. And when she did, points out Sankalia, she fell on the floor and got _pāṃsu_ or dust all over her body.7172 This, according to him, holds meaning. The reference to this episode, the dust in particular, was also preserved by Manmatha Nath Dutt Shastri73 whose translation departs from the Critical Edition in many places. That there is enough dust to warrant a special mention in both major translations holds meaning. This isn’t something you catch on a floor that hasn’t been swept since morning, this is something that would cover a mare if she were to roll on a dusty ground. That’s precisely the metaphor used.74 What kind of floor would that indicate? Certainly not possible in the case of stone or firebrick flooring.75 This leaves us with either mud or, at best, sunbaked bricks. Why would a home that’s built of stones have a mud floor?

With that, we are now back to the pre-stupa situation. Where are Valmiki’s skyscrapers? The _terminus ad quem_ now returns to the Gupta period if we need the multistory mansions and Puṣyamitra Śunga if just Aśvamedha would suffice. Just Aśvamedha won’t suffice, however, because Valmiki’s whole imagination hinges on imposing edifices which could not be built with mud. Sankalia places Valmiki’s cities—not just Ayodhya but also others like Kishkindha and Lanka—between 100 and 400 AD, _i.e._ the Kushana and Gupta periods although he also reserves room for fourth century BC if we were to grant Valmiki some artistic liberties.76

## Back to the Vedas

Remember we established that the name Ayodhya finds no mention in the entire Vedic corpus, including the Brahmanas and the Aranyakas? Some could argue we missed something. Atharva Veda mentions the term not once but twice, in 5.20.12 and in 10.2.31. The first mention is in an invocation of one of the war-drum as a Vedic deity for help with life’s struggles and victory over obstacles.77

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _Acyutacyutsamado gamiṣṭho mṛdho jetā pura-etāyodhyaḥ.
    Indreṇa gupto vidhathā nicikyaddhṛddyotano dviṣatām yāhi śībham._
    “Shaker of the unshaken, joyous, most dynamic, winner of battles, leading pioneer, unchallengeable, protected favorite of Indra, the ruler, well versed in yajnic programs, breaker of the courage and morale of adversaries, O leading voice of the nation, pray come forthwith and guide us to our goals through the battle ranks of life.” (trans. Tulsi Ram)

Here, a closer look tells us that rather than a standalone proper noun, the word is used as an adjectival compound. It’s the war-drum that’s being qualified as unchallengeable. No city is being referred to. At any rate, a proper noun has little reason to exist as a compound.

In the context of the second mention, it would seem that Ayodhya is being described as a mythical _pur_ or fortified city with eight concentric enclosures and nine gateways. That literal translation, however, is misleading. It is now understood that the entire thing is a metaphor for the human body with its eight _chakras_ or plexuses and nine orifices.78

     _Aṣṭācakrā navadvārā devānāṁ pūrayodhyā.
    Tasyāṁ hiraṇyayaḥ kośaḥ svargo jyotiṣāvṛtaḥ._
    “The human body is an invincible city of the gods, God Supreme and the divinities of nature. It has eight chakras: Muladhara, Svadhishthana, Manipur, Anahat, Vishuddhi, Lalana, Ajna, and Sahasrara. It has nine gates: two eyes, two ears, two nostrils, mouth, and the organs of excretion. In the city, there is a golden cave, replete with the golden light of Divinity, which is the paradisal path to heaven. This City is Ayodhya, the Invincible.” (trans. Tulsi Ram)

Although Tulsi Ram still sees Ayodhya despite the metaphor, American philologist William Dwight Whitney, who made the first English translation of Atharva Veda, notes it purely as a metaphor without any reference to a city.79 This is also the interpretation accepted by Lal who squarely dismisses the idea of Ayodhya being a mythical city.80 Lal points out two more mentions in the Atharva Veda, 19.13.3 and 19.13.7, but again dismisses both as terms of qualification rather than the name of a city or village.81 Outside of this Samhita, Lal notes the presence in one more work, the _Taittariya Aranyaka_ in 1.27. Here too, he sees no city. The Aranyakas are also a genre of Late Vedic works that are understood to postdate the Brahmanas. In sum, outside of the Atharva Veda and the relevant reference in Taittariya Aranyaka, the word Ayodhya does not appear in the entire Vedic corpus in any form or inflection. Not as a compound, not as an adjective, not as a common noun, and certainly not as a proper noun.

Lal’s contention, however, that the Ayodhya of Ramayana spatially coincides with the Ayodhya of reality, remains wanting in evidence. Sankalia too, like Lal, does not doubt the existence of Ramayana’s Ayodhya, but unlike Lal, he isn’t sure of its location.

## Elevation to Pilgrimage

Ayodhya is the birthplace of Rama which makes it a key pilgrimage on the Indian cultural and religious circuit. The modern city of Ayodhya enjoys this status because it’s understood to be the same as Valmiki’s metropolis. But if it wasn’t it would mean that the site was not of religious value at first and only acquired it over time as its identification with the city of Ramayana became mainstream. This means, if we could somehow establish that the present-day city was always a center of Hindu pilgrimage, we could strongly build a case for its antiquity. So, when did Ayodhya as we know it today, become a _tirtha_ or center of pilgrimage.

Let’s start with the oldest literary reference we have at hand, the Puranic corpus. The work of our interest is the _Matsya Purana_. Before we get to what it says about Ayodhya, let’s establish its periodization. The earliest it has been placed is fourth century BC, _i.e._ after the traditionally held epic era, by Ramachandra Dikshitar.82 He sugegsts an upper limit of fourth century BC and a lower limit of third century AD, since it does not mention the Guptas in its kings list.83 A wider consensus, however, exists on a slightly later vintage that brings the _terminus a quo_ to third century AD and _terminus ad quem_ to 500 AD.84 Late Puranic scholar Rajendra Chandra Hazra does a more granular dating and offers 275–325 AD for some of the earliest bits including chapter twenty-two and a lower limit of a much more recent 1100 AD for the later chapters.85 A critical examination of all these positions is outside the scope of this conversation, so we’ll stick with the mainstream scholarship that keeps the work in the third century AD at the earliest.

The reason we mentioned chapter twenty-two in particular when noting Hazra’s chronology is that it lists out a number of rivers and cities as preeminent spots for the _śrāddha_ ceremony. Among them are cities like Tamraparni, Bhadratirtha, Elapur, Rameshwar, and Ayodhya. Although a city—or cities, if going by Rhys Davids and Buddhist references—named Ayodhya should have existed before this composition, this reference would be the first to list it as a holy city, a pilgrim destination. Which means that at least by the second century AD, the association of current Ayodhya with Valmiki’s city had crystallized. But there is a problem.

The Matsya list does not mention Ayodhya by name. The word given is _Rāmādhivāsa_ or where Rama resides,86 which we assume to be Ayodhya because Valmiki said so. That’s reasonable but doesn’t in and of itself affirm the status of today’s Ayodhya. Paired with Puṣyamitra’s inscription, it somewhat can although the latter too does _not_ mention Ayodhya by name. But all hopes are not lost.

The third century also produced Vishnu Smriti. If this sounds like _Manusmriti_ , that’s because both works belong to the same genre called Dharmashastras. Vishnu Smriti is considered the last of this tradition and is variously placed between the second and tenth centuries. Patrick Olivelle places it between eighth and eleventh. Again, this dating is not what we’re to concern ourselves with here. We’ll just keep it coeval with the Manusmriti at its earliest although that’s being extremely generous. Just like the Purana before, this work too lists out a number of places most optimal for śrāddha rites. And on this list is, in 85.32, a place simply called _Sarayūtīre_ or the banks of the Sarayu.87 In light of the strong Sarayu-Ayodhya association, it’s reasonable to interpret this as referring to Ayodhya. But the fact that it stops shy of naming Ayodhya explicitly bothers.

While early centuries might seem dicey, we see relatively more concrete history emerging toward the end of the eleventh century. In that period, the Pratihaya king of Kanyakubja was ousted by the grandson of a Kalachuri vassal. With this conquest, the grandson established the illustrious Gahadavala dynasty that would last until the advent of Islam in the Indian heartland. His name was Chandradeva. In 1092 AD, this king is said to have taken a dip at the confluence of Sarayu and Ghaghra Rivers (likely two streams of the same river) which is where Ayodhya stands today. We know that from a Sanskrit inscription he left here memorializing himself and his bath.88 Finally this time, the city is mentioned by name.89

While this helps place Ramayana in modern-day Ayodhya, we’re still left with no answers to the Ayodhya conundrum in the pre-Gahadavala situation. That is if we take it as settled in the Puranas, which it actually isn’t. But before we close this conversation, a most unexpected piece of the puzzle warrants discussion—a Mughal source.

So, Valmiki measures Ayodhya as twelve _yojanas_ in length and three in breadth.90 Ami Ganatra computes this to a little over 2,300 square miles.91 In the final years of the sixteenth century, Akbar commissioned court historian Abul Fazl to put together an official chronicle of his reign. It was called _Akbarnama_. Part of this three-volume work was _Ain-i-Akbari_ , a collection of administrative reports from Akbar’s bureaucracy, much like a gazetteer. One such report pertains to _Awadh_. The name was a corruption of Ayodhya and applied to a province or _subah_ of the Mughal realm at the time.92 Fazl describes it in lofty terms and calls it one of the largest Hindu cities of India, and also the most sacred. He measures a past form as 148 _kos_ by 36 _kos_.93 In the same work, Fazl defines a kos as 5,000 _guz_ and a guz as two feet,94 which gives us a total area of alightly over 19,000 square miles for the “old city” as recorded by Abul Fazl.

This is close to ten times the size reckoned from Valmiki’s numbers. It would seem, Abul Fazl considered as the old city of Ayodhya the entire province of Awadh to the south of the Ghaghra. Obviously, a great exaggeration, but one could speculate a local memory of an extremely expansive metropolis in the region in an undefined past. But is that enough to push us beyond the realm of speculation?

## Conclusion

So, here’s what we have so far. Valmiki’s Ayodhya is in the vicinity of prominent sāla forests. It’s also close to Kalinga, which is in Orissa, but we can disregard that bit as a result of possible toponymic reapplication. What we cannot disregard is the sāla forests. These trees are native to a very specific territory that forms modern-day Jharkhand, Orissa, Chhattisgarh, and West Bengal. It is possible for a stray sāla thicket to be in central Uttar Pradesh, but it’s far more likely for Ayodhya to be in the east instead. Occam’s Razor.

Then there’s the dust. As Sankalia pointed out, Ayodhya homes were built of mud and not stones or masonry. This includes not just poorer quarters but even royal palaces. And those could not be more than two floors at best, a far cry from Valmiki’s eight. This stipulation would apply regardless of where we place the city, be it in the east or in its current location, because the entire Gangetic belt is rain-washed and thus unfavorable to large mud structures. It’s not impossible to find masonry in Ayodhya excavations today, but such finds are far more likely to be of a more recent vintage than Valmiki’s generally accepted timeline.

As for scriptural references, we have no Ayodhya in any Vedic work, neither Early nor Late. The dynasty of Ikṣvāku finds ample reference in Śankhayana Śrauta Sutra and Aitareya Brahmana, Kekeya and Aśvapati find references in Chandogya Upanishad and Śatapatha Brahmana, but no Ayodhya. At best, the Brahmana literature pegs an unremarkable village as the seat of Ikṣvāku administration.

Archeology in today’s Ayodhya has only given us late PGW artifacts and no sign of even a small-scale urban center. The key here, however, is that not much archeology has happened in the region to begin with. And not much is likely to happen in foreseeable future due to obvious political and ideological reasons. Even Koshambi fails to offer much in the way of comparison, for it was utterly destroyed in the early sixth century. The closest we have is the Indus Civilization, but Valmiki’s vision is far grander than even that.

So, where is Valmiki’s Ayodhya? Before that, the question ought to be, did it ever exist? Lal and Sankalia firmly believe it did. That it was not a myth. But if not a myth, where was it? That is something we can only speculate. Odds of it coinciding with the current city of Ayodhya are lower than it being further east. What’s also certain is the enormous gap between Valmiki’s vision and the real city of Ayodhya, wherever it lay. Artistic license must be granted here. With that, the question that inevitably arises is, is today’s Ayodhya sacred?

And the answer has to be yes. Has been for at least two thousand years, if not more. While the Śunga inscription does not mention Ayodhya by name, the fact that it was placed in today’s Ayodhya and refers to its patron as the Lord of Kosala is difficult to disregard. Also difficult to disregard are the literature and inscriptions from the common era that, despite not naming the city directly, hint at a place in today’s Ayodhya and thereabouts as an important _tirtha_. Regardless of whether Rama was born there, Ayodhya has been sacred to Hindus since long before some theologies even came into being.
