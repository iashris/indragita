     _sa sāgaram anādṛśyam atikramya mahābalaḥ, 
    trikūṭaśikhare laṅkāṁ sthitāṁ svastho dadarśa ha._
    “The mighty Hanuman crossed the inviolable sea, stood comfortably on the peak of Trikuta mountain and looked at Lanka.” (trans. Kataka)

With this, Valmiki establishes Lanka as a mystical island across the raging sea.12 Elsewhere in his work, he offers vivid elaborations of a city of skyscrapers, gold, blue lotuses, and formidable _rakshasas_. Lanka is the Shangri-La reachable only by those with special divine endowments. Today, we know it as the island nation of Sri Lanka. This identification is a culmination of centuries of reinforcement and finds strong support in a long list of material finds. The island is littered with sites mentioned in the epic. From the cave where Ravana meditated, to the enormous plateau on top of which sat his palace, to the spot where Sita was held captive, an entire catalog of Ramayana sites has attracted pilgrims from all over the Indian subcontinent and beyond for centuries. There’s even a giant footprint believed to be of Hanuman.

It’s strange, then, that there should even be a debate of any kind on the subject. That Lanka is Sri Lanka has remained part of mainstream scholarship for ages. What’s there to debate?

Turns out, a lot. There’s a growing body of academicians who question the very fundamentals of this association. They suggest removing the entire island nation from the equation and confining the epic’s entire geography to the Indian mainland. In this view, Lanka is located not in Sri Lanka but in India. Where in India? That depends on who we ask. Some suggest the vicinity of Delhi, others go with the Chhotanagpur plateaus in the east. Are they right? Does their position hold water against available scriptural and archeological data? That’s what we intend to explore in this article. If you wish to follow a similar exercise for Ayodhya, we’ve already done that here.

Before we get down to the brass tacks, let’s quickly outline our approach. The pieces we have at our disposal are literary, folkloric, geographical, ecological, and linguistic. We will examine them all. We will also examine theories from not just Hindu but also Buddhist works for a holistic understanding. The story of Ramayana, although originally Indian, has traveled far beyond the subcontinent over centuries. Associated folklores and traditions have emerged in places as far out as Southeast Asia and Sri Lanka. We will consider some of those too. And, of course, as the practice here goes, both hypotheses—that Lanka is Sri Lanka and Lanka isn’t Sri Lanka—shall receive their due limelight. One last thing to note before we proceed, and keep in mind throughout the investigation, is that the subject at hand is of a highly political nature and must be seen as such at all times. When a religious topic acquires a political character, every piece of evidence on either side of the argument becomes suspect of ideological biases. In the case of Lanka, there’s a clear divide of precisely that nature—The Hindu Right tends to place it in Sri Lanka, the Left in India, if at all. Every theorist in this domain is likely, although not necessarily, an ideologue. So, keep that in mind as we go through every piece of evidence advanced in support of either theory.

## From the Horse’s Mouth

We already saw at the beginning of this article the verse that placed Lanka as a magical realm of superhuman beings across the _samudra_ , _i.e._ sea. That’s _Sundara Kanda_. Even before that, Valmiki drops another mention of the place in _Kishkindha Kanda_.

     _ito dvīpe samudrasya saṃpūrṇe śatayojane,
    tasmiṃl laṅkā purī ramyā nirmitā viśvakarmaṇā._
    “The beautiful city of Lanka is an island, a hundred yojanas away from here. It was built by Visvakarma.” (trans. Kataka)

This time, there’s far less ambiguity around the location of the demon king’s capital. Not only is it a hundred _yojanas_ away from the mainland as reiterated in Sundara Kanda, but it’s also a bona fide island with the samudra all around it.34 Why should there be any debate on the matter then? Valmiki is, after all, the author of the whole story. So, it’s only fair that Lanka is where he says it is. While this in and itself is conclusive, so far as Valmiki’s work is concerned, we have more. In Sundara Kanda, again, there’s yet another verse where Hanuman, while still in flight, catches sight of Lanka which Valmiki describes as an island covered in trees, gardens, and a mountain named Malaya:5

     _dadarśa ca patanneva vividhadrumabhūṣitam,
    dvīpaṁ śākhāmṛgaśreṣṭho malayopavanāni ca._
    “Hanuman, the best among jumpers, even while in air he saw an island adorned with many trees, also gardens adjacent to Malaya mountain.” (trans. Kataka)

This verse serves to further reinforce Lanka’s identification as a _dvīpa_ , or island, across the sea from the mainland where Hanuman resides. Interestingly, though, the verse in question is 5.1.201, and the Critical Edition of Sundara Kanda jumps from 5.1.190 straight to 5.2.1.6 In the Gitapress Hindi translation, the verse is listed as 5.1.204 instead of 5.1.201.7 The conspicuous absence from CE concerns, but we’ll go with the Gitapress version anyway. The mountain is a crucial piece of the puzzle that we also encounter in 5.2.30 where Hanuman sits on top of it to strategize the search of Sita:8

     _tataḥ sa cintayāmāsa muhūrtaṃ kapikuñjaraḥ,
    giriśṛṅge sthitastasmin rāmasyābhyudaye rataḥ._
    “Standing on the mountain, the elephant among monkeys pondered for a while on the means of finding Sita, in which lay the welfare of Rama.” (trans. Kataka)

For some reason, 5.2.1, the verse at the beginning of this article, refers to what seems to be the same mountain as Trikuta. Some see the two as distinct peaks, while others see them synonymously. If Valmiki’s Lanka were in Sri Lanka, there must be a mountain to go with it. We know the Island is largely flat lowlands, but the south-central upcountry is mountainous. But that isn’t enough. For a peak to qualify as scripturally special, it ought to stand out. And sure enough, one does. It’s officially known as Adam’s Peak and measures more than 7,000 feet in height. This feature is special, not just to the Hindus, but also to the Christians, the Muslims, and the Buddhists. And the reason is a strange rock formation near its summit that resembles a gigantic footprint. Who the “footprint” belongs to depends on who we ask. To Hindus it’s Hanuman, to Muslims and Christians it’s Adam, and to Buddhists it’s Buddha. At any rate, popular tradition has identified this peak with the mountain Hanuman perched on upon his entry into Lanka, and for good reason.

Having said that, does Adam’s Peak really qualify? Trikuta means three peaks in Sanskrit. Probably Malaya is one of them? But the number three is key here. Adam’s Peak is a solitary mountain. Some scholars get around this problem by bringing in perspective. From certain vantage points, it’s possible to see Adam’s Peak as accompanied by two or more other peaks in the distance. This should come as no surprise given the region’s mountainous character. But is that sufficient to make the connection? Yes. At least, the Buddhists think so.

The Mahayana canon has a whole work dedicated to Buddha’s arrival into the island nation of Lanka. It’s called the _Lankavatara Sutra_. In the very first chapter, this work establishes an association between Buddha and Malaya, between Malaya and Lanka, and between Lanka and Ravana.9 Interestingly, Ravana is shown here in a positive light as someone who invites Buddha to his castle on top of the Malaya.10 Of course, Buddha obliges and begins preaching to the residents of the island which includes rakshasas, _yakshas_ , and other superhuman beings, exactly as described in the Ramayana.

Thus, we can see there’s at least a reasonable agreement on Sri Lanka’s candidacy between Valmiki and the Buddhists. The mountain question remains to be conclusively answered, though. But here’s a small issue with taking the Mahayana canon as evidence to prove the Ramayana—it’s timeline. Even earliest of Mahayana works do not go beyond the first century BC, which makes the entire canon younger than Valmiki by several centuries. Even within this corpus, not all works emerged simultaneously. Lankavatara Sutra, the work of our interest here, is typically placed as late as the fourth century AD.11 While certain parts may have an older vintage, we must remember that the first Buddhist expedition to the island only went during Aśoka’s reign which is roughly in the vicinity of 250 BC. Whichever way we slice it, Valmiki predates Lankavatara Sutra by centuries. And a later work cannot serve as evidence for an older work. We will revisit the mountain when we’re on the subject of geography. For now, let’s explore yojana.

## How Many Yojanas?

In ancient India, distances and lengths were measured in a variety of esoteric units and yojana was one of them. Valmiki too calculates the distance of Lanka from the mainland in this unit. A most straightforward test of Sri Lanka’s candidacy should then be to just match this value with the distance between India and Sri Lanka. Valmiki says hundred yojanas. The shortest hop across the Palk Strait today is thirty-five miles. But that isn’t very helpful, for the journey would have been from Rameshwaram, the starting point of the expedition, to Sigiriya, the site of Ravana’s palace. As the crow flies, these two points are a little over 135 miles apart. With that piece of information, all we’re left to do is convert yojana to miles and tally the values. So how long is a yojana? This is where we run into our first problem. The value depends on who we ask.

Indian cosmology largely derives from _Surya Suddhanta_ , a treatise that’s been dated to the middle of the first millennium AD. While it does not quite define the unit for us, it offers ample insights. This text measures the Earth’s diameter as 1,600 yojanas.12 Today we know the value to be a little under 8,000 miles. If Surya Siddhanta is right about the calculation, we get five miles to a yojana. But from more or less the same period we also have Aryabhata who uses an eight-mile yojana for his calculations.13 In a space of mere years, as we can see, the value has swung a whopping 60%. In 1911, a French paper posited yet another value based on information gleaned from _Markandeya Purana_ and other Common Era works—15,340 meters.14 Or a little over 9.5 miles. Now the gap is even wider. What was the value during Valmiki’s time?

While the precise dating of the epic is outside the scope of this investigation, we can agree that it’s closer to Kautilya’s _Arthashastra_ than to the Puranas. And Arthashastra not only mentions yojana but even defines it. English Civil Servant John Faithfull Fleet studied this definition and published a paper in the 1912 issue of the Journal of the Royal Asiatic Society. In this paper, he arrives at 4.54 miles as the value according to Kautilya.15 But that isn’t all. He further adds that even at the time of Kautilya, there were multiple definitions in currency. There was what he calls a _short yojana_ of 4.5 miles and a _long yojana_ of about 9 miles. The former was popular in Magadha and in Mauryan and Buddhist circles. The latter found prominence in astronomical works at large.16

Putting it all together, we get Magadhan values of 4.5 miles and 9 miles, Surya Siddhanta’s 5 miles, and Aryabhata’s 8 miles, in chronological order. Which of these was Valmiki’s value? Before we pick one, Fleet also questions Surya Siddhanta’s 5 miles. We arrived at this value by dividing the Earth’s diameter in miles by the diameter given in Surya Siddhanta in yojanas. One may and must wonder, as Fleet does, if the ascription of modern values to an ancient calculation is the right approach.17 With that, Fleet posits that the treatise is more likely to have used the long yojana for its calculations arriving at a very incorrect 14,400 miles.18 If we entertain this idea, our choices narrow down to 4.5 miles and 9 miles. We may disregard Aryabhata in this context. These are still wildly divergent values.

Valmiki’s hundred yojanas, therefore, are either 450 miles or 900 miles. To recall, Rameshwaram to Sigiriya is less than 140 miles. Not even in the ballpark. A straight line from Sigiriya would reach Bangalore and still be less than 400 miles. So, the hundred yojana from Hanuman’s start and end points is clearly fraught with issues.

There are three ways to resolve this. One is to move Lanka out of Sri Lanka, in which case it could be any point on the Indian mainland and Hanuman would have likely flown northward. But that doesn’t track because we need a large water body. Another option is to move the starting point further inland. If we revisit the verse that mentions Lanka’s distance, it’s a character called Sampati who informs Hanuman’s search party of the hundred yojana distance. Sampati is the elder brother of Jatayu, the anthropomorphic vulture. And he did not meet Hanuman in Rameshwaram. Instead, as Valmiki informs us in 4.58.7, the conversation takes place in the Vindhya region where Sampati has been living ever since he lost his wings:1920

     _nirdagdhapakṣaḥ patito vindhye'haṃ vānarottamāḥ,
    ahamasmin vasanbhrātuḥ pravṛttiṃ nopalakṣaye._

The Vindhya Range cuts horizontally across Madhya Pradesh, lying north of the Narmada River. Valmiki does not clarify where on this stretch the meetup takes place. It’s a 675-mile stretch that starts from Gujarat and ends in Chhattisgarh, a remarkably vague identification. A rough estimate of the distance can still be made. A line drawn from Sigiriya to the southernmost points in Madhya Pradesh stretches a little under a thousand miles. If we convert it to Fleet’s long yojana, we get a pretty acceptable value of 111 yojanas. Which comfortably addresses the distance question without moving Lanka outside Sri Lanka. But we still run into a problem a few verses down the line. In 4.57.25, Sampati tells the search party exactly how to get to Lanka:2122

     _ādyaḥ panthāḥ kuliṅgānāṃ ye cānye dhānyajīvinaḥ,
    dvitīyo balibhojānāṃ ye ca vṛkṣaphalāśinaḥ._
    “O monkeys traverse a hundred yojanas across the sea. On reaching the southern coast you will be able to see Ravana’s Lanka defended and surrounded by the sea. Now proceed swiftly and prove your strength.” (trans. Kataka)

Here, he specifically gives hundred yojanas as the distance across the sea, _i.e._ from coast to coast. Which brings us back to where we started. Coast-to-coast is less than a hundred miles, a far cry from Sampati’s nine hundred. And that’s where the third solution comes in.

What if Valmiki did not mean hundred yojanas in a numerically literal sense? It could just be his way of vaguely indicating a very long distance. We do that all the time. Like when you tell someone you haven’t seen them in a thousand years. So, Lanka is far from the Vindhyas, and it is also far away from Rameshwaram. This idea receives support from Hanuman’s encounter with the Naga goddess Surasa. In the famous encounter, Hanuman, on his way to Lanka, is stopped by Surasa who demands that he enter her mouth. To outwit her, Hanuman grows in size, prompting Surasa to widen her jaws further each time. Finally, he shrinks to a tiny form, swiftly enters and exits her mouth, fulfilling her demand and winning her blessings for his mission. The largest Hanuman grows, in 5.1.149, is ninety yojanas:2324

     _hanūmānacala prakhyo navatiṃ yojanocchritaḥ,
    cakāra surasā vaktraṃ śatayojanamāyatam._
    “Surasa widened her mouth to eighy yojanas and the heroic Hanuman grew up to ninety yojanas, looking like a mountain.” (trans. Kataka)

If Hanuman could grow to ninety yojanas, would he really need to fly in order to cross hundred yojanas of water? The only way to avoid the absurdity is to not take the numbers literally. Lanka is not a hundred yojanas away, it’s _very far away_. Surasa did not open her mouth eighty yojanas wide, she opened her mouth _very wide_. Hanuman did not grow to ninety yojanas. He grew _very large_ , larger than Surasa’s mouth. With this abstraction, we can easily escape the problems posed by numerical anomalies and inconsistencies. But what about Lanka? We still don’t know for sure. With the unit of distance abstracted, Lanka could be placed anywhere without a problem. So long as it’s surrounded by the sea. Sri Lanka fits the bill.

## Bridging the Ocean

Hanuman flew to Lanka. But those who couldn’t built a bridge. What Valmiki called Ram Setu still connects India and Sri Lanka as has been noted in a number of satellite images. If the bridge exists, so does Lanka. If the bridge leads to Sri Lanka, that’s where Lanka ought to be. In other words, Ram Setu supports Sri Lanka’s candidacy.

The feature is essentially a chain of limestone shoals in the shallow waters of the Gulf of Mannar and the Palk Strait. The chain spans roughly thirty miles between Pamban Island, also known as Rameshwaram Island, on the Indian side and the Mannar Island on the Sri Lankan side. While much of it is now underwater, some sections still rise above the water in certain areas. It consists primarily of sandbanks, coral reefs, and shoals, making it difficult for large vessels to navigate through the strait. When Islam came to the region, the structure acquired a new name. They mythologized that Adam, after his expulsion from Eden, descended onto what came to be known as Adam’s Peak and crossed into India via this causeway, hence calling it “Adam’s Bridge.” Of course, Valmiki predates these mythologies by over a thousand years.

Before we go any further, here’s a small detour. In 2022, Discovery released to its streaming platform a three-part show called _Legends of the Ramayana with Amish_. It was hosted by the renowned fiction author Amish Tripathi and followed Rama’s 3,000-mile journey from Ayodhya to Lanka. Along the way, Amish explores key sites and their geography gleaning information from both scripture and local folklore. The show went on to win a Best Documentary award for its depth and research. One of the topics discussed in the show is Ram Setu. It’d be a good idea to watch it before continuing.

So, the question is, is the feature truly Valmiki’s bridge? If so, the question of Lanka’s location becomes moot. Let’s start with the Pamban Island. On the extreme southeastern tip of this island is the ghost town of Dhanushkodi. From this point, Sri Lanka is a mere nineteen miles across shallow waters. By today’s standards, a bridge of this length is not an insurmountable challenge. Dhanushkodi used to be a thriving frontier village until a devastating storm in 1964 wiped it off the map. Many pre-storm structures still stand here. Interestingly, they’re all built from locally sourced corals. As calcium carbonate, dead corals are remarkably sturdy while still lightweight. And this is where we have our first piece of evidence—corals.

We know that the _vanaras_ , led by Nala and Neela, built the bridge by dumping enchanted boulders inscribed with “Rama” into the sea. While rocks would normally sink in water, these didn’t, thanks to the magic inscription. Dump enough boulders and you have something of a pontoon bridge, nineteen miles across. Problem is, it still fails to account for the current. Won’t the fast-moving ocean currents wash away any floatable objects dropped into the waters? Not necessarily, according to geologist Dr. Ritesh Arya who explains the entire process to Amish Tripathi in his Discovery show. He points out that the stretch between Dhanushkodi and Sri Lanka is a ridge of sandbars formed by deposition from two currents—one from the Bay of Bengal and the other from the Arabian Sea. Think of it as two opposing currents canceling each other out. It is only the gaps in this ridge that were filled by the vanaras with corals and pumice. This notion makes sense, suggesting that such a task could be accomplished in under a week.

All of this, however, remains little more than mere conjecture at this point. Proponents of the theory found solid ground when a short 2017 video by Discovery’s Science Channel featured Chelsea Rose, a researcher from Southern Oregon University, who supported the theory with scientific evidence. Her view aligns well with that of Dr. Arya—that the vanaras filled the gaps in the sandbars with corals. She claims to substantiate this with geological dating, noting that the rocks sitting atop the sandbars are three thousand years older than the underlying sandbars themselves.25

Conclusion? They must have been placed there by humans. But she does not disclose the dating methods used or their accuracy. Additionally, given the cyclic nature of sea levels in the region, it is natural for older coral polyps to grow over fresh deposits. In fact, since the end of the last Ice Age about 11,000 years ago, the average global sea level has risen by nearly 400 feet as shown in at least one climate study of repute.26 And corals thrive in rising sea levels.27

Furthermore, it is quite plausible for such shallow sea platforms to have been utilized by various peoples to cross from one side to the other. None of this definitively establishes human intervention in the making of this causeway. Shallow sea platforms like this are actually far from rare or unnatural.

In short, are sandbars possible? Of course, they are. Geologists call them tombolos. And there is a whole bunch of them all over the world. One excellent example is Macao. Today part of mainland China, the city was a separate island until as recently as the eighteenth century. Then a tombolo started forming between the mainland and the island just like the one between India and Sri Lanka and soon enough, the island was no more an island.

So once again, do tombolos or sandbars exist? Yes.

Can there be gaps in a tombolo? More often than not.

Can humans fill those gaps with debris and rocks? They absolutely can.

Is that the case with Ram Setu? Not with the evidence we have so far.

But what about the rocks inscribed with Rama’s name? We sure have found those around Rameshwaram, right? We haven’t. But that’s also because no such rocks were used in Ramayana. Nowhere in his composition does Valmiki mention the writing of anything on the rocks, nor does he mention floating rocks. It’s Nala who possesses this remarkable talent for building bridges, something he claims to have inherited from his legendary father. By the way, there is no Neela in the bridge project either, it’s just Nala. The enchantment as well as Neela’s involvement are both _Ramcharitmanas_ interpolations.

According to Valmiki, the setu was a straightforward land reclamation project in which objects were dumped into the water to construct a connecting ridge from the seabed upward. In other words, it was a wall more than a pontoon link. The materials used included rocks but not exclusively. Much of it consisted of various types of trees, including _sala_ or _Shorea robusta_. Put a pin on this name, we’ll return to it later.

Do note here that verses 6.22.44–74, that describe in vivid details the construction project,28 are conspicuously absent from the Critical Edition. The latter ends abruptly at 6.22.43.29 We get these in the Hindi translation by Gitapress, albeit with ever so slightly different numbering.30

To sum it all up, we are left with little in the way of evidence to support a man-made land-bridge between Rameshwaram and Sri Lanka. A land bridge exists that may have been above water at some point in the past, but that would be thousands of years before Valmiki. Without Ram Setu, locating Lanka in Sri Lanka won’t be easy. But we’re not done yet.

## Flora and Fauna

A most interesting mention in 6.22.72 (74 in the Gitapress edition) is that of _makara_ :31

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _sa nalena kṛtaḥ setuḥ sāgare makara-ālaye,
    śuśubhe subhagaḥ śrīmān svātī patha iva ambare._
    “That beautiful and lovely bridge constructed by Nala across the ocean the abode of alligators, shone brightly like a milky way of stars in the sky.” (trans. Rao and Murthy)

The Sanskrit term _Makara_ refers to an alligator—a species not suited to the ocean. Although alligators can tolerate salt water for a while, they are fundamentally freshwater creatures, confined to swamps, rivers, streams, lakes, and ponds.32 If Nala and his team encountered alligators in the water, it would have been unlikely they were in the sea. Some also interpret the word as crocodiles, in which case, the situation changes a bit, because saltwater crocodiles or _Crocodylus porosus_ exist. And Coastal Sri Lanka is very much part of their habitat. But they too are known to stay closer to the coastline, prefer brackish mangrove swamps and estuaries, and rarely venture into open seas. Could there be a few in the shallow waters between Rameshwaram and Mannar? Possibly, though not highly probable. Furthermore, these amphibians are more commonly found in the island’s southwestern regions rather than the north.33 A third translation of makara as goat or Capricorn is immaterial to this conversation.

This is a good time to return to that pin we dropped on _Shorea robusta_. Commonly known as sal, this is a large deciduous or semi-deciduous tree that can grow up to well over a hundred feet tall, with a sturdy trunk often over five feet in diameter and rough, brown bark. Its simple, ovate leaves are thick and leathery, measuring about six to ten inches long and four to six inches wide, turning a striking orange-red before falling. Flowering occurs from February thru April, featuring small, yellowish-white flowers, and its winged nuts, or fruits, mature in May or June. Sal is native to the Indian subcontinent and thrives in tropical and subtropical climates, preferring well-drained soils of sandy loam or clay. It predominantly populates mixed moist and dry deciduous forests in central and eastern India, Nepal, and Bangladesh. And the reason we’re discussing it here is its use in the making of Ram Setu.

In 2.22.54 (56 in the Gitapress edition), Valmiki lists sal trees among the objects Nala’s team dumped into the sea to build the bridge. But the tree is native neither to southern India nor to Sri Lanka. In fact, roughly below the Vindhyas, it’s all teak country. Sal is confined to the north of this line, even then, mostly to the east.

This habitat segregation was extensively studied and documented by a joint team of Japanese and Thai researchers in a 1998 paper.34 And this segregation isn’t incidental or random either. Teak or _Tectona grandis_ prefers Vertisols and Chromic Luvisols, two soil classes typically associated with the basaltic Deccan Traps. Sal, on the other hand, needs Ferric Luvisols and Dystric Cambisols, which are found only in northeastern reaches of the Peninsula and the Himalayan foothills respectively.35

If Nala’s men used sal as part of their aggregate, they couldn’t possibly be anyplace in the Deccan, much less on the eastern coast. Unless they hauled those from the north which makes zero logistical or technical sense. This one small detail in Valmiki’s narration deals a formidable blow to Sri Lanka’s candidacy. But the island nation is yet to throw in the towel.

## Saltwater vs. Freshwater

If Lanka were located anywhere on the mainland and surrounded by water, that water would have to be freshwater. We have established that the bridge was over a water body infested by freshwater amphibians. And that it was built using timber that does not grow anywhere near the sea. But a single verse in _Bala Kanda_ throws us off course. In 1.1.57 (72 in some editions), Valmiki mentions, for the first time, the term _lavaṇārṇavam_ :36

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _tato gṛdhrasya vacanāt saṃpāterhanumānbalī
    śatayojanavistīrṇaṃ pupluve lavaṇārṇavam._
    “After that, the strong Hanuman, upon the word of the vulture Sampati who was elder brother of Jatayu, flew across the salty ocean one hundred yojanas in breadth” (trans. Rao and Murthy)

The word is a portmanteau of _lavaṇa_ (salt)37 and _arṇava_ (sea).38 Arṇava means sea in a way neither samudra nor _sagara_ do. It alludes to the violent nature of the ocean’s currents, with a reference to the foam they make as they crash onto the rocks. Which makes arṇava the most unambiguous term for the sea by far, since rivers don’t form surf. Lavaṇa serves to further reinforce this reference. Now things change. Lanka is surrounded not just by any water, but seawater. In the most unambiguous terms ever.

But how do we reconcile this with sal and crocodiles? Lanka cannot be on the mainland because saltwater, and it cannot be in Sri Lanka because sal and freshwater crocs. A possible solution to this comes from a paper on Ramayana’s geography published in 2006 in a Hindi journal. In this paper, author Rai Kisandas theorizes that Valmiki’s lavaṇārṇavam is quite likely a lake on the Indian mainland.39 While this might sound outlandish, such inland saltwater reservoirs are neither impossible nor improbable. The Great Salt Lake of Utah, as the name implies, is saltwater and completely landlocked. As is the famous Dead Sea, despite its name, and dozens of others. Closer home, we have the Namtso Lake in Tibet, the Lonar Crater Lake in Maharashtra and the Sambhar Lake in Rajasthan, all saltwater and all inland. Clearly salt is not a maritime monopoly.

We still have arṇava to contend with, but if all else checks out, this could be easily explained away as a figure of speech for a difficult water body, just as a hundred yojanas is a figure of speech for a very large distance. But figure of speech may not be the only defense here. Deep in the tropical woodlands of eastern Madhya Pradesh, where the Vindhya meets the Satpura, three rivers originate. Narmada is one of them. Here lies the ancient pilgrim town of Amarkantak to the south of which, Rai notes, still lies a _paragana_ named Lavana.40 The interesting thing about Lavanais that its elevation is significantly lower than its surroundings, forming something of a trough. It’s quite possible that at some point in the past the place was full of water. The name Lavana might be indicative of its composition. Rai also speaks of an impregnable swamp around the place that existed until as recently as the last century, which lends further credence to the lake conjecture. He adds that a British Chief Commissioner of Central Provinces tried to navigate this swamp but didn’t succeed.41 We will discuss this a bit more in a later section.

Some experts go as far as to altogether remove Bala Kanda and _Uttara Kanda_ from the equation as later interpolation, but that is neither the right approach, nor necessary. While Uttara Kanda is doubtlessly a later addition, only some verses of Bala Kanda are, and 1.1.57 may not be one of them. The fact that the oldest extant manuscripts do not contain Bala Kanda does not conclusively prove that it was a later interpolation in entirety. By the way, despite its status as an interpolation, even Uttara Kanda is crucial to this investigation. We will visit it soon.

We’re still not done with salt. Or Madhya Pradesh. _Malatimadhava_ , an eighth century Sanskrit play, a river named Sindhu finds mention. Today we call it Sindh. On this river stood the city of Padmavati which is now identified with Pawaya in the Gwalior district. While Sindh itself is a tributary of Yamuna, it has tributaries of its own too. Among them, as listed in the play, is one called Lavaṇā, also identified as Lūn-nadī (lit. salt river).42 While it’s extremely rare for a river to carry saltwater, here we have one that apparently does. Of course, this river cannot be the location for the island kingdom we’re looking for, because it’s too insignificant, but it certainly establishes a precedent. At any rate, saltwater rivers are a rarity, not an impossibility. The largest river in the Thar Desert is named Luni River precisely for its excessive salinity. Outside of India, there’s a literal Salt River in Arizona.

Lanka is now comfortably back in the mainland. Before we move out of this section, let’s revisit the crocodiles one last time. We have learned that the water body that Nala bridged was infested with makaras or crocodiles. We also know that the term makara, now more commonly recognized in its corrupted form _muggar_ , could refer to alligators, freshwater crocodiles, estuarine (saltwater) crocodiles, or _gharials_ (also called gavials). While the Ganges is a well-established habitat for gavials, these creatures are not dangerous to humans, as they feed primarily on fish and other small river creatures and generally avoid human encounters. So, these are unlikely to be the fearsome makaras of Nala’s Dakshina Sagara. Which rules out the Ganges. Since the Amarkantak theory satisfies both saltwater and sala stipulations, any presence of a croc-like amphibian there should further bolster its case. Bringing it all together, it’s possible to succinctly articulate what we’re looking for—a large water body, preferably close to Amarkantak, that is home to a large number of crocodiles or alligators and is prominently associated with the story of Ramayana. This association, if possible, should be through Ravana. Is there any name that qualifies? Let’s explore one in the following section.

## Makaravahini

In Uttara Kanda 5.27 (slightly different wording and numbering in the Gitapress edition), a curious mention can be found in a description of Lanka’s formative days, long before Ravana:4344

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
    narmadā nāma gandharvī nānādharmasamedhitā,
    tasyāḥ kanyātrayaṃ hyāsīddhīśrīkīrtisamadyuti.
    “There was a Gandharvi named Narmada. She had three daughters who were resplendent like Hri, Sri, and Kirti.” (rough trans. Gitapress)

Valmiki described Lanka as an abode of Rakshasas, Nagas, and Yakshas. Gandharvas more often than not appear with Yakshas in much of Hindu mythology, some were even part of Kubera’s Yaksha army. So, the presence of a Gandharvi in Lanka doesn’t surprise. But this one is named Narmada. Does that ring a bell? Narmada is one of the three rivers that originate near Amarkantak. Could it be the water body we’re looking for?

This isn’t the only mention of Narmada in the interpolation. Sarga 31 recounts the story of Ravana’s encounter with and defeat at the hands of a Haihaya king named Arjuna.4546 The Haihaya were a clan of Yadava kings who ruled a region approximately corresponding to present-day Malwa in Madhya Pradesh. Their capital was the ancient city of Mahishmati, located on the banks of the Narmada River. Arjuna—variously known as Kartavirya Arjuna, Sahasrabahu Arjuna, and Sahasrarjuna—was a mythical king of this dynasty and was noted for his thousand arms. In Uttara Kanda, Ravana is said to have visited Mahishmati to challenge the king to a duel with the intention of usurping his kingdom. When he arrived, Arjuna was enjoying a bath in a river with his women. This river is explicitly named Narmada in 7.31.8 (9 in the Gitapress edition).4748

In the rest of the hymn, the mention repeats multiple times as the river is described in the most flattering manner. Of particular interest are verses 22 thru 25 where the word _jalaphena_ is used to describe the river’s foam.4950 If you recall, foam was a defining characteristic of the sea when we encountered the term arṇava earlier. Arṇava defaults to sea only because it needs foam. But foam isn’t exclusive to the sea as rivers crashing over rocks produce it too, as seen at whitewater rafting sites. The author of the Uttara Kanda clearly observed this phenomenon as well. Furthermore in 7.32.9, the author straight-up compares Narmada’s rising waters with the ocean’s tide.5152 The word sagara is used to eliminate any ambiguity in interpretation.

A most curious case is that of 7.31.27 where the second line starts with the compound _līnamīnavihaṅgormiḥ_ ,53 which translates into “waves in which fish and birds are hidden.” Nothing outstanding here, but this in Critical Edition. In the Gitapress edition, the same verse appears as 7.31.30 and the compound changes to _nakramīnavihaṅgormiḥ_.54 This changes the meaning. _Nakra_ is a synonym of makara, means crocodile or alligator.55 Now, Narmada becomes a river of crocodiles, at least in one version of Uttara Kanda. Further reinforcement for Narmada’s case comes from the abundance of crocodile-themed toponyms all over Madhya Pradesh, especially along this river. An inscription near Bhedaghat, for instance, records a village named Makarapāṭaka on the right bank of the river.56 This is some 160 miles west of Amarkantak and very close to Jabalpur. About four miles further west, accurately fulfilling the description, lies the village of Magaramuha.57 Similarly, we have villages like Makrohar, Makari, Magardha, Makronia, Magrora, and others, most of them in eastern Madhya Pradesh,58 the state with possibly the highest concentration of _makara-_ and _magara_ -themed place names in the world. Perhaps that’s why one of Narmada’s names is also Makaravahini, a name by which it’s still worshipped in at least one temple near Jabalpur.59 The goddess here is shown seated on a crocodile. But even Makaravahini isn’t the only assertion of Narmada’s crocodilian connection, we also have Nakreśi.60 Nakra, if you recall, is another Sanskrit term for crocodiles and perhaps alligators.

While the case for Narmada seems strong, we still have a few problems. Sri Lanka has a long list of archeological and historical sites that establish a long-standing relationship between the island nation and Ramayana. Placing Lanka within the Indian mainland would mean dismissing them all, not a wise move. We must examine every last one of them and satisfy evidence, if any, against their claims before we can even consider discarding them as immaterial. Let’s do just that now.

## Landmarks in Sri Lanka

Remember the Discovery show we spoke of a little earlier, the one hosted by Amish Tripathi? For this section, we will just follow everything posited in that show because it really is that extensive. One of the experts interviewed in it is Suna Jayawardhane, author of _The Line of Lanka_. Just as Amish is an expert on all things Ram, Suna is on all things Ravan. In the show, he tells us of Lakegala, a place deep in the woods, about two hours to the south of Sigiriya by road, where Ravana had his main citadel. The place still remains largely inaccessible or at least prohibitively difficult to access, but Ravana, of course, did not face such problems because he flew.

Ravana’s biggest scientific achievement, by the way, wasn’t his invincibility. It was aviation. Thousands of years before the Wrights, he is said to have aced the skies and no fewer than eleven place names all over the island attest to this fact. From Wariyapola to Variyakuttiruva to Varipathanchenai, all names, Suna explains, relate to aviation. He further informs us that these locations served as Ravana’s airstrips. One name could be coincidence, but almost a dozen? Surely, they were up to something out there.

The assertion, however, while it thrills, runs into problems etymology-wise. The common thread across all these words is _wariya_ (වාරිය). And Google translates it as irrigation. Wariya itself is a declension of _wari_ (වාරි) which is cognates with Sanskrit _vār_ (वार्), meaning water. The Hindi word for rain, _barish_ (बारिश) traces the same etymology. As we can see, all names pertain to water, and aviation demands air. A 2014 paper in the Journal of the Royal Asiatic Society of Sri Lanka echoes this observation and calls out the “imagined etymology” of “air-chariot-place” in the case of Wariyapola.61 In the same vein, the paper also questions the legitimacy of Seetha Eliya, a village deep in the tea country of Sri Lanka’s Central Province. This village is celebrated for the Seetha Amman Temple which is believed to be the site of Sita’s captivity. While visited by thousands of pilgrims each year, the name apparently did not show up on census records until 1881, conveniently _after_ the arrival of plantation workers from India.62

The temple at Seetha Eliya is special because it’s one of the rare few primarily dedicated to Sita. Typically, she’s always an accompaniment to Rama in Ramayana-themed temples. There are plenty of temples with only Rama or only Hanuman, but not only Sita. While both Amish and Suna, along with millions of others, see it as the site of Ravana’s notorious Ashoka Vatika, they also advance as evidence the footprint on Adam’s Peak, some 45 miles to the west. More than five feet long and a foot and a half across, this enormous footprint is believed to be of Hanuman when he first visited Sita. This does not track, not just because of the absurd dimensions, but because even taking it on face value, it’s hard to explain unless Hanuman landed on soft lava. Rocks are brittle by nature. And the last time there was lava in the region was millions of years ago. Also, why a solitary footprint? There ought to be at least two, unless he stood and sat the whole time on just one limb.

Some thirty miles from Seetha Eliya, in Uva Province, lies the town of Ella. Here, Amish introduces us to T. G. Mahesh Priyadarshan, a local speleologist. According to legends, a cave in this area served as another hiding place where Ravana kept Sita, and it is now known as the Ravana Cave, although the name is a more recent innovation. Inside, a network of tunnels and secret passages is said to have been constructed by Ravana’s father-in-law. Priyadarshan describes these features as “definitely man-made using chemical engineering,” but he does not elaborate on the nature of engineering involved. Notably, cave labyrinths like this exist globally and are formed entirely without human intervention. A famous example can be found in Kentucky’s Mammoth Cave System, a place that Ravana’s father-in-law could never have visited.

Next up is Shashi Dhanatunge, a former Vice Chairman of the Sri Lankan Civil Aviation Authority and a history buff. Amish introduces him as an expert who has extensively researched Sri Lanka’s “ancient aviation legacy.” Dhanatunge takes us to another cave deep in the jungles of Kurunegala in the North Western Province and points to the rock canopy at its mouth. He highlights a string of mysterious ancient symbols inscribed on the rock face, including one that looks like an arrowhead or an aircraft, depending on who you ask. He claims that the symbols are “definitely 5,000-6,000 years old” and were drawn by some “ancient astronaut.”

Even if we were to dismiss everything uncanny and improbable as pseudoscience, the symbols deserve attention. Why would someone from that long ago draw airplanes on rocks? Additionally, Dhanatunge isn’t the only expert to have examined these artifacts. A paper in the Royal Asiatic publication mentioned earlier references another scholar known only by his last name, Kiriella. This gentleman not only supports Dhanatunge’s theory but also proposes an even bolder dating of 17,500 BC.63 Worth pointing out that this is long before civilization, agriculture, or even writing. We just dismissed Priyadarshan’s chemical engineering claims using Kentucky’s Mammoth Cave as an example because there’s no way characters from Ramayana could have visited America. But Dhanatunge counters that notion with claims of evidence that Ravana ruled much of the western hemisphere as well.64

The same paper then quotes epigraphist and archeologist Malini Dias who squarely dismisses the whole thing as an elaborate hoax. The inscription at Ravana’s Cave, she confirms, were painted not by mysterious men from the Stone Age but a cab driver named Pathiraarchchi.65 The paper also confirms that the Seetha Amman Temple at Seetha Eliya was actually built by Tamil laborers in the nineteenth century.66

Coming back to Shashi, he also claims that all of Ravana’s “airstrips” were located on plateaus in order to “harvest wind energy.” He even names the tech involved here—mercury vortex engine. Although he doesn’t elaborate any further on what this really is, others have.

It all comes from a mysterious ancient text called _Vaimanika Shastra_. If ancient meant a little less than the First World War. The text was composed by a man named Brahmamuni Parivrajaka in 1959 and translated by another named Pandit Subbaraya Shastry fourteen years later. The work speaks of fuels made by mixing together urine with dung and mercury, besides other equally wacky items, mostly food. How that would work as propulsion, is anyone’s guess. But should one still be curious, there’s this excellent paper from 1974 by five scientists from the Indian Institute of Science. It’s titled _A Critical Study of the Work Vyamanika Shastra_ and offers a painfully detailed analysis of the “technology” in question.67

Clearly, the claim of Ramayana’s aviation satisfies neither science nor logic. But the flights of fantasy don’t stop here. According to the show, Ravana was not just flying between cities or kingdoms, he was doing interstellar. Not too far from his purported capital at Sigiriya lie the historic ruins of Anuradhapura. Here sits a strange rock that bears an inscribed chart called Sakwala Chakraya. Amish and many others see it as some kind of an ancient “stargate,” a portal to outer space, perhaps even time travel. Shashi adds further mystique to the artifact when he claims that every once in a while, the chart still emits “energy.” What energy could possibly emanate from a rock inscription, he doesn’t elaborate. But if not stargate, what could it possibly be?

Prof. Raj Somadeva of Colombo’s Postgraduate Institute of Archaeology has a far less exotic explanation—the drawing is a primitive map of the world as known to the locals when they drew it.68 Such strange artifacts exist all over the world in every civilization. There’s a whole cult of conspiracy theorists who see time-travel portals and extraterrestrial actions in everything from the Pyramids to the Great Wall to even the ruins of Macchu Picchu. So this stargate theory shouldn’t surprise. But let’s, for the sake of the argument, concede that Sakwala Chakraya really is some cosmic stargate. Fine, that still doesn’t make Ravana special as the Chakraya itself is far from unique even of its own wacky kind. What Prof. Somadeva said was only an echo of a postulation made more than a hundred years ago by British archeologist Harry Charles Purvis Bell, who likened it with other petroglyphs performing the same function from around the world—the Abu Ghurab Sun Temple of Egypt, the Gateway to the Gods of Peru, and others.69

So far, we haven’t found one Ramayana site or artifact in Sri Lanka that could hold up to even a hint of scrutiny. But we’re yet to explore it all. Remember the mountain Hanuman tore off the Himalayas? They say part of it fell upon Unawatuna and became what’s today known as Rumassala Hill. Some as credible as the famous sci-fi author Arthur C. Clarke spent years researching this geography. His findings?

Rumassala Hill has the lowest gravitational fields on Earth.

He even goes on to extrapolate that it’s for this reason satellites are programmed to never fly above this hill lest the dip in gravity veer them off course. Amish backs this up with the account of a meteor strike in the neighborhood. This time, they just might be on to something because an authority like NASA seems to agree. In 2003, the Gravity Recovery and Climate Experiment, a joint mission of NASA and the German Aerospace Center, studied the mapped the Earth’s gravity and published a detailed heatmap dubbed “the most accurate ever” for public reference.70 The color coding is blue for lower gravity and red for higher. And sure enough, Sri Lanka is as dark blue as it gets. But so are dozens of spots in Canada, West Indies, China, Nepal, Africa, and even Antarctica. Even in Sri Lanka, it’s the whole island and the surrounding sea, not just one special rock in the middle of the island. Long story short, a grand total of zero evidence supporting Sri Lanka’s case. Now let’s return to Lanka, the one without Sri.

## Etymology of Lanka

A quick note merits making regarding the name’s origin. While Lanka as a toponym was first recorded only in Valmiki’s work, there is no information in the story about where the word came from. Is it a corruption of an older term? A mispronunciation? A repurposed common noun? A derivative of some obscure adjective? A Munda import? We have no answers. Nor does the word have any cognates in other Indo-European vocabularies, as is the case with an overwhelming majority of Sanskrit words, but Monier-Williams offers three additional meanings in his seminal Sanskrit-English lexicon, all common nouns—an unchaste woman, a branch, and a type of grain.71 The same is echoed pretty much verbatim by Indian Sanskrit scholar and lexicographer Vaman Shivaram Apte in his dictionary.72 Both mention the proper noun, but it’s _laṅka_ as common noun that interests this discussion. More specifically, grain.

Grains are the fundamental source of nourishment and wherever they grow, there must be an abundance of freshwater. Could it be a river? In that case, we’re back to Narmada. Why Narmada out of thousands of other rivers? Besides the crocodiles and other ideas already discussed supporting its case, there’s a couple of names traditionally applied to this particular river that stand out—Laṅkā Pālī73 and Laṅkeśī.74 The names translate as protector of laṅka and goddess of laṅka, respectively. If Lanka were in Sri Lanka, it’d be absurd to call a river in Madhya Pradesh its protector or goddess. These epithets only make sense if either Narmada were seen as the life blood of the foodgrains cultivated in its basin, quite natural for any river of prominence, or if Lanka were located in the vicinity. Or both.

Here, the other interpretations of branch and unchaste woman are not relevant because it’d be strange for a river to be named protector and goddess of tree branches or prostitutes. The only reading that makes any sense whatsoever is that of nourishment, that is grains. With this, the case for Narmada becomes even stronger.

While on the topic of etymology, let’s also quickly discuss dvīpa before moving on to the next section. Valmiki has used the term for Lanka on multiple occasions, and we cannot dismiss it without consideration. If Lanka were a large polity as consistently implied by Valmiki, it could not be a river island. Large islands are only possible in large water bodies, like the sea. This might hurt Narmada’s case. But there are ways around this caveat.

Firstly, the term needn’t strictly refer to an island in a literal sense and has often applied to large landmasses without such qualifications, case in point being Jambudvīpa. Secondly, dvīpa’s etymology does not necessitate an island. The word is an early compound of _dvá_ (_द्व_), meaning two, and _áp_ (_अप्_), meaning water. In other words, “between two waters.” For a landmass to be a dvīpa, it just needs water on two sides. While the criteria today has evolved to water on all four sides, it wasn’t always the case. Technically, even a peninsula was a dvīpa. Ditto for land between two rivers like Mesopotamia. Or between a river and a lake. In fact, Monier-Williams defines it as an island, a sandbank, a peninsula, and even just any division of the terrestrial world, depending on the context.75 In other words, Lanka does not _have_ to be a literal island surrounded by the ocean.

## Where is Kishkindha?

The renowned British-era historian Narendra Nath Law had written extensively on the topography of Valmiki’s work. We have already seen his work on Ayodhya, now it’s time for Lanka. In 1928, he published a paper in the Indian Historical Quarterly that discusses Lanka at length. The swamp we put a pin on earlier, features in this paper.

In Kishkindha Kanda, when Rama asks Sugriva about the geography of their surroundings, the latter gave a long narrative of all the landmarks involved in the episode of his flight from his brother who was out to kill him. In 4.46.17, he says that he changed his direction from east to south and came up against the Vindhya Range:7677

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _diśaḥ tasyāḥ tato bhūyaḥ prasthito dakṣiṇam diśam,
    vindhya pādapa saṃkīrṇām candana druma śobhitām._
    “Then chased by Vali, I changed my present direction from the east towards the southern quarter where Vindhya mountain stood with its, beautiful sandal trees.” (trans. Rao and Murthy)

This definitively places Kishkindha in the north of the Vindhyas. This piece is crucial to our conversation, although for some reason, the verse does not appear in the Critical Edition where the episode falls in 4.45 instead of 4.46.78

This is the verse Law picks in his paper to establish that Kishkindha was well within the confines of northern India. He elaborates that it was on the northern slope of the Vindhyas while Lanka was on the southern.79 Law also makes use of the fact that when Sampati, the garuda, tells Rama about the location of Sita’s captivity, he claims to be giving an eye-witness account, something only possible if Lanka were on the other side of the mountain but not in Sri Lanka.80

The slightly more senior Marathi-language historian Chintaman Vinayak Vaidya also wrote extensively about Ramayana’s geography and its various inconsistencies. In his work, The Riddle of the Ramayana, he points out that Vali’s wife Tara was also called Vindhyavali, and takes it as indicative of Kishkindha being in the vicinity of the Vindhyas.81 Law takes this to conclude that the water body on the way to Lanka, therefore, must be a river and not the sea. And that brings us to the swamp.

About two miles from the source of Narmada is a spot canned Bhrigu Ashram. Visible from here is a hill on top of which stands a fort named after Rani Bakavali. Law locates this hill about ten miles from the Pendra Road station.82 This hill is of interest. It is not the easiest place to get to, for it is surrounded by an almost impregnable marsh. At least was when Law published his paper. Remember the Chief Commissioner who tried to cross a swamp? This was it. His name was Sir Richard Temple, and he was keen on reaching the hilltop. Alas, he had to abandon the expedition after his elephant got stuck in the quagmire.83 Law observes that the hilltop seems to be piercing through the mist from across the marsh, which matches Valmiki’s description of Lanka very closely. Among the locals there’s already stories of the fort hiding a treasure, something you’d expect from a mystical city of gold from the folklore.84 Not only does he imagine the marsh filled with water at some point in the past, but he also reiterates what many historians and linguists have already said on several occasions—that sagara is not a term exclusive to the sea.85 A number of lakes all over the country still bear sagara in their names. Valmiki described the sagara around Lanka as abundant in pearls, among other things. Law quotes Rai Bahadur Hiralal as confirming that some of the lakes in the Amarkantak region do indeed have pearls.86

## Secular Literature

We have gleaned all we could from the scripture. Now let’s look at some non-scriptural works that mention Lanka in any way, shape, or form. All these works expectedly come after Ramayana, some long after. If we’re to locate Lanka in Sri Lanka, a most logical place to look would be the island nation’s own history. And the oldest such source is none other than _Mahavamsa_ , the Pali-language chronicle held as the most authoritative record of Sri Lanka’s religious, social, political, and cultural history. If there was ever a Lanka in Sri Lanka, this work must have recorded it.

And it does.

In the chapter on the consecration of Vijaya, there’s a mention of a city inhabited by the yakshas, and it’s named Lanka.87 This should make Sri Lanka a strong contender, but there are two problems with it. Firstly, it’s a fifth-century work, which makes it highly unreliable as a record of anything that would have gone down during the time of Valmiki. Secondly, it makes no mention of Ravana. If a character as influential as him ever ruled any part of the island, it’d be strange for him to be absent in what is held to be the most seminal record of the country’s history.

Another work of a similar vintage that merits attention is Varāhamihira’s _Brihat Samhita_. It’s an encyclopedic Sanskrit text covering a wide array of subjects including astrology, astronomy, architecture, and natural phenomena. Highly influential in the fields of astrology and Indian sciences, it is celebrated for its vast knowledge on diverse topics such as weather, agriculture, gemology, and rituals. But not many know that it also covers geography.

In a chapter titled _Kurma Vibhaga_ , Varahamihira draws upon the Puranas to lay out a fairly detailed map of the world, of course centered around India. He divides the world into nine regions and lists out every kingdom and geographical features for each as known to him. Among the places he lists in the south, are Lanka and Simhalas.88 Simhaladvipa is one of the several names of the island nation, others being Taprobane, Serendib, and Ceylon. The fact that Varahamihira listed the Simhala country separately from Lanka indicates that at least he saw the two places as separate regions and not one.

But these are all works from the Common Era. Let’s get closer to when Valmiki lived. Take Aśoka’s example. We know he sent Buddhist missions to the island, most notably his own son. He also made references to this place in his famous rock edicts. Interestingly, not a single mention of Lanka exists in his entire epigraphical corpus. He does speak of the island, but the name he uses is Tambapanni, Pali for Tāmraparṇi.89 This is also the name that corrupts to Taprobane in Greek writings which started recording the island with Megasthenes’ _Indica_.90 This is even before Aśoka.

[courtesy Sir Edward Herbert Bunbury in “Chapter XVI Eratosthenes,” _A History of Ancient Geography among the Greeks and Romans from the Earliest Ages till the Fall of the Roman Empire_ , John Murray, 1883, p. 667.]

Thus, the closer we get to Valmiki, the rarer Lanka grows as a name for the island nation. There does exist a Thamirabarani or Tamraparni River in Tamil Nadu but it’s clearly an anachronism, so we needn’t take it up here. We could read Tāmraparṇi in Aśoka’s edicts two ways. Either he never sent any mission to Sri Lanka and the name in his edicts refer to some place in Tamil Nadu, or he did and there was no Lanka. Both could be true as well, but we won’t get into investigating Aśoka’s missions this time. Suffice to say that the name Lanka as applied to what we now call Sri Lanka is a more recent phenomenon.

Sometime in the opening decades of the Common Era came the seminal Greek compendium of world geography, the _Periplus of the Erythraean Sea_. In this work, we learn that the island of Taprobane was in some indefinite past known as Simoundou, a name no longer in currency at the time of the Periplus.91 We know that by the mid-first millennium AD, the name Sinhala has gone mainstream in works like Brihat Samhita, either as Sinhala or as any of its derivatives, such as Sinhaladvipa, Sihalam, Silam, etc. The common thread across all these names is _siṃha_ or lion. There are no lions in Sri Lanka, which makes the name a bit of a misfit. But it’s understood that the lion was likely a reference to a heroic character from the island’s history, _i.e._ Vijaya.92 As for the Periplus’ Simoundou, it could easily be explained away as a misheard corruption of Simhala or Simhalam.

Thus, we see that throughout the pre-Common Era, and even the first few centuries of the Common Era, the island has been consistently recorded in non-scriptural works as Taprobane, Simhala, or any of their corruptions. No Lanka whatsoever. Only Mahavamsa records it as Lanka but it’s from a much later period. And that’s where we start seeing a change. In India, litterateurs like Kalidasa start associating Simhala and the idea takes roots very quickly. In his hagiographical work _Raghuvaṃśa_ , Kalidasa alludes to whales in the waters surrounding Lanka, clearly implying that the kingdom is across the sea and not an inland water body.93 From that point on, that is, around 500 AD onward, Lanka’s association with Simhala continues to grow in prominence among Indians. Officially, the nation continued as Simhala, Sinhala, or Simhaladvipa. Taprobane and its iterations had completely fallen out of currency. Then came the Europeans.

The Portuguese were the first Europeans to arrive on the island. They had come here looking for spices and other trade opportunities. This was shortly after Vasco da Gama’s voyage to Goa, at the onset of the sixteenth century. Of course, they could not pronounce the strange non-Portuguese names and hence Simhala became Ceilão in their records. Later when the British came, Ceilão became Ceylon. This is the name that remained in effect until as recently as 1972 when the name was officially changed from Ceylon to Sri Lanka with a new constitution at the behest of the Marxist Lanka Sama Samaja Party. The party no longer exists, but the name continues. As does the misunderstanding.

## Other Theories

While we have only considered Sri Lanka and the Narmada basin—more specifically, Amarkantak—in our investigation so far, other exotic suggestions have been made as well. For example, using the Puranic description of world geography, one V. H. Vader advanced the theory that Lanka ought to be the archipelago of Maldives. He bases his theory on the similarity between the names Maldives and Malayadvīpa, a Mleccha continent described in Vayu Purana.94

In the Javanese version of the epic—there are more than three hundred versions in different parts of the world—Rama’s wife Shinta is abducted by Rahwana, the king of Alengka, which is obviously a corruption of Lanka. The Javanese locate Lanka or Alengka in Sri Lanka. But the version they follow is not directly based on Valmiki’s work. Instead, much of it comes from a seventh century Sanskrit poem called _Bhaṭṭikāvya_ or _Rāvaṇavadha_ ,95 and the rest from a number of indigenous Javanese folklores.

Speaking of Malayadvīpa, there also exist theories reading it as an etymological ancestor of the Malaya Peninsula. Just as Mahavamsa is the national treatise of Sri Lankan history, Hikayat Merong Mahawangsa is the authority on Malaysian history. More specifically, the Malay kingdom of Kedah. For that reason, it’s also often known as the Kedah Annals. Interestingly, this work also has copious references to Rama’s story. Colonel James Low, who translated the work into English, noted that it spoke of Lankapuri as turning into a desert after the great war and falling under the rule, not of Vibhishana but of Girda or Garuda.96 Low places this Lankapuri on the Langkawi archipelago with Sumatra as another possible alternative.97 The idea falls flat on its face, however, since the work Low bases his conclusions on was only produced at the end of eighteenth century.98

In sum, Lanka is everywhere one’s flights of fantasy can take them. Because given the antiquity and ambiguity of such composition, pinning it to any specific location with absolute accuracy is challenging. There are just too many anachronisms to deal with. Too many appropriations. Too many vague assertions. And too much at stake ideologically and politically. The latter muddies waters the most. Having said that, if we were to objectively distill every piece of evidence, however inadequate, at our disposal, into a single portable theory, more fingers point to Madhya Pradesh than to Sri Lanka. The latter has nothing whatsoever going for it other than the name. And as we’ve already seen, the name is an appropriation rather than tradition. The island nation was always Ceylon, whether as Ceilão, Silam, Simhaladvipa, or even Ilam. All corruptions of the same name. But never Lanka.
