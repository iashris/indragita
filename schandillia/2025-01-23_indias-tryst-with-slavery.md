Moroccan traveler ibn Battuta visited India in the fourteenth century and wrote about Hindu slaves dying while being trafficked across the mountain wall to the subcontinent’s northwest. He named the wall Hindu Kush or “Hindu killer.” Although pockets of India had already known Islam since the days of Ali, it’s only in the thirteenth century that it came under formal Islamic rule, although not all of it. An interesting aside here, Genghis Khan and Qutb-ud-din Aibak both started their careers in the exact same year, 1206. And both rose from slavery, Khan as a prisoner of war and Aibak as a child slave. From that point on, human trafficking has been a most consistent cultural motif of the subcontinent’s politics throughout the Muslim rule that only ended with the Europeans. Every Islamic regime had institutionalized slave trade baked into its administration. These slaves were not only war captives but also civil acquisitions.

The question that arises then is, did slavery come to India with the Muslims? We know Islam institutionalizes and unambiguously endorses slavery. Muhammad himself owned a few, as did every caliph, be it Rashidun or Abbasid. But again, did Islam bring slavery to India?

A most pervasive notion, particularly among certain nationalist circles, holds that the institution of slavery was alien to ancient Indian civilization—introduced first by Muslim rulers and later perpetuated by European colonizers. This perspective situates slavery, along with other social ills, firmly outside India’s cultural boundaries, thus distancing it from Hindu traditions and institutions. Such notions serve to establish civilizational and moral superiority by creating an idealized pre-Islamic past untainted by practices now universally condemned.

The view is, of course, aggressively contested by voices from the other side of the line that are best served by an India that was at least as regressive and evil as the rest of the world at the time, if not more. Naturally, the topic comes with immense political and ideological baggage which, as is often the case with such topics, clouds academic judgment. here we attempt to take a very dispassionate view of the matter and let the records speak for themselves. in keeping with our tradition, we’ll be lending an ear to voices from both sides of the divide and scrutinize every reference, every evidence with the maximum possible skepticism and with little regard for political correctness or social sentiments. May facts and nothing but facts prevail.

## Chaulukyan Slaves

The most well-known milestone in the story of India’s interface with Islam is Mahmud Ghaznavi’s raid on Somnath. When this happened, Gujarat was under a Hindu dynasty called the Chaulukyas, not to be confused with the Chalukyas of the South who also reigned at the same time. AMU historian Pushpa Prasad, in her seminal work on this dynasty, has made the crucial observation that slavery was indeed practiced in this regime before Ghaznavi’s invasions. If true, it would make slavery an Indian practice at least independent of Islam, of not older.

Prasad bases her observation on a rather obscure medieval work called _Lekhapaddhati_.1 Produced between the eighth and fifteenth centuries, the treatise serves as a compilation of administrative records, legal templates, and official correspondence used during the Chaulukya reign in Gujarat.2 It offers a rare glimpse into the bureaucratic practices and governance structures of the time, including land grants, tax collection, and judicial procedures. Of our interest are the four document nos. 49 and 59 (two physical documents assigned to each document number), where owners are mandated to inform the _pañcha mukha nagara_ , a Chaulukyan administrative unit, before any sale of real estate or female slaves.3 In the latter, a ten-year-old girl named Sampuri is recorded as voluntarily entering slavery to escape starvation after being abandoned by her family in a famine in 1230–31.4 No. 58 is a veritable _Dāsī Patra Vidhi_ or “Deed of Female Slave,” that stipulates all the terms and conditions around acquisition and maintenance of a new female slave.5 The document opens with the anecdote of one Rana Pratap Singha acquiring a fair, sixteen-year-old maiden named Panuti as spoils of a war he waged against a neighboring kingdom.6 She was then sold off to a merchant for a price of a little over five hundred _dramma_7 (a vestige of the Greek rule in ancient India). This at least establishes the existence of slavery in a non-Islamic realm on the Indian subcontinent.

Another manuscript of the same document gives the price as sixty drammas.8 It further goes on to list out the new slave’s duties and responsibilities. It also empowers the owner to punish her by beating should the slave commit theft or any other misconduct in the household.9 And should she commit suicide upon such a beating, her owners were to be deemed innocent and guileless because she died of her own fault.10 In the case of the girl who volunteered as a slave in no. 59, an entire pledge is laid out in the document. This is the oath she’s said to have taken in the presence of the pañcha mukha nagara:11

 _If I, so long as I live, while employed as a slave in your house or in any other house, commit theft, or, seeing a vacant room with nobody there, appropriate some article lying there, or, finding that begging has become easier, go elsewhere, or, in my youth, being tempted or enticed by some man, leave you, or mix with your enemies and do or cause to do things like cheating, conspiring, and such, then, based on this deed, you will catch me by my hair, bind and beat me, and again put me to the work of a female slave._

_I shall always, throughout the day and night, carry out the orders of all the members of your family. If I, feeling self-satisfied, out of mischievous intent, refuse to perform the duties when ordered to do so, you will punish me by kicking and beating me with sticks and torture me to death, for which you, my master, will be as free from guilt as if you had been absent. I declare this in the presence of the four varṇas that I will die by my own fault._

_You, my master, with your son, grandson, wife, and other family members, shall be absolved as if you had bathed in the Ganga. If I ever feign stomachache or commit suicide by jumping into a well or pond or by taking poison, the pañca mukha nagara should know that you, my master, are guiltless, and that I have died in consequence of my own previous actions and due to fate. My master, with his family members, should be deemed to be as pure as if he has performed the purificatory bath in the Ganga._

What these four documents establish at the very least is that slavery, both voluntary and involuntary, existed in Hindu societies that were yet to come under Muslim rule. No. 58 affirms that slaves were acquired as spoils of war in Hindu kingdoms just as they were in non-Hindu kingdoms. The scale may or may not have differed, but the idea was surely not alien to Indians.

Of some import is the fact that nos. 58 and 59 date to a period when the Delhi Sultanate was already in session. In fact, the vast majority of the documents making up the collection comes from years _after_ the Somnath raids.12 The Arab presence in Gujarat actually predates even the Sultanate, thanks to its position as a major maritime entrepôt to the Indian subcontinent. Some could use this to argue that the Chaulukyan slavery was likely an artifact of Islamic influence. We can certainly not draw clear conclusions solely on the basis of documents produced during the time of Islamic rule even if they were produced in non-Islamic territories. Both India and Hinduism have a rich tradition going back thousands of years. So, if slavery were to be a native Indian practice independent of Islamic influence, we sure ought to find it featured in works that predate not just Islam’s arrival in India, but the very genesis of it in Arabia.

## Slavery in the Shastra

Besides _Lekhapaddhati_ , Prasad also mentions _Brihaspati Smriti_ in her discussion of slavery.13 For this, she references an early translation by the nineteenth-century British Indologist Julius Jolly.14 The _terminus ad quem_ assigned to the work by P. V. Kane takes it as far back as fourth century AD.15 Even the likes of Jolly, who tended to advocate a more recent dating for Hindu works, place it latest in the seventh century,16 still predating India’s formal introduction to political Islam. Therefore, any reference to slavery in this work would be of significance to our conversation. Of which there are several. One most commonly highlighted piece from this work is 22.27–27:17

“In the case of women, men, gold, gems, the property of a deity or Brahmin, silk and (other) precious things, the fine shall be equal to the value (of the article stolen). Or double that amount shall be inflicted by the king as a fine; or the thief shall be executed to prevent a repetition (of the offense).”

So, no stealing people. While this sounds temptingly close to a blanket prohibition on slavery or forced subjugation of people, 9.21 in the same work also gives us this:18

“Female slaves can never be acquired by possession, without a written title; nor (does possession create ownership) in the case of property belonging to a king, or to a learned Brahmin, or to an idiot, or infant.”

The only way to reconcile the two seemingly conflicting mandates is to recognize that 22.27–27 is about theft of property. And theft always involves two parties, a thief and an owner. For something to be stolen, it must first belong to someone. The gold, the gems, the silk, the women being spoken of as being stolen here are all properties of whoever owns them. What it establishes is that you may not take someone’s slave by force. If you must, the right channel would be through a formal sale deed which is what 9.21 clarifies. This leaves one with little ambiguity over the status of slavery in the world of _Brihaspati Smriti_.

The text offers remarkably granular prescriptions on the acquisition, upkeep, and disposal of slaves. For instance, if a man is caught sleeping with another man’s female slave, the penalty it stipulates is servitude in her stead. In other words, _he_ must serve _her_ master as a hired servant19 to make up for the infraction of diverting her from her duties. In a section on inheritance, it stipulates the distribution of slaves among the deceased owner’s inheritors just like any other piece of property. If there’s only one female slave, she would be assigned to work for each son on rotation as per the will.20 Such granular prescriptions could only be made if the practice was mainstream enough to warrant legislative attention.

Roughly coeval with _Brihaspati Smriti_ is _Nāradasmriti_ , another prescriptive work of legal and social order that offers a structured approach to governance and community conduct. Here too, penalties are prescribed for various infractions, one of which is for theft of humans. Rule no. 28 on theft stipulates thus:21

“He who steals a man shall have to pay the highest fine; he who steals a woman (shall be stripped) of his entire wealth; and he who steals a maiden (shall suffer) corporal punishment.”

Just as is the case with BS 22.27–27, the above NS rule is commonly advanced as evidence of the ancient Hindu society’s anti-slavery stance. Again, the problem with that assertion is that the same work also states, in 7.13, that when a female slave is stolen and her services availed wrongly, the owner is to be compensated two _paṇas_ for each day of such unauthorized service. This is significantly lower than eight paṇas for a milch cow, thirteen for a bull, and sixteen for a horse.22 Clearly, the stance against slavery isn’t all that concrete. In fact, the rule 28 that speaks of corporal punishment for stealing maidens has only been found in Nepalese manuscripts,23 not that it negates the ruling itself.

NS 7.13, meanwhile, is of significance because it attests to not only the practice of slavery but also the abundance and ubiquity of slaves. Even in the theft section of the Nepalese recension that many take as evidence against slavery, we have rule 33 where if the slave belonged to a Brahmin, the thief would lose parts of his limbs.24 Which seems to also confirm that slavery wasn’t just a fringe practice among the warrior or ruling classes but a part of civil life in all caste groups, including Brahmins. _Nāradasmriti_ also speaks of fifteen different classes of slaves, including the “self-sold.”25 If there was one class of voluntary slaves, by extension, there were fourteen of involuntary slaves. This distinction is important because a number of nationalist narratives push the notion that a _dāsa_ was not a slave in the traditional Western sense of the term but an individual who had entered servitude out of his own free will.

We won’t dwell any further on this text, because while it has made its point, it’s still possible that the author was writing from his own immediate observations, in his own immediate environs. In other words, it’s possible that the practice was a peculiarity of a specific region, a cultural quirk, rather than a universal Hindu or Vedic phenomenon. _Brihaspati Smriti_ and _Nāradasmriti_ are parts of a much larger corpus in the common genre of legal prescription called _Dharmashastras_. The most well-known member of this corpus is _Manusmriti_.

If BS 22.27–28 and NS Theft.28 sound similar, that’s because they both trace their origins in Manu’s work. Much of what we read in BS and NS, can also be found in MS. A lot of borrowing existed between the various works of the Dharmashastra corpus. Verse 8.323 of Manu mandates nothing short of death for stealing men, women, and gems:26

     _puruṣāṇāṃ kulīnānāṃ nārīṇāṃ ca viśeṣataḥ,
    mukhyānāṃ caiva ratnānāṃ haraṇe vadhamarhati._
    “The one stealing men of noble birth, and specially women, and precious gems, deserves to be executed. And therefore, the king must execute the said thief.” (rough trans. Shastri)

It’s important to note here that the object of theft here isn’t just any man or woman but a man or woman of “noble birth.” The usage of the word _kulīna_ , Sanskrit for “noble” makes it mighty unambiguous. Of course, you could not enslave a member of the aristocracy.

So, what does Manu say on slavery elsewhere? In 8.413–14, he makes it very clear that Shudras are meant to serve the Brahmins, regardless of their will:27

     _śūdraṃ tu kārayed dāsyaṃ krītamakrītameva vā,
    dāsyāyaiva hi sṛṣṭo'sau brāhmaṇasya svayambhuvā._
    “But a Shudra, whether bought or unbought, he [Brahmin] shall make to do servile work; since it is for doing servile work for the Brahmin that he has been created by the self-born one.” (rough trans. Shastri)
    
    _na svāminā nisṛṣṭo'pi śūdro dāsyād vimucyate,
    nisargajaṃ hi tat tasya kastasmāt tadapohati._
    “Even though set free by the master, the Shudra is not released from service; since that is innate in him, and who can release him from it?” (rough trans. Shastri)

The second of the two verses above is of particular import. It firmly indicates the existence of unpaid labor with no role of will or volition. So much so, that even a release from the master does not guarantee emancipation from slavery. The moment one master releases you, you become available to another. That’s, by every definition of the word, slavery. The most prominent theme of all arguments against pre-Islamic slavery in India hinges on this semantic jugglery that servitude in India was paid and voluntary, hence not slavery in the Western sense of the term.

We’ve seen how NS classifies slaves into fifteen types. In 8.415, MS offers seven:28

  1. Captured in war

  2. Voluntary, seeking sustenance

  3. Hereditary

  4. Purchased

  5. Gifted

  6. Inherited

  7. By punishment




In the traditional Western sense of the term, slaves are acquired as war spoils and their status as slaves transcend generations. Both criteria, often denied by naysayers, are fulfilled by Manu’s slaves. The very next verse also adds that a _dāsa_ could not hold property and whatever belonged to him, belonged to their master, as was the case with offsprings and wives whose possessions belonged to their fathers and husbands, respectively:29

     _bhāryā putraśca dāsaśca traya evādhanāḥ smṛtāḥ,
    yat te samadhigacchanti yasya te tasya tad dhanam._
    “The wife, the son, and the slave—these three are declared to have no property; whatever they acquire, or produce, is property of whom they belong as wife, son, and slave, respectively.” (rough trans. Shastri)

The reference in 8.415 to slaves captured in war is actually a reiteration of 7.96 where women of the defeated are categorically named as possessions of the victor:30

     _rathāśvaṃ hastinaṃ chatraṃ dhanaṃ dhānyaṃ paśūn striyaḥ,
    sarvadravyāṇi kupyaṃ ca yo yaj jayati tasya tat._
    “Chariots, horses, elephants, parasols, wealth, grains, animals, women, all goods, and baser metals belong to him who wins them.” (rough trans. Shastri)

But since most kingdoms were likely to be ruled by families of noble birth, and almost all Dharmashastras prohibit the enslavement of such men and women, this verse most likely refers to women of lower birth captured in war. Which makes sense if we recall that in most such encounters the princesses of the defeated side were married off to the princes of the winning side instead of being enslaved.

There are several other references scattered throughout Manu’s works, but we take it that the point has been made. _Manusmriti_ has been dated to the second century AD, at the cusp of the Kushana–Gupta eras. That makes slavery in India at least half a millennium older than Islam. And since it’s highly unlikely that all Dharmashastras were produced in a single kingdom, it can be safely assumed that the institution was a feature in all Indian societies. At least most, if not all.

Even now, it could be argued that Manu’s world was a world of foreign rule. The Kushans were not natives to the land and although the Guptas were ascendent, much of Manu’s work was at least informed by the traditions and mores of the Kushan society. Is it possible then that slavery was a Kushan injection and not a native Indian tradition after all? Before the Kushans we had the Greeks, and they too had a well-established tradition of slavery at least in the Greek mainland. Therefore, for any answers regarding the nativity of Indian slavery, we need to rewind all the way to the Maurya, the last major autochthonous dynasty before the advent of the Greeks.

## Mauryan Slavery

The Mauryan era is best exemplified by three key historical artifacts—the writings of Kautilya, the edicts of Aśoka, and the observations of Megasthenes. If slavery existed in that society, it must find mention and elaboration in all three. Such a mention would also evidence the ubiquity of slavery in the Indian antiquity because the Mauryan realm covered pretty much the entire subcontinent, either directly or indirectly. Going in chronological order, we’ll first examine _Arthashastra_ , then _Indica_ , and finally Aśokan edicts.

So, the first question is, does _Arthashastra_ say anything about slaves? And the answer is, an entire chapter. Kautilya seems to find slavery so important that he dedicates an entire chapter, 3.13, to the subject, although it’s about both slaves _and_ laborers. The chapter opens with a seemingly strong objection to slavery and a prescription of fine—a sum of twelve paṇas—on anyone selling or mortgaging a Shudra.31 At first crack this might sound like an argument against slavery, but two things stand out. Firstly, the very fact that a public figure is having to write about a practice in and of itself attests to the prevalence of the said practice. You wouldn’t prohibit something, much less mention it, if it were an alien concept to your society. Secondly, Kautilya does not prohibit the sale and mortgage of all Shudras, only those who aren’t already slave by birth. Which in no uncertain terms implies the existence of Shudras who _are_ slaves by birth and thus _can_ be traded. To further eliminate any lingering ambiguity, the same passage also reiterates that the prohibition only applies to Aryans and not Mlecchas.32 To clarify, the key difference between Shudras and Mlecchas lies in their societal positions within the Aryan framework. Shudras occupy the lowest tier within the four-varna hierarchy, forming an integral part of Aryan society, whereas Mlecchas are considered outcasts, existing entirely outside the Aryan pale.

Of course, the word used by Kautilya is dāsa. Is dāsa, slave in the Western sense of the term? In the Shastras, we’ve noted it is. But in Kautilya’s work, there seems to be a slight departure. 3.13 further elaborates that a dāsa could not be deprived of his dues33 and a _dāsī_ could not be compelled to serve a male master while he was bathing.34 He further lists out a number of jobs a slave could not be forced to perform, including the carrying of the dead, sweeping of bodily discharge, etc.35 Violence against slaves is also expressly forbidden.36

This is not even remotely what slavery in the non-Indic context looks like. Slaves in the Western society, for instance, were absolute chattel of their masters. So, while the upkeep and sustenance were obvious requirements, a master was not obligated by law to provide. Nor was there any prohibition on sexual or physical abuse of slaves. But before we rush to conclusions, it’s worth entertaining the idea that Kautilya possibly meant all these safeguards not for dāsas in general but only Aryan, that is Shudra, dāsas in particular. For instance, the stricture against violence only applies if the slave were of a high birth.37 High-birth slaves were a possibility in cases where slavery was a punishment for debt default.

To that effect, Kautilya lays out a number of stipulations protecting the right of a slave to regain his “Aryahood” should he so choose to. For one, if a Shudra or any other Aryan except Brahmin had fallen into slavery by virtue of debt default, they had the right to emancipation upon the fulfilment of their debts.38 Even if a slave were sold to a new master, he retained the right to emancipation upon the payment of his price to the purchasing master.39 Which raises an important point of wages. In the Western societies, there was no question of a slave buying his freedom because he had no income or possession. In Kautilya’s worldview, he did. Dāsas were paid for their services, even if minimally so. failure to pay one’s dāsa made the owner liable for penalties and punishments.40

To further widen the gulf between dāsa and slave, Kautilya did not recognize hereditary slavery. The offspring of a voluntary slave would not be slave by virtue of lineage.41 But if Kautilya mentions “voluntary,” does it mean offsprings of other slaves were excluded from this privilege? There’s nothing to conclude either way. _Arthashastra_ also does not say anything about Mleccha slaves and whether these safeguards extend to them as well as the Shudras. One way to read this is as applicable to all in the absence of specific qualifications. But Kautilya already makes an exception for them at the beginning of the chapter when he says that Mlecchas can be enslaved for life even when his Aryan counterparts cannot. He also makes an exception for fugitive slaves who once recaptured are to be slaves for life.42

In sum, _Arthashastra_ goes out of its way to safeguard the dāsa’s rights. Thus, at least institutionally slavery seems to have been a forbidden practice. Many questions still stand, such as whether owners were punished for not paying their Mleccha slaves, or whether Mleccha slaves have property rights. They were certainly not exempt from lifelong servitude. This also doesn’t tell us what the society actually looked like. Sure, Kautilya said slavery was wrong, but did the lay aristocrat follow it diligently? What was the society’s attitude to slave labor? In other words, Kautilya tells us about the ideal, not the practical. For that, we need a work that’s more descriptive than prescriptive. Something like a travelogue or a journal. Which brings us to _Indica_ , the seminal work on the Mauryan life by the Greek ambassador Megasthenes. Before we leave Kautilya, however, a most significant point warrants attention— _Arthashastra’s_ dāsīs were _not_ sex-slaves. This was among the most significant contrast with the status of slaves in the Western and later Islamic worlds.

Megasthenes came to Pataliputra toward the end of the fourth century BC after Chandragupta’s war with Seleucus I Nicator. He was here for almost four years during which he extensively documented his observations of life in the Mauryan capital. As a member of the royal court, he was not much privy to the goings-on in the world at large, but he had the front-row view of the life in the palace. If slavery was practiced informally by the Mauryan aristocracy, he is sure to have noted.

The unfortunate thing about _Indica_ is that it no longer exists. What we have today is a mere reconstruction from fragments and references found in the works of later Greek and Roman writers such as Strabo, Pliny, Diodorus, and Arrian. The fragment of relevance to our investigation come from Diodorus Siculus or Diodorus of Sicily, who produced a monumental forty-volume work on universal history, _Bibliotheca Historica_ , more than two hundred years _after_ Megasthenes. This work includes Megasthenes’ Fragment I which tells us that “of several remarkable customs existing among Indians, there is one prescribed by their ancient philosophers which one may regard as truly admirable: for the law ordains that no one among them shall, under any circumstances, be a slave.”43

This sounds like a blanket ban on all forms of forced servitude, regardless of the subject’s caste status and crime. So, if Megasthenes saw no slavery during his stay, and Kautilya codified an entire body of laws enshrining the rights of dāsas, are we to take it that slavery was indeed an unfamiliar concept at least to the Mauryan society? We’ll return to this but first let’s see what Arrian has to say. He too used much of Megasthenes’ writings to produce his own Indica, nearly four centuries after Megasthenes and two centuries after Diodorus. He too seems to concur with Diodorus’ reading that slavery was not known to India of the time. Quoting Megasthenes, he writes that “all the Indians are free, and not one of them is a slave.”44 He further adds that “Indians do not even use aliens as slaves, and much less a countryman of their own.”45

Unfortunately, we’ll never know what Megasthenes really said, but it won’t be too off to triangulate from two references separated in both time and space. Especially when they both seem to agree with each other. To reiterate, one is a slave only if the slavery is perpetual, they have no right to possession, and the master has absolute right over them. And Kautilya seems to prohibit all three. The Mauryan dāsas could not, therefore, be slaves legally. And going by Megasthenes, they couldn’t be slaves in reality either. So, as we asked before, does that mean the Mauryan society was truly slavery-free? A 1978 paper in the _Journal of the Economic and Social History of the Orient_ by Rekha Rani Sharma seems to think, no.

This paper takes a different approach to defining slavery. Plato and Aristotle, for instance, saw slaves not as a caste but an entirely separate race of humans born to serve and nothing else.46 Aristotle even went on to spell out that “the slave is a piece of property which is inanimate and useful for action rather than for production.”47 Sharma sees this, and rightly so, that this is starkly in contradiction to what Kautilya philosophized who accorded slaves the right to both life and emancipation.48 And since Megasthenes was operating under the ideals of Aristotle and Plato, what he saw in India did not strike him as slavery, even though it was.49 But Sharma’s corollary hinges on one central assumption, and a big one, that what we had in India and what they had in Greece were both slavery. She first assumes dāsa as synonymous to slave and then proceeds to point out that only the nature of it was different between India and Greece. But if a dāsa did not fulfil the most fundamental criteria of slavery, _i.e_. perpetual and unconditional servitude, is it even fair to treat the terms synonymously? At least going by this, it would seem that while Sharma’s observations are spot on, her conclusions are, for the lack of a better term, hasty. Which is strange because, besides Mgasthenes, Arrian, and Diodorus, she also cites a name few would recognize today, in support of India being slavery-free.

Onesicritus lived in the fourth century BC and as someone who accompanied Alexander on his India campaign, predates even the Mauryans. Therefore, his take would be just as significant as that of Megasthenes, even though he never really got to cross the Beas. He wrote, and Sharma eminently points it out, that of all territories in the Greek realm, Mousikanos was the only one where slaves didn’t exist.50 In his writings, Onesicritus observes that the inhabitants of the country of Mousikanos “employed instead of slaves, young men in the flower of their age, as the Cretans employ the Aphamiôtai and the Lacedemonians the Helots.”51 Mousikanos was those days what the Greeks called Sindh, by most academic reckonings today. The Aphamiôtai were a social class in ancient Sparta, comprised of people who had, for some reason or the other, had lost their Spartan citizenship. These “outcasts” were often employed in neighboring Crete as menial labors and servants, similar to dāsas in India. Lacedemonians were practically Spartans, and after the subjugation of Laconia and Messenia, they employed the natives as agricultural serfs while Spartans focused solely on military training.

While the conditions of both Aphamiôtai and Helots were pitiable, and killing one rarely attracted real-world consequences, neither were slaves by any definition of the term. Which is exactly why Sharma’s conclusions seem hasty. If Onesicritus is aware of the serfdom paradigm through the Spartans and Cretans, so must be fellow Greeks both during and after him. Therefore, if Megasthenes saw the dāsa system and concluded that it wasn’t slavery, it’s less likely to be a misunderstanding and more likely to be a recognition that it truly wasn’t slavery but serfdom that was in vogue in pockets back home in Greece. A similarly hasty conclusion as Sharma, by the way, is also drawn by the celebrated historian and Sanskrit scholar of the early twentieth century, Pandurang Kane who observes that the treatment of slaves in India was so good that a foreign observer like Megashthenes accustomed to the treatment of slaves in Greece thought that there was no slavery in India.52 But, again, Megasthenes couldn’t possibly be unaware of the analogous practices in Crete and Sparta.

It is still possible, however, that Onesicritus missed out much because he only experienced a tiny region on the far western fringes of the subcontinent, and for a very short amount of time. Alexander spent less than a year in the region and spent it all fighting. It is also possible that Megasthenes did not get to experience how the citizenry at large operated since much of his interface was with the royals in Pataliputra, who may have treated their dāsas with generosity thanks to their own affluence and dāsas’ abundance. All of this is possible, but was it? The only way to conclude is by examining the third piece of the puzzle, Aśoka’s edicts.

Aśoka lived and reigned within a hundred years of Alexander, Megasthenes, and Chandragupta. He introduced to India the practice of writing down laws and bulletins in the form of public edicts for mass dissemination, a learned borrowing from the Achaemenids. His rock and pillar edicts are preserved and widely studied to this day and are among the most studied pieces of epigraphy in the history of civilization. A common theme of all his edicts is that of mutual respect and compassion among his subjects. He mandates proper courtesy to friends, comrades, relatives—and slaves.53 The word used is, of course, dāsa. At first crack this may seem like a mundane, “respect everyone” kind of homily where slaves are accorded the same regard as the non-slave. Which paradoxically makes them non-slaves by definition. But the edict also uses the word _bhṛtya_ right after dāsa, as in _dāsabhṛtakeṣu_.54 And bhṛtya is Sanskrit for servant. If servant is being mentioned separately from slave as a compound, the two must be related ideas but distinct. We know servants have rights, that is they earn wages and can switch employers should they wish to. These are not the kind of rights slaves enjoy. Does that mean at least in Aśokan times, dāsas were indeed slaves and not mere indentured labors or servants? The water gets murky again. At least three different edicts mention slaves and in every single one of them, it’s part of the compound dāsa-bhṛtya in one or the other inflection—dāsabhṛtakeṣu in MRE 13, and dāsabhṛtake in MRE 955 and MRE 11.56 Major Pillar Edict 7 too mentions the compound dāsabhṛtakeṣu.57 Interestingly, while dāsa doesn’t exist in isolation in any edict, bhṛtya does. At least once, in MRE 5, the servant is mentioned without the slave.58 This couldn’t be casual. Clearly some kind of distinction existed between the dāsa and the bhṛtya in Aśoka’s society. But what could be the nature of that distinction? And would that be sufficient to assign the dāsa the attributes of a slave in the technical sense of the term? Any attempt to answer this question, one must recall the fact that Kautilya does not extend his generosity to dāsas who are Mleccha, and that such slaves do not have the right to emancipation in _Arthashastra_.

This brings us back to where we started. While we know for sure India had slavery in every sense of the word long before the arrival of Muslims, we still don’t know with confidence if we did during the Mauryas. Reasonable arguments are possible to be made in support of either conclusion. It seems, we need to rewind further back in time for some resolution.

## Slavery in Epics

The period leading up to the rise of the Mauryans is traditionally held to be the period of the epics. At least that’s when it is held that the _Ramayana_ and the _Mahabharata_ were produced. We won’t get into the dating and authorship of these works because they’re outside the scope of this conversation. But if slavery was a feature of that society, there must be some unambiguous reference to it in both. Let’s begin with _Ramayana_.

Valmiki drops multiple references to dāsas and dāsīs throughout his work, and the most notable are in _Ayodhya Kanda_. In 2.32.15–16, we have Rama himself instructing his brother to make a gift of dāsīs to a highly learned Brahmin:59

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _kausalyām ca yāaśīrbhir bhaktaḥ paryupatiṣṭhati,
    ācāryaḥ taittirīyāṇām abhirūpaḥ ca vedavit.
    tasya yānam ca dāsīḥ ca saumitre sampradāpaya,
    kauśeyāni ca vastrāṇi yāvat tuṣyati sa dvijaḥ._
    “Oh, Lakshmana! Which brahman is studying Taittiriya (a school of yajurveda), a preceptor, a man of conformity; a knower of Vedas, serving Kausalya with his devotion and blessing, to him see that he is duly gifted conveyance, servant maids and silken clothing till he gets satisfied.” (trans. Murthy)

The same verses appear in 2.20.13–14 in the Critical Edition by Dr. P. L. Vaidya.60 K. M. K. Murthy, whose translation we’re looking at, translates dāsī as a female servant here, as is done by many nationalist thinkers today. One might wonder, though, if a servant, whose biggest distinction from a slave is that he or she retains the right to personal liberty and cannot be treated as “possession,” could be gifted away to people as if they _were_ possessions. Or perhaps dāsas inhabited some undefined realm between slave and servant? The other mention comes in 2.77.3 where Bharata donates a bunch of dāsas and dāsīs, along with livestock, to Brahmins:61

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _bāstikam bahu śuklam ca gāḥ ca api śataśaḥ tathā,
    dāsī dāsam ca yānam ca veśmāni sumahānti ca._
    “In that ceremony performed in honor of the dead king, Bharata the son of Dasaratha gave multitudes of white goats, hundreds of cows, servants and servant maids, vehicles and very big houses to Brahmans.” (trans. Murthy)

The Critical Edition carries the same verse as 2.71.2.62 Again, the interpretation by Murthy is maids and servants. And again, the dilemma of servants being donated and gifted away as chattel stands unaddressed.

Just as in the case of Aśoka’s edicts, however, we find that Valmiki uses bhṛta as a separate term for servants. While some ambiguity, on a stretch, could be accorded to dāsa, bhṛta carries a very specific meaning, but more on that in a bit. First let’s see the reference in 2.100.7:63

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _kāla atikramaṇe hy eva bhakta vetanayor bhṛtāḥ
    bhartuḥ kupyanti duṣyanti so anarthaḥ sumahān smṛtaḥ_
    “When there is delay in giving bread and wages, the servants become incensed against their master and become corrupt; and that is said to be a great unfortunate occurrence.” (trans. Murthy)

Again, the Critical Edition has it in 2.94.27.64 Instead of dāsa, the word Valmiki uses here is bhṛta, and Murthy translates it as servant. While it’s always possible for two distinct words to mean the same thing, bhṛta has something more going for it. The word exclusively carries the sense of remuneration for the services rendered. The root translates directly into sustenance or wages and carries the same etymology as _bharaṇa_65 which gives us the compound _bharaṇa-posṣaṇa_. No such exclusivity exists for dāsa. In fact, the interpretation of dāsa as paid servant or indenture labor is based more on assumptions than etymology. One could still argue that slaves too ought to be nourished for them to be productive. In that sense, bhṛta loses its exclusivity. Except, Valmiki makes the wages reference abundantly clear in the bhṛta verse. Either to break the tie or to add to the confusion, Valmiki drops yet another term in 2.2.37–38,66 2.2.25–2667 in Critical Edition:

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _saṃgrāmātpunarāgamya kuṅjareṇa rathena vā,
    paurān svajanavannityam kuśalaṃ paripṛcchati.
    putreṣvagniṣu dāreṣu preṣyaśiṣyagaṇeṣu ca,
    nikhilenānupūrvyācca pitā putrānivaurasān._
    “After returning from battle, Rama goes to citizens on an elephant or a chariot and enquires about their wellbeing as though they were his own kinsmen, like a father does to his sons. He asks about their wives and children, about the sacred fires, about their servants and students, always completely as per the due order.” (trans. Murthy)

The word is _preṣya_ , and it has universally been translated as “servant,” and is rooted in _preṣa_ , meaning “to summon” or “to send forth,” like on an errand. The Hindi word _preṣaka_ meaning “sender” is still used in Indian postal parlance. But the etymology in and of itself says nothing about the nature of servitude, nor whether it’s paid for or remunerated.

In sum, we have three terms of service in Valmiki’s work—preṣya, bhṛta, and dāsa. Of these, only in bhṛta’s context is remuneration spelled out, and only in dāsa’s context is gifting and donating of humans spelled out. Does that conclusively establish the slave nature of dāsa in _Ramayana_? Not really, but it does indicate so strongly enough for it to be a possibility. Now let’s look at the _Mahabharata_.

In Sabha Parva, 2.54.16–17,68 when Yudhishthira plays the game of dice with the Kauravas in hopes of winning back his kingdom, he offers his dāsas in wager:69

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _yudhiṣṭhira uvāca:
    etāvantyeva dāsānāṃ sahasrāṇyuta santi me,
    pradakṣiṇānulomāśca prāvāravasanāḥ sadā.
    prājñā medhāvino dakṣā yuvāno mṛṣṭakuṇḍalāḥ,
    pātrīhastā divārātramatithīnbhojayantyuta,
    etadrājandhanaṃ mahyaṃ tena dīvyāmyahaṃ tvayā._
    “Yudhishthira said:
    I have thousands of serving-men, skilled in waiting upon guests, always attired in silken robes, endued with wisdom and intelligence, their senses under control though young, and decked with earrings, and who serve all guests night and day with plates and dishes in hand. With this wealth, O king, I will stake with thee.” (trans. Ganguli)

In his translation, Kisari Mohan Ganguli interprets dāsa as a male servant, which is consistent with the notion of no slavery in ancient India. But another celebrated translator, the late Bibek Debroy, interprets it as a male slave when he quotes Yudhishthira as saying, “I have thousands of male slaves. They are always dressed in fine garments and are skilled and ready to serve. They are wise, young, skilled and intelligent and wear polished earrings. With plates in their hands, they feed the guests day and night. O king! These are my riches that I now gamble with you for.”70 Not just this, shortly before wagering his dāsas, Yudhishthira had also wagered his dāsīs, in 2.54.12:71

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _yudhiṣṭhira uvāca:
    śataṃ dāsīsahasrāṇi taruṇyo me prabhadrikāḥ,
    kambukeyūradhāriṇyo niṣkakaṇṭhyaḥ svalaṃkṛtāḥ._
    “Yudhishthira said:
    I have a hundred thousand serving-girls, all young, and decked with golden bracelets on their wrists and upper arms, and with nishkas round their necks and other ornaments.” (trans. Ganguli)

Here too, whereas Ganguli translates dāsī as serving-girls, Debroy prefers slave when he quotes Yudhishthira thus:

“I have one hundred thousand slave girls. They are young and extremely beautiful. They wear bracelets and armlets, necklaces of gold coins and wear ornaments.”72 One ought to wonder at this point, the utility of qualifying a serving-girl or even a slave girl as “extremely beautiful” or “young.” Sure, young might indicate agility and industry, but beautiful? The only reason a female slave might be prized or even considered for beauty is if she were to be taken for sexual purposes. We saw that in the case of _Lekhapaddhati_ too, where female slaves are particularly prized for their youth and beauty. That seems to affirm one of the biggest criteria for slavery—involuntary sex. Kautilya prohibits this, but it seems to be prevalent in the _Mahabharata_. The episode of Draupadi’s molestation after the Kaurava win at the dice game further fortifies this conclusion.

In fact, at one point, Debroy quotes Vidura as saying to Dhritarashtra, “O king! There are three who cannot own property—a wife, a slave and a son. Whatever riches they obtain, belongs to the one who owns them.”73

There go property rights. If a dāsa is barred from personal possessions and he belongs to his master, if a dāsi can also be used for sex, are we still talking about servants and maids, and not slaves? To further lend weight to this idea, Debroy categorically separates slave from servant as distinct stations in a footnote describing the name Sairandhri, the pseudonym assumed by Draupadi during her incognito. He defines the term, in a footnote, as “a female servant who works in the inner quarters and is therefore superior to a common slave girl,”74 thus affirming the suspicion that slavery existed in the era independent of occupational servitude.

So, where does that leave us then? There’s slavery throughout the Shastra period, there’s slavery in the Mauryan period (albeit probably only reserved for Mlechhas in the strictest sense of the term), and there’s slavery in the epics. Can we _now_ conclude that slavery was native to India? We could, but there’s still Megasthenes to contend with. Although we have reasoned that his exposure might have been limited, we still don’t have any concrete primary source from the period that illustrates the practice in Pataliputra. The best we have is Kautilya whose mandates against the abuse of dāsas might be interpreted as indirect evidence of slavery being a social practice even if frowned upon by the likes of Kautilya. The _Mahabharata_ remains by far the most damning indictment of the Indian society’s familiarity with slavery as a social norm in the Iron Age. But India is far older than that. Was the practice _truly_ native? Or still a foreign injection in the middle of the first millennium BC? Time to look into the Rigveda but before that, a quick rundown on the state of affairs in the brief window between the Vedic and Mauryan periods where Shramanic traditions like Buddhism took shape.

Buddhism started with Siddhartha Gautama, also known as Gautam Buddha, in the middle of the first millennium BC. Although it would later spread to lands as far as China and Sumatra, its initial decades were confined to Magadha. Broadly understood to have originated as an eastern countercurrent to the western social stratification, Buddhism, even during the time of the Buddha, shows clear signs of caste consciousness.

* * *

* * *

But our subject is slavery. Did Buddhism acknowledge or uphold slavery? Short answer, quite possibly. And the long answer can be found in an earlier essay on Buddhism and caste. To dwell on one particular example, let’s take the Brahmi inscription from Alluru in Tamil Nadu. Originally part of a vihāra’s pillar, the inscription notes down the gifts made to the institution by a wealthy patron. And part of the list is _dāsī-dāsa_.75 Now, one may again raise the ambiguity in the reading of dāsī, but just before dāsi-dāsa, in the same inscription, we also have _pesa-rupāni_.76 If _pesa_ rings a bell, that’s probably because it’s the Pali corruption of Sanskrit preṣya, which we know specifically refers to a paid servant. Thus, if pesa is mentioned along with dāsa, it should be safe to assume that they’re not used synonymously and dāsa is being meant in a sense not covered by pesa. Ergo, slave. The gifting of slaves to a vihāra is quite the indictment, but this is a much later example, well within the Common Era, so not very useful when looking for signs of slavery in fifth century BC. Do we have anything from Buddha himself?

Turns out we do, actually. At least in one story involving Bimbisara, the ruler of Magadha, when Buddha preached. Those days, the Sangha was open to all. Anyone seeking renunciation could get themselves ordained as a Buddhist monk, or _bhikkhu_. Which led to a situation where debt slaves were getting ordained as monks to escape their obligations. When Bimbisara raised it with the Buddha, the latter promulgated the rule that any dāsa seeking to join the Sangha must first seek approval from their master.77 Now, whether we consider someone serving to make up for their debts as slave or servant is a matter of personal interpretation. But if the one serving is not free to make their own life choices even outside their service—requiring master’s approval to join a Sangha being one example—could they truly be called servants?

In _Aṅguttara Nikāya_ , Buddha categorically lists living beings as one of five forbidden trades.78 These are things you could not only not consume or use, but also not do business in. Living things could mean animals, but meat is already mentioned in the list.79 So, the other likely possibility is humans. Unless he was against the idea of buying and selling milch cows and draught animals for non-meat purposes. One is free to draw their own conclusions, but the argument for slavery seems more valid than tenuous at this point. Now let’s finally turn to the Vedas.

## The Dāsas of Rigveda

Rigveda is where dāsa originates. In fact, it would seem that the terminology gained currency pretty late in the Rigvedic chronology. We find copious mentions of the term in the work, but a disproportionate number of those appear in the tenth _maṇḍala_ which happens to be the last of the ten. But the term itself derives from Dasyu, a race of tribesmen who are understood to have been at odds with the Aryans during the early years of the Vedic era. It’s for this reason, scholars theorize, that Dasyus came to be assigned negative connotations, such as those of bandits or barbarians. As opposed to the Aryans who were civilized and of noble blood.

As the Aryans won their battles against the Dasyus, captives were taken home for menial labor as prisoners of war. This was a common practice all over the world and had been right through the pre-modern world. Since these were captives, they didn’t have to be compensated for their services. And thus, Dasyus became dāsas and dāsas came to characterize involuntary free labor. Until the acquisition of this class of free labor, the Aryan society did not have much in the way of division of labor. Philologist Michael Witzel upholds the notion on etymological grounds and notes Dasyu and dāsa as the Vedic appellations for a North Iranian tribe of a similar name.80 These peoples were constantly at war with the Aryans throughout the Late Bronze and Early Iron Age. Rigveda clarifies in the most direct of ways the barbarian status of Dasyus in 10.22.8:81

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _akarmā dasyur abhi no amantur anyavrato amānuṣaḥ,
    tvaṃ tasyāmitrahan vadhar dāsasya dambhaya._
    “The Dasyu of non-deeds, of non-thought, the non-man whose commandments are other, is against us. You smasher of non-allies, humble the weapon of this Dasa.” (trans. Jamison)

The word _amānuṣaḥ_ further crystallizes their status as non-human or subhuman. If this is how they were viewed, it’s not improbable that they were treated as non-human workmen under absolute control upon capture. But all this is conjecture. A more direct indication of slave labor comes from 10.62.10:82

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _uta dāsā pariviṣe smaddiṣṭī goparīṇasā,
    yadus turvaś ca māmahe._
    “And also, two barbarian-slaves to perform service, each with his allotted (gear), along with an abundance of cows, have Yadu and Turva (each) magnanimously bestowed.” (trans. Jamison)

Here, two dāsas are being offered for service along with an abundance of livestock. Again, could these be slaves? Or manual laborers? Could servants be offered, gifted, or sold? While Jamison translates the word as barbarian-slave in this context, H. H. Wilson, a colonial Indologist, translates it as servant:83

“Yadu and Indra speaking auspiciously, and possessed of numerous cattle, gave them like (appointed) servants, for the enjoyment (of Manu Sávarni).”

Which is just as plausible. Servants can always be appointed to serve a new employer. There is no way to conclude with conviction that these appointees were not remunerated for their services, or that their employers had absolute authority over them. More and more indigenous scholars of the subject are now beginning to advocate for Wilson’s interpretation. Also, since the early Vedic society was not yet stratified, it’s not unlikely that any kind of service rendered by an individual was in exchange for some kind of gratuity. If that were the case, we can conclude that the Vedic Aryans did not engage in slavery.

If the Rigvedic society did not know slavery, we can pretty much assume that the concept was not native at least to the Aryans, although for a more rounded conclusion it would pay to examine pre-Indic cultures like Sintashta and BMAC. But what about the Harappans? Did they practice slavery?

## Slave Labor in Harappa

Now we’re entering truly uncharted terrains. Almost everything from this point on is going to be educated guess or deductive speculation at best. We have no literature from the period and whatever little epigraphy we have so far remains scarce and undeciphered. We have already discussed the existence of caste-like stratification in the Indus Civilization in an earlier essay on the origins of caste, so reading that beforehand would serve well here, for there’s little value in reinventing the wheel.

* * *

* * *

While social stratification is expected in post-neolithic cultures like the IVC, which includes the existence of a special class of people exclusively entrusted with tasks of absolute menial nature, such as sanitation, from that to slavery would still be a leap of speculation.

Daya Ram Sahani, one of the pioneering archeologists of the Indus Civilization, discovered a well-room at a Harappan site designated HR-B.84 He theorized that the well-room served as a source of drinking water for anyone thirsty.85 It had a neat brick paving on the floor with shallow pits meant to hold pots of water.86 Somewhat akin to a modern-day water fountain but more elaborate and likely manned. Another Indus archeologist Stuart Piggott further built upon this imagery and saw it as suggestive of coolie labor of colonial India.87 Also in agreement, albeit with caution, was Mortimer Wheeler who considered slave-quarters as one of the possible identifications for the structures around the well-room.88 As readily apparent, none of this is based in anything more concrete than speculation and imagination. Sahani takes the well room as manned because someone must be employed to draw the water for the patrons, but it might as well be a self-service amenity. Piggott bases his conclusions on nothing but Sahani’s speculation. Wheeler too assumes it _might_ have been slave-quarters because Sumer and Egypt had those. This is as far from concrete as it gets.

Of course, as the earlier article confirms, a scavenger class is more likely than not to have existed in the Indus society. This class was responsible for clearing out the cisterns and operated mostly in the night. And these lived in segregated quarters outside of the citadel’s walls with restricted movements. Question is, was it paid labor or slave labor? Right now, with all that we have at our disposal, it’s impossible to answer that question. But we must keep in mind that while the presence of slavery in their society may be contested, their awareness of slavery cannot.

The Indus peoples maintained strong mercantile ties with the Western world. Ships from Meluhha carried exotic animals, carnelian, lapis lazuli, timber, and other goods to Dilmun, Sumer, and Egypt, returning with wool and bitumen. The discovery of Indus seals in Sumer points to the presence of Harappan outposts in Mesopotamian port cities. Both Egypt and Mesopotamia relied heavily on slave labor. Given such close contact, it is improbable that the Harappans were entirely unaware of this system. Merchants returning from overseas likely brought back accounts of Sumerian temples, Egyptian pyramids, and, inevitably, slaves.

## Conclusion

A clear pattern seems to emerge. The further back we go in time, the more speculative and deductive our understanding of history gets. The deductive part is crucial. Just because there’s speculation, it doesn’t all become a conspiracy theory unfounded in logic and reason. In the absence or scarcity of direct written records, triangulation from available artifacts and other indirect observations acquire immense value as tools of study. And with help of such tools, we can safely admit that slavery was more likely to be a Vedic feature than not. About the IVC society, even indirect inferences don’t help, and paid labor is as likely a possibility as slavery.

The epics, especially the _Mahabharata_ , tilt heavily in favor of slavery. In this context, one might see Valmiki’s Manthara as an exemplar of dāsīs enjoying far more liberties and influence than slaves in the non-Indic world. But that would be an incorrect reading on two grounds. Firstly, Manthara is an important figure, pivotal to Valmiki’s story, and may or may not epitomize the average dāsī in the society. Secondly, such examples aren’t exactly a novelty to the world at large either. In Ancient Mesopotamia, around the same time as Valmiki, we have a class of people called _shublugal_ , or “king’s slave.” These were nominally slaves but received land grants and special privileges from their masters for their service. Of course, the king had the authority to take away their belongings without compensation, but so did any slaveowner anywhere.

After the epics, things get a little fuzzy with the Mauryans where slaves are either so better off than their Greek counterparts that they didn’t even register as slaves to foreign travelers, or they simply didn’t exist. The latter would seem highly unlikely given their mentions in Aśoka’s edicts and Kautilya’s writings. It should be reasonable to then assume that they existed but were just less oppressed than their Greek counterparts.

We open the Common Era with the Shastrification of the Hindu tradition, led by the emergence of the Dharmashastra. Works of this genre, such as _Manusmriti_ , _Brihaspati Smriti_ , and _Nāradasmriti_ , make the presence of a thriving slave culture abundantly evident. Sections like 8.413–15 leave little to speculation or even deduction. In sum, from the Kushana period onward, slavery is a documented reality of the Indian society with none of the ambiguity of the past. Not that Kautilya’s law-code and Aśoka’s edicts do not evidence slavery before.

Finally, as we emerge from the Antiquity and enter the early decades of Medieval India, we start to see things more in-the-face than ever before. Works like _Lekhapaddhati_ document not just slavery in the truest sense of the word but also hint with ample clarity to even sex-slavery where a female slave was worth more than a male slave, and a prettier female slave was worth more than a non-pretty female slave. Every reference to a female slave is preoccupied with her beauty and youth. Another peculiarity that jumps at us in this period is the relative ubiquity of female slaves and a corresponding rarity of male slaves. Every slave mentioned in _Lekhapaddhati_ , for instance, is a girl. Zero exception. Even in the Shastras, that came before, we find visibly more female slaves than male. Sections like BS 9.21 make it a point to gender their references, and whenever they do, it’s always female. Of course, that shouldn’t be read as evidence of slavery being exclusively for girls, but it certainly makes one wonder if female slaves were more sought after or ubiquitous than male.

In sum, no, slavery was not an Islamic or Western injection. The institution has existed here since long before their arrival. What can still be said with reasonable objectivity is that both nature and scale of slavery changed drastically after their arrival. The sheer volume that ended up forcefully enslaved after the twelfth century was unprecedented in our history and without parallel in any part of the contemporary world. And that fact must be acknowledged in the same breath as the fact that slavery existed before them.
