# Bhojashala of Dhar

*Setting the Record Straight on the Controversial “Ayodhya of Malwa”*

About an hour and a half of easy driving from Indore is the historic city of Dhar. Today a quaint little township with little in the way of action or buzz, this was once the center of one of the most fiercely contested regions in the subcontinent, Malwa. First entering prominence with the advent of the Paramaras in the tenth century. From that point on, the city built and retained a reputation for culture and learning for at least four unbroken centuries before starting its yield to nearby Mandu.

Dhar is littered with countless buildings of historical significance, which makes it a useful portal to the India of medieval times. Unfortunately, it also makes the city a flashpoint of communal unrest and political controversies. That’s because for a good part of the period in question, Malwa was under Islamic rule. Naturally, this meant the coming in contact of two objectively disjoint cultures, a situation that has rarely been without its fair share of violence. Of all its structures, our attention is on what many call the “Bhojashala.” As is the case with countless other structures all over the country, this one is now an Islamic place of worship, the Kamal Maula Mosque. One can already see where this is going now. To Muslims, it’s a mosque and it was not built over any Hindu temple. To Hindus, it was Bhojashala or Bhoja’s school before the invaders turned it into a mosque. Déjà vu?

But wait, this doesn’t sound like Babri or Gyanvapi where temples were desecrated. This was a school. So why the fuss? Because the school also had a temple. To Saraswati, the Hindu goddess of learning, wisdom, and arts. So, one could say a temple was desecrated to build a mosque in its stead. While not as big-ticket a case as Ayodhya, Mathura, and Kashi, Dhar too has had its share of headlines over the past few decades. For the longest time, the issue has been placated with a simple administrative accommodation where both Hindus and Muslims are allowed to conduct their sacerdotal activities on a staggered basis. Until 2003, Hindus could pray here all day long once a year, on Basant Panchami, also known as Sarasvati Puja. Muslims got every Friday through 3:00 PM for their weekly congregation. Starting 2003, Hindus can also have it every Tuesday. Although not ideal, this still gives both parties enough to keep them from rioting. But what if the dates clashed?

This happened in 2013, when Basant Panchami fell on a Friday. Muslims were entitled to the place between 1:00 PM and 3:00 PM, but Hindus were entitled to it all day long. Neither party budged. Result? Violence. Three years later, the situation recurred. The ASI again made a similar accommodation—Hindus all day, with a two-hour window from 1:00 PM through 3:00 PM for Muslims. Which triggered the same kind of unrest. Clearly, this solution was not sustainable in the long run. Therefore, calls for a final settlement have started gaining traction over the years. Either a temple, or a mosque, can’t carry on being both. So that’s the controversy. Does it hold water? Is Kamal Maula Mosque really the Bhojashala?

The only reasonable way to settle the debate is by answering the following five questions:

  1. Was the mosque built with temple materials?

  2. Was the term Bhojashala in use before the British?

  3. Was the place used as a school before the Muslims?

  4. Did they worship Saraswati at this place?

  5. Was it already gone before the Muslims came?




Every single one of these questions need to have clear, irrefutable answers before we can establish the true identity of the structure. That’s precisely what we’ll attempt to do here.

## Repurposed of Rebuilt?

The most common practice under Muslim rule was that of repurposing old temples as mosques. This was done either by completely destroying a temple and building a mosque in situ using its materials, or by just converting the existing structure into one without any destruction. Either way, the presence of Hindu motifs or inscriptions inside a mosque is held as a reasonable indicator of desecration, even if not entirely foolproof as we’ll see in a bit. So, can that be said of the Kamal Maula Mosque too? If yes, it’d be a major score in favor of its Hindu claimants.

The first indication, albeit far from adequate, in this direction comes from an East India Company administrator John Malcolm. Before his stint as Governor of the Bombay Presidency, Malcolm spent four years helping the Company administer Central India. It’s during this period that he traveled across Malwa and made extensive notes on its political and cultural history, all of which he subsequently published as a two-volume memoir in 1824. In this work, Malcolm notes that the mosques and other Islamic structures in Dhar appear to be made from materials of the city’s finest temples. To support this judgment, he mentions a large stone slab he retrieved from the _minbar_ or pulpit of a ruined mosque. This slab was paved in the flooring of the pulpit and bore inscriptions in Sanskrit.1

The presence of a Hindu inscription in a mosque, especially as part of flooring, sure hints at desecration, but is that sufficient. He comments on all Muslim structures with just one stone slab from one ruined mosque as evidence. Was that ruined structure the Kamal Maula Mosque? He doesn’t specify. All this establishes beyond doubt is that at least one mosque in the region had employed materials from desecrated temples. We clearly need more.

Our next reference comes from yet another Scotsman, the celebrated architectural historian James Fergusson. Best known for his works on the contentious structures of Jerusalem, Fergusson also studied and made extensive notes on India’s architectural heritage. Fortunately for us, his _History of Indian and Eastern Architecture_ dedicates an entire section on Malwa. Here, he speaks of two mosques in Dhar and identifies both as built entirely of materials from destroyed Jain temples.2 Of the two is the Kamal Maula Mosque which he refers to as the Jama Masjid. After a detailed architectural illustration of the mosque, Fergusson confirms that everything about the structure’s interiors, other than the western wall is of a Hindu provenance.3 He too agrees with Malcolm that the materials, especially the pillars, were sourced from temples destroyed elsewhere.4 Do note that the words Hindu and Jain are used more or less interchangeably in his writing. So now we have specificity.

Even John Marshall posits that several Muslim structures in Dhar and Mandu were built using temple remains in his piece for the multi-volume _Cambridge History of India_. Although he doesn’t specifically name the Kamal Maula Mosque, much less Bhojashala, he’s categorical in the nearby Lat Masjid being built with pillars stripped from local Hindu temples.5 It’s possible to extrapolate this to Kamal Maula Mosque because Marshall also says that when the Muslims arrived, they found Hindu temples “in abundance” at both Dhar and Mandu, and “appropriated or despoiled” them for their purposes.6 Around the same time as Marshall, another Briton Ernest Barnes wrote an extensive article on the architectural relics in Dhar and Mandu for the Bombay Asiatic Society. He too reiterated what Marshall and Malcolm said. That the mosque was built with remains of Hindu temples destroyed elsewhere. He bolsters his position with references to epigraphy dating back to as early as the eleventh century.7 These include a couple of very interesting serpentine inscriptions and some imperial panegyrics that we will examine a little later.

But there still remain unresolved problems. Both Malcolm and Fergusson confirm that the mosque is built with temple remains. But that does not inherently imply appropriation. If temples were destroyed elsewhere, the argument that the mosque was built _over_ a temple falls short. Our two Scotsmen do not support replacement. Nor do Marshall and Barnes. Another big problem is that we still haven’t established if the site was in service when the Muslims arrived. If it was, was it as a school as suggested? Did it have a Saraswati temple on site? These are the questions we’re yet to answer. But we will.

## Bhojashala a British Invention?

The entire controversy hinges on the assumption that the Kamal Maula Mosque was Bhojashala before the Muslims. Of course, Hindus argue in the affirmative. But Muslims see it as a colonial invention. According to them, the very term was coined by a British officer either out of ignorance or out of malice. The latter cannot be ruled out, especially after the fiasco of 1857 when the Company faced a stiff military resistance from a united native force on scale for the first time. Although the British won this round, it was enough to alarm them. Hindus and Muslims had fought together, even if with vastly different objectives in mind, and dividing them became a political imperative in a post-Mutiny regime. Cooking up stories about Hindu temples being desecrated by Muslim invaders was an easy way to accomplish that.

The word Bhojashala made its debut in an 1896 work by the controversial German Indologist Alois Anton Führer. At the time he worked for the Archeological Survey of India and had established himself as quite the sensation in the study of Indian antiquity. He is credited with establishing Buddha’s links with Lumbini and many other firsts. Unfortunately, his career ended in disgrace over proven allegations of forgery and plagiarism. Be that as it may, Bhojashala it was. From this point, every British paper on the subject referred to the structure as a Sanskrit school founded by the medieval king. Let’s examine a few.

Three years after Führer’s publication, an official British India gazetteer spoke of a mosque in Dhar which used to be the fabled Sanskrit school.8 To support this claim, the article points to the pillars that bear several Sanskrit grammar tables and verses praising one of Bhoja’s successors Naravarman.9 In total, including panegyrics to other Paramara princes, the article estimates as many as 4,000 verses in both Sanskrit and Prakrit inscribed on marble slabs in the flooring and on pillars.10 We will discuss these inscriptions in a later section.

Coming back to Barnes’ article on the subject, it categorically refers to the Kamal Maula Mosque as Bhoja’s school.11 In support of his argument, he points to the local Hindus who had traditionally referred to it as _Raja Bhoj ka Madrassa_.12 While he does not use the exact word Bhojashala, he makes no ambiguity of the mosque being an erstwhile school. We’ve already seen him agree with the likes of Malcolm that the mosque was built with remains of temples destroyed elsewhere.13

But these, as we’ve noted earlier, are all British sources. The very contention is that the term Bhojashala did not exist in currency before the British. So far, all we have is that the structure was built from temple parts. Is there anything contemporary that could establish pre-British Bhojashala? We can look, but first, let’s examine a most referenced refutation of everything we have so far.

## Michael Willis’ Refutation

Canadian Indologist Michael Willis created quite the stir in 2012 when he published his paper on Bhojashala and the politics surrounding its story in the Journal of the Royal Asiatic Society. His overarching position is that while Hindu materials went into the construction of Muslim structures in Dhar, which includes the mosque in question, it was driven less by conquest and more by pragmatism. According to him, just as people converted from old faith to new, so did structures. Temples that were already in ruins and disuse were repurposed into newer buildings, such as mosques or palaces. In other words, no desecration was involved.

In support of this position, Willis points to the 1879 edition of William Kincaid’s _History of Mandu_. Kincaid was a civil servant in British India who wrote extensively on the subject of Malwa’s material heritage. In fact, his work on Mandu, which first came out in 1844, was the second of its kind after Malcolm’s. He wrote this book under the curious pseudonym of _A Bombay Subaltern_. In this work, Kincaid speaks of Kamal Maula’s tomb which sits within the Kamal Maula complex.14 He also speaks of a well in the premises and of its legend as a miraculous source of wisdom, the _aql ka kuan_.15 But makes no mention of any pre-Islamic school of Sanskrit, much less by the name Bhojashala. Willis takes this as evidence that there was none.16

We have already noted Führer and his appetite for fabrication and sensationalism. He was the first to posit the idea of Bhojashala. His mentor was none other than German philologist Georg Bühler who traveled all over Rajputana and made detailed notes of his epigraphic findings. He was particularly into Paramara history and Bhoja’s literary acumen in the context of Sanskrit philology. Interestingly, while Bühler writes extensively about the Paramaras’ temple frenzy and Bhoja’s Sanskrit scholarship, he writes nothing about a Bhojashala. This, Willis, takes as a telling sign because Bühler “actively engaged with traditional knowledge-holders and knew about Sanskrit schools.”17 Just to be sure, however, Willis also notes that Bühler never personally visited Dhar.18

Willis’ next and final salvo attacks the word itself. He raises, and rightly so, the validity of the term Bhojashala as a Sanskrit name for Bhoja’s school. That’s because _śala_ is Sanskrit for house, not school, which is how we get terms like _dharmaśala_. For school, Sanskrit has compounds like _vidyalaya_ , _vidyapitha_ , or _jnanapitha_. Following that logic, Bhojashala would simply translate as _house of Bhoja_ and not school.19 Therefore, Willis argues, even if Bhoja built a school, it could not have been called Bhojashala.

With that, we can put to rest the debate over the Bhojashala as a name. But we’re still left with the task of conclusively disproving there being a school of Sanskrit, by whatever name other than Bhojashala, at the spot. If that were true, the name itself becomes immaterial. So far, we’ve only examined British sources. Now let’s look at some texts closer to the time of Bhoja.

## Parijatamanjari

Although the term Bhojashala was coined by Alois Führer, it really caught on only after endorsement from India’s very own K K Lele. He was the Superintendent of State Education when Barnes published his work on Malwa. Seeing the interest his work generated, Barnes organized a unit dedicated to investigating the region’s medieval history. Lele was appointed to helm this unit. This was in the fall of 1902.

Barnes was convinced of the mosque’s Hindu origins not only because Führer said so but also because so did the locals. But both are easy to dismiss as weak, which is what Willis did too. And this is where Lele comes in. A big factor in Barnes’ theory was Lele’s retrieval and reading of the inscriptions on a black marble slab from the _mihrab_ or prayer niche of the Kamal Maula Mosque in the winter of 1903.20 Interestingly, when the slab was first located on the wall, it was with the inscribed face turned inside.21 Lele was a Sanskrit scholar, so translating the inscription was not a difficult task. It was a Sanskrit play—with a sprinkling of Prakrit, another language of Lele’s expertise—from the time of Arjunavarman, a Paramara scion from the early thirteenth century.

Sanskrit was heavily deployed in performing arts and several plays were composed in the language back in the day. It was a common practice to inscribe these plays on temple walls to aid actors with their lines. Like medieval teleprompters, if you will. Why temple walls? Because temples, typically of Sarasvati, were often the venues of such performances. The very prologue of the drama makes this clear by categorically naming the temple of Sharadadevi, another name for Sarasvati, as the venue of its first performance.22 Therefore, the presence of this inscription in the mosque could be a crucial piece of the puzzle, even though it still would not establish an _in situ_ appropriation of the temple or school, for the slab could also have been brought in from a temple destroyed elsewhere.

While Lele’s report was later added to _Epigraphia Indica_ , the celebrated compendium of Indian inscriptions, it does not include his translation. For that, we’ll refer to his original, the _Summary of the Dramatic Inscription Found at the Bhoja Shala_. Here, we find hints of the play’s strong association with a Sarasvati temple in Dhar, which is named as Dharanagar in the inscription.

In the third and fourth stanzas, the _sutradhara_ or stage-manager informs us what we just noted above, that the play is to be performed in the temple of Sharada or Sarasvati in Dhar.23 To ensure there’s no ambiguity on the identity of the temple, given Dhar was home to dozens, it further qualifies it as the “chief of all temples in Dhar and is now full of good poets, connoisseurs, experts in various arts, those with sympathetic hearts, and savants who are well-versed in the three Learnings, who have come from all the quarters of the world.”24 This establishes two things:

  1. That this was the most important temple in the entire Paramara domain, and

  2. That it also served as a renowned school of arts which attracted learners from far and wide.




What this still doesn’t establish is that the Kamal Maula Mosque was built exactly where this temple stood. But the fact that the inscription was found in the mosque is a pretty reasonable hint in that direction. This Sarasvati association is an important part of our story, we’ll return to her later.

## Prabhavakacharita

The Paramaras were not doing very well when the Muslims arrived. By the twelfth century, they had adversaries all around, given the economic and political incentives Malwa offered. Among them were the neighboring Chalukyas who made several bids on the city. Toward the end of his life, around the year 1135, their king Jayasimha defeated the Paramaras and succeeded in annexing Dhar to his domain. Nearly a century and a quarter later, this conquest became the subject of a Svetambara Jain text titled _Prabhavakacharita_. Named after its author Prabhachandra, this text offers perhaps the most elaborate depiction of life at the storied temple-cum-school.

Although Islam came to Malwa around 1231 with the invasion of Delhi’s Iltutmish, Kamal-al-Din only showed up in 1291. So, the mosque honoring him could not be older than that, in which case the text in question clearly predates it, even if only by a couple of decades. Any mention of a temple-school in it, therefore, should be a strong enough lead for us. And one such lead can be found in a section that reads thus:25

It speaks of an elegant Sanskrit grammar curriculum developed by Bhoja himself, which students recite and study every day at the school. The term used for school is of interest here. Normally, it’s vidyalaya or _pathashala_ , but the snippet uses a far from common _vidyamatha_. The word itself is a portmanteau of _vidya_ , i.e. knowledge, and _matha_ , i.e. monastery. So, the venue must serve as both a place of worship as well as a place of education—a temple-school. This is exactly what the Bhojashala of history would be if true. Although the writing is not coeval with Bhoja himself, the two being a good two centuries apart, it still serves our purpose because it came before the Sufi in question. The fact that it doesn’t mention the exact name Bhojashala is immaterial to the conversation since it describes what Bhojashala is said to be anyway. The word pathashala also appears in the final line of the snippet.

## Prabandhacintāmaṇi

 _Prabandha_ is a genre of Jain literature in colloquial Sanskrit from medieval Gujarat and Malwa. Works in this genre typically consist of biographical narratives of inspiring personalities with an element of fantasy and legend. Much of its corpus was produced in the final centuries before the advent of Islam. _Prabandhacintāmaṇi_ is a collection of such works compiled in the early years of the fourteenth century. This makes it coeval with Kamal-al-Din Chishti’s stint in Malwa. But since it speaks of men like Bhoja and Jayasimha, the Chalukya king who annexed Malwa in the twelfth century, it can still offer crucial insights and serve its purpose.

Gleaning from a 1901 translation by British Indologist and Sanskrit scholar Charles Henry Tawney, we learn that while Bhoja was a Brahmin, he was not only tolerant of Jainism but also positively predisposed to it. The prabandha narrates the story of a Jain monk proselytizing the faith to him who subsequently warmed up to the philosophy.26 In this narration, the author speaks of a Sarasvati temple named _Sarasvati-Kanthabharana_ (“Sarasvati’s necklace”) which has an antechamber with three doors.27 This name is key. Because it’s also the name of Bhoja’s Sanskrit treatise.28 The same treatise we saw spoken of in Prabhavakacharita earlier.

Although this work came out after the arrival of the Muslims, it’s consequential not only because instead of contradicting earlier works it reinforces them, but also because it establishes Bhoja’s positive attitude toward Jainism. Why this matters, will soon become apparent. The fact that this work applies the name of Bhoja’s treatise to the temple also further crystallizes the central academic role played by the temple back in the day. Everything checks out. With that, we can finally return to the two serpentine inscriptions that Barnes spoke of in his work.

## The Serpents

Also known as _sarpabandha_ , these were first spotted on one of the pillars in the mosque and translated by Lele29 who then reported them to Barnes. The set consists of two inscriptions, one with a single serpent and the other with two in a complex helical formation. The single-serpent inscription lists out the terminations for eighteen verbs and twenty-one nouns in various inflected forms, for a total of thirty-nine terminations. The double-serpent inscription is slightly larger and contains verb conjugation tables for the three persons and three numbers in ten Sanskrit tenses and moods. Two sets of terminations are offered for each tense, one for _parasmaipada_ or transitive form and the other for _atmanepada_ or intransitive form. That’s a total of 180 terminations.30

Just above the snake-tables, on the same pillar is a Sanskrit poem of two stanzas. These announce Udayaditya and Naravarmana as the saviors of all four _varṇas_ and of the letters of the alphabet.31 The latter could be an allusion to knowledge. Of the two, Udayaditya has been named as the patron who commissioned the inscriptions. He was Bhoja’s grandson and a later successor. Naravarmana was Udayaditya’s son and successor, and his reign ended almost exactly a hundred years before the arrival of Muslims in the region. This places the inscriptions well within the pre-Islamic period even if not coeval with Bhoja himself. The dating receives further reinforcement from Lele who placed some of the letters in the eleventh and twelfth centuries.32 The script is the same Nagari that we Indic speakers are all so familiar with, but certain characters are in an archaic form that went out of fashion long before the Muslims showed up.

So, we have thus far irrefutable evidence that the mosque was built from the remains of at least one Hindu structure, that the Hindu structure was a major scholastic monastery that attracted enthusiasts from distant lands, that Sarasvati was worshiped, and Sanskrit prosody was taught there, that it was established by Bhoja.

We also have some basis in Prabandhacintāmaṇi and Prabhavakacharita, both of which took form after the Muslims had started coming to Malwa, to assume that the school was operational through Chalukya invasions all the way to the time of Kamal-al-Din Chishti. But this position isn’t without contest. Therefore, before we proceed to anything else, it’s important that we settle this. The school’s continuity is a crucial aspect of the contemporary debate around its identity. If it was already in disuse and ruins when the Muslims arrived, the question of it being repurposed as a mosque becomes moot.

## Jayasimha’s Invasion

Prabhavakacharita details the invasion of Malwa and its subsequent annexation to the domain of Chalukya king Jayasimha (not to be confused with Bhoja’s immediate successor with the same name). While this invasion took place in 1135, Prabhavakacharita only came in 1277. That’s a gap of more than a hundred years. Isn’t there something more primary? American Indologist Sheldon Pollock, who has studied this text extensively, gives us an answer in his 2006 book _The Language of the Gods_.

In it, he points to an inscription that was put up on Jayasimha’s orders immediately after his conquest of Malwa to commemorate the event. We will examine the inscription in a bit but first let’s see what Pollock has to say. He confirms that Jayasimha personally visited the school and witnessed classes being conducted day and night.33 This makes sense given Bhojashala’s enduring fame as a center of arts and learning. However, Pollock also says that Jayasimha then sacked the capital and looted the library,34 which one can safely extrapolate to Bhojashala itself. Part of the loot was also the famous grammar that Bhoja had codified, Sarasvati-Kanthabharana.35 Not happy with Bhoja’s grammar, Jayasimha later commissioned Jain saint Hemachandra to improve upon it and produce a more elegant work. The work was subsequently completed as _Siddhahemacandra_. This fact is attested to by Hemachandra himself in a note toward the end of his work and later also admitted by Prabhavakacharita.

But appropriation of one work, or even several, does not in and of itself imply destruction. It’s still possible that the city was sacked, and its library looted, but the building itself was left standing. In which case, it’s possible that the academics would have later, maybe after a brief window of confusion during the invasion, resumed their activities at the school. This checks out with the dating of the serpentine inscriptions which clearly seem to have either escaped the loot or been made after the sack. Which brings us to the commemorative inscription of Jayasimha we spoke of earlier, the only primary source of the invasion.

[courtesy Saurabh Saxena in “Bilpank – Virupaksha Mahadev Temple,” _Puratattva_ , 7 December 2022, https://puratattva.in/bilpank-virupaksha-mahadev-temple. Accessed 5 May 2024.]

It’s the Bilpank Praśasti made by Chalukya poet Śripala at the king’s behest and named after the village in Ratlam where it was found. The inscription makes itself easy to date by spelling out the date at the end—Saturday, June 7, 1141. Of its thirty verses in total, only three are of import to us here, verses 22, 23, and 24. The latter two read thus:36

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
    (23) When in good will he toured the land of Malwa,
    conquered by the power of his arms, he came upon
    the God Virupaksha indecorously housed
    in a ruined temple. He paid worship and had a vision of the God
    illuminated by the light from the crescent moon in His hair,
    by the fire from His forehead eye, by the glow
    from the jewels in the heads of the snakes on His chest.
    
    (24) Then, in devotion to the Supreme Lord
    he had a temple built, with a towering summit,
    high as a mountain, and a place
    where lovely birds come soaring in their play.

Virupaksha here is just another name for Shiva. Jayasimha was likely a devout Shaivite who found a neglected Shiva temple in Bilpank during his tour of Malwa in the aftermath of the conquest. Being a Shiva worshipper, he ordered its restoration and consecration the result of which still stands in the village to this day. The inscription still sits in the temple’s _antarala_ , the antechamber to the _garbhagriha_. These verses somewhat contradict Jayasimha’s destructive image as painted by Pollock. But that’s just one temple, and of his favored sect. He could still be wildly destructive otherwise? Which brings us to verse 22:37

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
    (22)  He has restored to respect what had fallen into disrespect,
    the city of Avanti that had lost its protection;
    and though hard even for him to do, he has rebuilt Dhara
    He destroyed enemy fortresses and emptied the country
    of Malwa of the people of Malwa.
    Not just in name, clearly, is he “master of political power.”

This one paints a rather interesting picture. Clearly, Jayasimha doesn’t seem terribly fond of the people of Malwa which is why he “emptied the country” of them. This, of course, could be poetic exaggeration, but the fact that he wasn’t terribly kind to them holds. Having said that, the verse also informs that he restored Avanti to its former glory. Avanti is generally read as synonymous to Ujjain, the site of the famous Mahakal Temple. Again, this too has Shiva connection. But the verse goes further and tells us that he also _rebuilt_ Dhar and destroyed its fortress. Perhaps the buildings in the city were damaged in the war? If that’s the case, he must have restored the Bhojashala too. But it’s equally possible that he destroyed it after looting the library. There’s no way to conclusively affirm either position.

We can, however, brush aside the simplistic notion that Jayasimha was into wholesale destruction and therefore must inevitably have destroyed the school. And once again, the fact that sarpabandha inscriptions were either made after the sack or made before but escaped the sack, is consequential to this investigation. Either he didn’t destroy Bhojashala, or he did but later restored.

What we still don’t have irrefutable evidence for is that the Kamal Maula Mosque itself was Bhojashala. It’s quite likely that Bhojashala was repurposed as a mosque, but it’s just as likely that the Bhojashala stood elsewhere, was destroyed, and its remains then used to build the mosque where it stands today. There’s only one way to resolve this, the goddess.

## The Dhar Sarasvati

In 1875, William Kincaid came in possession of a sculpture in the old city palace at Dhar. Sometime in the 1880s this artifact was entered into the British Museum inventory where it remains to this day (access number 1919,1224.1). It’s a female figure identified by the museum as Ambikā. This is contentious but before we get to why, let’s get acquainted with the character.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!QY_w!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F321c8bbb-c901-421f-89dd-f0dd130410a7_421x914.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!QY_w!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F321c8bbb-c901-421f-89dd-f0dd130410a7_421x914.png)**Fig. 4** : The contested figure of Ambā, noted as a yakshini by the British Museum and Michael Willis, and Sarasvati by Hindus. [courtesy Museum no. 1909,1224.1, British Museum. Accessed 5 May 2024.]

Ambikā is a prominent divine figure in Jain traditions where she’s named as a _yakshini_ , one of twenty-five. _Yakshas_ are a class of nature spirits in Indic traditions with both benevolent and malevolent personalities. The benevolent ones are often worshiped as tutelaries and family deities. Yakshini is the female counterpart. In Jain traditions, they are often depicted as protectors of _tirthankaras_. Ambikā, for instance, protects Neminatha, the twenty-second tirthankara. Long story short, she’s a demigoddess.

Hindus claim her as the Goddess Sarasvati. If true, this would strengthen the case for Bhojashala because it was found in Dhar. Luckily for us, the man who sculpted it left a detailed inscription at the statue’s base in the late Nagari script:

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
    Auṃ. Vararuci, who is intent on the dharma of the Candranagarī and Vidyādharī [branches of the Jain religion] of srīmad Bhoja the king, the apsaras [as it were] for the easy removal [of ignorance? by...?], that Vararuci, having first fashioned Vāgdevī the mother [and] afterwards a triad of Jinas, made this beautiful image of Ambā, ever abundant in fruit. Blessings! It was executed by Maṇathala, son of the sūtradhāra Sahira. It was written by Śivadeva the proficient. Year 1091.

Right off the bat, this places the artifact within Bhoja’s tenure, although the inscription itself came more than three decades after his death. Furthermore, it also names the deity Ambā, instead of Ambikā. This matters and we’ll soon see why. It also indicates that the sculptor was a Jain, for he had sculpted a bunch of other Jain figures besides Ambā.

Remember we spoke of Bhoja’s positive attitude toward Jainism in the section on Prabandhacintāmaṇi? This is where it becomes consequential. If Bhojashala as an academic monastery was indeed a historical reality, the presence of Jain deities in it would only be possible if the king were favorably predisposed to the faith. Bhoja was a Hindu for sure. But he must have tolerated and even welcomed Jain figures at his school. Now we’re still left to resolve the Ambikā–Ambā confusion.

Why the museum insists on calling her Ambikā when the inscription clearly says Ambā is best known to them. Although it’s not entirely incorrect since Ambā can and sometimes is taken synonymously with Ambikā, there’s a catch. Ambikā is just a yakshini, but Ambā is also an epithet for the supreme mother goddess in Hindu traditions. Parvati, Durga, and Sarasvati are all expressions of the same mother deity. Thus, Ambā could just as legitimately be Sarasvati. And that’s not mere conjecture. Rigveda 2.41.16 reads:38

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _ambitame nadītame devitame sarasvati
    apraśastā iva smasi praśastim amba nas kṛdhi
    “_ O best mother, best river, best goddess, Sarasvati—
    we are like ones unlauded: make a laud for us, mother.” (trans. Jamison)

In this ode to the sacred Rigvedic river, the root _ambā_ is used twice as a synonym for mother. This is not the same as the goddess of our story, but it amply demonstrates the versatility of Ambā as a noun. Thus, the Ambā of Vararuci is at least as likely to be Sarasvati, if not more, as a Jain yakshini. The sculptor being Jain doesn’t pose a problem either because Sarasvati is just as sacred to Jains as she is to Hindus. The oldest Sarasvati figure, in fact, comes from Mathura’s Kankali Tila and is of a Jain provenance. This still doesn’t sway the verdict either way with conviction.

Even the iconography isn’t much help, for it could apply to either character—from the elephant goad, which could just as well be a writing stylus, to the female attendant with the garland, which could just as well be a gandharva. The two figures at the feet could be the yakshini’s two children Priyankara and Shubhankara. But since one of them seems to sport a beard, it could be someone else. Brahma, perhaps? Ambā the yakshini always carries a mango or a mango twig. That would be the most telling sign. Unfortunately, our statue is missing in two of its four arms, so we’ll never know. For now, the best we have is that it’s as likely to be Ambikā as Sarasvati.

Another thing we still haven’t established is that the artifact originally sat in what’s today the Kamal Maula Mosque. One can, however, conjecture in that direction on the basis of the fact that the city palace where the figure was found is notably close to the mosque. Perhaps the statue was removed before the building was taken over by the Muslims? Hard to conclude.

## Takeaway

In sum, only a thorough survey of the site can throw up the answers we need. What we have so far is that the mosque was definitely built with remains of desecrated temples, that Bhoja’s school definitely existed even if not by the name Bhojashala, that Sarasvati was definitely worshiped there, that it was definitely a major enough center of arts and learning to attract patrons from faraway kingdoms, that it definitely did not suffer closure when the Chalukyas invaded, and that it was definitely operational when the Muslims arrived.

The only question that remains to be answered with conviction is whether it was the same building that now houses the mosque, or some other that was destroyed for building materials.
