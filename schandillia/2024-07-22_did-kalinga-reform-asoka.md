# Did Kalinga Reform Aśoka?

*A Detailed Fact-Check on One of Ancient India’s Most Consequential Landmarks*

- date: 2024-07-22
- audience: only_paid
- url: https://www.schandillia.com/p/did-kalinga-reform-asoka

Aśoka invaded Kalinga. Massacred thousands. Felt remorseful. Embraced Buddhism. Renounced violence. So goes the story as we’ve been told for generations. Over recent years, however, this narrative has come under intense scrutiny, as have all others. People are now beginning to question the veracity of this story because the argument, like most others from Indian history, has acquired a clear political and ideological color over time. Whether or not Aśoka converted has become part of a larger Hinduism-Buddhism debate. It’s not just whether Aśoka repented, it’s whether Hinduism inspires violence while Buddhism discourages it. In this context, Hinduism always stands for Brahminism and Buddhism for caste upliftment. We have already noted in an earlier article how the difference between the two philosophies was fuzzy at best for the longest time, we’ve also noted how “Brahmin” was as coveted a title in Buddhist traditions as it was in Vedic. This time, we examine how Aśoka influences the discourse. In this article, we hope to find out if his remorse and subsequent conversion to Buddhism holds water against objective scrutiny and answer the following questions:

  1. Did he convert to Buddhism?

  2. If he did, was it before or after Kalinga?

  3. Was he apologetic about Kalinga?

  4. Did Kalinga make him renounce violence?




Much of this really just boils down to what really happened after the Kalinga War. It is understood that he reigned for about three decades after the event. Examining his policies and actions during those three decades should help answer all the above questions and settle the argument one way or the other.

## The Major Edicts

The most primary source of this claim is none other than the emperor himself. Aśoka holds a highly underrated distinction—of being among the first Indians on record to write. Before him, communication was mostly oral. Which is in and of itself an oddity given Indians had a rich tradition of making seal inscriptions long before the Mauryans. In fact, every Bronze Age civilization wrote. Why this changed for India in the Iron Age remains a subject of intense and inconclusive speculation. Be that as it may, Aśoka became the first to commit stuff to writing on scale in India. His royal edicts are the oldest on the subcontinent. He likely adopted the practice from the Achaemenids who ruled a large part of what’s today Afghanistan and Pakistan just before Chandragupta Maurya inaugurated his reign. Anyway, this discussion isn’t about the history of writing, so we’ll leave it at that. Of import to this context is one such edict from Kalsi in Uttarakhand—Major Rock Edict XIII.

Aśoka commissioned a number of imperial inscriptions all over his realm, some on rocks, others on pillars. Some announce matters of general State policy, ethics, and principles. They were made on either large rock surfaces or tall pillars. We call them Major Rock and Pillar Edicts respectively. Others contain shorter, more specific messages, often related to administrative orders, local issues, or personal reflections of Aśoka, and were made on smaller rock surfaces and shorter pillars. These are Minor Rock and Pillar Edicts. There are a total of twenty-four Major Edicts, of which fourteen are on rocks and ten on pillars.

The one in question likely came up in Aśoka’s thirteenth regnal year or thereabouts and opens with a reference to the Kalinga War that it says took place in the ninth regnal year.1 That is after eight years of ruling as king. The inscription speaks of both carnage and the remorse it inspired. It says that the war claimed more than 100,000 lives and uprooted 150,000.2 But now that the war had been won and the land of Kalinga conquered, Dēvānāmpriya Priyadarśin or Aśoka was determined to study “morality” and impress it upon his subjects. The emperor then expresses “profound sorrow” and “remorse” for the massacre,3 but this is about as far as he goes in the way of remorse. He sure uses the word “deplorable,”4 but is hardly apologetic about his personal role in it. What’s more deplorable than the slaughter, according to the edict, is that it included Brahmins and Sramanas (Buddhists and Jains).5 In later snippets, a great deal is made of morality and virtues. In these lines, Aśoka insists on spreading the gospel throughout his realm and beyond—to the Cholas, to the Pandyas, to the Andhras, to the Kambojas, to even the Greeks and the Lankans.6

There is no mention whatsoever of Buddha or Buddhism. Only one mention of Sramana where it’s listed alongside Brahmins as the groups that “shouldn’t have been slayed” in Kalinga.7 While Sramana is often read synonymously with Buddhist, the word includes many other groups and, as we’ve seen in an earlier article on the subject, isn’t exclusive to non-Hindu doctrines. That said, there’s another reason people read this edict as proof of conversion to Buddhism—its generous use of a very Buddhist term, _dhamma_.8 The word is Prakrit for _dharma_. Vedic works never use Prakrit, they’re exclusively in Sanskrit. While this may seem to indicate Buddhism, it’s still a matter of language. Edicts were made in the language of the region for maximal absorption, which makes perfect sense. The Prakrit vernacular basket, which included several tongues including Pali, was the subcontinent’s _lingua franca_. It’d be absurd to then put up imperial messages in Sanskrit which only a tiny handful would understand. Reading Aśoka’s usage of dhamma as evidence of him converting to Buddhism is like reading a Lebanese Christian’s usage of Allah as evidence of their converting to Islam. The evidence is inadequate. Aśoka may or may not have been a Buddhist at the time of the inscription, but the usage of Sramana and dhamma alone doesn’t confirm that. Could there be anything else that might add weight to the position? We’ll see in a bit. But for now, let’s return to remorse.

In sum, while Kalinga does seem to have had a reforming effect on Aśoka’s psyche, as apparent from MRE XIII, it would be incorrect to take it as a conclusive proof of his remorse or apology. The edict’s language is akin to a modern-day politician personally orchestrating, leading, and executing an episode of violence and later “regretting” it an “unfortunate incident.” Regret is not apology. But did he even regret? The edict clearly says so, but the sentiment itself can and must be brought into question in light of two observations. First is a snippet in the same edict where Aśoka speaks of his “power to punish”9 those who refused to submit to his rule, “in spite of my compassion.”10 Why would a warrior apologetic about his own violence use such a threatening language? Intimidation and regret generally do not go together. But let’s not conclude anything just yet.

Which brings us to the other observation that involves location. Kalsi is in Uttarakhand, Kalinga is Orissa, and there’s more than a thousand miles between the two. It’s amusing that if there’s regret, an admission thereof is so conspicuously missing from the site of the very event that’s being regretted. None exists even in the vicinity. A thousand miles is too far even by today’s standards. Surely, the Kalingan victims had no way of reading about their aggressor’s “change of heart” declaration in a faraway Kalsi. In fact, the fourteen Major Rock Edicts have been found inscribed in six different locations all over the subcontinent. At this point, it’s important to understand the difference between edict and inscription.

The message is the edict. The representation is the inscription. Just as a book can have several copies, an edict can have several inscriptions. Aśoka ruled over an enormous territory and a time when mobility was severely limited. Therefore, inscribing an edict at one place would be extremely ineffective from the communication standpoint. Multiple copies at strategic locations were needed. Which is exactly what he did. Thus, the fourteen Major Rock Edicts were inscribed at not only Kalsi but also Sopara near Mumbai, Girnar in Saurashtra, Mansehra and Shahbaz Garhi in Pakistan’s Khyber Pakhtunwa, and Erragudi in Andhra Pradesh.11

But that’s not to say there’s no edict in Orissa at all. There are, in fact, eleven. At not one but two sites—Dhauli and Jaugada. The three that are missing are XI, XII, and XIII.12 Yes, the very edict that speaks of the war in Kalinga is for some reason missing from Kalinga. Of the six MRE XIII sites listed above, the closest is Erragudi and even that is more than 700 miles away.

Besides the eleven remaining edicts, Kalinga also has two others not found elsewhere, hence called Kalinga Edicts. These are also part of the MRE collection but typically kept out of numbering which stops at XIV. Some authors count them as XV and XVI, but most refer to them as just First and Second Separate Major Rock Edicts.13 Dhauli is by most reckoning the presumed site of the war. That’s where we have XV. The Second or XVI is in Jaugada, about a hundred miles to the south. The absence of MRE XIII from Dauli is particularly noteworthy. But what about the two Kalinga Edicts?

Both edicts address the _Mahamatras_ or bureaucrats at the Kalingan capital of Tosali and list out a range of administrative changes under the new Mauryan regime.14 Again, the word dhamma appears a couple of times but no mention otherwise of Buddha or even the Kalingan bloodshed. As if it never happened. It’s strange that amidst all the talks of regime change and administrative overhauls, there’s no talk of the 150,000 Kalingans killed in the war and no remorse for the carnage. These edicts were put up simultaneously with the one in Kalsi that _does_ regret the bloodshed.

All said and done, it’s not entirely unlikely that Aśoka was at least _into_ Buddhism, if at all not a full-blown Buddhist himself, at the time of these edicts. While the liberal usage of dhamma in and of itself may not amount to much, in combination with something else it can. One translation of MRE XIII mentions the word “convert.” But not for Aśoka himself. Remember the section where he threatened forest-dwellers with dire consequences for insubordination? So, this translation elaborates it as refusal to “submit, pacify, and convert.”15 To what? Another translation answers—to “this way of life,”16 which one may, within reason and in conjunction with the usage of Sramana and dhamma, as Buddhism. So, it can be admitted that the emperor was certainly involved with Buddhism at the time of these edicts. Still, none of this establishes if he was a convert or just an aficionado at that point. Let’s go with convert for the sake of the argument. The question that even then remains unaddressed is, did he convert _after_ Kalinga? Or was it long before? To answer this, we ought to look beyond the edicts.

## Dīpavaṃsa

This is a non-canonical chronicle of Buddhism from Sri Lanka, the oldest of its kind. The name is a portmanteau of _dīpa_ or island and _vaṃsa_ or chronicle—Chronicle of the Island. The work is in Pali, a Prakrit vernacular popular in Buddhist works, and was first translated into English in 1879 by German Indologist Hermann Oldenberg. Although oldest as a historical account, the text only dates to the third century AD with some scholars even positing fourth. Which means at least half a millennium since than the War. Certainly not a primary source. But what still makes it a solid piece of the puzzle is the fact that it’s a Buddhist work, produced by Buddhist monks, from a Buddhist vantage point. This is important, for the most notable empire-builder on the subcontinent converting to Buddhism as an apology for a most notable episode of violence would be too tempting a testimony to be given a pass in a Buddhist work. If anything, it should be highlighted, perhaps even exaggerated.

So, what does it say about Aśoka? Does it confirm his conversion? Of course it does. And in no passing reference, _Dīpavaṃsa_ dedicates an entire chapter of no fewer than ninety-nine verses to this topic.17 As expected, a ruler of Aśoka’s stature converting to Buddhism must be a mighty important event in a work that celebrates Buddhism and chronicles its landmark. Although one could still doubt Dīpavaṃsa’s authenticity in this matter given the motivation it has for fabrication, we won’t nitpick anymore and admit in good faith that the king did switch to Buddhism at some point in his illustrious life. But the question remains, at which point? When he converted is far more important to answer than if he. Fortunately for us, Dīpavaṃsa does not leave that question unaddressed. In 6:18, it informs that Aśoka converted to Buddhism, three years into his reign:18

     _Asoko rajjaṃ kâresi Pâṭaliputte puruṭṭame,
    abhisitto tîṇi vassâni pasanno buddhasâsane._
    “Asoka ruled in Pataliputra, best of towns,
    three years after his coronation, he was converted to Buddha’s faith.” (trans. Oldenberg)

But the storied war took place in his ninth regnal year. Which implies that Aśoka was already a Buddhist of six years by the time he entered the battle. In less flattering words, he was a Buddhist invader of Kalinga under whose watch over a hundred thousand Kalingans lose their lives by his own admission. Admission which, by the way, does not offer any apology.

Interestingly, Dīpavaṃsa makes zero mention of Kalinga, much less the war over it. What then was Aśoka’s motivation according to this text? And that’s what brings us to possibly the best-known lore about the man—that he massacred his brothers, all one hundred of them, to get the throne.19 Of course, this is legend, like a whole lot of things written back in the day. Or at least an exaggeration of what might have actually happened. Perhaps there was a power struggle and he happened to prevail having killed all his siblings—of whom there certainly weren’t a hundred—in the process. Fratricide wasn’t really uncommon back in the day. Anyway, upon taking the throne, Aśoka started exploring doctrinal merits of the numerous sects and creeds that thrived in his domain.20 Dīpavaṃsa does not explicitly tell us that this was triggered by remorse over killing his brothers, but it’s not such an unlikely possibility either.

In his quest, the new emperor organized several debates and spiritual discourses involving all sixty-two doctrines active in his territory and found each of them deficient.21 Then one day he ran into a young Sramana called Nigrodha and finally settled on Buddhism.22 Thus, Dīpavaṃsa tells us four things about Aśoka:

  1. He did convert to Buddhism,

  2. The conversion happened five years before Kalinga,

  3. It took three years, and

  4. The process started in the aftermath his bloody coronation.




Again, no mention of Kalinga, no mention of a war, and no mention of remorse even for the fratricide. So that’s Dīpavaṃsa. While it tries its best to show Aśoka in a flattering light, it does offer a glimpse of his dark side too, and without any explanation. One still ought to keep in mind that the work is from more than five hundred years _after_ the emperor in question. For further investigation, we’ll now examine a text of a relatively recent vintage.

## Aśokāvadāna

So far, we have discussed edicts and chronicles written in the Pali vernacular. Now, let’s explore some sources in Sanskrit. _Avadāna_ is a genre of Buddhist literature that aims to teach moral lessons and virtues, much like the Jātakas. These tales typically recount the Buddha’s past lives, often in his own voie, illustrating the karmic cycle through straightforward examples of good and bad deeds and their consequences. While much of this corpus exists in Pali, there is also a substantial collection in Sanskrit. Part of the latter group is the _Aśokāvadāna_. As the name suggests, it is a hagiographical account of Emperor Aśoka, seeking to merge his grandeur with the teachings of Buddhism.

This text is essentially a hymn to Aśoka’s greatness and benevolence and is believed to have been composed by Buddhist monks in Mathura as early as the second century AD,23 which makes it substantially older than Dīpavaṃsa. In fact, when the latter was expanded into a more polished and literary _Mahāvaṃsa_ around the middle of the first millennium, the Aśokāvadāna had exerted a great deal of influence upon the finished work.24 Hence, its non-canonical status must not be seen as a dent in its significance. Also, while the oldest composition has been dated to the second century, that is more than three hundred years after Aśoka, its oral tradition has been placed much closer to the emperor in question around second century BC.25 In other words, this is certainly closer to primary than Dīpavaṃsa. So, what does it say about Aśoka and his conversion?

On that front it weaves a rather amusing tale of magic, and something called “Aśoka’s Hell.” As the name suggests, this was a torture chamber, most likely situated in Pataliputra, although the location isn’t explicitly given in the text. It was elaborately constructed to inflict maximum suffering on those who were imprisoned there and was filled with various instruments of torture. But before we get to that, let’s talk about how the work describes Aśoka in the lead-up to it. This was immediately upon his accession to the Mauryan throne after having killed his half-brother and fellow contender to the throne, Susīma.26 Thereafter, Aśoka ordered the execution of no fewer than five hundred ministers for having conspired against him.27 A number of concubines were burned alive as well.28 Consequently, the emperor earned the moniker of _Caṇḍāśoka_ or “the cruel Aśoka.”29

And now we can finally return to the “Hell” that he commissioned immediately upon taking the throne and executing the ministers. It was a beautiful building that gave no hint of the barbarity it housed. To man the place and carry out the executions, he hired one Girika, “the cruelest man in the kingdom.”30 Girika was to remain inside permanently. And no one else entering the place was to come out alive.

Unaware of this fatal caveat, legend has it that a Buddhist monk named Samudra stumbled upon the palatial structure and decided to explore it.31 Predictably, Girika informs him that he can no longer leave and that he would now be subjected to torture and death according to the chamber’s rules.32 Samudra pleads for a seven-day grace period, which is granted.33 During those seven days, Samudra observes other prisoners undergoing torture and attains Enlightenment or Arhatship. On the eighth day, it is Samudra’s turn. To everyone’s, including Girika’s, surprise, nothing seems to affect him. They boil him alive in a cauldron and yet he continues to meditate unscathed.34 Aśoka is promptly summoned to witness the miracle. Awed by what he sees, Aśoka decides to abandon all violence and convert to Buddhism.35

This legend of Aśoka’s conversion is today central to all Buddhist narratives from India to Tibet to China, with only slight variations in finer details.36 What’s important here is not the details of this “Hell,” but the complete absence of Kalinga from the entire discourse. We already noted how the Dīpavaṃsa does not see Kalinga or the infamous war. It’s the same with Aśokāvadāna as well, it seems. There are two key takeaways from this work— _**a)**_ The conversion happened immediately upon Aśoka’s accession, and _**b)**_ It was triggered by a monk’s miracles and not a battlefield carnage.

Of course, the legend of Samudra is just that, a legend. But the absence of Kalinga from two different narratives, both post-Aśokan, must be seen with meaning. A bloody war triggering remorse and subsequent conversion is too powerful a trope to be eschewed in works explicitly meant to promote the said faith. But before we conclude anything, there’s some more primary writing to contend with, this time a Minor Edict.

## More Rock Edicts

While the Buddhist sources skip Kalinga altogether, the edicts don’t. We have already examined the one at Kalsi where Aśoka seems to regret the war. There, the war was dated to his ninth regnal year with no direct mention of Buddhism or conversion. But even before the fourteen Major Edicts, came what we call the Minor Edicts. One such specimen is in a village in Karnataka called Maski. It’s also referred to as Minor Rock Edict I. This inscription is dated to Aśoka’s eleventh regnal year which makes it three years older than the Majors.

The Maski Edict tells us, right in its opening passage, that Aśoka became a lay Buddhist disciple “more than two and a half years” before.37 Which means sometime in the early days of the eighth regnal. Kalinga, just to recall, took place in the ninth regnal. The inscription also tells us that he became more intimately involved with the _Sangha_ a year earlier.38 If we take this one year of his involvement with the Sangha as a post-discipleship event, the total window expands to three and a half years, placing his conversion in the seventh regnal. Thus, even if we go by Aśoka’s own admission, which by the way still doesn’t jive with any of the Buddhist sources, he was already a Buddhist convert when he went to war with Kalinga.

Buddhism eschews violence, and therefore any conversion into it is automatically seen as renunciation of all kinds of bloodshed, be it political or ritual. Major Rock Edict I, however, challenges that view in the context of Aśoka. Also inscribed around the same time as the rest of the MREs, this relatively small piece of epigraphy announces a comprehensive ban on all ritualistic slaughter in the Mauryan capital, in line with the emperor’s ideals of nonviolence. But at the same time, it allows the slaughter of a deer and two peacocks in the imperial kitchen until such a time, that could be banned as well.39 In other words, there was a staggered approach to nonviolence. Instead of ending it outright, the mandate was of gradual reduction. A mighty pragmatic approach, no doubt, but this is four years _after_ Kalinga.

## The ‘Marxist’ Position

This school of Indian historiography endeavors to elevate Sramanic creeds, most notably Buddhism, in esteem, much to the detriment of the eternal villain that is Brahminism. Buddhism is seen as the emancipator of the oppressed, the cosmic antithesis to the ever-so-barbaric Brahminical patriarchy. In this scheme of things, the idea of a bloodthirsty conqueror reforming after a devastating war and his subsequent interface with Buddhism, should make for a powerful political and ideological statement. Which is probably why it’s usually the Left-leaning camp that typically espouses this position today. But contrary to all expectations, D. N. Jha, one of the high priests of Marxist Indian historiography, breaks this mold.

He rejects squarely all suggestions that Kalinga had any bearing whatsoever on Aśoka’s conversion.40 But he doesn’t stop at that. He further teases that while Aśoka’s association with Buddhism remains undeniable, there’s zero evidence supporting either his involvement in the Buddhist Council of 250 BC or him ever donning “the robes of a Buddhist monk.”41

Taking things even further, another renowned historian often tagged “Marxist influenced,”42 Romila Thapar, questions even the very idea of conversion.43 She examines the entire epigraphic corpus and reads meaning in a number of conspicuous omissions on Aśoka’s part. One such omission that we already noted was that of Buddha or his precepts in MRE XIII,44 but Thapar notes more. Such as that of the word _Nirvāna_ in several of his rock and pillar edicts where he speaks of _Svarga_ , a patently Brahminical concept, as the ultimate objective.45 For a practitioner of the Buddhist creed, that’s a rather peculiar usage. Buddha never preached a supernatural heaven. While Vedas preach the attainment of Svarga as the pinnacle of divine grace, Buddhism preaches Nirvāna or catharsis from the cycle of life as that pinnacle. Thapar also notes the translation of the word dhamma as Greek _eusebeia_ in certain edicts as evidence that the term was used not as a proper noun specific to a particular religion but as a common noun to mean “pious conduct” or “sacred duty.”46 She expressly discourages the reduction of Aśoka’s dhamma to the precepts of a single creed and elaborates that the general code of ethics embodied by the word was just as familiar to other creeds of the time, such as Jainism, as it was to Buddhism.47

In sum, we have two eminent names from the Marxist school of historiography that reject any linkage between Kalinga and Aśoka’s Buddhism. One of them calls into question even his Buddhism.

Verdict? You be the judge. The edict is contemporary, but it says nothing about conversion, just mentions the word dhamma a bunch of times. The Buddhist corpus is not contemporary, but it unambiguously confirms the conversion as unrelated to Kalinga. And then there is one Marxist historian who places the conversion before Kalinga and another who doubts the conversion itself. At the same time, it must also be kept in mind that Aśoka did not undertake any major campaign of conquest after Kalinga. At least none that we’re aware of so far. How one reads that remains a matter of subjective interpretation and, to a great extent, ideological bent.

The mainstream, meanwhile, continues to maintain the narrative we’ve been handed down through the ages—that the bloodshed at Kalinga triggered a change of heart, and of faith.
