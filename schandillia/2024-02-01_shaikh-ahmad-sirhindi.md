# Shaikh Ahmad Sirhindi

*The Underrated Forebear of Aurangzeb’s Fundamentalism*

Akbar took the throne in 1556. While, in keeping with the Timurid–Mughal tradition, his tenure had its fair share of violence and zealotry, something interesting happened toward the later decades of his life. Akbar is best known for his policy of communal syncretism codified as _Ṣulḥ-i-kul_ or the policy of universal peace. First came the abolition of _jizya_ , in his twelfth regnal year, then the _ibadat khana_ or “prayer hall” for multidiciplinary religious discourse about a decade later. The exercise culminated in the formal launch of the famous _Din-i Ilahi_ or “Religion of God” creed in his twenty-sixth regnal year. But none of this happened in a vacuum. While much can be said about his continued Islamist zealotry that ran parallel to his reforms, it’s a subject for another day. These reforms came with an objective—to make Akbar a _de facto_ caliph of Indian Islam, to help consolidate his hold on the vast, unwieldy subcontinent.

Akbar’s tenure encompassed a pivotal moment of great eschatological import in Islamic history, the first, and so far the only, Islamic millennium; AH 1000 roughly coincided with 1591 AD. And the emperor saw it as a divine omen, for it fell within his reign. Eager to make the most of it, he had embarked upon the journey toward his elevation to spiritual despotism decades in advance. The _Infallibility Decree_ of 1579 making him the supreme arbiter in all religious matters of Islam was the first major expression of this ambition. Of course, these measures did not go unnoticed and unopposed. Many Islamic jurists and theologians criticized his reforms as tyrannical and heretical. Among them was a young leader of the Naqshbandi Sufi order named Ahmad al-Faruqi al-Sirhindi, Ahmad Sirhindi for short.

Born in Punjab just four years before the abolition of jizya, Sirhindi grew up in the thick of Akbar’s reforms. He was only fifteen when the Infallibility Decree came out. To talk Sirhindi, it’s crucial to talk Sufism. The ideology is closely associated with mysticism and syncretism in popular narratives. Often glossing over its adequately prominent militant aspect. Sufism is as much about mysticism as it is about puritanism, and puritanism directly inspires religious triumphalism. A more detailed discussion of Sufi militancy is outside the jurisdiction of this article. Just as religions have sects, Sufism has orders or _tariqa_ , each floated by and named after a distinguished saint. Sirhindi was already trained in the orders of Chishti, Qadiri, and Suhrawardi before his interface with a fourth that would go on to define the rest of his life—Naqshbandi.

Babur being a renowned example, foreign Naqshbandi initiates had been in India for nearly a century then. But it was only toward the later years of Akbar’s reign that the order formally entered the subcontinent with an Uzbek _khwaja_ named Baqi Billah Berang. This was the dawn of the new Hijri millennium and a period of great doctrinal disruption in India. Not just Muslims turning away from the Qurʾanic hardline, but also Hindus experimenting with mysticism through the new-wave Bhakti Movement and a whole other creed splintering off in Punjab in response to the Islamic expansionism. With the emperor himself toying with polytheism and syncretism, Islamic thought leaders felt an immediate threat to their relevance and Naqshbandi came as a welcome solution.

Sirhindi joined the order in the year 1600 and quickly emerged as the most prominent figure at the seminary. When Berang passed away three years later, the mantel expectedly passed to Sirhindi, now entrusted with the responsibility to herd the flock back to its Islamic fundaments. He was up against the single most powerful man on the subcontinent, Akbar. The task wasn’t going to be easy by any means.

## The Crisis of Islam

A most notable aspect of Sirhindi’s thoughts is his unilateral assumption of India as an Islamic territory. In his eyes, Muslims belonged, the rest, the _kafirs_ , didn’t. He stated this in no uncertain terms in a letter to the Mughal noble Shaikh Farid Bukhari, better known as Murtaza Khan, where he refers to India as “the land of Islam.”1 In the letter, he resents the fact that before the Mughals, the kafirs, i.e. Hindus, had grown so strong that they’d rule the land of Islam with unislamic laws and execute any Muslim trying to enforce Islamic orders. But this was just an analogy to serve his bigger point—that even then, Islam was never under as much threat as it was now under Akbar.2 It warrants to be noted here, though, that amidst all his anti-kafir rhetoric, he was still not keen on expansionism. At least not in this missive. Because at one point he categorically states that “the Muslims should follow their creed, and the kafirs should follow theirs.” Akbar believed in synthesis, Sirhindi in segregated coexistence.

Despite this resentment, there was never an open direct conflict between him and Akbar, for the latter only grew liberal with age and was often indifferent to these machinations not just in his capital but even in his court. Along with Sirhindi, a whole cartel of orthodoxy had come under the immediate influence of Naqshbandi precepts. We have already met one of them, Murtaza Khan. Lahore’s governor Qilich Khan was another such example. Even someone as liberal as the Hindustani poet and Tulsi Das’ patron Abdul Rahim Khan-i-Khanan couldn’t escape its appeal.

With Sirhindi, it was a double-whammy of orthodoxy because, before his Naqshbandi encounter, he had spent some time under a Kashmiri Sufi who introduced him to the philosophies of Mir Sayyid Ali Hamadani, a Sufi of the Kubrawiya order.3 Hamdani, to the unaware, is the face of Kashmir’s large-scale Islamization and the primary influence on Sikandar Shah Miri, the tyrant notorious as Butshikan or “the Iconoclast.”

To carry out his mission of bringing India “back to its Islamic roots,” Sirhindi needed his messages to carry. To that end, he adopted for dissemination, a medium of religious propaganda long perfected by the likes of Sharfuddin Yahya Maneri before him—letters. The one he wrote to Murtaza Khan was part of this exercise. He would write these letters to a smaller set of individuals at first, then these would be compiled as what scholars call _maktubat_ for wider circulation later. Think epistles of early Christianity.

Akbar died only two years after Berang, so Sirhindi did not get to criticize him much while he lived. But more than made up for it after his passing wielding much influence—indirectly, of course—in the ascension of Jahangir. Murtaza Khan we just met personally pulled strings in Jahangir’s favor at the council of nobles convened to discuss succession. And this he did only after securing from the prince the commitment to bring a swift end to the doctrinal decadance of his late father.4

From this point on, Sirhindi rarely held back. His missives grew more intolerant, more poisonous with each subsequent writing. In one addressed to Lahore’s viceroy and a devout Naqshbandi, Lala Beg, he flat-out criticized Akbar’s ban on cow slaughter as an interference in the religious freedom of Muslims5 calling the practice of _zabiha_ (slaughter) “one of the greatest rituals of Islam in India.”6

##  A Sufi’s Ego

A major impediment to Sirhindi’s likability, at least at first, was his inflated sense of self-worth. Sufism, across orders, involves a lot of extreme meditation and other esoteric practices, often venturing into the realm of crazy. Therefore, mystical experiences are not uncommon at least among the khwajas and the seniors. Sirhindi had his experiences too, which unfortunately manifested as hubris. Known for its unremitting auterities in both lifestyle as well as thoughts, Sufism takes great exception to such expressions of pride.

This goes all the way back to his early days when Berang was still alive. If hubris weren’t enough, there was an element of hypocrisy as well. So Akbar’s, and other Mughal rulers’, practice of using long chain of imperial titulature and epithets is common knowledge. Akbar, for instance, had himself addressed as nothing short of _Shahenshah as-Sultan al-’A‘azam wa-’l-Khaqan al-Mukarram, Maliku ’s-Sultanat, ‘ala’ Hadrat ’Abu ’l-Muzaffar Shahabu-’ddin Muhammad Akbar, Shahenshahe-Sultanatu ’l-Hindiyyah wa-’l-Mughaliyyah_. Sirhindi, in true Sufi spirit, objected to this. More than that, he objected to Akbar’s attempts at granting himself a caliphal stature. At the same time, however, he also didn’t mind making claims of his own mystical experiences and spiritual elevation to the level of none other than Abu Bakr, Islam’s first Caliph and successor to Muhammad.7 This was an overreach even by Naqshbandi standards which saw Abu Bakr as the source of all Islamic wisdom and the order’s spiritual ancestor.

These claims expectedly earned him much admonition from Berang and even threats from mainstream Islam. The _ulema_ at one point demanded his execution for blasphemy.8 Whether he truly meant to disrespect the Caliphs is immaterial, for the impression surely helped land him in jail as we’ll learn toward the later part of this story. None of this impacted his growth as a force against the degredation of Islamic values in the Mughal realm. Nor did his ego wane with this growth. Akbar tolerated this just as he did much else in his final years. But Jahangir wasn’t to be this charitable.

## Shariʿa by the Sword

Contrary to the popular Sufi image of political indifference and confinement to personal salvation, many preachers took an active part in not only the State’s political affairs but also military. Sirhindi was one such example. Obsessed with restoring Muhammad’s glory in the subcontinent, he pushed for statewide enforcement of Shariʿa with imperial fiats under his banner of _wahdat al-shuhud_. This, in fact, was his very deal with Jahangir at the time of his ascension. In a letter to Akbar’s foster son Mirza Aziz Koka, better known by the epithet Khan-i-Azam, he refers to the fifteenth-century Naqshbandi Sufi Khwaja Ahrar as an example of the ideological transactions between Sufism and statecraft. He relates how Ahrar cultivated close ties with the royalty and nobility to better carry out the propagation of Shariʿa9 and writes “Shariʿa is under the sword”10 as a metaphor for Islam’s dependence on royal, violent if necessary, patronage for survival.

In a sharp departure from Akbar’s syncretic ideals, Sirhindi endeavored to maintain segregation of the highest order. Not only theological but also cultural isolationism in the service of Shariʿa underscored his early interactions with politics. Things would drastically change over time. Such was his intolerance to unislamic practices that at one point he refused to enter Delhi because the city was celebrating Nowruz, according to him a thoroughly pagan festival.11

## The Rawafid and the Kafir

Over time, however, Sirhindi’s intolerance grew from isolationist to triumphalist. His letters became increasingly vitriolic against, not just kafirs but also non-Sunni Muslims, a case in point being a letter to Mirza Fathullah Hakim in which he unambiguously denounces Shiʿa and Khwarij as deviants and unworthy of Allah’s grace. His line of reasoning here is that one cannot obey God without obeying Muhammad and one cannot obey Muhammad without obeying his companions or the first Caliphs, namely Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, and Ali. And since cursing the Rashidun (minus Ali) is not an uncommon practice among many Shiʿa, they automatically became unislamic in Sirhindi’s books. While cursing the Companions may or may not necessarily be a Shiʿa leitmotif, Sirhindi certainly saw it as such.

The same vitriol against the Shiʿa also featured in his most notorious anti-Shiʿa polemic titled _Radd-i-Rawafid_ released in the form of a pamphlet.12 It would be translated by none other than Shah Waliullah Dehlawi about a centuty down the line.13 In this pamphlet he reiterates the same diatribe against those who do not follow and revere the three Companions, albeit in a much stronger language. In fact, the word _rawafid_ itself is a derogatory term for Shiʿa and translates into “those who reject.” Yet another piece that reflects the exact same sentiment is a letter he wrote to Abdul Rahim Khan-i-Khanan.

Unfortunately for the Shaikh, however, many higher administrative positions had traditionally been filled by Persian nobles, all of whom professed the Shiʿa theology. These diatribes, therefore, made Sirhindi mighty unpopular at the court. As we’ve already noted, by the time of Jahangir’s coronation, the Sufi was already neck deep in palace intrigues and political machinations. Such was his influence that when, in 1610, Qazi Nurullah Shustari was executed by flogging for heresy on Jahangir’s orders, it was said to be at the behest of none other than Sirhindi.14 Shustari had been a favorite of Akbar who served as a very senior Mughal emissary in Kashmir. While this brought the Shaikh considerable power, it also earned him many enemies. His only protection was his influence over men like Khan-i-Khanan to whom he even wrote seeking aid against the growing Shiʿa clout in the Mughal court. A veritable crusade had been opened against the Shiʿa and Sirhindi was only getting started. One must note that bordering the Mughal realm to the West was a very Shiʿa Safavid territory. This reality would bear consequences for the Sufi, but more on that a little later.

If Sirhindi’s stance against the Shiʿa was that of contempt, his stance against non-Muslims was even worse. As we briefly discussed earlier, it started with segregationism and over time burgeoned into full-blown ethnic cleansing. Even the earlier, segregationist phase reeked with derogation bordering on violence. For Shiʿa, the worst he had was that they were deviants for not revering Muhammad’s companions. But for Hindus, he had a whole array of pejoratives. While in one letter to Khan-i-Khanan he openly names the Hindus as enemies, his real vitriol pours out in another letter to Shaikh Farid Bukhari or Murtaza Khan where he asks righteous Muslims to shun the company of Hindus the way they should shun the company of dogs:1516

     “One who respects the kafirs, dishonors the Muslims. Respecting them does not merely mean honoring them, but giving them a place in one’s company and talking to them. Like dogs they should be kept away and if there is any worldly business which cannot be attained without them, then without taking them into confidence, only minimum contact should be established.”

Contrary to the popular notion of Sufis being tolerant, syncretic, mystiques with nothing but love and compassion in their hearts, Sirhindi’s writings present to us a man of immense political will and a most violent kind of contempt for a community that made up the overwhelming bulk of the society he inhabited. While at one point he called cow slaughter an integral Islamic ritual, at another he went further to prescribe the practice simply as an affront to the Hindus.17

Furthering his anti-Hindu agenda, he actively campaigned for the elimination of non-Muslims from all positions of administration. Those he saw as instantly incompatible with India, which he unabashedly referred to as _Dar al-Islam_ or Islamic State.18 Not stopping there, he even prescribed their subjection to disgrace and humiliation.19 No quarter must be given. In this regard, the old Shaikh aged like gangarene, growing only deadlier with each passing year. The climax ultimately came when he admitted to wishing nothing short of an ethnic cleansing of the kafirs in yet another letter where he said “every open and hidden effort should be made for their destruction.”20 Note with meaning his use of the expression “and hidden.” In the same letter, he even names destruction of kafirs or “enemies of the Prophet” his “uppermost desire” as a _faqir_.21

## An Ideology of Derision

While Sirhindi was clear on the place of non-Muslims in his society, he was also clear on their treatment. They must be relegated to the bottom of the social pyramid, and while there, they must compulsively be treated with constant humiliation and degradation. One major tool to accomplish that was something Akbar had abolished when toxic seer was still a toddler.

For the longest time, jizya has been presented as a fair compensation due to non-Muslims who aren’t obligated to fight in wars or offer _zakat_. Often interpreted as a medieval form of “protection money.” Seen that way, it doesn’t sound all that oppressive assuming the rates do not differ significantly. On the contrary, it only seems fair that one community not be singled out in the name of religion. But Sirhindi saw things differently. To him, jizya was not a mere tool of taxation, but a useful instrument of subjugation. In his views, as elaborated in his letter to the good old Murtaza Khan, its primary function was to humiliate the non-Muslim by deliberately impoverishing them to the extent that they aren’t able to match the lifestyle of their Muslim counterparts.22 He posited jizya as a divine institution expressly meant to elevate the status of Islam above the kafirs.23 Goes without saying, he was thoroughly against Akbar’s refusal to reinstate the policy and remained so his whole life because it wouldn’t happen until Aurangzeb.

The Sufi consistently held Hindus and Shiʿa as the enemies of Islam and just as consistently wished for Islam’s _victory_ over those enemies. In his many letters, he in no uncertain terms expressed happiness over the “dishonoring of the enemies of the faith.” In 1606, when the fifth Sikh guru Arjan Dev died (executed by some accounts) in Mughal custody in Lahore, Sirhindi celebrated it with a lot of glee as he wrote to Murtaza Khan:2425

    “These days the accursed infidel of Gobindwal was very fortunately killed. It is a cause of great defeat for the reprobate Hindus. With whatever intention and purpose they are killed—the humiliation of infidels is for Muslims, life itself. Some fourteen years later, when on Jahangir’s orders, a Hindu commander named Bikramajit successfully concluded the siege of Kangra, the Sufi, who was in prison at the time, congratulated the emperor for not just conquering the fort, but also humiliating the Hindus therein and desecrating their idols. Such was his intolerance to non-Muslims that when it came to giving Bikramajit his blessings before the campaign, he only did so with a great deal of reluctance even though the campaign was for a Muslim empire.26 We’ll get to the prison bit a little later. The following year, Jahangir, in a celebration of victory, visited Kangra and ordered the slaughter of a cow27 and construction of a mosque inside the fortress.

## Prison and Compromise

Sirhindi’s political influence only grew stronger under Jahangir, especially given the emperor was already under a moral debt to the Sufi for the throne. Such was this influence that at one point, he was appointing his own _Chief Khalifas_ in the Mughal army. The ulema, which had earlier been scandalized by the Sufi’s placement of himself on the same spiritual level as the Companions, now gradually started to embrace the reforms. Their bigger alarm had been Akbar’s flirtations with autocracy and departure from the Islamic core and were keen on putting in place a system that would prevent a recurrence of such despotism. In Sirhindi, they saw a welcome solution. Placing religion above statecraft could help keep the emperor in check while also eliminating any threat from the Hindus, Sikhs, Shiʿa, and other infidels.

Through his letters to highly placed men in the Mughal administration, Sirhindi effected countless appointments. Almost every such appointment replaced a Hindu employee with a Muslim one, someone from among Sirhindi’s followers. Where no position existed, he influenced the creation of one, a prime example being that of the Chief Khalifa in the army. In fact, this was probably taking things too far. An external influence of this scale, on the armed forces no less, should and must alarm any monarch hoping for a decent shelf-life. So Jahangir made a move.

Barely months before the Kangra campaign, Sirhindi was framed on charges of diminishing Abu Bakr.28 This is one accusation the controversial Sufi has endured since his earliest days. Do recall the reprimand from his master, Berang, when he lived. The charge this time, however, wasn’t valid as is evident from several of his letters in response to various followers asking him about his views on the Caliph. It was a mere pretext. While curtailing his influence was already a strong enough reason for Jahangir, the Naqshbandi chronicles also speak of Sirhindi’s refusal to bow to the emperor as a potential trigger when the blasphemy charge weakened.29 The custom, known as _sijdah_ , was an iconic Mughal tradition where anyone appearing before the emperor was required to bow repeatedly facing the emperor, typically three times, before any interaction. Refusal to bow was easily a serious case of insubordination and disrespect of the crown. Having said that, Jahangir does not mention this as a reason in Tuzuk-i-Jahangiri. Besides, he’d also abolished the custom for members of all ulemas. It’s hard to say if this consession extended to Sirhindi given he was no member of an ulema. Be that as it may, the Sufi was on trial, which concluded in conviction. He was sent to Gwalior where he’d spend two years in prison.

The Sufi’s release is an interesting tale of ego satisfaction where two men make compromises out of compulsion and yet claim moral victory to their respective audiences. While Jahangir records that Sirhindi was released after the latter’s apology and promise to stay away from State affairs, Naqshbandi chronicles record that it’s the emperor who apologized and agreed to implement all of Sirhindi’s reforms:

  1. Abolition of sijdah,

  2. Reinstatement of jizya,

  3. Legalization of cow slaughter,

  4. Ban on heresy and apostasy, and

  5. Strict censorship and enforcement of Shariʿa




Of course, no contemporary records establish the implementation of any of these. As we’ve already noted, jizya wouldn’t return until Aurangzeb more than half a century later. So what could possibly have led to the Sufi’s release anyway? The answer lies in a woman—Nur Jahan.

Earlier married to a Turkoman named Sher Afgan Khan, Nur Jahan went on to become Jahangir’s twentieth and final legally married wife. Jahangir’s son Khurram, who’d later succeed him as emperor Shah Jahan, was almost twenty when the couple got married. This was no easy marriage. Both Noor Jahan and Sfghan Khan were originally from Persia as Mehr-un-Nissa and Ali Quli Istajlu respectively. Both had entered India during Akbar’s reign with Istajlu taking up a new job, and a new name, in the Mughal army and growing up the ranks from there. As Persians, naturally, both were Shiʿa. See where this is going?

When Khan died and Jahangir saw a potential wife in the widow, religion became a major barrier. Besides the ulema, a prime source of opposition was the governor of Malwa, Mahabat Khan. He had reason to protest. His own daughter was married to a Naqshbandi initiate and the Naqshbandis, as we’ve already seen, were in a non-negotiable opposition to all things Shiʿa. This was one powerful opposition and a potential coup if not handled tactfully. Another source of opposition was none other than Prince Khurram himself. The only ace up Jahangir’s sleeve in this deadlock was the Sufi locked away in Gwalior. It’s easy to picture a tradeoff. _You sanction my marriage, and I restore you to your old glory_.

The first thing Sirhindi did upon release was write a letter thanking the emperor for his grace. In this letter, he reinforces his favorite adage, “religious law thrives under the sword.”30

## A Troubled Legacy

While it’s hard to quantify or even qualify the exact nature of direct influence Shaikh Ahmad Sirhindi wielded at the Mughal court, he undoubtedly shaped the Indian Muslim psyche of his time and times to come. Despite the Islamic character of the Mughal dynasty, Indian Islam was on the brink of disintigration in the face of myriad theological innovations from the Sikh Movement to the Bhakti Movement to the Muslim community’s own march into eclecticism, thanks to the likes of Kabir and Rahim. Sirhindi came as a force of Providence who deployed every resource at his disposal, his very being, into plugging of this potential disintegration. If Hindu–Muslim relations stand out as the worst example of inter-communal interface in India, Sirhindi gets a major blame as its progenitor.

Sirhindi’s fundamentalism has continued to inform the Muslim thought in the subcontinent right to this day. Even intellectuals otherwise seen as liberal and unorthodox to a degree like Iqbal, Sir Sayyid Ahmad Khan, and even Abul Kalam Azad, couldn’t escape the militant Sufi’s influence. Azad, in fact, even went to the extent of placing himself as an ideological successor31 and wrote a whole book, Tazkira, aggrandizing the radical in direct conflict with his otherwise relatively tolerant persona.32

Sirhindi died at the age of sixty in 1632, leaving behind a whole body of bitter Muslims who would never see Hindus the same way again. His work would be taken to its logical conclusion only about a century later when a brand new Naqshbandi with views just as radical would develop his tenets further and unknowingly usher in the age of militant Islam.
