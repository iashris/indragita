# The Brahmin vs. the Sramana

As the Aryans gentrified the east, i.e. the Lower Gangetic Plains, and found iron, a rapid phase of infrastructural upgrade followed, heralding what we now term the Second Urbanization. Until that point, the most influential polity on the subcontinent was the Kuru-Panchal kingdom based roughly in modern-day Haryana. With iron, the power center shifted east, and Magadha rose to prominence. With this rapid political change also came a radical doctrinal evolution.

The Aryans of the west were steeped in the Vedic code of physical sacrifice. But those in the east increasingly developed a distaste for violence and the strict regimentation of the Vedic creed which brooked no error in rituals. Instead, they gravitated to a more “inner peace” motif where self-renunciation trumped external sacrifice. While extreme ritual purity epitomized the western doctrine, the eastern doctrine championed extreme austerity and penance. The former is known as Brahminism (_brāhmaṇa_ in Sanskrit and Pali), and the latter as the Sramana tradition (_śramaṇa_ in Sanskrit, _samaṇa_ in Pali). It’s the latter that would ultimately evolve into non-Vedic creeds such as Buddhism, Jainism, Ajivikas, and many others. Of these, only Buddhism and Jainism have endured. Even Brahminism absorbed elements of this doctrinal shift, integrating concepts like _karma_ and rebirth into its first post-Vedic scriptural corpus, the Upanishads.

In sum, the conflict between śramaṇa and brāhmaṇa is not exactly a conflict between Aryans and non-Aryans, but between two Aryan bodies. It’s the classic west vs. east story in its most primitive iteration. Now the question is, was there truly a conflict in the sense we understand the term? Of course, this was no crusade where one community sought to eradicate the other, but was it even spiritually well-entrenched? Was there no common ground between the two schools of thought? These are the questions we’ll attempt to answer here. Barring a brief conversation on Jainism, we’ll confine ourselves to Buddhism as the representative of the Sramana thought in the context of this discussion. Today, Buddhism and Hinduism are at irreconcilable odds both doctrinally and politically, especially politically. Was it the same 2,500 years ago? What did the two think of each other? A relevant question that deserves a whole article unto itself but will still be touched upon here is, what was Buddhism’s relationship with caste?

Sometime in the fifth century BC, although dating him remains an exercise in educated guesswork at best, Panini became the first to corral the Sanskrit language into a formal grammar and syntax in his seminal _Aṣṭādhyāyī_. But Aṣṭādhyāyī was a difficult read and remained out of the lay Brahmin’s faculties for a long time. In the third century, a Vedic priest named Katyayana offered a solution with _Vārttikakāra_ , an explanatory commentary and elaboration on Aṣṭādhyāyī. Another hundred years and Patanjali from Magadha further expanded Katyayana’s explanations with _Vyākaraṇa-Mahābhāṣya_. Sanskrit grammar had come one full circle, from codification in a far western Gandhara to being explication in a far eastern Magadha. This was the journey of not just a language but also of its champions and speakers, the Brahmins. The Vedic Aryans were already in Magadha long before Panini, but a significant ideological and social friction persisted between the Brahmins and the Sramanas. So much so that when Patanjali elaborated on one of Katyanaya’s _varttikas_ , he listed _Śrāmaṇabrāhmaṇam_ as an example of a _dvanda_ compound—a kind of collective noun made up of two distinct singular nouns, if you will—that stands for eternal hostility:1

     _Yeṣāṁ ca virodha ityasyāvakāśaḥ.
    Śramaṇabrāhmaṇam._
    “For those whose opposition is thus apparent.
    The ascetic and the Brahmin.” (rough trans.)

While this is an interesting, even if not wholly intended, commentary on the relationship between the two broad communities in Patanjali’s Magadha, the animosity was not just limited to brāhmaṇa vs. śramaṇa, but even expanded to eastern Brahmins vs. western. By the Upanishadic period, a significant divergence had emerged even within the Vedic community. While the east came to be influenced by a strong śramaṇic substrate, the west continued to hold its fort as the more “pristine” affiliate of the Vedic creed. We see this reflected best in the famous Brihadaranyaka-Chandogya rivalry.2 The former emerged in the Videha region in the east and the latter in the west, likely the Kuru-Panchala region.3 Both upheld their respective philosophy as the more authoritative.

At any rate, Patanjali’s example already establishes that the Brahmins and the Sramanas did not like each other very much. The Upanishadic rivalry also establishes that even Brahmins didn’t like very much fellow Brahmins whose ideology was colored by Sramanic influences. But was it always the case? It sure is today, especially between Brahmins and Buddhists. But was the friction just as strong back in the day? How much of it is really attributable to caste? While it’s hard to answer most of these questions given the scarcity of textual evidence from the time, we will try to glean whatever we can from whatever we have at our disposal.

## Brahmin in Buddhist Scripture

Part of the pre-sectarian or Theravada scriptural corpus is the Pali canon called _Saṃyutta Nikāya_. Of its fifty-six discourses or _saṃyuttas_ , one titled _Iddhipādasaṃyuttaṃ_ places a great deal of importance on what it calls the “four bases of power.” These are _chanda_ (desire), _viriya_ (will), _citta_ (thoughts), and _vīmaṃsā_ (investigation). Mastery of all four is posited as the key to a wide variety of physical and mental superpowers. And who are the people who achieve that? The _Samaṇabrāhmaṇa_ , of course.4 The expression _samaṇā vā brāhmaṇā vā_ appears generously throughout the discourse with no fewer than half a dozen instances.5 This pairing of samaṇa and brāhmaṇa as exemplars of doctrinal preeminence makes for a reasonable indication of the two being placed on equal footing in the Buddhist worldview. Both are seen as spiritually elevated beings in possession of faculties not accessible to the layman. For all its rejection of the Vedic pantheon, the text even speaks of unmistakably Vedic ideas like _Brahma·lokāpi_ or “Brahma’s world.”6 In yet another discourse, this time on _indriya_ or senses, the Buddha elaborates on the five human faculties and expects the Sramana and the Brahmana alike to be in control of all five.7 Again, this places the two on equal footing at least so far as spiritual eminence goes.

Another great window to the Buddha’s own take on the Brahmins is the good old Dhammapada. Part of the same collection that gives us the Saṃyutta Nikāya, the _Sutta Piṭaka_ , Dhammapada is a collection of direct quotes from the Buddha himself and is therefore the most widely read and recognized member of the Pali canon. In these aphorisms, Buddha qualifies Sramana and related identities as _bhikkhu_ (mendicant) and _muni_ (sage). The running theme is that of privileging action or karma over appearance. According to Buddha, it’s the transcendence of good and evil that qualifies one as any of these rather than shaven heads (Sramana),8 literal begging (bhikkhu),9 or reticence (muni).10 While this does not expressly qualify the Brahmin, the implication is evident. The tradition of mendicancy is far from exclusive to Buddhism and enjoys a strong presence in the praxis of Hindu orthodoxy too. Even Vishnu would play the mendicant in one of his ten avatars, albeit that’s a much later, Puranic development. To this day, part of the _upanayana_ rituals is the subject seeking and receiving alms from his mother before his symbolic entry into _brahmacharya_. In fact, brahmacharya itself is an Upanishadic concept and might as well be a Sramanic influence.

A more direct take on Brahmins appears in the final chapter which is entirely dedicated to the topic and is tellingly titled _Brāhmaṇa Vagga_.11 The chapter consists of forty verses, each serving to qualify the Brahmin in Buddha’s views—from he who meditates12 to he who has given up all evils,13 to he who holds no attachment.14 A commonly used term in Buddhist writings is _arahat_ or _arahant_. It refers to someone who has shaken free from the cycle of birth and attained _nirvana_. In that sense, Buddha himself is an arahat. In a sense, the relationship between the arahat and the brāhmaṇa in Buddha’s sermons should be a pretty good reflection of the relationship between the Vedic orthodoxy and Sramanic heterodoxy at the time. In Brāhmaṇa Vagga, Buddha expressly forbids the two from hurting or disrespecting each other:15

     _Na brāhmaṇassa pahareyya
    n’âssa muñcetha brāhmaṇo
    Dhī brāhmaṇassa hantāraṁ
    tato dhī yassa muñcati.

    “One should not strike a Brahmin, nor should a Brahmin vent on one who has struck him. Shame on him who strikes a Brahmin. More shame on him who gives vent.” (trans. Thera)

But if a Brahmana is one with no attachment,16 no likes and dislikes,17 no desires,18 no cravings,19 no sins,20 and no worldly bonds,21 isn’t he “enlightened” already? How is he any different from the arahat? Turns out, Buddha thought so too. Which is why in verse 420 of Dhammapada, he submits that he makes no distinction between the two:

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _Yassa gatiṁ na jānanti
    devā gandhabbamānusā
    Khīṇāsavaṁ arahantaṁ
    tam ahaṁ brūmi brāhmaṇaṁ._
    “He whose destiny is unknown to gods, _gandhabbas_ (gandharvas), and men; who has destroyed all corruptions; and is far removed from passion, the arahant—Him I call a Brahmin.” (trans. Thera)

This is the closest the two appellations have gotten in the Buddhist thought, both referring to an enlightened, elevated individual whose spiritual station is a function of austerity and penance, rather than lineage. The attribution is further affirmed in another Sutta Piṭaka text called _Sutta Nipāta_. Of a similar vintage to Dhammapada and coming from the same collection, the _Khuddaka Nikāya_ , verses from this work find mention even in some Asokan inscriptions.

Several verses from this work reiterate Buddha’s position that caste labels are functions of deeds and not birth alone. One such verse qualifies not only Brahmins but also the outcast thus:22

     _Na jaccā ‘vasalo’ hoti, na jaccā hoti brāhmaṇo.
    Kammanā ‘vasalo’ hoti, Kammanā hoti brāhmaṇo ti._
    “No birth a wastrel—or a Brahmin—makes,
    it is conduct that wastrels makes, and Brahmins too.” (trans. Chalmers)

The entire _sutta_ or discourse is, in fact, titled Vasala Sutta and is dedicated to defining the so-called “outcast.” There’s another sutta in the same work that similarly speaks of Brahmins, the _Vāseṭṭha Sutta_.23 The verses here reiterate everything Buddha says about the “true Brahmin” in Dhammapada. Most notably, one of them acknowledges the existence of birth-based caste assignment and calls it an antiquated practice:

     _Samaññā h’ esā lokasmiṁ nāmagotthaṁ pakappitaṁ,
    sammuccā samudāgataṁ tattha tattha pakappitaṁ,
    dīgharattam anusayitaṁ diṭṭhigatam ajānataṁ;
    ajānatā no pabrūvanti: ‘Jātiyā hoti brāhmaṇo.’_
    “By general consent men’s names and clans
    are useful designations current passed;
    it was long ere time evolved the novel view,
    till then unknown, that birth a ‘Brahmin’ makes.” (trans. Chalmers)

The key takeaway here is that Buddhism has, since its earliest days, acknowledged the Brahmins and held them in high esteem. The only departure from the prevailing state of affairs was that it shifted the spotlight from lineage to deeds. Therefore, Brahmins who practiced austerities enjoyed as much privilege in Buddhism as they did in the Vedic orthodoxy.

At this point, it’s important to touch upon a common misunderstanding stemming from a selective reading of a section of the _Aṅguttara Nikāya_ , another member of the Sutta Piṭaka collection. This work quotes Buddha himself as saying, not once but a good five times, that “a Brahmin is well born of both his maternal and paternal sides, of pure descent, unassailable and impeccable with respect to birth as far back as the seventh paternal generation.”24 Many read this as Buddha’s unreserved endorsement for birth-based Brahminhood, but they couldn’t be further from the truth. A more complete reading reveals that this is just Buddha reiterating to a Brahmin what the latter said about himself. Buddha repeats the Brahmin’s own introduction five times with reference to Brahmā, _deva_ , bounded, unbounded, and _cāṇḍāla_ , and asks him which of the five categories of Brahmins he belonged to.25 Although the Buddha does state that these designations were created by the ancient composers of Vedic hymns,26 he offers no express endorsement whatsoever for such taxonomy.

To condense it all, neither Buddha nor early Buddhists harbored any animosity against the Brahmins. If anything, there was a certain amount of esteem for the Brahmins as well as the Vedic _varṇa_ classification. However, whereas the prevailing orthodoxy associated Brahminhood to descent and rituals, Buddhist thought associated it with personal thoughts and actions. To Buddha, a true Brahmin was also a true Sramana, and vice versa. Having learned the Buddhist view of Brahmins, let’s now turn our attention to another Sramanic creed, Jainism.

## Brahmin in Jain Scripture

The difference between śramaṇa and brāhmaṇa wasn’t just a matter of heterodoxy vs. orthodoxy, but also that of language. While the orthodoxy preferred Sanskrit, the language of all Vedas and Upanishads, the heterodoxy preferred one of several vernaculars collectively called Prakrit. Pali, the language chosen by the Buddhists, was one of them. The Jain corpus, on the other hand, preferred Ardhamagadhi and Maharashtri Prakrit with some works also in Sanskrit. For the purpose of this discussion, the only difference between the two bodies of work is in their respective terms for Brahmin. In Pali and Sanskrit, it was brāhmaṇa whereas in the Prakrits of the Jain corpus, it was _māhaṇa_.

The Jain canon is collectively called the _āgamas_ , the oldest members of which were composed in Ardhamagadhi before later compositions shifted to Maharashtri Prakrit. Among the first such works is the _Ācārāṅga Sūtra_ which is where we begin our investigation. Question is, does it mention Brahmins, and if so, positively or negatively? Among the first mentions is where Mahavira himself is addressing a māhaṇa as the “wise one.”27 Right off the bat, this is a definitive attestation to the esteem Brahmins enjoyed in the Jain worldview despite doctrinal differences. Having said that, some philosophical contradictions between the Sramana and the Brahmin have also been flagged in this work, even though the very fact that the verse mentions sāmaṇa and māhaṇa together—as _samaṇā ya māhaṇā_ —indicates the equal footing the two identities enjoyed in Jainism too.28 Just as they did in Buddhism. The ultimate attestation, however, comes from the fact that on several occasions, the text identifies Mahavira himself as a Brahmin.29303132 These verses even name Kashyap as his _gotra_ or clan. While a departure from the Kshatriya origins posited for the Buddha in the Pali canon, both seem comfortable with identifying their respective originators by their birth-assigned caste identities.

The pairing together of sāmaṇa and māhaṇa is not a feature exclusive to Ācārāṅga but rather a theme that run across the entire āgamic corpus. Another notable example is the _Vyākhyāprajñapti_ , better known as the _Bhagavati Sūtra_. Here too, the two are paired in the context of spiritual elevation. Commenting on the work, J C Sikdar too concurs that this frequent pairing of the two is indicative of their equal status and identical social standing.33

Outside the āgamas, another corpus that forms an equally crucial part of the Jain monastic curriculum is the _Mūlasūtras_. Of the four works that make up this corpus is the _Uttarādhyayana Sūtra_. And just like the āgamas, it has an entire chapter, the twenty-fifth, dedicated to defining the “true Brahmin” and de-hyphenating him from his lineage and rituals. Most of the verses here end in the expression _taṁ vayaṁ brūma māhaṇam_ , or “him we call a true Brahmin.” In that sense, it’s a clear departure from the established Brahminical praxis that emphasized bloodline and fidelity to rituals as identity-defining elements. But it does something else too. The discourse is mainly about sacrifice and its role in a Brahmin’s spiritual elevation. Interestingly, to illustrate the point, it names the Vedic concept of Agnihotra as the “most essential subject in the Vedas.”34 The same verse also names the _dharma_ of Kashyapa, a Brahmin saint and the patriarch of one of the Brahminical gotras, as the best of dharmas.35 Thus, while the discourse does a great job of separating Brahminhood from overt rituals and lineage, it also invokes a Vedic element and a Brahmin gotra to make those points which might seem contradictory to some. Upon closer look, there seem little conflict between it and the Vedas besides its reading of sacrifice as personal austerities rather than ritual violence. The monk delivering the sermon is even praised toward the end of the discourse as the knower of the Vedas36 clearly acknowledging the preeminence of a most Brahminical scripture. In other words, while the Uttarādhyayana Sūtra, like other works in its genre, denies preeminence to the Brahmins who merely boast of Vedic scholarship, sacerdotal diligence, and lineage, it does not reject or make light of those attributes either.

Things however changed over the centuries, and it would be incorrect to dismiss episodes of clear social and doctrinal conflict. A strong testimony of this change comes from a later body of exegetical work called _Cūrṇis_. While not themselves part of the canon, they expound and comment on the āgamas and are held as valuable scriptural accessories. Composed partly in Prakrit and partly in Sanskrit, as many as twelve were produced in the first half of the first millennium. One of them is the _Niśītha Cūrṇi_. Given that the Cūrṇis post-date Mahavira by over half a millennium, they duly capture the evolution of Jain-Brahmin relationship over this period. In Niśītha Cūrṇi, there’s a palpable animosity between the two communities as reflected in the Jain practice of calling Brahmins _dhijjāti_ or “of condemned caste.”37 At times, a māhaṇa and a samaṇa could not even be seen physically together in public.38 While this speaks to the social friction between the two communities, the doctrine still held Brahmins and Sramanas on equal footing. The same Niśītha Cūrṇi that speaks of Jains and Brahmins not getting along well in the society, also sees Brahmins as divine being, literal gods on Earth or _bhudeva-pratyakṣadeva_ , sent by Prajāpati himself.39 Interestingly, the Niśītha Cūrṇi also recognizes _Brahmabandhu_ , a concept of birth-based Brahminhood.40 This is in sharp departure from the āgamic position and reflective of the changing attitude. The assignment of highest spiritual and social position to the Brahmins, however, remains consistent with the āgamic identification of Mahavira himself as a māhaṇa or Brahmin.

## Sramana in Vedic Texts

Since we’ve already established the position of Brahmins in Sramana thoughts, it’s only fair to also explore the position of Sramanas in Brahmin or Vedic thought. Let’s start with the very beginning, i.e. the Rigveda. Here, we find a hymn—10.136—dedicated to a class of ascetics called _Keśin_ or “the long-haired.”41 It identifies the Keśin as offsprings of _Vātaraśana_ and describes them as flying through the skies and bravely consuming poison alongside _Rudra_.42 More than a thousand years later, this attribution finds a second mention in a Puranic work called _Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam_ or _Bhāgavata Purāṇa_ :43

     _Barhiṣi tasminn eva viṣṇudatta bhagavān paramarṣibhiḥ prasādito nābheḥ priya-cikīrṣayā tad-avarodhāyane merudevyāṁ dharmān darśayitu-kāmo vāta-raśanānāṁ śramaṇānām ṛṣīṇām ūrdhva-manthināṁ śuklayā tanuvāvatatāra._
    “In the sacred sacrifice, upon being propitiated by the great sages, O Vishnudatta, Parikshit, the Lord Himself (Vishnu), to bestow favor upon King Nābhi, entered the womb of Queen Merudevī in the royal chambers. He assumed this holy incarnation with the desire to reveal the dharmas of the immortal sages of Vātaraśana.” (rough trans. Jain)

The point of interest here is “immortal sages of Vātaraśana.” Hiralal Jain notes the presence of Vātaraśana and reads these sages as the Keśin of RV 10.136.44 At the same time, G. V. Tagare interprets them as nude Sramanas performing austerities with a vow of eternal celibacy and draws parallels with sky-clad or Digambara Jains.45 But without any further corroboration, these could all amount to mere speculation based on a chance occurrence of the name Vātaraśana in two unrelated texts. Do we have any? Turns out we do.

Centuries before the Puranas came a collection of ten books of hymns called _Taittiriya Araṇyaka_. Taittiriya was a disciple of Yāska which places him in the fifth century BC. That is, if the work is indeed his. At times later scriptural works have also been named after revered saints of the past out of respect, a good example being _Yājñavalkya Smṛti_ which came several centuries after its purported author. Either way, Taittiriya Araṇyaka, like all Araṇyakas, comfortably predates the Puranas and is unanimously considered part of the Yajurvedic canon. In this work, verse 2.7.1 unambiguously identifies the ascetics of Vātaraśana as Sramanas.46 This leaves little doubt that the Keśin of the Rigveda were none other than Sramanas of later works in both orthodox and heterodox traditions. Scholars of Vedic and Buddhist studies like Govind Chandra Pande also seem to agree that the possibility of Sramanic traditions being part of the Vedic world is rather high47 and sees a link, even if tenuous, between the mid-Vedic concept of _punarmṛtyū_ or repeated death and the Sramanic concept of _punarjanma_ or rebirth.48 At any rate, the Upanishads have a clear influence of eastern Sramanism, especially in its adoption of karma.

Speaking of Upanishads, there’s a famous dialog between the sage Yājñavalkya and king Janaka of Ramayana in the _Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad_ which further reaffirms the integration of Sramanic concepts within the Vedic philosophy. In this dialog, the sage explains the character of soul or _ātmā_ and posits that in the post-conscious state, an individual transcends all identities and relationships held otherwise held during consciousness. Among the identities Yājñavalkya lists as examples are śramaṇa and _tāpasa_49—yet another evidence of the Sramanic tradition’s positive acknowledgment in the Vedic and Upanishadic mainstream.

## Sramana in the Epics

There’s an interesting discourse in chapter 180 of Mahabharata’s _Vāna Parva_ , also known as _Araṇya Parva_. Both _vana_ and _araṇya_ are Sanskrit for jungle, the setting for this story. In this chapter, Yudhishthira is in a conversation with the king of serpents and is asked to define the “true Brahmin.” The answer is surprisingly reminiscent of how the word has been defined in both Buddhist and Jain scripture. The king of the Pandavas lists truthfulness, charity, forgiveness, kindness, and most importantly, asceticism as the defining qualities of a Brahmin.50 Not lineage and not ritual purity. In fact, a couple of verses down the line, he even clarifies that even a Shudra carrying those qualities would become a Brahmin and a Brahmin without them, a Shudra.51 When the serpent-king then questions the validity of birth-based castes, since it’s all about deeds anyway, Yudhishthira further reaffirms that good behavior and good behavior alone makes one a Brahmin.52 He attributes the futility of birth-based caste assignments to rampant inter-caste miscegenation in the society.53 This indicates two important things about Mahabharata’s world—upward mobility and permeable caste barriers for the purpose of marriage. Which is as close to the Sramanic ideal as it gets.

The point is further reiterated in the thirteenth book, Anushasana Parva, where in a lengthy discourse on caste, Shiva tells his wife about all the ways people can move up or down the caste hierarchy. He lists out conducts that can turn a Brahmin into a Shudra and vice versa. There’s a clear caste mobility in these verses.54 In 13.143.49, Shiva posits a higher placement for a Shudra of good deeds than a _twice-born_ of bad deeds.55 The following two verses further fortify this position by stating that neither birth nor rites define Brahminhood and that all caste assignments ought to be functions of conduct.56

There are many other verses scattered throughout the epic that repeat the same point in different contexts and in different voices. In some places it’s the gods such as Shiva, at others it’s humans such as Yudhishthira. Therefore, one can pretty much conclude that at least the Mahabharata, at least in its narrative, is pretty much consistent on its definitions of castes with Sramanic canons, i.e. the piṭakas and the āgamas. The term Sramana too finds abundant mention in the work in various contexts, the first couple of instances being in the very first book in verses 3.136–37:57

There’s no ambiguity on the designation of the Sramana in these verses. 3.136 clearly describes him as a naked mendicant, which is how all Buddhist and Jain works define the term.      _Kathakāścāpare rājañśramaṇāśca vanaukasaḥ_ ,
    _Divyākhyānāni ye cāpi paṭhanti madhuraṃ dvijāḥ._ “Narrators of the Lord’s devotees, forest dwellers who uplift others, and those who recite beautiful stories with sweet emotions.” (trans. Satwalekar)

In all instances, the Sramana is shown in a light of esteem and reverence. Even in the last example, he’s someone who “uplifts others.” Thus, both orthodoxy and heterodoxy seem to have ample respect for each other’s philosophies. Both Brahmins and Sramanas enjoy equal esteem in Brahminical texts, and both Brahmins and Sramanas enjoy equal esteem in Buddhist and Jain texts. Both seem to place _guṇa_ and karma above birth. And yet, one subtle but key distinction must be made between the two schools of thought in the latter context.

Remember when Shiva spoke of caste mobility in his discourse with Parvati in Anushasana Parva? He sure said that a Shudra could become a Brahmin with good deeds and a Brahmin could become a Shudra with bad, but he did drop a very subtle caveat in 143.30—next life. A Shudra of good character, good deeds, and good education could absolutely become a Brahmin. But in another life.59 Evidently, there’s some reluctance in actually privileging action over birth. That’s why the same chapter makes caste mobility unconditional in later verses as we noted earlier.

## Sramana in Secular Records

While the religious corpus we’ve examined thus far gives us a reasonably clear picture of the brāhmaṇa-śramaṇa relationships before the Common Era, for a complete picture we must also consider the large body of non-scriptural, non-canonical work at our disposal. Much of this comes in the form of grammar or exegetics, a prime example being Panini’s seminal Aṣṭādhyāyī. This work lists a large number of grammatical and syntactical rules for the Sanskrit language and illustrates each with a number of demonstrative examples. One such illustration comes in 2.1.70 and involves the word Sramana in a compound— _kumāraḥ śramaṇādibhiḥ_.60 This is an example of the untranslatable Sanskrit concept of _tatpuruṣa_.61 These are idioms where the first word modifies the second. An example in English would be, say, birdfeeder. Here, the main word is _feeder_ , and the modifier _bird_ tells us that it’s meant for birds. Or cellular phone, where _cellular_ tells us what kind of _phone_ it is. In Panini’s example, śramaṇādibhiḥ is a compound of śramaṇā (ascetic), _ādi_ (beginning or and so on), and _bhiḥ_ (by or with) and translates roughly into “by the ascetics and others.” Kumāra is a young and unmarried boy and modifies the Sramanas to convey the sense of young, virgin ascetics. Panini lived in the farthest of west in Gandhara, far from the Sramanic heartland of Magadha. The fact that he qualifies them as virginal indicates that not only were Sramanas well recognized and acknowledged all over the Aryan country but also held in high esteem everywhere. Most scholars place Panini before Buddhism and Jainism by at least a century.

We already noted at the very beginning of this article another work of Grammar that emerged within Magadha several centuries later and included Sramana in a strange compound called dvanda. Patanjali, who lived in the second century BC, advanced Śramaṇabrāhmaṇam as an example of a compound that expresses eternal conflict. While on the one hand we have Mahavira being venerated as a māhaṇa and Buddha calling Brahmin and Sramana one and the same, on the other hand we have Patanjali positioning the two communities on opposite sides of the social divide with a hint of ideological hostilities. But this isn’t as big an anomaly as it appears at first crack. As we saw in the case of Niśītha Cūrṇi, the Brahmin-Sramana relationship experienced much social metamorphosis as we draw closer to and into the Common Era. While the two communities became social rivals, as would be expected of any other pair anywhere, they remained doctrinally aligned. Niśītha Cūrṇi itself speaks of Brahmins being the most spiritually superior beings sent to the Earth by Prajāpati himself, while at the same time it also tells us how a Brahmin and a Jain couldn’t intermingle in the society. There were several layers to the relationship.

## Brahmin in Secular Records

The same Patanjali who names Brahmins and Sramanas as perpetual rivals, also tells us about what a true Brahmin is. Frankly, he says nothing new here. We’ve heard this before from Yudhishthira and from Shiva too. According to his Vyakarana Mahabhashya, a Brahmin is a man of no desires and no aspirations other than those of learning, his only obsession. The three central qualities he defines Brahminhood by are intellect, lineage, and actions:

     _Trīṇi yasyāvadātāni vidyā yoniś ca karma ca,
    Etac chivaṁ vijānī hi brāhmaṇāgrayasya lakṣaṇam._
    “Three things which are pure: knowledge, origin, and deed,
    Know this well to be the sign of the foremost Brahmin.” (rough trans.)

He reiterates this position at least a couple of times with a slightly different choice of words, replacing deed with austerity:

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _Tapaḥ śrutaṁ ca yoniś cety etad brāhmaṇakārakam,
    Tapaḥśrutābhyāṁ yo hīno jātībrāhmaṇa eva saḥ._
    “Austerity, learning, and origin—these constitute the making of a Brahmin.
    He who is devoid of austerity and learning is merely a Brahmin by birth.” (rough trans.)

In both instances, there’s a clear stress on birth, but just as clear a stress is placed on actions and character. Mere birth is not sufficient for Brahminhood in Patanjali’s world. The same is prescribed in the Mahabharata too. And almost the same is prescribed in both āgamas and piṭakas, the only deviation being on the question of birth and lineage. Even then, as we saw in the case of Uttarādhyayana Sūtra, Jain literature does not completely dismiss the utility of gotras, a birth-based attribute, or even the Vedas.

## Buddha and the Brahmins

Thus far we have discussed broadly the doctrinal and social relationships between Sramanas and Brahmins, from native literary perspectives. Sometimes it pays to zoom out and see things from a foreigner’s perspective too. We know India has been home to a large number of foreign settlers, pilgrims, and invaders over its long history. So there must be some who could tell us about this social structure as they saw it. But before we get to that, let’s circle back to Buddha for a quick minute and explore an interesting question—Did Buddha discriminate between Brahmins and non-Brahmins? To rephrase the question in a more neutral tone, what was Buddha’s attitude toward Brahmins?

American author Joseph Walser throws some light on the subject in his paper62 on early Buddhism and its relationship with the Vedic creed, a must read for anyone looking to dive deeper. He points us to a verse in _Samantapasadika_ where the Buddha prescribes instant ordination without _parivāsa_ or probation for anyone who is either a Sakya or an Agnihotra Brahmin.63 For all others there’s a mandatory probationary period before formal initiation into the Buddhist order. What’s interesting here is the special privilege being extended to a class of Brahmins over others. What’s even more interesting is Buddha’s nepotistic reasoning behind extending the same privilege to members of the Sakya clan: “This exceptional privilege, O Bhikkhus, I grant to my kinsmen.”64 If you’d recall, Buddha is also called Sakyamuni for his Sakya descent.

Another curious illustration of the Brahmin privilege in Buddha’s sphere is in the way he addressed his Brahmin and non-Brahmin disciples. For instance, one of his closest disciples was a Brahmin named Maudgalyāyana (Moggallāna in Pali). In most conversations, Buddha is shown to address him as brāhmaṇa in the second person.65 Now, one can see this as an acknowledgment of and appreciation for Maudgalyāyana’s spiritual eminence, because at any rate Brahminhood in Buddhist works is a function of deeds and character rather than lineage. But Walser finds that explanation inadequate, for the Buddha never uses that vocative for members of other castes who have similarly acquired _Brahminhood_ through austerity and penance.66

Walser also notes a strong distinction in the curriculum Buddha chose to teach his Brahmin disciples vs. non-Brahmins. A central doctrine in Buddhism is that of non-self or _anattā_. This syncs well with another Sramanic doctrine, _sanatan_. What they entail is a topic for another day, but we do know that Buddha preached these. According to Walser, however, he did not preach this doctrine to everyone. There was a clear tendency to preach anattā to non-Brahmins rather than Brahmins.67 Principal Brahmin disciples like Mahākāśyapa (Mahākassapa in Pali) and Piṇḍola Bhāradvāja, who was also an arahat—were apparently never taught about the doctrine of anattā.68 Similarly, another most definitive Buddhist concept, the Noble Eightfold Path, is taught to Brahmins far more than non-Brahmins.69

There are several ways to read this pedagogical _discrimination_. One could be as an indicator of Buddha’s own placement of accomplished Brahmins in a higher doctrinal standing than the rest. Another could be a slightly more practical function of the disciple’s existing knowledgebase. What this means is that Buddha likely tailored his sermons to his audience’ spiritual backgrounds. Non-Brahmins, for instance, were more likely to absorb the concept of anattā since they would not have to unlearn the Brahminical concept of _atman_ as a prerequisite.70 Similarly, a Brahmin could be more receptive to the concept of _samādhi_ and the Noble Eightfold Path because it wouldn’t clash with his pre-existing bias toward _atman_.71 We can only guess. Whatever be the case, though, it’s demonstrably incorrect to conclude that Buddha and Brahmins were in any kind of mutual conflict, either social or doctrinal. And now we can finally zoom out.

## A Foreign Perspective

The best foreign accounts of the time come from the Greeks because they were the only foreign powers to have ruled over a meaningful part of India for a meaningful period before the Common Era. Even before they formally began to rule, much cultural and social exchange had already taken place between the Indian and Greek polities. The Mauryan and the Seleucid empires shared a direct border for over a hundred years. Naturally, no foreign power knew India better than the Greeks.

And yet, we have little in the way of written accounts from the time. The only Greek record of India we have at our disposal for now is Megasthenes’ Indica. Even that isn’t extant in its entirety, and we only have fragments to work with. Herodotus too wrote about India, but he never visited the subcontinent. In fact, he didn’t visit most of the places he wrote about which reflects in the numerous known inaccuracies in his writings. Mehasthenes was a Seleucid ambassador at the Mauryan court in Pataliputra in the early years of the dynasty. He spent about two years in the Magadhan capital and wrote extensively about the social setup as he saw it. This was toward the final years of the fourth century BC. If Buddha lived in the fifth century, as held by traditions, that’s a hundred years between him and Megasthenes. So no primary account of Buddha’s times, but still a reasonably reliable account of life in Buddha’s sphere of influence in the Buddhist heartland under a Hindu polity. Remember, Asoka was still a child when Megasthenes was in Pataliputra.

Like most readers even today, Megasthenes too confuses occupational divisions with doctrinal divisions in his work. Indica sees the Indian society as divided into not four but seven classes or castes, mostly along occupational lines. The four varṇas of the Vedic system are included in this list. Curiously, on top of this list sat two classes of philosophers. This is probably in line with the Athenian worldview where philosophers and teachers held a higher social status as compared to workers, warriors, traders, and even rulers. The other six groups are farmer, herders and hunters, traders, warriors, overseers, and administrators. The two classes of philosophers in Indica are _Brachmanes_ or Brahmins and _Sarmanes_ or Sramanas.

As an aside, an interesting intellectual parallel drawn by Megasthenes is between the Brahmins of India and the Jews of the Levant when he says, “All that has been said regarding nature by the ancients is asserted also by philosophers out of Greece, on the one part in India by the Brachmanes, and on the other in Syria by the people called the Jews.”72

Thus, in the fourth-century Magadha, it would seem, both Brahmins and Sramanas enjoyed high social status under a Hindu ruler. This is not likely to have changed when the polity switched to Buddhism under Asoka. There are several ways scholars have interpreted this positioning of the two classes together at the top of the social hierarchy in Indica’s Pataliputra. In his critical translation of Indica, Scottish philologist John Watson McCrindle argues that there was little overt distinction between a lay Brahmin and a follower of Buddha.73 McCrindle’s work, is based on fragments first assembled and translated into Latin by E. A. Schwanbeck. The latter admits to there being a confusion on the true identity of the Sramanas but eventually tilts in favor of them being Buddhists.74 German Orientalist Christian Lassen, on the other hand, disagrees and takes a more nuanced position. In _Indische Altertumskunde_ , his voluminous German language work on Indian antiquities, he argues that originally a follower of Buddha was designated _upāsaka_.75 Those among the upāsakas who took the vow of chastity, lived off alms, and were formally ordained into Buddha’s order were called bhikkhus.76 It’s these bhikkhus that were also often called Sramana, a term that also applied to Brahmin ascetics.77 According to Lassen, it’s only by Asoka’s time that Sramana became exclusive to Buddhism.78

## Conclusion

Now whether Sramana and Brahmin were one and the same is mostly a question of technicality and also, as Lassen posits, one the answer to which likely changed with time. But this minor technicality aside, the distillate of this discussion remains that to the non-discerning Greek eyes, both held comparable esteem in the society. Both Sramanas and Brahmins sat on top of the social pyramid as thinkers and diviners. Did this equation deteriorate with time? Absolutely. The Niśītha Cūrṇi we’ve discussed before already leaves little to doubt that the two communities eventually grew to see each other as social rivals. There were political dimensions to these changes as well. Kingdoms where Jainism or Buddhism received overtly partial patronage from the royalty witnessed much resentment from the Brahmins, and vice versa. Don’t forget that Sramanism dominated the scene through much of Mauryan, Greek, Parthian, Scythian, and Kushan reigns. Brahmin hegemony only truly reemerged from the shadows after the fall of the Kushans. With a brief disastrous Hun interlude, the Guptas emerged as the first truly Brahminical polity of scale in all of Classical Antiquity. That’s when much of Brahminical literature was _Shastrified_ and works like Manusmriti were produced. This doesn’t mean the end of Buddhist and Jain thoughts though, for the Guptas were active patrons of all faiths of the time. Nalanda was actively supported by them despite its Buddhist character.

A major testimony to this amity is the adoption of Gautam Buddha as one of the incarnations of Vishnu in the Puranic literature that emerged during the Shastrification of the Vedic philosophy. On the Buddhist side, the same could be seen in the emergence of the Mahayana creed almost around the same time as the rise of the Guptas, and Vajrayana after their fall. With these emergent flavors, a number of Hindu deities, most notably Ganesha, got incorporated into the Buddhist pantheon. The language of scripture also witnessed a gradual shift from Pali to Sanskrit. A similar evolution is also noted in the Jain doctrine where deities like Lakshmi and Saraswati became part of the Jain tradition. In fact, the oldest rendition of a Hindu deity in human form is a figure of Saraswati found in Mathura that was donated to a local temple by a Jain metalworker named Gova.79

In sum, the difference between Brahmin and Sramanic thoughts was overwhelmingly in the area of doctrine and philosophy, not in prestige. The line between the two systems was extremely permeable and nonviolent. Members of the two communities could intermarry and often did. Not very different from the relationship between Hindus and Sikhs of Punjab (the violent episodes of the late twentieth century notwithstanding).
