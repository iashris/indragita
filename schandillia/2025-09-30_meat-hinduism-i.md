# India’s Relationship with Meat—I

*Exploring the Evolution of India’s Carnivory from the Harappans to the Epics*

- audience: only_paid
- url: https://www.schandillia.com/p/meat-hinduism-i

---

The Hindu dietary tradition has been a source of immense debate, one that often devolves into violent exchanges. Especially on the subject of meat. For the longest time, Indian diet is understood to be primarily meat-free, thanks to the longstanding Indic stricture against violence. The only violence permitted in most instances is those that help preserve or restore _dharma_. Violence for food is borderline taboo. Needless to say, this has led to a situation where meat-eating is increasingly being seen as antithetical to Hindu values. While beef is a strict no-no, meat in general is frowned upon in many circles.

At the same time, we do have a large number of communities where meat isn’t just permitted but enforced as divine offerings. A most well-known example is that of Bengal and the neighboring Jharkhand where goats are sacrificed during Durga Puja and partaken in as the _prasāda_ from the goddess. Animal sacrifice is also a common feature in Bihar and a few other places as part of life events, such as wedding. Even without ritualistic slaughter, there are a number of Brahmin communities that consume meat without any injunction. These include, but are not limited to, Kashmiri Pandits and Maithili Brahmins.

Who then is the real Hindu? Can one consume meat and still be as Hindu as one who doesn’t? The question isn’t as close ended as it seems. It warrants a thorough exploration of what our vast scriptural corpus says on the matter. This is what we aim to do in this article. We will start right from the beginning and proceed from there, examining both textual and archaeological exhibits along the way. We will consider not only what the layman ate but also what the sacerdotal authority stipulated. Because tradition must account for both. In the interest of depth, we divide the discussion into two parts. This one goes only as far as the epics. In a later piece, we will explore the Shastras, the Puranas, and the Shramanic traditions like Buddhism and Jainism.

## Prehistory

It’s extremely challenging to conclude with absolute certainty what our prehistoric ancestors ate. This is for a variety of reasons the most crippling one being our continued inability to read their writing. We’re still far from figuring out the nature of their writing, much less its content. A related factor is the very fact that they wrote so little. The longest known Indus inscription is less than thirty symbols long. That’s not a whole lot to work with. We don’t have large bodies of monumental inscriptions the likes of which made it possible to decipher Mesopotamian and Ashokan writings with much greater ease. Having said that, educated guesses can still be made, sometimes with a fair amount of conviction.

For instance, they were an agricultural community, had been since as early as Mehrgarh, long before Harappa and Mohenjo-daro. From that, we can most certainly assume that cereals formed part of their diet. But did they eat meat?

We know for a fact that the Harappans enjoyed an intimate relationship with animals. Since the earliest of times, they had domesticated the likes of humped bull, sheep, water buffalo, donkey, and perhaps even elephants.1 While much of values was obtained from these animals in the way of wool, draught, and dairy, our Indus ancestors, it would seem, were also meat-eaters.2 This is evident from the presence of meat among funerary offerings, as well as the discovery of clay sling balls, copper fishhooks,3 arrowheads, and knives, which point to hunting and fishing as common practices. Their diet, it has been argued, encompassed beef, mutton, pork, and poultry, along with the flesh of gharials, turtles, and tortoises, in addition to fresh river fish and dried fish obtained from the coast. Half-burnt bones and shells found in and around dwellings further attest to these dietary habits.

The mere presence of bones does not in and of itself establish meat consumption as food, for they could belong to animals that died naturally or killed by other predators. That’s why a deeper look at the condition of these remains is warranted. Marks on bones, if any, can reveal much about the nature of the animal’s death. In the Indus Valley, we have those in abundance. Remains from sites like Farmana, Girawad, Masudpur, Bagasra, Shikarpur, and Kanmer bear cut marks made not by claws and teeth of a predator but by metal blades fashioned by men.4 Charring and vitrification patterns further show that cattle and buffalo were processed differently from sheep, goats, and deer, suggesting varied methods of preparation and perhaps a preference for roasting certain meats.5 Differences between sites—such as selective butchery at Bagasra versus uniform practices at Shikarpur—point to social or cultural distinctions. For example, it’s found that people within city walls cooked meat differently from those without.6

For the longest time, the Harappans were also thought to have pioneered chicken domestication. That notion, however, has been discarded since as newer research have attributed the credit to China. A 2011 paper, for example, places IVC chicken domestication around 2500 BC, but that of Neolithic China around 6000 BC.7 Another from 2022 dismisses even this claim and places IVC chicken as late as 800 BC, the pioneers being Neolithic Thailand around 1650 BC.8 It explains the “chicken” bones from 2600 BC Mohenjo-daro as a case of misidentification.9

Having said that, we must not dismiss the idea of poultry exports from the IVC to the contemporary Mesopotamia. Mesopotamian sites have yielded multiple tablets that speak of _dar Meluhha_ , or “bird of Meluhha,” which could refer to any of the fowls from hens to partridge.10 Two conclusions could be drawn from this. Either these birds were recreational commodities—think cockfights, etc.—or they could be exported for food. If they were exported for food, it’s not unlikely that they were consumed as food as well, although in the absence of further material evidence, it’s wiser not to conclude either way.

While we may or may not take evidence of poultry consumption as final, meat of animals and fish are harder to dismiss. That our Indus forefathers were meat-eaters, is a fact that now enjoys universal academic consensus. By the beginning of the first millennium BC, we have a new population in the mix. Did they consume meat of any kind?

## Vedic Period

Now things start to get a tad easier. Since the Aryans documented their way of life at length in enormous compositions known as the _Vedas_ , we’re no longer at the mercy of educated guesswork from secondary indicators. We can just gleam a great deal directly and unambiguously from the horse’s mouth.

Our first exhibit comes from the _Rigveda_ itself, albeit a much later verse in its chronology, 10.16.4:11

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _ajo bhāgas tapasā taṃ tapasva taṃ te śocis tapatu taṃ te arciḥ,
    yās te śivās tanvo jātavedas tābhir vahainaṃ sukṛtām u lokam._
    “The goat is your portion. With your heat, heat it up. Let your flame heat it up, let your beam.
    The propitious bodies that are yours, o Jatavedas, with those convey him to the world of good (ritual) performers.” (trans. Jamison)

This hymn, along with the one preceding it, pertains to the cremation ritual, where Agni, the fire, is exhorted to bear the departed to the realm of the ancestors. The goat in 16.4 is posited as a votive offering to Agni. Of course, the offering must be consumed by someone upon the conclusion of the ritual. Having said that, Monier-Williams gives multiple other meanings of _aj_ ,12 the word for goat in this verse. Some of those are in the context of non-Rigvedic texts, so we won’t consider them here. In the Rigveda too, however, the word means several things other than goat. Let’s see if or how they fit.

Aj can refer to a variety of deities including Brahma, Kāma, a Rudra, a Marut, and even Agni himself.13 But this reading doesn’t fit the verse in question, for it’s absurd to offer a portion or _bhāga_ of one deity to another. Aj is also the sign of Aries, which is understandable in light of its hircine connotations.14 And it’s also a kind of mineral.15 Again, offering of a mineral in a fire ritual, cremation or otherwise, is not only absurd but not part of Hindu ritual anywhere at all. But before we close the case, there’s one small problem.

Monier-Williams’ aj can also denote a kind of rice,16 a meaning that aligns as plausibly as the goat. The offering of rice to the sacrificial fire is by no means unfamiliar in Hindu ritual practice. We are thus left with a deliberate and meaningful ambiguity. But we’re not done yet.

In the same _mandala_ , 89.14 explicitly references a slaughterhouse:17

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _karhi svit sā ta indra cetyāsad aghasya yad bhinado rakṣa eṣat,
    mitrakruvo yac chasane na gāvaḥ pṛthivyā āpṛg amuyā śayante._
    “When will vengeance be yours, Indra, such that you will rend the demonic power of the evil one, which besets (us),
    And that those who bloody their allies will lie there like cows at slaughter, in the same way as (Vrtra), embracer of the earth?” (trans. Jamison)

Here, Indra is invoked as the champion of order and protector of his followers, urged to unleash his might against demonic forces that oppress the people. The verse appeals to him to shatter the power of the “evil one,” much as he once struck down Vṛtra, the cosmic serpent who encompassed the earth and withheld its waters. The imagery of treacherous men lying slain “like cows at slaughter” underscores both the totality of Indra’s expected victory and the brutal fate awaiting those who betray their kin. This verse, unlike the last one, offers a mighty unambiguous vindication of meat-eating at least in the later Rigvedic period when the tenth mandala would have been composed.

The word in focus here is _yacchasane_. While its translation by Jamison as slaughterhouse and by early Orientalist Horace Wilson as “place of immolation” might trigger doubts of Western or colonial bias in some readers, we also have Dayanand Saraswati translating it as _hatyāgār_ , _hatyā_ being Hindi for slaughter.18 But let’s dig deeper just to be sure. Yacchasane is a compound of _yat_ and _śasana_. It’s the latter that holds the key. Monier-Williams translates it as “slaughtering” or “killing” as a gerundial noun.19 The word derives from the root verb _śas_ which itself translates as “to cut down,” “to kill,” or “to slaughter.”20 This should remove all ambiguity surrounding RV 10.89.14. We won’t get into whether the animal implied, is specifically a cow or just cattle in general; for that refer to an earlier essay specifically on beef. For now, we have a clear indication of meat of some sort in the time of the tenth mandala.

In the first mandala—not the first chronologically, mind you—we have a reference to the hunting of birds, presumably for food. 1.92.10, the verse in question, speaks of the wife of a hunter cutting up and dividing the birds, as a metaphor:21

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _punaḥ-punar jāyamānā purāṇī samānaṃ varṇam abhi śumbhamānā,
    śvaghnīva kṛtnur vija āminānā martasya devī jarayanty āyuḥ._
    “The divine and ancient Uṣas, born again and again and bright with unchanging hues, wastes away the life of a mortal, like the wife of a hunter cutting up and dividing the birds.” (trans. Wilson)

Stephanie Jamison, however, posits a slightly different translation:22

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
    “Being born again and again though ancient, (always) beautifying herself to the same hue,
    like a successful (gambler) with the best throw who diminishes the stake (of his opponent), the goddess keeps diminishing the lifetime of the mortal as she ages him.”

No mention of birds or hunter this time. So, what is it? The word of note is _āminānā martasya devī_ , which Wilson translates as “wife of hunter” and Jamison as “goddess who keeps diminishing the lifetime of the mortal.” Dramatically disjoint readings. Let’s ask Dayanand Saraswati. He translates the expression as the “(female) hawk that destroys birds in flight.”23 Now we have a hunter’s wife, a mortal-slaying goddess, and a bird-preying hawk. Only one works for the meat-eating proposition, and we have no basis to conclude which, particularly because this is a metaphor. But we’re still not done with the Vedas.

In the same mandala we have a hymn dedicated to the most well-known of all Vedic rites, the _ashvamedha_ or “the horse sacrifice.” The hymn speaks of not only the sacrifice but also, just so there’s no ambiguity, goes into the gory details of the slaughter and the cooking of the meat thus obtained. In 1.162.11, the cooked meat of the sacrificed horse is explicitly sought in alms by those looking forward to partaking in the delicacy:24

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _ye vājinam paripaśyanti pakvaṃ ya īm āhuḥ surabhir nir hareti,
    ye cārvato māṃsabhikṣām upāsata uto teṣām abhigūrtir na invatu._
    “Those who inspect the prizewinner when cooked and who say about him; ‘It smells good! Take it off (the fire)!’
    And those who draw near in hopes of a share of the meat of the steed—let the applause also of those urge us on.” (trans. Jamison)

The animal is _vājin_. Jamison translates it as horse, as does Wilson,25 but Saraswati reads food made with cereals. Monier-Williams offers a wide array from horse and stallion to hero and warrior.26 Although the hymn’s context being that of a horse, it’s not unreasonable to go with Wilson’s and Jamison’s translations. But even if we leave that bit undecided, we stumble upon _māṃsabhikṣām_ , a compound of _māṃsa_ , meaning “flesh,” and _bhikṣā_ , meaning “alms.” While Saraswati differs from Jamison and Wilson on vājin, all three seem to agree on māṃsa.27 Even here, there are those that translate the word as pulp, e.g., of a fruit, in the same way as flesh can mean the flesh of an orange, for instance. But that is a figure of speech that does not supersede the meaning most befitting of the context.

Not only does the Rigveda establish meat eating, but it also affirms the notion of pure and impure meats. In 4.18.13, no less than Indra himself confesses to consuming the entrails of a dog in a state of destitution, implying the meat’s taboo status under normal circumstances:28

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _avartyā śuna āntrāṇi pece na deveṣu vivide marḍitāram,
    apaśyaṃ jāyām amahīyamānām adhā me śyeno madhv ā jabhāra._
    “Out of need I cooked for myself the entrails of a dog. I found none who was merciful among the gods.
    I saw his wife being dishonored. Then the falcon carried the honey here to me.” (trans. Jamison)

Saraswati reads this more as a figure of speech than a literal admission of dog consumption,29 but the very fact that dog consumption is even a figurative idea affirms the meat’s taboo status. And if one meat is taboo, there must be another that isn’t. Otherwise, it makes no sense to expressly prohibit one animal in particular and not all.

To reiterate, we’re not discussing bovine meat here because an entire essay has previously dealt with the subject of beef at length.

That the early Rigvedic period is a meat-eating society is most evident in the prevalence of ritualistic sacrifice across the board. A most well-known illustration is in the legend of Sunahsepa in 1.24. In verse 13 of this hymn, Sunahsepa is bound to a three-footed post in preparation for his sacrifice:30

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _śunaḥśepo hy ahvad gṛbhītas triṣv ādityaṃ drupadeṣu baddhaḥ,
    avainaṃ rājā varuṇaḥ sasṛjyād vidvām̐ adabdho vi mumoktu pāśān._
    “Since Sunahsepa, seized and bound in three stocks, called upon the Aditya,
    King Varuna should set him loose. Let him—the knowing one, never cheated—release the fetters.” (trans. Jamison)

Although the sacrifice doesn’t go through, thanks to Varuna’s timely intervention, the very mention indicates that ritual slaughter of non-plant entities, even if not human, was a thing.

In later Vedic centuries, we also witness the emergence of purpose-built sacrificial stakes called _yūpa_ or _yūpastambha_. These wooden pillars served as the posts to which animals were tethered before sacrifice, their execution believed to ensure prosperity for the community. Though most were perishable, later stone examples like the Isapur Yūpa preserve their form and symbolism, often replicating the ropes and bearing solar motifs described in ritual texts. There are abundant mentions of this contraption in the _Shukla Yajurveda_ commentary called _Shatapatha Brahmana_ , a work dated to the early years of the Second Urbanization and attributed to a sage named Yajnavalkya. SB 1.6.2.1, for the first time, offers an explicit etymology of the term:31

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _yajñena vai devāḥ,
    imāṃ jitiṃ jigyuryaiṣāmiyaṃ jitiste hocuḥ kathaṃ na idam manuṣyairanabhyārohyaṃ syāditi te yajñasya rasaṃ dhītvā yathā madhu madhukṛto nirdhayeyurviduhya yajñaṃ yūpena yopayitvā tiro’bhavannatha yadenenāyopayaṃstasmaddyūpo nāma tadvā ṛṣīṇāmanuśrutamāsa_
    “Verily, by means of the sacrifice the gods made that conquest (of the world of heaven). When they had conquered, they said, ‘How may this (celestial region) be made unattainable by men?’ They then sipped the sap of the sacrifice, as bees would suck out honey; and having drained the sacrifice and effaced the traces of it with the (sacrificial) post, they concealed themselves: and because they effaced (ayopayan, viz. the sacrifice) with it, therefore it is called yūpa (sacrificial post). Now this was heard by the Ṛṣis:” (trans. Eggeling)

The yūpa also repeats appearance in SB 3.1.4.3, 3.4.3.15, 3.2.2.2, 3.6.4.1, 3.7.1.27, and 11.7.3.2. In every single one of these instances, the context unambiguously indicates one and only one usage—ritual slaughter.

Having established the prevalence of carnivory among Vedic peoples, it’s also important to understand that we’re talking about a large demography spread across a large geography here. And of course, a thousand-year timeline, if not more. It’d be absurd to ascribe to such a mass a uniform eating habit across time and space. Today, we have those who find nothing wrong with meat and those who find it reprehensible. There’s no reason to believe such a spectrum couldn’t have existed back in the day. Which is why while we have some verses establishing meat-consumption, we also have others frowning upon it. Take 1.187.8 thru 10, for instance:32

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _yad apām oṣadhīnām pariṃśam āriśāmahe,
    vātāpe pīva id bhava.
    yat te soma gavāśiro yavāśiro bhajāmahe,
    vātāpe pīva id bhava.
    karambha oṣadhe bhava pīvo vṛkka udārathiḥ,
    vātāpe pīva id bhava._
    “When we bite off a full share of the waters and plants,
    O you friend of the winds—become just the fat.
    When we take a share of you when mixed with milk or mixed with grain, o Soma,
    O you friend of the winds—become just the fat.
    Become the gruel, o plant, the fat, the steaming suet,
    O you friend of the winds—become just the fat.” (trans. Jamison)

The hymn is an ode to food— _annastuti_ —and the verses in question list out man’s sources of nourishment, attempting to sacralize them. This list includes _oṣadhi_ (plants), _gavāśiro_ (milk), _yava_ (barley), and _karambha_ (gruel), but no meat. While this is far from a definitive list, the fact that it doesn’t include meat of any kind warrants being noted.

As we approach the later centuries of the Vedic Age, we start noting a general discomfort with carnivory, something often attributed to the Shramanic influences in the Magadha region. This discomfort is most evident in the context of beef. Again, this does not imply a wholesale replacement of carnivory with vegetarianism. Which is why the same Yajnavalkya who relishes bovine meat in SB 3.1.2.21, also warns of the grave spiritual peril attached to eating cow or ox flesh, describing it as tantamount to consuming “everything” and leading to rebirth in ignominious forms—in the same verse, no less:

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _athainaṃ śālām prapādayati,
    sa dhenvai cānaḍuhaśca nāśnīyāddhenvanaḍuhau vā idaṃ sarvam bibhṛtaste devā abruvandhenvanaḍuhau vā idaṃ sarvam bibhṛto hanta yadanyeṣāṃ vayasāṃ vīryaṃ taddhenvanaḍuhayordadhāmeti sa yadanyeṣāṃ vayasāṃ vīryamāsīttaddhenvanaḍuhayoradadhustasmāddhenuścaivānaḍvāṃśca bhūyiṣṭham bhuṅktastaddhaitatsarvāśyamiva yo dhenvanaḍuhayoraśnīyādantagatiriva taṃ hādbhutamabhijanitorjāyāyai garbhaṃ nirabadhīditi papamakaditi pāpī kīrtistasmāddhenvanaḍuhayornāśnīyāttadu hovāca yājñavalkyo ‘śnāmyevāhamaṃsalaṃ cedbhavatīti._
    “He (the Adhvaryu) then makes him enter the hall. Let him not eat (the flesh) of either the cow or the ox; for the cow and the ox doubtless support everything here on earth. The gods spake, ‘Verily, the cow and the ox support everything here: come, let us bestow on the cow and the ox whatever vigor belongs to other species!’ Accordingly, they bestowed on the cow and the ox whatever vigor belonged to other species (of animals); and therefore, the cow and the ox eat most. Hence, were one to eat (the flesh) of an ox or a cow, there would be, as it were, an eating of everything, or, as it were, a going on to the end (or, to destruction). Such a one indeed would be likely to be born (again) as a strange being, (as one of whom there is) evil report, such as ‘he has expelled an embryo from a woman,’ ‘he has committed a sin;’ let him therefore not eat (the flesh) of the cow and the ox. Nevertheless, Yajnavalkya said, ‘I, for one, eat it, provided that it is tender.’” (trans. Eggeling).

The _Atharvaveda_ , in 5.19.5, condemns the consumption of beef as an affront to the _pitṛs_ (ancestors), and calls the slaughter of cows, cruel.33 In 12.4.38, it further warns that one who kills or cooks a cow, even if barren and even in private, risks the loss of progeny, as Bṛhaspati himself is said to deprive such a transgressor of sons and grandsons.34 There are several other injunctions against meat throughout the Late Vedic corpus, and just as many allowances, if not outright statements of encouragement. While one could enumerate each such instance as above, doing so would be redundant, for the essential point that our Vedic ancestors unquestionably partook of meat remains firmly established. Let’s therefore move on to the era _after_ the Vedas.

## Post-Vedic Period

This period is technically everything starting with the Second Urbanization, roughly 600 BC onward. But we’ll only cover the part before the Common Era in this section. This is where we have the formalization of Sanskrit courtesy Panini and a whole new body of scripture called the _Sutras_. These writings can give us a fairly deep insight into all aspects of the gastronomical practices of the time. We know that meat, beef in particular, was starting to be frowned upon toward the later centuries of the Vedic Age. But did this evolve into full-blown vegetarianism across the board in the post-Vedic period? One would expect so, but then one comes across _Pāraskara Gṛhya Sūtra_ 1.19.7 thru 11 where meat is part of a newborn’s _annaprāśana_ ritual:35

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _bhāradvājyāmāṃsena vākprasārakāmasya
    kapiñjalamāṃsenā-annādyakāmasya
    matsyairjavanakāmasya
    kṛkaṣāyā āyuṣkāmasya
    āṭayā brahmavarcasakāmasya
    sarveḥ sarvakāmasya._
    “(He feeds the child) with flesh of (the bird called) Bhāradvājī, if he wishes (to the child) fluency of speech,
    With flesh of partridge, if abundance of nouṛṣment,
    With fish, if swiftness,
    (With flesh) of (the bird) Kṛkaṣā, if long life,
    (With flesh) of (the bird) Āṭi, if desirous of holy luster,
    With all, if desirous of all.” (trans. Oldenberg)

_Gṛhya Sūtra_ are works that provide detailed rules and procedures for domestic rituals, ceremonies, and _saṃskāras_ (rites of passage) within the household. They serve as practical manuals for conducting ceremonies such as birth, marriage, and death according to Vedic tradition. Various sages have authored their own Gṛhya Sūtra, the one by sage Pāraskara is what we’ve referenced above. The 19th _kaṇdika_ or the first _kaṇda_ deals in the ritual of annaprāśana, the ceremonial feeding of solid food to an infant for the first time. In both Indic and Western translations, the latter by Hermann Oldenberg,36 these verses enumerate birds and fish as foods, each believed to confer specific virtues upon the child.

Even if there’s a revulsion against meat in some quarters, it’s evidently not strong enough to be named a social taboo and certainly not universal.

Coeval with the Gṛhya Sūtra are the _Dharma Sūtra_ , works that codify the duties, ethical conduct, and legal obligations of individuals and communities. Part of this corpus is the one by sage Āpastamba. In PGS 1.19.7–11 we witnessed meat-eating at infancy. In ADS or _Āpastamba Dharma Sūtra_ 2.8.18.13 thru 15, we witness it at death:37

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _naiyamikaṃ tu śraddhaṃ snehavadeva dadyāt.
    sarpir māṃsamiti prathamaḥ kalpaḥ.
    abhāve tailaṃ śākamiti._
    “At the [monthly] sraddha which must necessarily be performed, he must use [food] mixed with fat.
    The first [and preferable] alternative [is to employ] clarified butter and meat.
    On failure [of these], oil of sesame, vegetables and [similar materials may be used].” (trans. Pandeya)

Here, meat is not merely permitted or tolerated but is explicitly ranked as the foremost choice in the hierarchy of foods for the śrāddha ritual. All non-meat items come secondary, further cementing the status of meat as a more-than-acceptable part of the Indic diet.

But this is far from the only endorsement of meat in ADS. Verses 2.7.16.25–27 go even further and straight-up recommend bovine meat with a higher preference to buffalo than to cow:38

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _saṃvatsaraṃ gavyena prītiḥ.
    bhūyāṃsamato māhiṣeṇa.
    etena grāmya araṇyānāṃ paśūnāṃ māṃsaṃ medhyaṃ vyākhyātam._
    “Beef satisfies [the Manes] for a year.
    Buffalo’s [meat] for a longer [time] than that.
    By this [permission of the use of buffalo’s meat] it has been declared that the meat of [other] tame and wild animals is fit to be offered.” (trans. Pandeya)

This too is in the context of śrāddha. The very next _khaṇḍa_ opens with three verses that recommend even more exotic meat such as those of rhinoceros and cranes:39

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _khaḍgopastaraṇe khaḍgamāṃsena-ānantyaṃ kālam.
    tathā śatabalermatsyasya māṃsena.
    vārdhrāṇasasya ca._
    “[If] rhinoceros’ meat [is fed to Brahmanas seated] on [seats covered with] the skin of a rhinoceros, [the Manes are satisfied] for a very long time.
    [The same effect is obtained] by [offering the] flesh [of the fish called] śatabali,
    And by [offering the] meat of the [crane called] Vdrdhrdnasa.” (trans. Pandeya)

In the Dharma Sūtra of Vasishtha, an entire section deals with the subject of forbidden food and in that section, we have a long list of animals that _can_ be eaten. VDS 14.39, for instance, allows one to eat porcupine, hedgehog, hare, tortoise, and monitor lizard, creatures we typically no longer consume as food.40 14.40 makes the unusual allowance of domestic animals possessing teeth in only one jaw, with the equally curious exception of camels. The most curious ruling is on the bovines. while 14.43 and 45 expressly prohibit most bovines including milch cows, draught oxen, and young calves, 14.46 references Late Vedic works, Vajasayeni in particular, to contradict the same and allow meat of milch cows and draught oxen as “pure.”41

There are several other endorsements of carnivory throughout the Sūtra corpus, but even these examples suffice to demonstrate that our post-Vedic forebears not only consumed meat as part of their regular diet but also drew from a far wider range of animal sources than is common today.

Beyond textual evidence, archaeological remains also attest to meat consumption. While such evidence cannot specify which communities consumed meat, it clearly confirms that meat was part of the diet. A notable example comes from Hastinapur, where excavations are categorized into numbered “periods” extending back to pre-Vedic times with intervening gaps. Period I spans approximately 1400 to 1200 BC, Period II corresponds to the Painted Gray Ware horizon from roughly 1100 to 800 BC, Period III to the Northern Black Polished Ware phase from around 600 to 250 BC, and Period IV extends from about 180 BC to 220 AD, with subsequent periods following accordingly.

In the archaeological report published by the ASI in 1985, the section on Hastinapur by the renowned archaeologist B. B. Lal provides detailed accounts of animal remains from all periods. This includes an enormous corpus of zebu (the domestic humped cattle of India) remains majorly from Periods II and III, that is both Vedic and post-Vedic times.42 Then we also have buffalos43, sheep44, goats,45 pigs,46 and swamp deer or _barasinga_.47 As with the zebu, an overwhelming majority of these remains are from the 1100–250 BC window. Lal makes it amply clear, with reference to the nature of cut marks and signs of charring, that these remains are from animals killed specifically for food and not perished of natural causes.48

It can now be safely concluded that meat was as ubiquitous in the Hindu diet at the very least through the dawn of the Common Era. But in the interest of thoroughness, we should also look into the epics. Two works form the fulcrum of popular Hindu imagination—the Ramayana and the Mahabharata. Since both took shape in the post-Vedic world, well before the Common Era, we should expect them to bear out ample references to meat-eating if it indeed formed a prominent feature of the Aryan diet.

## The Epics

As Bhishma lay awaiting death in the battlefield, he delivered to Yudhishthira a set of instructions on morality and ethics. These sermons form the Mahabharata’s thirteenth book, the _Anushasana Parva_. In most traditional editions (critical editions like the BORI edition), chapter 117 of this _parva_ deals with the duties of kings and rulers, emphasizing the importance of justice, protection of subjects, and adherence to dharma even under political or military pressures. Of interest to our discussion are two lines in this very chapter, verse 13.117.16:4950

This is a mighty strong endorsement, even if conditional, coming from a man of Bhishma’s stature.

Interestingly, Bhishma accords a greater significance to the rhino—an animal we no longer eat—than all other animals. Equally interesting is his assignment of higher value to ox beef than even dairy. But the Mahabharata is replete with meat references even outside the Anushasana Parva.

In _Sabha Parva_ 45.9, Dhritarashtra asks his son why he’s growing leaner despite enjoying all regal luxuries. Among the luxuries he lists, is rice cooked with meat.52

Biryani? Doesn’t matter. All that does is that Duryodhana consumed meat. The word for meat here is _piśita_ ,53 which comes from the same root _piś_ that gives us the flesh-eating demonic being called _piśaca_. But he was a Kshatriya and to many, a meat-eating Kshatriya would hardly sound exceptional. What about the Brahmins? Did they consume meat?

The most abundant reference to meat-eating can be found in the third book of the epic, the _Vana Parva_ , also known, less commonly, as _Aranyaka Parva_. Verse 47.7 is of special significance as one where we find the most irrefutable evidence of carnivory among Brahmins:

This is when the Pandavas were on their thirteen years of incognito exile. Yudhishthira feeds the Brahmins all meats fit for consumption including black deer. This description of a crowded butchery is a reasonable indicator of meat’s ubiquity in the common man’s diet. It’s possible that meat-eaters were more likely to be non-Brahmins than Brahmins, but its status as taboo in the Hindu society already stands on shaky grounds, if at all. Rantideva is known for his generosity toward his subjects and his devotion to Vishnu. The verses above indicate that a vast number of animals were slaughtered in his kitchen throughout the year. Although it does not specify whether the recipients included Brahmins or whether the animals comprised bovines, it still attests to the prominence of meat in the Aryan diet during the Mahabharata.

The very existence of a list of animals prohibited as food implies that all others were permitted. While the list encompasses a range of creatures—from certain fishes and tortoises to all carnivorous animals—it notably omits others such as chickens, sparrows, pigeons, and herbivorous beasts. What’s striking about this list is that it’s expressly meant for Brahmins. Thus, even the most restrictive _varṇa_ does not have a wholesale ban on meat.

There are a number of other verses throughout the Mahabharata that speak of meat as food. Some prohibit its consumption outside of ritualistic sacrifices, others don’t. Some allow all kinds, others permit a more restrictive list. Some allow it without exception, others allow it only under extreme circumstances. This can be understood as the result of the work being a culmination of several layers accreted over centuries with contributions from multiple authors. But we’re still left with the other epic, the _Ramayana_. Understood to be older than the Mahabharata, this work is also commonly assumed to be more restrictive on dietary and other habits. But is it?

One of the most widely known episodes of the Ramayana is where Rama killed Vali in an ambush. What isn’t as widely known is the conversation between the two that followed. A dying Vali condemns his assailant for deceit, Rama’s response to which forms the 18th hymn of _Kishkindha Kanda_. Of our interest are the verses 4.18.38 thru 41:60

When the trio—Rama, Lakshmana, and Sita—set off on their fourteen-year exile, one of their first stations is the northern bank of the Ganges. The meaning is “portion” without regards for its nature. But some translators, uneasy with the idea of a meat-eating Rama, reinterpret it as plant food.     “There did Sriramachandra and Lakshmana, both brethren, hunt four great wild beasts—ṛśya, the white-footed antelope; pṛṣata, the spotted deer; varaha, the wild boar; and ruru, a mighty deer with black stripes. Here, Chaturvedi interprets maṃsa as _kanda-mūla phalādi_ , but there’s no word in the original verse that supports such reading. In fact, it’d be absurd for the princes to kill so many animals and then go back to eating fruits. That’d mean the animals were killed for mere amusement, an act highly unbecoming of someone of Rama’s moral stature. A rather strong indicator of Rama’s carnivory is in Chaturvedi’s own translation of verse 36.41 of the _Sundara Kanda_ where Hanuman is speaking with Sita as Rama’s messenger in Aśoka Vatika:

     _na māṅsaṅ rāghavō bhuṅaktē na cā.pi madhu sēvatē.

    “Sriramachandra ji hath renounced the eating of flesh and the drinking of wine. He abideth ever as a forest-dwelling ascetic, partaking of the fruits and roots that grow in the wild; and on the third day he consumeth food fit for sustaining the body.” (trans. Chaturvedi)

Rama, informs Hanuman, has given up meat and wine in grief over Sita’s abduction, consuming only the bare minimum of vegetarian fare to sustain life. The fact that Hanuman specifies Rama’s abstinence from meat and wine to his own wife is a strong indicator of this not being routine, but a temporary state of affairs triggered by grief. Also of significance is that the abstinence is informed by grief and not asceticism. In sum, Rama, like other Kshatriyas, _did_ consume meat under happier circumstances.

When Bharadwaja learns that prince’s army is waiting at a distance while he has come alone to the hermitage, he orders that the soldiers be brought in so he may extend proper hospitality to them. Thereafter, the sage invokes Vishvakarma to miraculously arrange a magnificent space, complete with abundant food, wine, and women, to receive his royal guests. Part of this arrangement, as we learn in 2.91.67, is an abundance of mutton and pork:64

     _rajasā dhvastakēśō vā naraḥ kaścidadṛśyata,
    ājaiścāpi ca vārāhairniṣṭhānavarasañcayaiḥ._
    “...nor could one behold even a single man with dusty and disheveled hair. There with heaps of the flesh of goats and boars, and of sundry other excellent viands...” (trans. Chaturvedi)

Continuing the theme, 2.91.71 adds deer, chicken, and even peacock to the list of meats:65

     _prataptapiṭharaiścāpi mārgamāyūrakaukkuṭaiḥ,

    “There were heaps of the flesh of deer, peacocks, and chickens, well-cooked and cleanly dressed. There were thousands of vessels for holding rice, and hundreds of thousands of platters for keeping viands.” (trans. Chaturvedi)

Clearly, meat was no taboo in the royal households of Ramayana. This is further reinforced by the fact that even though the one consuming is a Kshatriya, the one serving is a Brahmin. An ascetic no less. If a Brahmin sage has no qualms serving meat to his guests, if Rama has no qualms partaking in meat except when grieving, is the injunction against the item really that integral to the Hindu tradition?

Once again, it’s not that meat is encouraged or tolerated across the board. There do exist instances where it’s forbidden or at least frowned upon, just as there exist those that accept it without a fuss. We witnessed this diversity in the Mahabharata and in the entire Vedic corpus as well. At this point, it should be safe to assume that any further enumeration of meat-related verses from the Ramayana would be pretty much redundant, for the point that needed to be made, has already been made. With abundant clarity.

## Conclusion

It can now be conclusively stated that meat consumption was an established and widely accepted feature of the Hindu diet at least up to the dawn of the Common Era. Both textual and archaeological evidence—from the Rigveda and later Vedic texts to the Dharma Sūtras, Gṛhya Sūtras, and epic literature—affirm that meat was not only consumed but often ritually sanctioned, with certain animals accorded specific roles in sacrificial and domestic ceremonies. While beef eventually became taboo in many contexts, other meats remained permissible, and even prestigious, across large swathes of society. The Hindu dietary tradition, therefore, has never been monolithic—it accommodated both meat-eating and abstention, with social, ritual, and regional factors shaping practices. Meat-eating, when aligned with dharmic principles or ritual norms, was entirely compatible with Hindu identity.
