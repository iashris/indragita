# The Many Resurrections of Pataliputra

Just as the human heart sits not in the center but slightly to the left, ancient India’s heart sat not in the center of its Gangetic expanse but slightly to its left, to the East. This heart, contrary to popular imagination was not Hastinapur, Mathura, Varanasi, or Ayodhya, but a name that barely inspires awe today—Patna. Then, Pataliputra. From its most modest origins as a clump of shanties amid a flowering grove to its zenith as the imperial capital of the Mauryas, and eventually to its rebirth as a sacred pilgrimage site, Pataliputra’s story is one of perpetual reinvention. It isn’t just bricks and battles, it’s a window into the very soul of ancient India, revealing how geography, politics, commerce, and spirituality came together to shape one of the world’s oldest continuously inhabited regions.

What makes Pataliputra so captivating? Imagine a metropolis that hosted philosophers like Buddha, emperors like Ashoka, and attracted invaders from faraway Greece and Persia, only to fade into ruins before rising again as a spiritual lighthouse. In an era where history often feels dry and distant, Pataliputra’s tale pulses with drama—prophetic floods foretold by the Buddha himself, romantic legends of flying lovers founding cities, and the quiet migrations of scholars fleeing decay. Yet, this is no fairy tale, it’s grounded in inscriptions, excavations, and ancient texts that paint a vivid, verifiable picture.

As we dive into this longform exploration, we’ll trace Pataliputra’s journey from a strategic outpost to a bustling urban powerhouse, through periods of unparalleled prosperity under dynasties like the Mauryas and Guptas, and into its decline amid natural disasters and political intrigues. We’ll examine the forces that led to its de-urbanization, the emigration of its inhabitants, and its ultimate sanctification as a tirtha, a holy place drawing pilgrims across centuries. By the end, you’ll see why Pataliputra isn’t just a footnote in history books but a living legacy that continues to influence Bihar’s cultural landscape today. Buckle up for a journey through time that uncovers the metamorphosis of a city that refused to die even in decay.

## The Flowers of Confluence

It’s extremely rare for a river to be masculine in Sanskrit, thanks to the general Indic tradition of river goddesses. One such rarity is Son, _Śо̄ṇa_ in Sanskrit. Originating deep in the jungles of Amarkantak in Chhattisgarh, this river runs east for a few hundred miles before turning north to ultimately pour into the Ganges not far from Maner in Bihar. At this confluence of the masculine and the feminine was once a pocket of beautiful pink vegetation still common in the region—the trumpet flower. In the neighboring Bengal they call it _pārul_ , but here, in Sanskrit, it was _pāṭalī_. Botanists now assign it the name _Stereospermum chelonoides_. More than two thousand years ago, perhaps three, a tiny unassuming settlement took shape among these groves. Nestled in the angle between two large perennial rivers, this hamlet was destined for greatness right from the beginning. They called it Pataligrama, or “the village of pātalī.”

This wasn’t a unique ecosystem in this regard. So far as we know, the flower has lent its name to at least two other cities, both in the angle of two water bodies. There’s a Pāṭala near Kannauj at the confluence of Ramganga and Ganga. And there’s a Patala in a faraway Sindh where the Indus meets the sea. Of the three, only Pataligrama survives to this day, albeit with a very different name. This straggling gentrification also came to acquire alternative names such as Pādalipura, or the “pādali town.” _Padali_ was just a local Pali corruption of Sanskrit pātali. But men have settled lands by the river since the beginning of time. What makes this one so special?

When we speak of Pataliputra’s origins, we speak of the mid-first millennium BC, give or take. By this point, the Aryans had long established domain in the region. Not just established domain but even developed a distinct social and spiritual character of their own. The Aryans of the East and the Aryans of the West had significantly diverged in not only liturgical practices but also their philosophies. While the West was dominated by Vedic practices, the East had absorbed a great deal of local philosophies collectively referred to as _shramana_. It’s here that the ideas of karma and moksha made their debut in the Hindu thought in a brand-new body of scripture called the Upanishads. The East-West divide most famously manifests in the Brihadaranyaka-Chandogya rivalry.1 These are the oldest Upanishads known to us and comfortably predate both Siddhartha Gautama and Mahavira, the two most prominent shramanic figures on record. _Brihadaranyaka Upanishad_ originated in Videha whereas _Chandogya_ originated in Kuru-Panchal, and both rejected each other’s authority on philosophical grounds.

And it is this shramanic substrate that gives us our first mention of the village in question. The _Mahaparinibbanasutta_ , a pivotal work of the Pali canon, recounts the Buddha’s visit to the site, where he beheld a fort under construction by order of Ajatasatru, the formidable ruler of the Haryanka dynasty. Impressed by the engineering feat, Buddha nonetheless prophesied ruin through flood, feud, and fire—a foretelling that would haunt the city’s history centuries after.

But why _-putra_? Was it a mere etymological corruption of _-pura_ or “town”? This is where our investigation leaves history and enters the realm of mythology, because historical records are so scant. An eleventh century work attributes it to a Brahmin named Putraka in an expectedly fanciful anecdote. This work is none other than Somadeva’s _Kathasaritasagara_.

So the story goes that once upon a time there lived in Kanakhala, a Brahmin from the Deccan whose name we do not know. This Brahmin and his wife were there to perform austerities on the banks of the Ganges. Kanakhala is today identified with Haridwar, or at least thereabouts. As a result of these austerities, the couple was blessed with three sons. As they grew older, the three brothers went to the Magadhan capital of Rajagriha, today’s Rajgir, for higher learnings. Education completed, they headed back to the Deccan where they married three Brahmin sisters. But soon calamity struck. A famine, brought on by drought, drove the brothers to abandon their wives, one of whom was pregnant. The three women, destitute, sought shelter with Yajñadatta, a family friend, where the middle sister gave birth to a boy named Putraka. Raised with shared love by the three, the child became the object of divine favor. Shiva, moved by their devotion, decreed that each morning a hundred thousand gold coins would be found beneath his pillow. Thus began Putraka’s ascent to wealth and kingship.2

Though fortune favored him, treachery soon followed. Putraka’s own father and uncles, envious of his rise, plotted his death. Lured to a shrine under false pretenses, he narrowly escaped assassins and, keeping his word to remain silent, vanished into the Vindhyan wilderness. There, chance brought him upon two Asura brothers locked in combat over three magical treasures—a pair of shoes that could fly, a staff that made visions real, and a pot that produced food at will. With cunning, Putraka tricked them into a race, seized the prizes, and soared away through the skies.

Disguised in a humble dwelling, he lived in secret until word reached him of Pātalī, daughter of King Mahendravarman, kept like a jewel in her father’s guarded palace. Love struck swiftly. With the aid of his enchanted shoes, he entered her chamber by night and sealed their union in secrecy. When discovered, Putraka carried Pātalī off in flight, and together they descended by the Ganges. There, to soothe his bride, he used the staff to sketch upon the earth the outlines of a city. In an instant, towers and battlements rose, armies gathered, and life stirred in the streets. From Pātalī and Putraka it took its name, Pāṭaliputra—a city born of magic, destined to be the seat of wealth, politics, and learning.3

This story, although mythological, warrants attention to a curious detail about it. Somadeva was a Kashmiri Brahmin. In his work, Putraka’s story is narrated by Vararuchi, who is typically associated with Kerala. Putraka’s grandparents are from the Deccan, and his parents are born in Haridwar. Putraka himself is born in the Deccan but establishes a city way out in Magadha or Bihar. Few Indic works outside of the epics embody such geographical and cultural range. Almost as if designed for a national integration message. By the way, no Mahendravarman has ever ruled in Magadha although the Pallavas of the South, whose reign had folded a few centuries earlier, did have at least two kings bearing that name. But fantasy aside, when did humans really gentrify Pataliputra?

While it’s hard to assert with any amount of certainty when humans came to inhabit any given area (hunter-gatherers could have been there for thousands of years leaving behind little in the way of material remains), the finding of artifacts identified with the Northern Black Polished Ware confirms that the region was supporting a thriving human society at least as far back as 600 BC.4 NBPW is associated with what we now call India’s Second Urbanization which saw the rise of major ancient metropolises such as Kaushambi and Ujjain. This is the Late Iron Age where agriculture has already matured, and _janapadas_ are starting to coalesce into _mahajanapadas_ , the precursor to empires. India had sixteen of those, Magadha being one. But how did this unassuming clump of hutments come to be the linchpin of ancient India’s political landscape?

## Haryanka: From Village to Outpost

Demonyms typically derive from toponyms. The opposite has often been the case with ancient India. That’s how from the Bharata tribe we got Bharat and from the Angas, Anga. In the same vein, Magadha was an eastern people named in the later books of the Vedas after whom the region got its name. For the longest time, its capital was in what’s today, Rajgir. It’s in the middle of the sixth century BC that the region steps out of the realm of legends and enters verifiable history, even if only circumstantially so, with the emergence of the Haryanka dynasty.

The first two rulers of this dynasty, Bimbisara and Ajatashatru, were both based in Rajgir, although the latter did feel the need to have an alternative. Ajatashatru, a figure of Shakespearean intrigue, infamous for India’s first recorded case of patricide, faced immense threat from the powerful Licchavi confederacy across the Ganges. To counter this, he needed a frontier outpost that could keep trouble as far away from the capital as possible. For this, he chose Pataligrama. This is the same Ajatashatru whom the Buddha had made his grim prophecy to in the anecdote above. To understand the mechanics of this decision, we’ll rewind to the dynasty’s founder Bimbisara, generally placed between 544 and 493 BC. So there’s the Magadhas and there’s the Angas, and the two, as it happens, do not like each other very much. In today’s terms, Magadha is roughly Bihar and Anga, Jharkhand. Yes, they are separate today and they were separate way back. Until Bimbisara invaded it and annexed it into Magadha, forming what we sometimes call Greater Magadha. That’s how Bihar and Jharkhand ended up as a single unit until their separation toward the end of the last century.

Bimbisara also forged a number of alliances through marriage, the most notable being the one with Kosaladevi which brought him Kashi in dowry and helped secure his western frontiers. Another equally notable alliance was with the Licchavis whose chief’s daughter would become Ajatashatru’s mother. The Licchavis were also the head of the Vajjika confederacy to the north. This, and not Athens, is where democracy was born. Bimbisara also married the princess of Madra, roughly today’s Punjab, although this isn’t emphasized much in records.

Why Ajatashatru killed his father isn’t very clear. Some Buddhist traditions blame the Buddha’s cousin Devadatta for instigating him, Jain traditions place it squarely on the prince himself. Ceylonese Buddhist sources like Mahavamsha, on the other hand, soften the blow by calling it a willful abdication on Bimbisara’s part. At any rate, Ajatashatru became king. Whether he forced his father out or straight up killed him, one thing that every source agrees upon is that this event soured his relationship with his maternal relatives, the Licchavis. This was around 492 BC. Although Magadha was already a rising hegemon in the East by this time, the renewed threat from the Licchavis to the North of the Ganges was something to reckon with. It’s under these circumstances that the need for a frontier town as a defensive launchpad against the northern adversaries was felt.

To that end Ajatashatru identified the village of Patali as the site and dispatched trusted ministers Sunidha and Vassakara to erect a fort, a move immortalized in Buddhist suttas. Soon, Pataligrama was a bustling military outpost complete with cyclopean fortifications and all else that comes with being named a military outpost. This wasn’t mere defense, it was offense cloaked in strategy, positioning Magadha to strike at Vajji, the Licchavi-led alliance of eight clans.

The commercial allure amplified this military edge. Situated at the Ganga-Sona confluence, Pataligrama commanded trade routes linking the northwest to the east, from Taxila’s markets to Tamralipti’s ports. Goods like iron, textiles, and spices flowed through, enriching local coffers. Ajatasatru’s conquests—Vajji, Malla (a mountainous monarchy north of Vajji), and Kasi (gifted by Prasenajit upon his sister’s marriage)—swelled Magadha’s domain, making remote Rajagriha untenable for administration. This conquest also drove the Licchavis soundly out of power and, barring a very few, out of country. Some fled to Nepal, others to Tibet. But they would return, and we’ll meet them when they do.

The next big upgrade for Pataligrama came on the watch of the next dynast, Udayin. Rajgir’s biggest advantage was its hilly environs. It was also its biggest disadvantage. Situated in a valley surrounded by five hills, this was the best naturally fortified town for its time. But that also made it completely landlocked and disconnected from the major riparian trade routes crossing Magadha, mainly the Ganges. Pataligrama, on the other hand, was smack dab on a riparian superhighway connected to both East and West. It made perfect sense as the imperial seat. What also made this a bigger imperative under Udayin than ever before was his conquest and annexation of Avanti. Besides, Magadha was now a sprawling imperial powerhouse that encompassed not just Anga and Kashi but also the perpetual thorn in its side, the eight territories of the Vajjika confederacy.

## Maurya: The Zenith of Power

With a brief Shaishunaga interlude, the Haryanka reign was succeeded by the better recognized Nanda dynasty. Although this too lasted a mere two generations, it remains embedded in the popular conscience as the precursor to the Mauryas. While the Shaishunagas mostly ruled from Vaishali and Rajgir, Pataliputra remained the seat of the Nanda monarchy throughout its brief tenure. At which point Pataligrama became Pataliputra is hard to pinpoint with precision, but Pataliputra it was in all records by now. When Alexander made his bid on the subcontinent, his scribes noted “Palibothra” as the capital of the realm to the East of the Hydaspes or Jhelum. Which makes Magadha the largest empire of the time on the Indian subcontinent. But this was still not its peak.

Chandragupta inaugurated the Mauryan regime around 320 BC. By the time of his death, thanks to the shared western frontier with the erstwhile Achaemenids and Chandragupta’s engagements with the Seleucids, there was already a healthy influx of Persians and Greeks in the Magadhan capital. Megasthenes, the Seleucid ambassador at the Magadhan court, described _Palimbothra_ as a sprawling metropolis, 80 stadia (about 9.2 miles) long, 15 stadia (about 1.7 miles) wide, girded by a moat 600 feet broad and 30 cubits deep, with a rampart boasting 570 towers and 64 gates, situated at the confluence of the Ganges and Erannoboas or Son.5 His awe at its prosperity underscores the early development’s success. Though the Son has since receded, these ancient accounts paint a vibrant hub. In his accounts, Palimbothra was a city of immense prosperity and grandeur.

Under his grandson Aśoka, Magadha became a continental behemoth that rivaled Alexander’s domain at its peak. Governing an entity this large is a logistical and administrative nightmare even today. That’s why we compartmentalize the task by dividing the whole into multiple smaller units called states or provinces. We call it federation. Aśoka needed to innovate if he were to continue keeping his handle on his domain. That’s where two big imports from his western neighbors came in handy—federative administration and inscriptions. The Greeks had learned these from the Achaemenids and Aśoka picked it from the Greeks. The latter called their subdivisions _satrapies_ , a derivative of Old Persian _satrap_ , itself cognates with Sanskrit _kṣatrapa_. This is the same etymology that gives us more familiar terms like _kṣetra_ and _kṣatriya_. Communication was the other crucial piese of the equation which was famously solved by his rock and pillar edicts. This idea too was a direct import from the Persians. Long ago, it was an Achaemenid inscription that mentioned, for the first time, the name Hidush which would later become Hindu and Hindustan.

While it’s common knowledge that Aśokan inscriptions were trilingual given the racial diversity of his empire, a much lesser-known fact is that a specimen also exists bearing only Aramaic and nothing else. It’s a tablet that was found in a place called Pul-i-Darunteh in the Laghman valley of Afghanistan, not far from the Pakistan border today. This is one of the oldest epigraphic references to Pataliputra as the capital of Magadha. 

> As an interesting aside, Aramaic is the language said to be spoken by Jesus although he also must’ve been proficient in Koine Greek and Hebrew.

By the time of Aśoka’s death, Pataliputra was a commercial nerve center connected not only to the Hindu Kush in the Northwest and the Bay of Bengal in the East, but also Bharuch on the Arabian Sea in the West and even trade routes that went South across the Vindhyas. This would prove to be the pinnacle of Pataliputra’s glory, for after Aśoka the empire entered a slow by steady phase of decline. The western reaches, including Gandhāra and Arachosia, broke away quickly, some falling under Indo-Greek control. The ruler at Pataliputra now controlled a fraction of the empire his predecessors lorded over. Having said that, Pataliputra itself retained its spot as the subcontinent’s economic and geopolitical hub.

## Shunga: The Turbulent Interlude

The Mauryan rule folded with the assassination of its last dynast Bṛhadratha. The Assassin was none other than his own general, Pushyamitra Shunga. This was 185 BC. The dynasty had a total of four kings before finally being supplanted by the Kanvas. Pushyamitra presided over a troubled domain. The Greeks had long eyed Pataliputra and the Mauryan decline following Aśoka’s death offered them an irresistible window of opportunity to act. While they didn’t do much through the decline, a nascent Shunga rule was too tempting an opportunity to pass on. The Greeks had already established themselves over the northwestern part of the subcontinent by now and the man on the throne was Menander I or Milinda. Pataliputra at the time was littered with Buddhist monasteries and institutions and Pushyamitra did not like the religion very much, although later accounts of systematic Buddhist persecution on his watch are exaggerations if not outright fabrications. This is what made the opportunity all the more tempting. Milinda was a Buddhist convert and was placed well to position himself as the savior of Pataliputra’s _sanghas_ from the tyranny of their king. Counting on their help, he invaded the capital roughly around 155 BC.

That the Greeks conquered Pataliputra, is borne out by both Greek sources like Appolodorus and Indian sources like the Yuga Purana.6 But conquering and holding are two very different things. No contemporary records confirm that Milinda extended his domain that far East. Given his own base being located in Sagala, today’s Sialkot, it would have been an insurmountable challenge to hold Magadha anyway. While he was successful in reaching the Ganges and projecting Indo-Greek power deep into India, the Shungas remained in power anyway. Therefore, it’s more likely that Pushyamitra and his successor negotiated, paid tribute, or reached some kind of truce that persuaded Menander to withdraw. But why? Why can’t we entertain the possibility that the Shungas just fair-and-square defeated the Greeks, or Yavanas as they were called in India thanks to their Ionian origins, and humbled Menander? There’s a very strong archaeological reason for that.

Menander’s forces did not just invade Pataliputra, they utterly decimated it. They set it on fire. Relying on Megasthenes’ observations, Greek historian Arrian would later write of Pataliputra being enclosed by a wooden palisade, pierced with loopholes for archery. If true, it’s not hard to imagine it being so susceptible to fire from an invading army. The reason this isn’t mere speculation is because in 1912, D. B. Spooner, an American archaeologist working with the Archaeological Survey of India, began excavating the ancient mounds of Pataliputra. At Kumhrar, he came upon the most striking testimony of the city’s destruction—a twelve-inch layer of charcoal and ash running beneath later Gupta remains.7 Scattered through this stratum lay innumerable fragments of the Mauryan pillars, once polished to a mirror finish and arranged in precise rows across a great hall.8 The wooden superstructure that had supported them had clearly perished in fire, bringing the pillars down into the softened soil. That’s how Spooner concluded that the city’s monumental heart had been consumed in a conflagration, its wooden defenses and timbered halls making it fatally vulnerable to an invader’s torch.

The Buddha’s grim prophecy made to Ajatashatru had finally come true.

After such a devastating blow, it’s hard to imagine a victorious Shunga army driving off a humbled Greek force. A vassalized Magadha makes more sense instead. The Greeks and the Shungas also exchanged diplomatic missions over later years, as is told to us in the famous Heliodorus pillar of Vidisha in Madhya Pradesh. This state of affairs lasted a good eighty-odd years before it finally gave in to the Kanvas. It might amuse to know that history came a full circle 73 BC when the Pushyamitra’s grandson, the third Shunga dynast, came to be assassinated by his own _amatya_ or minister, Vasudeva Kanva, who then founded an even shorter-lived dynasty than the Shungas. Do recall that Pushyamitra himself had acquired the throne after a similar assassination of his own Mauryan employer.

The Kanva reign is not noted for being particularly peaceful, for it is during this brief window that Magadha faced the landmark invasion that gave us recorded history’s first mention of the name Bharatavarsha and the first material evidence of its geographical confines at the time. Aśoka had once infamously ravaged Kalinga, today’s Orissa, an event erroneously associated with his conversion to Buddhism. More than two centuries later, Kalinga got its revenge.

It was either during the reign of Vasudeva or his son Bhumimitra when Kalinga invaded Magadha under its new Jain king Kharavela. History records two invasions separated by three years. Although Kharavela may not have gone as far as Pataliputra, he did succeed in taking Anga. An inscription commemorating his Magadhan campaigns and reign in general still sits in the Hathigumpha cave near Bhubaneshwar. In this seventeen-line inscription in Prakrit, there’s a mention of Kharavela sending his armies to conquer “Bharatavarsha” implying unambiguously that at least at the dawn of the Common Era, the supernational entity of Bharat did not include Kalinga and was most likely confined to the Gangetic plains, with Magadha as its biggest constituent. At any rate, the Kanva reign came to an end around 30 BC.

## Kushan: The Storm from Xinjiang

Interestingly, the Greek rule ended in mainland Greece with the Roman conquest of Corinth in 146 BC, in the Middle East with the Roman annexation of Syria in 63 BC, and in Egypt with the death of Cleopatra in 30 BC, So from 30 BC to about 10 AD was a forty-year window when there was only one Greek-ruled polity in existence and it was not in Greece but in India. But while the Greeks could never manage to directly rule Magadha, another invading force did.

Besides mainland Greece, Egypt, and the Middle East, the Greeks also ruled over a kingdom in what’s today Afghanistan, parts of Central Asia, and northwestern India. It’s this entity, recorded as the Graeco-Bactrian kingdom, that later spawned the Indo-Greek domain. The end of the Graeco-Bactrian polity came in 30 AD when a man named Kujula Kadphises brought together five aristocratic Yuezhi clans into a single political unit strong enough to overthrow the Greek rulers in Bactria and placed himself on the throne. The Yuezhi were an Indo-European people who originated in northwest China’s Gansu and Xinjiang regions. One of the five aristocratic clans was the clan of Guishuang to which Kujula himself belonged. We recognize it better as the Kushans.

It’s Kujula’s great-grandson Kanishka who finally brought Magadha into the Kushana domain. This was in the second century AD. By this time, the Kanvas were long gone, and the region was under various poorly attested Magadhan rulers. The Puranas mention that the Andhras or Satavahanas succeeded the Kanvas and exercised power in northern India as well, though their direct control over Magadha is debated. By Kanishka’s time Magadha was no longer the imperial heartland and political powerhouse it had been under the Mauryas. Instead, it was a provincial prize contested by stronger powers. That Kanishka made successful expeditions against Pataliputra is something both Chinese and Tibetan sources affirm. Numismatic evidence—copper coins found in Pataliputra bearing the name of Kanishka’s father Vima Kadphises—leads some to suggest that the region might have fallen under Kushana influence even before Kanishka, but the coins could also be a result of trade with the Kushans.

More assertive evidence comes in the shape of a 185 AD Kushana inscription from Kailvan bearing a date in the Kanishka or Saka era. Kailvan, a village in the Nalanda district of Bihar, lies about forty miles from Patna. Although Kanishka annexed Bihar, his capital remained in Purushapura, today’s Peshawar.9 And distance hurts governance. The Kushana reign proved short-lived in Bihar and a series of rebellions soon uprooted them from the region for good.

Born in the first century AD in Pataliputra, a man named Supushpa is understood to have respawned a dynasty long lost to its adversaries when Magadha was still in its nascent years. This was the Licchavi dynasty. Its last known dynast was defeated and driven out of his homeland by Ajatashatru almost half a millennium before. We don’t know for sure if Supushpa ever got to rule over Magadha or even is erstwhile dynastic seat across the Ganges from Pataliputra, but his successors sure built a lasting line of monarchy in Nepal. We know this from an eighth-century Nepalese inscription by a Licchavi ruler named Jayadeva II.

After the Kushans, number of petty chieftains ruled various parts of Magadha without a central authority or capital. Pataliputra witnessed continued decline over the following centuries, in both political as well as commercial significance. In fact, ever since it was burned to the ground by the Greeks some 400 years before. When Pushyamitra Shunga took the throne, his _ashvamedha_ ceremony was officiated by Patanjali himself. Even as the decline was already in motion, the saint had called Pataliputra the most intellectually eminent city in contemporary Bharat. Just a few centuries down the line, it was now a sorry shade of its former glory. But this was all about to change now.

## Gupta: The Short-Lived Gilded Age

In the third quarter of the third century, somewhere along the Kashi–Prayaga–Kaushambi circuit ruled a local chieftain by the name Shrigupta, more likely an epithet than a proper name. His grandson Chandragupta I later assumed the title of Maharajadhiraja and became the first monarch of what we now call the Gupta Empire. For this to happen, Chandragupta I forged a marital alliance with the Lichhavis by marrying their princess, and that brought Pataliputra within the Gupta sphere of influence. Could that mean that the Licchavis ruled Pataliputra in the wake of Kanva decline after all? We don’t have a definitive proof, but they sure would have exerted some kind of indirect authority over the city at the very least.

It’s Chandragupta’s son Samudragupta who finally made Pataliputra the imperial capital. This was Magadha’s moment to shine after centuries of unrest and political decay.

The era of the Guptas is often referred to as India’s “Golden Age” and not without merit. The lead-up to the Guptas was a period of significant social and religious upheaval. It was during this period that the Brahmanic creed started to emerge from the shadows of its Sramanic counterparts as a prominent doctrinal order. The scripture too underwent a dramatic shift with a whole new body of work called _Dharmashastras_ supplanting the prominence of earlier Upanishadic canon. Some scholars have called this the _shastrification_ of Hinduism. The most notable product of this process is the famous _Manusmriti_ although it’s just one of several Smritis produced in the early centuries of the Common Era. Another notable line of scripture that came out of this churn was the _Puranas_. Caste or varna lines now started to become more pronounced after having been relatively fluid for well over a millennium, endogamy became a bigger deal than before. Rigvedic deities like Indra and Varuna faded in the background as the likes of Vishnu and Shiva took the centerstage, although this process had started way back with the Upanishads and this was just the culmination of a 500-year-long shift. Some of India’s first temples emerged during this period as material iconography, a theme with origins in the Graeco-Kushana cultural injection, became increasingly mainstream. Although the Guptas granted enthusiastic royal patronage to all Indic traditions, Vaishnavism emerged as their mainstay.

And Pataliputra, even in its decline, was right at the heart of this _shastric_ codification of the Hindu doctrine. The Guptas played the definitive role in reviving the imperial luster of the decaying city. By the time of Chandragupta II, the city was already back to being somewhat of the metropolis that once led the Second Urbanization. When Fa-Hien of China visited his court in the early years of the fifth century, he noted a thriving urban center with wealthy, prosperous, and virtuous inhabitants.10 Though some scholars posit Prayaga or Ujjain as alternate Gupta seats, Fa-Hien’s silence on them affirms Pataliputra’s primacy. But this glory was not to last.

## Hun: The Shadow of Kaliyuga

As the century drew to a close, a brand-new force had entered the Indian northwest and steamrolled its way across the Gangetic plains with lightning speed. The polity traces its origins to the same Gansu province in China that once birthed the Kushans. These were the Huns. Just as Kujula Kadphises brought together the five Yuezhi clans under a single Kushana polity, Toramana brought together four or so disparate clans of a Hunnic branch known as the Alchons under a single banner in the middle of the fifth century AD. The Guptas, as we’ve already noted, were a Vaishnavite polity. The Huns brought a Shaivite prominence to the scene. But this was unlike all preceding invasions. This was destructive in a whole other way.

Toramana’s military juggernaut found little match in the Indian subcontinent and cities after cities fell to him like the proverbial houses of cards. Cities that refused submission suffered absolute and total decimation. Koshambi, for instance, was burned to the ground and never recovered from its ashes. Pataliputra too fell to him for a brief window although the hold was short-lived before the city slipped back out of Toramana’s control. This was the Alchon branch, one of several such as Kidarites, Hephthalites, and others. These branches all sprang from Central Asia and China and advanced in different directions.

* * *

* * *

While the Alchons headed to India, another group went West and entered Europe almost simultaneously. The most recognized figure of this line is Attila. Shortly before Toramana sacked the Gangetic plains in India, Attila sacked the heart of Europe. His horsemen swept across the Danube frontier, ravaging the Balkans and forcing the Eastern Roman emperors into humiliating treaties. He then turned westward, pressing into Gaul where his advance was finally checked in 451 AD at the Battle of the Catalaunian Plains by the Roman general Flavius Aetius allied with the Visigoths. The following year, Attila stormed into northern Italy, reducing Aquileia and other cities to ruins before withdrawing under mounting famine, disease, and diplomatic pressure.

Although Attila died in 453 AD, the disturbances he had created triggered an irreversible loss of Roman prominence ultimately leading to its end as an imperial power. The Fall of Rome is traditionally placed in 476 AD even though the process was a little less abrupt. This event had a profound impact on Pataliputra.

Trade with Rome was a major contributor to the Gupta economy and that of the Kushans before. As Rome declined, so did this trade. India was the world’s biggest economy alongside China and much of it was owed to the markets in Rome. With that gone, Magadhan economy weakened and as economy weakened, so did its military. This too contributed to its fall when Toramana’s forces arrived. The Huns lasted only two kings in India, Toramana and his son Mihirakula, but their impact proved outsized.

Mihirakula failed to maintain his hold on Pataliputra as he was stretched too thin having conquered too much too quickly. He would ultimately die in Kashmir thus ending his line. This wave of destruction spawned concepts like Kaliyuga and Kalapani.

Besides the Huns, climate too played a crucial role in Pataliputra’s decline. Having lost its industrial economy in the wake of Rome’s fall, Magadha became a largely agrarian economy. And agrarian economies depend heavily on climate. The Gangetic plains are notorious for their flood-famine cycles and now these cycles proved far more destructive to the economy than they did so far. When Hiuen Tsang visited Magadha in the seventh century, he recorded vivid accounts of flood-induced desolation and Mihirakula’s destruction in Pataliputra. His writings describe numerous buildings and properties ruined by frequent inundations, reflecting the city’s decline. By his time, Pataliputra had ceased to be the premier city of northern India, its place supplanted by Kannauj. In fact, Tsang even notes Kannauj-based Harsha as the “emperor of India” and explicitly notes Pataliputra as being in neglect and disrepair.11

Archaeological evidence corroborates this susceptibility. Excavations at Kumhrar indicate recurring inundations, while Waddell suggests that the broader region of Pataliputra may have been prone to vast flooding.12 Further indications of the city’s desertion in the late Gupta and post-Gupta periods are evident in excavation reports from Mahabir Ghat and two other nearby sites, underscoring the gradual abandonment of this once-magnificent urban center. The abandonment had an unexpected impact on the religious makeup on the subcontinent at large. Most citizens who emigrated out of Pataliputra and other Magadhan cities were Brahmins. As these Brahmin families settled in distant lands, they brought with them the seeds of Vaishnavism and to these new lands. Much of this happened to be to the South of the Vindhyas. An enduring proof of this cultural pollination is the city of Cuddalore in Tamil Nadu. Settled by Magadhan Brahmins in the wake of Hunnic invasions, the city was named Pataliputtiram before it became Cuddalore. Speaking of Cuddalore, the old kernel of the city still sometimes goes by the name Thirupathiripuliyur, the Tamil rendering of Pataliputtiram.13

Ironically, while Pataliputra’s name was being applied to new cities being established elsewhere, the city itself became so desolate that it came to be referred to as just Nagara or Shrinagara, a generic toponym. As Magadhan rulers lost their grip on the region, the Palas of Bengal moved in. When Dharmapala issued a land grant of four Pataliputra villages in the eighth century, his plates referred to Pataliputra not as an imperial city but a mere camp. Several seals from Nalanda refer to Pataliputra as just Nagara. Similarly, when Dharmapala’s son Devapala granted parts of Pataliputra to the Brahmins in an attempt to rejuvenate it as a Shaivite _tirtha_ , he refers to the place as Shrinagara. We can see this reference in his Munger Plate.

The exodus lasted centuries despite all attempts to rehabilitate the city by several rulers. Even as late as twelfth century, Brahmins of the region were emigrating to places like Kannauj and Gujarat and securing land grants from local rulers. Despite this depopulation, Pataliputra gradually reemerged as a sacred site or tirtha. Even during its darkest days of the sixth century, the city was being noted as a center of religious activity in sources like the Asanpat Nataraj inscription of Naga ruler Satrubhanja.14 The Guptas were already donating cows and gold to Hindu, Buddhist, and Jain institutions even in the face of devastating Hunnic invasions.

Between the eleventh and thirteenth centuries, Pataliputra further gained significance as a Shakta tirtha, centered around Patan Devi. The Pali term _pattana_ , meaning township, gave rise to the modern name Patna, and contemporary records refer to it with terms such as Pattana, Nagara, Mahapattana, and Mahanagara. Thus, after centuries of decline and emigration, Pataliputra underwent a process of religious sanctification, becoming a revered site for Shiva worship while supporting Jain, Buddhist, and Shakta practices, and emerging as a symbol of spiritual integration in early medieval northern India.

Such, however, was the desolation after the fall of the Guptas, that in the excavation site of Kumhrar signs of human resettlement have only been dated to the seventeenth century.15 That’s more than a thousand years of desolation. That’s not to say that people didn’t live there, they most probably did, given its status as a tirtha, but the gentrification must have been too sparse to reflect in material findings.

## Conclusion

Pataliputra’s journey from a nameless arboreal village to imperial colossus, through decline to sacred haven is a reminder that the city that barely registers attention in a conversation of India’s urban centers, was once the nursery of its philosophical, political, doctrinal, and military identity. This was no Hastinapur or Kashi, but this was the heart of India’s heart. Magadha was a late entrant to the urbanization game and was often looked down upon by the Kuru Panchala elites as lesser Aryans, but it not only held its sway, but also emerged as a power to reckon with both politically and intellectually. When the rest of India fell to invading powers, Magadha stood its ground for the most part as the subcontinent’s only hope of resilience. In modern times, Afghanistan is often called the “graveyard of empires.” In premodern times, if a region could claim the title, it has to be Pataliputra.
