Jean-Antoine Dubois, a French priest, arrived in Pondicherry, shortly after his ordination in 1792. His own country was convulsing in the throes of a violent revolution that would last a decade. Three years earlier, a new beheading machine had tasted royal blood and was now thirsty for more. This was a period of great social upheaval in France where the Church had received a dangerous amount of negative attention from the Revolutionaries. Dubois’ flight to India was to save two lives—his own and that of his missionary order. Pondicherry, as the seat of the French government in India, made a perfect staging ground for his work. He saw this densely populated heathen land in the east as theologically fertile land ready for harvest. In order to convert them out of their heathen ways, he found it necessary to understand those heathen ways. Therefore, he put himself to the study of the local culture and language. Alongside, as was common among foreign visitors in India, he took extensive notes. Over time, he had a great compilation of his understanding of the Indian way of life.

Dubois wasn’t the only one doing this. When he came to India, Britain’s William Jones was also in the process of exploring the Hindu way of life in order to better understand their theology. He was studying and translating an ancient work on Hindu code and, by extension, caste—the Manusmriti. This translation came out in 1794 when Dubois was still working on his caste treatise.

Four years later, neighboring Mysore went to war with the British for the fourth time and lost. Its king Tipu fought valiantly and died fighting. His two sons were taken prisoners, and they spent the rest of their lives as pensioners of the British East India Company as Mysore slid under British rule. With this new development, Dubois saw opportunity and moved to Mysore. Here, he handed the British resident the notes he had taken over half a decade hoping to get it published. The resident, in turn, sent it to the then Governor of Madras, Lord William Bentinck. Although Bentinck didn’t last in Madras and got recalled to London in 1807 over his handling of the Vellore Mutiny in 1806, Dubois’ work remained on track to publication. By 1816, it was in press.

Dubois’ work on caste is not the first and certainly not the best known on the subject today. But at the time, it was among the most influential in the subcontinent. Its effects would be seen decades after his death in 1848. While the British researched caste to improve administration, Dubois researched caste to improve conversion. Ultimately, both failed. Instead of converting Hindus to Christianity, he wound up himself converting to Hinduism. Jean-Antoine Dubois became Dodda Swami who wore saffron and ate no meat. In fact, in one of his letters, from 1820, he called the whole conversion business “impractical” and regretted that the authority of such a large body of “enlightened and independent” people was being disregarded and subverted by “men of mediocrity.”1

Fast forward to 1857. India revolted and lost, crystallizing its position as a British Colony. Out went the Company, in came the Crown. This pivotal moment offered the British not only a victory but also lessons. Thus far, the preoccupation was with revenues. The East India Company had the charter, and the British Crown had the share in its revenues. India was merely a source of that revenue. How that revenue came was none of the Crown’s business. The Company had pretty much a free hand in how it ran its affairs in India. Which is why there was enough unrest to ultimately foment a nationwide rebellion. This was lesson number one. The preoccupation needed to shift from mere revenues to order. Without order, there was no economy. It’s to that end that a thorough understanding, appeasement, and exploitation of the subcontinent’s caste dynamics became an immediate imperative.2 After all, a prime trigger for the Mutiny was Pandey’s Brahmin sentiments.  
  
This is where Dubois’ work and Manusmriti acquired mainstream prominence. They helped the new administration understand the strengths and weaknesses of each caste group and also their equations with each other. This way they could put their strengths to better use while also minimizing the odds of different caste communities joining forces against the government.

American academic and an authority on the subject of British colonialism Nicholas Dirks even goes on to claim that the British essentially “reinvented” caste as we know it. A rather bold assessment, one could argue, but given how they went all-out on reinforcing all caste fault lines in their administration, it does seem to hold water. One could say the social organization along caste lines became increasingly canonical and institutionalized.3 So much so that entire communities were labeled “criminal tribes” for efficient policing. Regiments were formed on the basis of caste for the first time. Even laws were codified as applicable differently to different castes. All of this was done with a singular objective—to improve governance through better organization. A kind of a scientific approach to a sociological matter, so to speak.

Before we even begin to scrutinize Dirk’s claim, let’s define caste. In the Indian discourse, two terms are bandied about all the time— _varna_ and _jati_. Which of these maps to caste, only the inventors of the term can tell, namely the good old Portuguese.4 To Barbosa, a Portuguese writer who used the term in a 1516 work, it referred to a kind of occupational assortment that had little to do with birth or lineage.5 But then, an even earlier work from 1444 defined _casta_ as race or breed.6 A 1563 work termed it as an occupational division that passed from father to son, i.e. hereditary. Then a 1567 work introduces the idea of hierarchy:7

    “In some parts of this Province the Gentoos divide themselves into distinct races or castes of greater or less dignity, holding the Christians as of lower degree, and keep these so superstitiously that no one of a higher caste can eat or drink with those of a lower...”

So, to them, caste seems to be three things—hierarchical, occupational, and mutually exclusive. Now whether we map it to jati or varna, is immaterial to this conversation. In fact, let us reduce it further with a presupposition—the story of caste is the story of endogamy. Then the question is, did the British invent endogamy or reinvent it? Sure, they made caste more visible in administration and census—the first, in 1870, listed over sixty for Bihar alone8—but was that the cause, or effect? This question is particularly pertinent because in the 1870 census, the British reported a great deal of cross-pollination across caste lines at least in Bihar and UP:9

    “In fact, no line whatever exists. Even in the higher castes there is reason to believe that considerable intermixture with aboriginal tribes took place, and Mr. Carnegy, in his tract on the Races of Oude, gives instances where Rajputs, such as the Ponwars, have taken wives from Pasi and other aboriginal races within the last hundred years, and that without any degradation of caste resulting to their descendants.”

If the caste lines did not prevent interbreeding in 1870 but they did later, does that mean people were less particular about such divisions in the past? The reason endogamy is so crucial to this conversation is because a lack thereof indicates an insignificance, if not absence altogether, of another caste dimension—untouchability. In the Indian context, endogamy and untouchability go pretty much hand-in-hand. Both are two sides of the same uniquely Indian coin. We will discuss untouchability a little later but first let’s establish if endogamy was truly nonexistent or even less prevalent before the British. Which brings us to Manusmriti, the ideological fount of all caste discourse in India.

## The Shastras

As the dominion over northern India—Magadha in particular—passed from the Kushans to the Guptas in the fourth century, the region also underwent a doctrinal switch. The Kushans, although tolerant to all faiths, visibly privileged Buddhism. Before them, the Sakas, Mauryas, and Greeks too had extended enthusiastic patronage to Buddhism. With Guptas this changed. Under them, the spotlight shifted back to the Vedic creed after a long lull and Vishnu replaced Buddha as the deity of royal reverence. Magadha had toggled between _Śramaṇa_ (Buddhist and Jain but mostly Buddhist) and Brahminical hegemonies repeatedly for over half a millennium now.

All this while, the Hindus of the east had also been alive to the growing Buddhist dominance in the west, first under the Mauryas (who also spanned east), then under the Greeks. While the Kushans and others presented them with the necessity to consolidate their hold on the society, the Guptas offered them the opportunity. It was time for formalization or “Shastrification” of Hindu conduct to preserve its core from erosion by the _mlecchas_ or foreigners. This was their second big opportunity, the first being under the Shungas more than four hundred years earlier. They could not afford to lose it. We call this Shastrification because during this period, a large body of work called _Dharmashastras_ was produced. The process had already begun under the Kushans and continued through the Guptas. Part of this corpus is a work titled _Manava Dharmashastra_ or Manusmriti. The work had one mission statement—to codify Brahmin puritanism and immunize it against any potential threat, cultural or ideological.

The Manusmriti occupies a peculiar space in Hinduism. While it is often cited as a foundational legal text that enshrines the caste system within Brahminical ideology, its historical status remains complex. Despite its initial popularity, the Manusmriti never achieved canonical status nor formal inclusion within any Hindu polity’s legal code. This is even true of the Gupta Empire, whose legal framework was based on another work that we will explore shortly. But even that’s not the most intriguing aspect of Manusmriti’s status. The most intriguing aspect is where it _did_ find adoption—across the border, in the Theravada Buddhist Pagan Kingdom, today split between Myanmar and Thailand. This is particularly noteworthy given the apparent incompatibility between Buddhist principles and the social stratification advocated by the Manusmriti. Paradoxically, these Buddhist rulers sought to legitimize their own legal codes by invoking Manu’s divine authority. What they did was strategically retroject Manu into their own etiology as the celestial lawgiver to Mahasammata,10 the first worldly king in the Pagan charter myth,11 much like Sumerian Alulim. To further reinforce this association, they named their legal corpus _Dhammasattha_ , Pali for Dharmashastra.12 This linkage served as a formidable justification for their exercise of power.13 It was, in other words, their “divine sanction.” Now back to caste, a concept Manu is said to have first codified.

Manusmriti is as much about crime and punishment as it is about social order, order being a euphemism for stratification. Although it never became State Law in India, much of it found active adoption in the society at large. Either that, or Manu just codified what was already in praxis around him. Sort of turning a social practice into a doctrinal fiat, so to speak. If we pin caste on Manu, it’s important to date his work. That’ll give us a date of caste codification in our society. How do we know it’s closer to 400 AD than 1200 BC, as proposed by certain ideologues?  
  
The answer is gold coins. Verses 131 to 137 of the eighth chapter detail the fascinating way people paid for goods and services back then.14 This system used a surprisingly wide range of denominations ranging from a floating speck of dust to a gold coin or _suvarna_. In fact, the highest unit of currency in Manu’s world is not one but four gold coins, a _nishka_.15 And this isn’t mere definition, gold coins actually enjoyed active circulation too. We know this because 8.213 flags as infraction, a donor’s attempts to recover his donation, especially one made toward “a pious purpose,” and stipulates an amercement of one suvarna as penalty.16 Similarly, 8.361 imposes one suvarna upon anyone caught interacting with another person’s wife despite an expressly prohibition.17 If fines were being imposed in suvarnas, it’s reasonable to assume that they must have been commonplace.

But here’s the thing: Although introduced by the Greeks as early as second century BC as evident from a Bactrian quarter suvarna found in Taxila,18 gold coins did not enter the Indian mainland until the Kushans between 100 and 120 AD. There is mention of suvarna in Arthashastra too, which could place gold coin in the Mauryan period. But it’s likely not representative of active currency since no gold coinage from the period has so far been found in India, least of all from the eastern heartland which is where Manusmriti emerged. This assigns Manusmriti a _terminus a quo_ of 113 AD which is when Kushan emperor Vima Kadphises, the man who is unanimously understood to have introduced gold to Indian monetary system,1920 took the throne.

Now, Vima Kadphises’ domain was confined to Afghanistan and parts of northwestern India. It’s only under Kanishka that the Kushans expanded to Pataliputra. So Manusmriti could not have been older than Kanishka who died in 150 AD. Furthermore, for infractions to be fined in gold, the coinage must have been accessible to the masses rather than reserved to the mercantile aristocracy. Therefore, some scholars allow another fifty years or so for the coinage to percolate to the relatively lower rungs of the economy. Which moves Manusmriti to around 200 AD.21

And with that, we enter the cusp where the Guptas are emerging as a new dynastic power. In the context of caste, the most definitive thing Manusmriti did was the codification of endogamy and hypergamy. _Gotras_ or patrilines, although first seen in the Vedas, acquired a new prominence and complex rules emerged around who could marry whom.

As already discussed, Manu’s code was never the law of the land in India. The Guptas looked to a different work for their legal framework—Yajnavalkya Smriti. The work came after Manusmriti and is widely held as the official law-book of the Gupta regime.22 Now, although Yajnavalkya lived nearly a thousand years before the Guptas, the work is named after him merely out of respect, as was a common practice in Hindu traditions. The likes of D. C. Sircar place his Smriti at least a hundred years after that of Manu,23 which makes it around 300 AD or later.

Despite the varna hardlines imposed by both Smritis, the society seemed surprisingly relaxed in terms of inter-caste marital alliances. A Vakataka inscription on the walls of Ghatotkacha Caves near Ajanta illustrates this best. Made around 500 AD, when the Brahmin Vakatakas ruled the region, it serves as an ode to Buddha as well as a laudatory tribute to the commissioning donor, a Vakataka minister named Varahadeva and his lineage. Among the verses is a reference to Ravi whose father was a Brahmin and mother a Kshatriya.24 This and several examples illustrate the prevalence of inter-caste marriages up until the sixth century if not later. But there was a catch. It was always the girl marrying _up_ into a higher caste than the other way around, e.g., a Kshatriya girl marrying a Brahmin boy, etc. We call it hypergamy. This was not considered violation of varna rules as established by the Smritis, unless it involved a Shudra. Yajnavalkya Smriti even allowed a measure of caste mobility where Shudras were allowed to become traders, farmers, artisans, and at times, even soldiers.25 It’s, however, toward the later years of the Smriti period that inter-caste marriages started losing popularity.26

Therefore, at least on the question of endogamy it would seem that things started to grow less flexible only with the advent of the Smritis. Until that point, there was a fair amount of endogamy and social mobility between castes in the Indian society. So, can we then say with conviction that caste started around 1,700 years ago with Manusmriti? We could, but there’s the Epics to contend with. But before we get to those earlier sources, let’s park the question of endogamy. While inscriptions and Smritis give us a fair idea of when endogamy within castes would have mainstreamed, they’re not sufficient. We need a more concrete corroboration because texts do not tell the whole story. This is what we’ll address once we’re done scouring pre-Gupta literature for evidence of caste. Now back to the epics.

## Before the Shastras

Both Ramayana and Mahabharata talk about characters like Sabari and Karna, respectively, who suffered clear caste disadvantages in their lives. Shabri is an untouchable whose leftover food is strongly forbidden for Kshatriyas like Ram. Karna is a Shudra competing against whom in duel is considered an affront to a Kshatriya prince’s dignity. Both are strong cases of irreconcilable caste divide in their respective societies. Therefore, it would follow that varna must have been an important social reality when those works were composed. If we go with conventional scholarship that the epics were penned between 800 and 500 BC, caste moves back another millennium or so. But is even that the final destination?

Between 1000 and 500 BC, we’re in a period called later Vedic. This is the era of the epics but also of the various later and post-Vedic literature, most notably the Upanishads. An investigation into this corpus could tell us something about the state of caste at the time. We have seen a couple of examples in the epics, but it cannot be overlooked that both Ramayana and Mahabharata are also social commentaries on the downsides of caste discrimination. While Ramayana tells us about Shabari’s lowly status as an untouchable, it also speaks of Ram embracing her as an equal and partaking in her leftovers. While Mahabharata tells us about Karna being a Shudra, it also tells us about his elevation to royalty by none other than a Kshatriya. Ekalavya, despite being an outcast, is written in a laudatory light and idolized for virtues like obeisance and valor. Now, there are two ways to read this. Either caste was so strongly entrenched that the epics had to step in to preach against it. Or it was too weak and permeable that the epics saw no scandal in its heroes crossing them. Both notions have merit. Which is why we need more.

Part of the later Vedic corpus is at least one Veda that does seem to touch upon untouchability. Yajurveda 30.21 lists ten different categories of men suitable for ten different “sacrifices.”27 On that list is _chandala_ , a class of untouchables that works with corpses.2829 Chandala is understood to be a product of a Brahmin-Shudra intercourse. Among the first to clearly mention him as “bad birth,” a penalty for past sins, is Chhandogya Upanishad, verse 5.10.7.30 It literally compares chandala with dogs and pigs and calls their lives “disgusting.”3132 This time, untouchability is significantly less ambiguous. This work forms part of a wider canon called Upanishads. A total of 108 are counted—collectively called _Mutikā_ —some more important or privileged than others. Chhandogya is among the earliest and thus one of the most privileged. More or less coeval with it is the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad. Here too, at least one verse—4.3.22—mentions chandala.33 The discussion is about the enlightened state of a Yogi in which all varna identities, including chandala, cease to matter.3435

So, the Upanishands seem pretty confident about untouchability being well-entrenched at the time of their composition. Now the question is, what _is_ that time? The later Vedic window spans a good five hundred years, but we must attempt to narrow it down further to the best of our ability. Chhandogya and Brihadaranyaka are the oldest of all but later than the Vedas, of course. That places them well after 1000–800 BC, which is the _terminus a quem_ for the last of the Vedas. While precision is impossible, scholarly consensus lies in the 700–500 BC window—after the Vedas but before Buddha.36 This makes untouchability about 2,700 years old.

Worth noting is also the geography. So far, we’re still confined to North India because the authors of the Upanishads had no awareness of the regions south of the Vindhyas. Upper Indus and Lower Ganges mark the western and eastern boundaries respectively. While it’s nearly impossible to accurately assign any work from the time to a specific territory within this realm, those in the know place Brihadaranyaka in UP-Bihar and Chhandogya in the Punjab-Haryana.37 That’s because Brihadaranyaka speaks of several events that take place in Videha, an eastern kingdom mostly associated with Ramayana’s Janaka.38 Chhandogya, on the other hand, shows an affinity to the Kuru-Panchala country in what’s today the Punjab-Haryana region.39 It holds in high esteem the Kuru-Panchala theologian Uddalaka Aruni unlike Brihadaranyaka which vilifies him.40 This is also reflective of an intense doctrinal rivalry between the two polities.

Toward the end of the Upanishadic period, many more identities, e.g., _nishada_ , joined the chandalas as the untouchable collective. Interestingly, Vivekananda Jha posits that there was no express prohibition against interdining between the four varnas during this period.41 Nor was there any on inter-varna marital alliances.42 This complicates matters, for we’ve already seen evidence of untouchability in Yajurveda and at least two early Upanishads. But interdining and intermarriage contradict untouchability. Truth, perhaps, lies someplace in between. One could argue that untouchability, although emergent, was yet to take a dominant space in the wider social landscape. This would have rapidly changed in the post-Vedic period, between 600 and 200 BC, when _jati_ emerged as a new social taxonomy with varying degrees of mutual contact.43 This phenomenon coincides with extensive use of iron for production and construction.44 Could the two be related? Jha says yes. Iron improved economy but expanded wealth disparity. With this disparity came social stratification and the emergence of hereditary occupational groups called jatis.45 It’s only at this point that untouchability truly went universal. We will soon see why Jha’s take on the matter, matters. So, here’s what we have so far:

  1. No untouchability before 1000 BC,

  2. Some untouchability by 600 BC, and

  3. Well entrenched untouchability by 200 BC




All dates approximate, of course. We could end our conversation here, but things are still far from settled. So far, it’s only an assumption that endogamy mainstreamed with the Shastras and untouchability more or less with the Upanishads. But what about the Vedas? Yes, we have noted caste hierarchy in Yajurveda, but that’s a much later work. We still have the first of the firsts to contend with, the Rigveda.

## The Rigveda

While Yajurveda establishes a clear social hierarchy with ten “caste” groups in 30.21, RV 10.90 or _Purusha Sukta_ is where caste hierarchy makes a dramatic debut as a concept.46 In the Rigvedic universe, Brahma is the primordial Creator. The twelfth verse of the hymn in question posits that Brahmins came from his head, Kshatriyas from his arms, Vaishyas from his thighs, and Shudras from his feet.47 That’s as stridently hierarchical as it gets, although many often argue that it allegorizes a division of occupation and not significance. What it truly signifies is immaterial to the question at hand, namely whether caste divisions and mutual exclusions existed in the Vedic society. In simpler terms, did Rigvedic Brahmins marry Rigvedic Shudras? This verse makes it significantly unlikely. Brahmin, in case it isn’t evidently already, traces an etymology to Brahma. Shudra, on the other hand, comes from _kṣudra_ , Sanskrit for small, insignificant, low, vile and a variety of other adjectives, none of them positive. Now, someone born out of Brahma’s head and even named after him isn’t very likely to procreate or even engage positively with someone who is by definition small, low, and vile. Therefore, it’s not unwise to concede caste endogamy as a feature of the Rigvedic society. But there’s a catch.

The hymn was probably never part of the original corpus. This was first noted by the celebrated English orientalist and botanist Henry Thomas Colebrooke. He is hailed as Europe’s “first great Sanskrit scholar,” and he died when even Max Müller was still a boy. Colebrooke’s contemporaries were stars of Indology like James Prinsep and William Jones. He studied the Vedic corpus extensively and delivered a number of lectures on the metric and rhythmic structure of the Rigveda and its scholia. A collection of these lectures was published posthumously by his son in three volumes in 1873. In it, one of the footnotes elaborates that the Purusha Sukta is composed in a meter that’s decidedly more modern as compared to the rest of the corpus.48 It further states that the hymns of the Rigveda were compiled and organized after the Sanskrit language had acquired its final, most refined form.49 This would mean after Panini. The Purusha Sukta belongs to this post-compilation period.50

The same was echoed by John Muir in his seminal _Original Sanskrit Texts on the Origin and History of the People of India, Their Religion and Institutions_ , the first volume of which came out in 1858. He makes a clear distinction in the diction and ideas of Purusha Sukta as compared to the rest of thousand-odd hymns.51 Besides Purusha Sukta, he also adds 10.71, 10.72, 10.81, 10.82, 10.121, and 10.129 to this list of later additions.52 That’s two sources agreeing that Purusha Sukta is a modern composition. But modern how? Exactly what about it makes it modern? Max Müller offers some answers.53

One of those pertains to how the work sees seasons. Rigveda seems to divide the year into three seasons, unlike six in later times. In older verses, such as 10.161.4, these three seasons are _sharad_ (autumn), _hemant_ (winter), and _vasant_ (spring). But in 10.90.6, it changes to vasant, _greeshma_ (summer), and sharad.54 Basically, hemant or winter disappears and greeshma or summer emerges. Almost as if the climate had grown warmer with time. Greeshma does not occur in any other hymn outside of Purusha Sukta.55 Perhaps the hymn was composed when the Aryans were already in the Gangetic Plains unlike the bulk of the corpus that was born in the Punjab-Haryana region? Having said that, it must be noted that the entire tenth _mandala_ is a relatively later composition and Müller did not have the information we have today, such as the path of Aryan movement within the subcontinent.

At any rate, many scholars now agree that caste divisions during early Vedic times did not exhibit the impermeability that later came to characterize the Gupta period. Nor was it hierarchical. The divisions did exist nonetheless and only grew stronger by the time of the Upanishads. Ultimately, Manusmriti crystallized further the fault-lines that already existed and helped make endogamy a thing for the entire “Hindu” community. But who are the scholars that uphold this view? And that brings us back to Vivekanand Jha. He, Irfan Habib, Romila Thapar—all seem unanimous on untouchability not being a Rigvedic feature at all. In a 1990 Presidential address at the Indian History Congress, Jha posited that the fourfold varna system was _definitely_ not an Aryan introduction.56 At this point, the lay reader might wonder, why Jha, Habib, and Thapar in particular? Besides the fact that they’re all celebrated, accomplished historians, there’s a political reason behind the choice. All three belong to what’s often called, the “Marxist school of historiography.” They lean Left. Does that affect their scholarship? We won’t attempt to answer that question here, but in India, Left leaning directly corresponds to anti-Brahminical. And since caste happens to be the Left’s strongest grouse against the Brahmins and since Brahminism itself follows from the Vedas, assigning caste to the Vedic period is the most obvious narrative one should expect from India’s Leftist quarters. In that context, the choice of these _Marxist_ historians categorically dissociating caste from the Vedic society holds meaning. Of these, we will be returning to Thapar at a later point in this article.

Do note that Rigveda does mention terms like _dasa_ and _dasyu_ that may seem to indicate some untouchable class. But the scholars we mentioned above all seem unanimous on the reference being more to slaves or unpaid laborers, likely scored in wars, than a caste group. At any rate, as Jha informs, the only reference to the fourfold varna system in the Rigveda is in the Purusha Sukta,57 so that’s a pretty strong argument against the system’s antiquity.

So where does that leave us? Scriptures don’t seem to be telling the whole story. Did endogamy exist in Vedic India or not? Since books wouldn’t answer let’s turn to something else, the most irrefutable piece of the puzzle—the DNA.

## Genetics

Before we start, a very quick biology refresher is in order. DNA, the blueprint for life, comes organized into segments called genes. These genes are further organized into structures called chromosomes. Each cell in our body contains twenty-three pairs of chromosomes, with one pair determining our sex. This sex chromosome pair consists of either X or Y chromosomes. Men have the XY pair, while women have XX. All chromosomes live within the nucleus of the cell. But there’s one set that lives outside the nucleus, in the mitochondria. We call it the mitochondrial DNA or mtDNA. And here’s the most interesting bit about them—only mothers can pass them on. That’s because the sperm’s mitochondria reside in its tail which dissolves away during fertilization. In other words, Y chromosome DNA comes exclusively from the father, mtDNA exclusively from the mother. Scientists use this crucial piece of information to map genetic ancestries going back thousands of years. They also use it to determine the amount of admixture between diverse gene pools. With these fundamentals out of the way, we can now start with genetic studies in the context of our caste question.

A 2001 study of the nonrecombining portion of the Y chromosome from about two thousand men representing forty-nine Eurasian populations established Central Asia as a major reservoir of genetic diversity for patrilineal chromosomes.58 These findings were also juxtaposed against linguistic patterns in Eurasia and found some correlations. The study found a remarkable diversity in the Y chromosome pool in every major Eurasian community including India which further helped establish a migratory pattern over thousands of years. To put it simply, Indians exhibit a high degree of Y-chromosomal haplogroup diversity, indicating multiple paternal lineages that have coalesced over millennia.

But four years later, another study analyzed over eight thousand Indian genomes and concluded the exact opposite for the mtDNA.59 It found that the Indian mitochondrial pool is notably homogenous, dates back tens of thousands of years, and is unique to India, i.e. is indigenous. To rephrase it, our matrilineal ancestry is thoroughly autochthonous to the subcontinent. It supported the hypothesis of a single, rapid dispersal event of anatomically modern humans (AMH) out of Africa along the southern Asian coastline.

On the surface the two findings might seem to contradict each other, but they don’t. The 2001 study establishes the paternal lineage whereas the one from 2005 does paternal. In layman terms, India has had a thriving indigenous population for thousands of years (since arrival from Africa around 60,000 BP). Then new populations came in, mated with preexisting ones, and created new gene pools. These newcomers were overwhelmingly male, and their arrival was in multiple waves over thousands of years. The indigenes they mated with were, of course, female. And that’s why there’s much diversity in our Y chromosomal DNA but little in mtDNA. So that doesn’t sound like endogamy. Much interbreeding prevailed for thousands of years. Then came a population bottleneck. Which brings us to a third study by David Reich and others in 2009 where India was found to exhibit a greater genetic diversity than even Europe.

Reich concluded that this diversity could only come from “population bottlenecks.” Think of it this way: When two or more gene pools interact, they interbreed. And the more they interbreed, the less diverse the resulting gene pool, or higher the entropy. On the flip side, the less they interbreed, the more distinct their respective descendant pools, i.e. lower the entropy. Reich defines the bottleneck as a result of this phenomenon, when a small endogamous but prolific population gives rise to a large distinct gene pool. Long story short, while a lot of interbreeding happened for the longest time, it all stopped after a while and people grew increasingly insular within their respective social groups. Sounds like endogamy? This bottleneck came at different times for different populations. According to Reich, at least for one group in Andhra Pradesh, this bottleneck was in the first millennium BC.60 Which corresponds to, guess what, the Vedic Age. Do note that a population bottleneck is the _starting point_ of endogamy within the group in question.

In the case of the group that we just discussed—the Vysyas of Andhra—geographic separation appears to have little bearing on their genetic isolation. Reich’s findings suggest that despite close physical proximity to other communities, the Vysyas succeeded in maintaining a distinct genetic profile. This observation is further supported by the emergence of similar genetic isolation patterns in other populations. In genetics, sustained endogamy, akin to inbreeding but acting over a broader range of relationships, is predicted to elevate the frequency of recessive genetic disorders within a population. This is because harmful genetic errors receive reinforcements from both parents in such cases. What this means is that any community that has practiced endogamy over enough iterations should exhibit one or more conditions uniquely prevalent within its members. And sure enough, the Vysyas exhibit a dramatically severe response to muscle relaxants than others.61

In conclusion, Reich acknowledges that Vysyas represent merely one instance, potentially among hundreds, of populations exhibiting founder effects and subsequent endogamy. However, this trend is not ubiquitous. Within the samples analyzed by his team, only one-third displayed evidence of sustained endogamy.62

Reich then published a third study in 2009 where he, along with Lalji Singh and Kumarasamy Thangaraj, studied twenty-five diverse groups in India for their genetic heritage. This paper introduced us to the terms Ancestral North Indians (ANI) and Ancestral South Indians (ASI).63 Here, Reich broadly divided the Indian gene pool into these two subgroups based on their genetic ancestry. No, this is not Aryan vs. Dravidian, although the ANI ancestry pervades North Indians far more than South Indians and within South India, upper caste Hindus far more than others. More controversially, the ANI ancestry traces origins outside India—Iran, Central Asia, Europe—whereas ASI, inside.64 Today, we have no pure ANI or ASI population but a mix. And ANI features everywhere, only in varying degrees as stated above. It’s a continuum, if you will. The only population with no ANI ancestry inhabits the Andamans. How come? The answer is genetic admixture. The aforementioned study confirms an intercourse between the two gene pools on the mainland at some point in time but stops short of saying when exactly.

And that brings us to a far more comprehensive study by Priya Moorjani in 2013. Reich, Thangaraj, and Singh were all part of this project. This time seventy-three population groups were studied as compared to twenty-five of the previous study and involved twice as many experts.65 From this study, a window emerged—from 2200 BC thru 100 AD.66 It proposes vigorous genetic admixture during this window allowing for widespread proliferation of the ANI ancestry.  
  
Now, we need to keep in mind that genetics don’t directly establish untouchability, they establish endogamy. Can the two correlate? Yes, but must they? Not really. Technically, you wouldn’t marry whom you wouldn’t touch. But that’s only a problem when an untouchable is involved. Between, say, a Kshatriya and a Brahmin? There can be marital exclusion without any untouchability. Vedic and post-Vedic references have already confirmed little to no endogamy during the time, but emergence of untouchability after 1000 BC, mostly around 600 BC. Moorjani’s study finds vigorous ANI-ASI admixing between 2200 BC and 100 AD. Don’t forget, the first 400 years or so of this period corresponds to the Harappan Civilization.

Even the intermixing comes to a stop after 100 AD which almost coincides with the emergence of Dharmashastras including Manusmriti. So, to ask once again, where does that leave us now? While genetics cannot say anything about caste _per se_ , it does date endogamy quite conclusively for us. Considering endogamy as a defining feature of caste, besides hierarchy, we could say caste is at least as old as the Vedas. But:

  1. it wasn’t as rigid as it’d become later; only a fraction of the communities practiced strict endogamy,

  2. it wasn’t necessarily hierarchical as our epics are full of episodes where men and women have freely permeated caste boundaries, and

  3. caste lines were drawn along high-level occupational diversity, i.e. it was just the four varnas and not a myriad jatis.




Things, however, changed after the Hindu resurgence toward the end of Kushan reign which is when Manu came out with his contentious magnum opus. Under the Guptas, caste lines got reinforced with fresh enthusiasm and endogamy became, or was made, a more widespread practice than ever before. Was this the result of Manusmriti? Or was Manusmriti a result of changing circumstances? We don’t know for sure, but the latter makes more sense. So how do we reconcile genetics and scripture into an elegant, unified theory? By separating endogamy and untouchability.

The early Aryans practiced neither endogamy, nor untouchability. But they did have a class divide between the aristocracy (priests, warriors, merchants) and the serfdom (dasyus, etc.). The former was divided into three varnas and the latter made up a fourth. By about 1000 BC, this divide further crystallized and untouchability emerged as a class outside the varna system. another four hundred years or so, and a new taxonomy along occupational lines emerged, they called it jati. Untouchability was by now firmly entrenched. By the early centuries of the common era, as more foreign communities came to rule and occupy the subcontinent, a new wave of Hindu revival kickstarted. This wave brought an abrupt end to the intermixing and cemented endogamy as an integral part of the Vedic creed. This model adequately satisfies both scriptural and genetic findings.

Further solidifying the evidence of genetic intermixing is a large-scale 2019 study involving over a hundred experts from various disciplines. This research, the most extensive to date, analyzed the genetic sequences of 523 individuals who lived within the past 8,000 years.67 Notably, this project included David Reich and primarily focused on the origins of Aryans. However, it also sheds light on the intermingling of the Steppe and indigenous Indian gene pools. We’ll only discuss the latter as Aryan origins is a whole other subject outside the scope here. The study pinpoints the second millennium BC as the period when significant intermixing began.68

Prior to this, ANI and ASI existed as distinct genetic clusters, potentially coexisting geographically but with minimal gene flow. The arrival of Aryans, possessing a greater Eurasian genetic component, triggered a period of intense admixture. As concluded by the previous study, this intermixing likely continued until around 100 AD.69 It’s against this backdrop that one must read what Reich said about some groups practicing endogamy since as far back as the first millennium BC. At this point only one question remains unanswered: When did it go south?

## The Tamil Country

Our preceding discussion has focused exclusively on the situation north of the Vindhyas. The pertinent question that now arises is, at what point in time did untouchability become established in the southern regions? Two primary perspectives can be considered. The first posits that untouchability likely arrived alongside the southward spread of Brahminism. This interpretation suggests that its introduction occurred during the Sangam period, roughly between 600 BC and 300 AD.

Notably, Sangam literature provides no evidence of the practice of untouchability.70 While it acknowledges social distinctions based on profession and geographical location, there is no indication of enforced segregation. In fact, some communities that would later be classified as "untouchable" appear to have enjoyed a respectable social position within the context of these texts.71 For example, the fishing community of _Parathavars_ (also called _Paravars_ or _Parathars_) received glowing tributes and accorded decent social standing in Sangam classics and yet ended up as the untouchables to later Brahmins, likely due to their fishing vocation.72 If this means anything, South India should be seen as untouchability-free until at least 300 AD, if not later.

Our earliest documented reference to untouchability in Tamil literature comes from _Acharakkovai_ , a work of didactic poetry from the post-Sangam era, likely composed no earlier than the seventh century. Verse 6 of this work identifies the _Pulaiyars_ , a meat-eating community, as untouchable.73 It specifically prohibits drinking water touched by a Pulaiyar, mirroring the treatment of chandala in post-Vedic texts. Notably, both communities are associated with handling corpses. Besides Pulaiyars, Pallans and Parayans were also classified as untouchables and made to live in demarcated quarters called _Cheris_ , away from the village to avoid contact.74

Interestingly, the institutionalization of both endogamy in the north and untouchability in the south was a product of Hindu revivalist movements in the respective regions. In the north it was the Shastrification of the creed, in the south it was the Bhakti Movement. The end of the Sangam era, often called the Golden Age of the South, ushered in a doctrinal schism in Hindu praxis. A section worshipped Shiva as the supreme deity, and another went with Vishnu. The Shaivites are called Alvars and the Vaishnavites are called Nayanars. While the movement took the Hindu doctrine out of Brahminical clutches to the masses, in their vernacular, it also most ironically reinforced the ideas of social hierarchy and segregation. Untouchability in particular and casteism in general thrived under all post-Sangam polities in Tamilakkam, especially the Cholas under whom the outcast settlements called _Teendacheri_ or Cheri are said to have emerged.

The Teendacheri claim is a little difficult to verify in the absence of a conclusive primary source other than Acharakkovai, so one mustn’t rush to commit. But since the work follows Sangam and is coeval with the Cholas, one can take it with a reasonable amount of confidence. So, with all that we’ve examined so far, here’s what one should take as a very rough timeline of caste, untouchability in particular, in India:

  1. 600 BC: Emerges in the north

  2. 200 BC: Firmly establishes in the north

  3. 600 AD: Emerges in the south

  4. 900 AD: Firmly establishes in the South




But this is based on what we have examined so far. Have we examined everything? We did go as far back as the Rigveda, that’s great. But could we go any further? India is far older than the Vedas and civilization has existed here since as early as 2600 BC. Sure, there’s no caste system, no untouchability in the Rigveda. But what about the Harappans? For the longest time the working theory has been that the Indus Civilization was a headless, egalitarian Utopia with no hierarchy and no violence. Let’s revisit that notion just to be sure.

## The Harappans

Untouchability is a feature unique to the Hindu caste hierarchy. Social hierarchies and group exclusions exist in all cultures, but untouchability exists only here. The notion of “purity,” which is what informs this practice, is a deeply religious one. We have already established that endogamy and untouchability started for a small minority of communities during later Vedic and even Upanishadic period, then expanded to all others in Shastric or Puranic times, i.e. Late Antiquity. What’s still puzzling is how. If not the Rigveda, whence? Where did the idea of ritual purity emerge? We have explored scripture, we have explored genetics, now it’s time we explored archeology and see what the state of society was in the fabled casteless wonderland of Harappa.

Frankly, archeology does not offer direct or easy answers. We just need to triangulate the pieces it does offer and draw the most educated conclusions. Not an impossible task, we do that for prehistory all the time. There are six such pieces we have at our disposal, the first being that the Harappans had religion.

One of the most compelling indicators is their funerary practices. Unlike simple disposal of the dead, cremation—a practice observed more widely Harappa than in Mohenjo-daro where burial was practiced too—suggests ritual. In many cases, the ashes were subsequently placed in urns which were then buried along with a number of grave goods. These are undeniable signs of ritual and ritualistic disposal of the dead is universally held as a sign of belief in an afterlife, itself a most reasonable indicator of organized religion. Grave goods and funerary urns bear out a desire to equip the deceased for their journey beyond. Besides, artifacts like the enigmatic Pasupati seal, the iconic Linga, the fig-leaf deity, and numerous depictions of a “Mother Goddess” point toward not only an elaborate body of rituals and sacerdotal practices but a whole pantheon of diverse deities.

The second finding is that the Harappans practiced segregation. This ties in with the civilization’s spatial organization. Indus cities are known for their remarkable urban planning. Besides grid-like streets and well-defined functional zones, these cities also featured a rather strong sense of segregation. The typical Mature Harappan city was always divided into two distinct sectors—a western acropolis and an eastern lower town. The western sector was typically built on higher ground, often artificially elevated with earth and rocks. But it wasn’t just the height that separated the acropolis from the rest of the city. It was the walls. The elevation was to keep out flood waters, for the Indus is an unpredictable river. But the walls were obviously not for water. The eastern lower town consisted of low-cost quarters meant likely meant for non-elites. This lower-lying region was also more susceptible to seasonal flooding. Walls further subdivided the lower town, creating distinct districts within it. This was a peculiarly insular civilization as we’ll see next, but for now, the question is, why such a high degree of social segregation? There was certainly no violence because archeology hasn’t thrown up much weaponry or signs of large-scale carnage. Then what were the walls meant to keep out?

Accessed 29 June 2024.]

And that brings us to our third point—the Harappans were insular. They engaged in a lot of trade with civilizations across the mountains and overseas. We have found Harappan trading outposts far beyond their core territory. One such example is the settlement of Shortugai, located on the Amu Darya River (historically known as the Oxus) on the fringes of the Bactria-Margiana Archaeological Complex or BMAC, also sometimes called the Oxus civilization. Harappan seals have been found in Mesopotamian sites as well, which is how we come to know of Meluhha, an ancient name associated with a trading partner of Mesopotamia, likely referring to the Indus Civilization or a port therein.

And yet, not one foreign artifact has ever been found in the entire 400,000 square miles of the Indus Civilization. Not a single Mesopotamian seal, not a single Oxus hoard. Despite a thriving mercantile engagement with Mesopotamian cities over centuries, the Harappans never adopted the cuneiform for their writing. Why the resistance to foreign influence? To this day, the Indus or Harappan Civilization remains the only culture to have maintained such a high degree of insularity and for such an extended period.

The fourth major finding from archeology is that the Harappans restricted movements. Not just into their cities but also within. An overwhelming majority of seals found at various Harappan digs are tiny and feature a hole in the center. Why the hole? The only reason a seal might need one is if something were to be threaded through it. Something like a string. Archeologists like Ernest Mackay interpret them as amulets worn by all citizens.75 Further affirming this position is the worn-out condition of the specimen that indicate they must have been worn on the person of the owner.76 Then the question is, were they ornamental? Or did they serve a more practical purpose? Clues might lie in what the “amulets” feature besides the hole. Most of them feature either a divine figure or a scene depicting an episode from the life of one.77

Interestingly, all specimens from a site feature a common depiction,78 a clear indicator of them being tied to political zones or quarters, thus identifying the wearer as a native of such a quarter. This led Mackay to posit that they were likely issued at shrines or places of worship and worn by men and women at all times.79

If we now recall that the quarters within a city were surrounded by walls, it makes logical sense that mobility between the quarters was probably restricted, and domiciliary identifiers like the amulets were used to enforce those restrictions—somewhat like rudimentary “travel documents,” for venturing in and out of their respective quarters. But this is just a hypothesis, and not Mackay’s own conclusion. We need more in order to admit it with confidence. Fortunately, we do have at least one episode from a more modern time that approaches corroboration.

A similar system of domiciliary identification was reported to be in place on a much smaller scale in the late eighteenth-century Bombay by an Austrian traveler named Charles Hügel. He was visiting a fort near Bombay shortly after its capture in 1756 where just before the entrance, he was struck by a wooden seal of Shri Angria as a mark of approval.80 Without the seal, one couldn’t enter the gateway. Hügel is told that the seal was a form of “visa” because the master of the fort wasn’t literate.81 If it was practiced this recently, it’s not improbable to have been practiced in Bronze Age Harappa and Mohenjo-daro either.

The fifth key piece of the puzzle is that the Harappans had division of labor. The fact that Harappan towns were divided into walled quarters obviates further evidencing of social stratification, but what adds to the mountain of proof is the fact that they were a highly industrialized society, one indicator of which is the fact that the amulets we just saw were evidently mass produced.82 Aside from that, the commerce was heavily export oriented. Carnelian, lapis lazuli, tin, copper, and other raw materials were sourced from places as far as Iran and Afghanistan and finished goods such as beads, trinkets, bronze implements, boats, etc. were exported to Mesopotamia and Egypt. While agriculture remained a mainstay, livestock rearing was also a major industry. Peacocks and chickens are essentially India’s gifts to the world at large. Such level of industrial diversity clearly indicates professional specialization leading to social divisions. Question is, do these divisions qualify as stratification?

The walls would say yes. The division between an elite upper town and a non-elite lower town would say yes. But we need more. Which brings us to the darkest aspect of the Harappan Civilization’s most renowned feature, its cutting-edge sewage system. And that’s the sixth and final piece of the puzzle—Someone maintained Harappa’s excellent sanitation.

The Harappans were one of the first, most likely _the_ first, to have a robust sanitation system so ahead of its time, many parts of the world could still learn from them. Their urban centers featured a sophisticated network of drainage canals lining all major streets. This could also explain why Harappan homes rarely, if ever, had windows facing the street and depended more on internal courtyards for ventilation and lighting. Such practice can still be found in many rural Indian hutments. These drainage channels received wastewater from individual households, often located several blocks away, via mass produced terracotta pipes. However, the distribution of these benefits appears to have been uneven. Archaeological evidence suggests that elite residences enjoyed a distinct advantage, being situated farther away from the final disposal points for sewage.83 This spatial arrangement resulted in a more favorable olfactory environment for the privileged class. Conversely, dwellings associated with lower social strata were constructed in closer proximity to the disposal sites, subjecting their occupants to an undesirable sensory experience.84

This limited access to a seemingly mundane amenity, i.e. proper waste disposal, already suggests a kind of urban class hierarchy. This stratification extends beyond spatial organization. While gravity largely took care of the effluent, waste had to oftentimes be manually filtered to keep the drains flowing. The cesspits that were installed around the corners would fill with sludge and someone would’ve had to physically descend into them to scoop it all out.85 Given the vastness of the drainage, especially in cities like Mohenjo-daro, it’s now understood that the job was assigned to a specialized manual taskforce that worked nights while the rest of the city slept.86

So, this is what we have so far:

The Harappans were religious, avoided contact (purity?), practiced urban segregation and restricted movement between social zones (discrimination?), had division of labor with occupational ghettos (jatis?), and had a separate class of nighttime sanitation workers who were forced to live closer to the cesspit than the rest of the city (untouchability?). Of course, we can’t still be confident in the absence of conclusive material and epigraphic evidence. A tenuous link may be drawn between Mohenjo-Daro’s Great Bath and the idea of ritual purity, which then could be juxtaposed against the presence of a seemingly untouchable class in the Indus society, but it’d still be educated triangulation of multiple isolated pieces, “educated” being the keyword here. And such conclusions are not being drawn merely by the lay ideologue but also established historians—of a Marxist color, no less—like Romila Thapar.

In her _Past and Prejudice_ , Thapar posits that while the existence of a fully-fledged caste system in the Harappan civilization remains debatable, there is evidence suggesting the presence of social stratification that could have laid the groundwork for later developments.87 The archaeological record of Harappan settlements reveals a clear tripartite social division.88 She further argues that any society that structures itself along such impregnable social hierarchies would eventually evolve caste-like systems and that the precondition to such developments is not the presence of Aryan or Dravidian communities.89

We are free to conclude that there was no caste system in the Harappan culture as we ae yet to decipher their writing. But it stands demonstrated that their society had every feature we currently attribute to the Hindu caste system. Whether or not they were endogamous, we don’t know. But we know beyond doubt that the totem pole of Harappan caste hierarchy had both lowly sanitation workers and citadel-dwelling priestly elites, and the twain were separated by walls, height, and probably amulets.

Long story short, caste likely originated in Harappan cities. Short story even shorter, since the Rigvedic Aryans did not have the concepts of untouchability and endogamy, it’s just as likely that they picked this practice from the Late Harappan indigenes.

Coming back to the British, was their caste census and caste-based administrative recruitment a driver of caste division? Or was it merely a pragmatic response to existing fault lines in the wake of a caste-driven violence (Mangal Pandey’s outrage)? This one we know with certainty.
