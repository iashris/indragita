In 1956, Claire Patterson of the California Institute of Technology or Caltech made a groundbreaking discovery about the age of Earth using a technique called radiometric dating. At the time, scientists had already known for over half a century that certain elements spontaneously release radiation and transform into lighter elements through a process known as radioactive decay. Uranium, for example, decays into lead over millions of years through a series of intermediate elements. The rate of this decay is predictable, allowing scientists to measure the ratio of uranium to lead in a rock and calculate how long the process has been occurring. Since Earth’s oldest rocks are often altered or destroyed over time, Patterson turned to meteorites—space rocks formed at the same time as Earth and largely unchanged since. By analyzing the uranium and lead in meteorites, Patterson determined that Earth is about 4.5 billion years old, a figure that has since been confirmed by further studies and remains widely accepted today.

Earlier attempts to estimate Earth’s age, such as those by Lord Kelvin in the nineteenth century, suggested it was much younger—around 20 to 40 million years—based on the rate at which Earth was losing heat. Back in the seventeenth century, Bishop James Ussher of Ireland famously calculated that Earth was “created” in 4004 BC, which suggests an Earth age of around 6,000 years, based on genealogies in the Bible. But thousands of years ago, the Atharva Veda had already calculated a value that matches, with remarkable approximation, the value accepted by the scientific community today. Naysayers dismiss it as motivated Hindutva interpretation with no basis in reality.

Similarly, India’s Vedic ancients had also discovered other values crucial to modern physics, like the speed of light and gravity, long before there even was physics. Again, scholarship stands divided on the topic with one side standing by the claims and holding them as Vedic India’s scientific supremacy, and the other dismissing it wholesale as a late Hindutva fabrication out of political and ideological motivations. Both cannot be right. With this investigation, we hope to find out just which one is. Read with an open, apolitical, and irreligious mind, for truth follows reason and reason is not beholden to religion. As always, we shall examine multiple works by multiple scholars from both sides of the aisle in the interest of objectivity and completeness.

## Age of Earth

The Atharva Veda, known as the fourth Veda, comprises hymns, spells, and incantations that pertain to daily life, health, and practical wisdom. It explores various aspects of human concerns, such as illness, love, protection, and prosperity. The text is divided into twenty books or _kandas_ , each subdivided into hymns or _suktas_ which are further made up of verses or _mantras_. One such mantra is 8.2.21, which is often cited as a reference to human lifespan and, intriguingly, an arithmetical formula that, when resolved, gives the age of Earth:

     _śataṁ te ’yutaṁ hāyanān dve yuge trīṇi catvāri kṛṇmaḥ
    indrāgnī viśve devās te ’nu manyantām ahṛṇīyamānāḥ_

In 2013, Dr. Tulsi Ram Sharma published his English translation of the Veda and in it, he detailed the calculation. This wasn’t the first such work as Atharva Veda has been translated to both English and Hindi by several authors in the past. But this translation instantly caught much attention due to its reading of 8.2.21. The verse itself is quite cryptic and makes little sense at first glance:1

“O man, for you we fix a hundred years, and ten thousand years, and, further, ten thousand multiplied by two, three and four, years of age.”

Of the two lines in the verse, we’ll only confine our gaze to the first, for that’s the line that gives us the formula. According to Dr. Sharma, the first line is a mystical statement that gives the age of humanity or Earth as 4.32 billion years. If true, this is remarkably close to the modern value of 4.543 billion years. Considering the Atharva Veda was composed more than three thousand years ago, that’s an astonishing feat. If our ancients were really able to estimate a value like the age of Earth to this degree of precision, at a time when there was no writing and no scientific tools, India truly was the trailblazer in the world of physics.

To arrive at the figure, Dr. Sharma considers the total duration of a four-yuga cycle, which is 4,320,000 years, and multiplies it by 1,000.2 The duration of each cycle is divided as follows: Kaliyuga (432,000 years), Dvapara Yuga (864,000 years), Treta Yuga (1,296,000 years), and Satyuga (1,728,000 years).3 We will return to the nitty-gritties of these values a little later. For now, suffice to know that this is how he arrived at the value of 4.32 billion years.

But, as we already noted, Dr. Sharma is not the only one to have translated and interpreted the Atharva Veda. In fact, the first English translation, credited to English Indologist Ralph Griffith, goes as far back as 1896. Let’s see how he interprets the verse:4

“A hundred years, ten thousand years we give thee, ages two, three, four.”

Again, we’re only concerned with the first line here. The second line is just an ode to the gods seeking their blessings and is irrelevant to this investigation. Here, we find no formula and no mystical allusions. While Griffith’s translation more or less matches that of Dr. Sharma with slight variation, he offers no commentary that even alludes to any kind of geological timeline or the figure of 4.32 billion.

In 1905, the American philologist William Dwight Whitney, another non-Indian, attempted the task and published his own translation of the work:5

“A hundred, a myriad years, two periods (_yugá_), three, four, we make for thee.”

While this translation is a slight departure from the other two in semantics, it’s still no less cryptic. The problem of ambiguity remains, as does that conspicuous absence of any reference to the age of Earth or a figure running in billions. In 1980, one Devi Chand published yet another English translation, this one a little more verbose than those by the non-Indians:6

“O man, thine is the age of a hundred years, with two intervals of day and night and three seasons of summer, winter and rains, and four stages of childhood, youth, middle age, and old age. We render your age unbroken and complete.”

Now the reading gets clearer. So, the numbers two, three, and four correspond to the two quotidian intervals, three seasons, and four stages of human life, respectively. But it must be noted here that this is just an educated guess, for the verse itself does not clarify what the numbers correspond to. It does carry the word “yuga” but does not explicitly connect it to any of the three numbers that follow. At best, we can run it with two because that number comes right before yuga, but that wouldn’t make much sense as we know there are four of those and not just two. Once again, there’s no mention of a geological age of any kind. Unless we scroll down to the footnote where Chand admits that interpretation.

In the footnote, a solution is given with the assumption of the verse being a cryptogram of sorts. So the following numbers find mention in the first line of 8.2.21: _śata_ or hundred, _ayuta_ or ten thousand, _dve_ or two, _trīṇi_ or three, and _catvāri_ or four. The solution involves multiplying śata by ayuta to get the value of one million. To this, it then suggests appending dve, trīṇi, and catvāri to the left in the right-to-left order, which gives the final value of 4,321,000,000 or 4.32 billion.7 This looks like an awful lot of force-fitting to derive a pre-determined value. Which is likely why the author distances himself from this interpretation by putting it in a footnote and clarifying that this is how “some scholars” translate the verse.8

The most immediate question that arises here, for instance, is why the right-to-left placement of 2, 3, and 4? And why the concatenation? While the same method seems to have been employed by Dr. Sharma in his commentary, he does shed some light on the source of these decisions. As we’ve already noted earlier, 4,320,000 years is the value he arrived at by adding up the durations of all four yugas. He then goes on to define the cosmic age as a thousand such cycles, which is 4.32 billion years.9 One then ought to wonder, why thousand? Is it just because the verse says ayuta? But then, ayuta means ten thousand, not thousand. Another question one ought to ask is, whence 432? If we look at the verse, the numbers are listed in the order 2-3-4 and there’s no indication whatsoever that this should be flipped. Dr. Sharma calculates the value of 4,320,000 years, the total duration of a four-yuga cycle, by multiplying 432 by 10,000, a _niyuta_. But the word niyuta does not appear in the verse at all. Even if it did, how do we know it ought to be multiplied? Also, niyuta does not translate to a fixed value, it just means “very large number.” To assume it means 10,000 is yet another giant stretch in the service of force-fitting a pre-determined value to a millennia-old verse. But Dr. Sharma is not the inventor of these calculations. He references two older works as his sources—Kshemakarana Das Trivedi10 and Dr. Sripad Damodar Satavlekar,11 both Hindi. So, let’s look at them too.

Trivedi published his translation the same year Griffith released the second edition of his. His translation reads as follows:12

“O man, for you we do to a hundred and ten thousand years, two yugas, three yugas, and four yugas, respectively.”

While the translation itself seems to mean nothing at face value, Trivedi also offers a detailed commentary explaining his interpretation of the extremely cryptic statement. Part of this commentary is a table illustrating his calculations that result in the figure of 4,320,000 as the total length of a four-yuga cycle in “human years.”13 Let’s understand these calculations.

The fundamental premise of this cosmology is that time moves slower for the gods than it does for us mortals. Trivedi posits a factor of 360, _i.e._ 1 divine year = 360 human years. Each yuga is further subdivided into three phases—the dawn years or _sandhyā varṣa_ (this might sound counterintuitive to ears accustomed to reading sandhya as evening), the core years or _yuga varṣa_ , and the dusk years or _sandhyāṃśa varṣa_. The core period is ten times the duration of either twilight period. Also, the yugas are not uniform in length. The shortest is Kaliyuga with two hundred twilight years (hundred dawn years and hundred dusk years) and a thousand core years, in divine terms. Thus, a total of 1,200 years. From that point on, every preceding yuga adds a hundred divine years to each of its twilight phases. So, Dvapara gets two hundred dawn years, two hundred dusk years, and two thousand core years, for a total of 2,400 divine years. Treta similarly gets a total of six hundred twilight years with three thousand core years, all adding up to 3,600 divine years. And finally comes Satyuga with eight hundred twilight and four thousand core years for a total of 4,800 divine years. The complete cycle, thus, adds up to 4,800 + 3,600 + 2,400 + 1,200 = 12,000 divine years. Which translates into 12,000 × 360 = 4,320,000 years.

This is what Dr. Sharma takes as the duration of single four-yuga cycle when he speaks of the age of universe as a thousand such cycles, arriving at the figure of 4.32 billion.14 But there are several problems with this premise. First of all, Trivedi does not source these numbers from the Veda. He gets them from _Manusmriti_ , more specifically verses 1.69–70, by his own admission.15 While Manusmriti does speak of yuga durations in divine years, it does not specify the numerical relationship between divine and human years:16

     _catvāryāhuḥ sahasrāṇi varṣāṇāṃ tat kṛtaṃ yugam,
    tasya tāvatśatī sandhyā sandhyāṃśaśca tathāvidhaḥ._
    “They say that four thousand years are what is the Kṛta-cycle; as many hundred years form the juncture; and of equal measure is the juncture-end.” (rough trans. Jha)

    ekāpāyena vartante sahasrāṇi śatāni ca._
    “In each of the other time-cycles, along with their junctures and juncture-ends, the thousands and hundreds are reduced by one.” (rough trans. Jha)

But that does not mean the correlation is out of Trivedi’s whim. The figure 360, along with the rest of Manusmriti’s calculations, comes from yet another work of a similar vintage, the _Vishnu Purana_. According to this work, a divine day corresponds to a human year. Given that, in Puranic calculations, a year consists of 360 days, a divine year thus equates to 360 divine days or 360 human years.17

So, the arithmetic does seem to have textual validity, but Atharva Veda is more than three thousand years old and the Purana in question, less than two thousand. The Vedic verse we set out with, does not define the yugas. a work that came more than 1,500 years later does. A later work cannot serve as evidence for a claim attributed to an older work. Let’s examine the Atharva Veda verse anyway:

     _śataṁ te ’yutaṁ hāyanān dve yuge trīṇi catvāri kṛṇmaḥ_

Here is a breakdown of the statement, one word at a time:

  * _śataṁ_ (शतम्) — “a hundred”

  *  _te_ (ते) — “your”; this is the second-person pronoun in the singular, meaning “for you” or “your.”

  *  _’yutaṁ_ (अयुतम्) — “ten thousand”

  *  _hāyanān_ (हायनान्) — “years”; this term refers to years, from the root _hayana_ (a year).

  * _dve_ (द्वे) — “two”

  *  _yuge_ (युगे) — “in a yuga”; Yuga here can refer to a specific age or epoch, often used in Hindu cosmology to denote an era or a long cycle of time.

  * _trīṇi_ (त्रीणि) — “three”

  *  _catvāri_ (चत्वारि) — “four”

  *  _kṛṇmaḥ_ (कृण्मः) — “we do” or “we make”; derived from the root _kṛ_ (to do or to make), kṛṇmaḥ is the first-person plural form, meaning “we make” or “we accomplish.”




Putting it all together, the verse can be interpreted as:

“We [wish] for you a hundred [years], ten thousand [years], two yugas, three, and four—thus do we make [the prayer].”

In essence, this verse appears to be a blessing for longevity, using different multiples of time—hundreds, thousands, and yugas—as a poetic expression of wishing for a long life. There is nothing in the verse that defines either yuga in terms of years or cosmology in terms of yuga-cycles. There’s no indication whatsoever that the universe is a thousand yuga-cycles old, as claimed by Dr. Sharma. Therefore, it’s safe to conclude that the Atharva Veda does not tell us about the age of the universe, nor does it attempt to. Even if it did, we know for a fact that the age of universe and the age of Earth are not even remotely the same thing. If we take the yuga-cycle as calculated in the Purana, and multiply it by a thousand, although doing so would be a highly arbitrary move, we get 4.32 billion years as the age of the universe, not just Earth. Today we know that the universe is more than three times as old.

Having said that, we must grant that for the longest time, humans would have had no reason to see the universe as separately from Earth. To them, God would have created everything more or less simultaneously. Thus, even if the figure of 4.32 billion years was posited as the age of Creation in the Purana, it won’t be wrong to read it as the age of Earth in their worldview. And although the value cannot be attributed to the Atharva Veda, it’d still be a remarkable feat if an early century work like the Vishnu Purana managed to achieve this level of numerical precision in its imagination. We’re talking about a period when the Christian world was still reckoning this value in thousands, six thousand to be exact.

But the question of how we arrive at the decision that the universe (or Earth) is a thousand yuga-cycles old remains unresolved. For now, it appears to be a simple case of retrofitting. The only value we’re sure of is 4.32 million, which is nowhere close to the age of Earth.

## Gravity

In the late seventeenth century, Newton discovered gravity through observation and mathematical reasoning. The famous story of an apple falling in 1665 or 1666 often marks the start of his inquiry, though he didn’t credit it as the sole moment of discovery. Newton realized the same force pulling an apple down also governed celestial bodies, like the Moon orbiting Earth. His _Principia Mathematica_ (1687) formalized universal gravitation and transformed physics. However, this well-known story faces a challenge as gravity may have been known in India centuries before Newton, with some tracing its mention to the ancient Rigveda.18

A large number of verses from not just Rigveda but also Yajur and Atharva Vedas are cited as proofs of Vedic India’s familiarity with gravity. Let’s examine them all. First on our list are RV 1.6.5 and 8.12.30. Both verses are translated by the proponents of Vedic gravity as:

This translation can be found repeated verbatim by a large number of accounts on all social media platforms from Twitter to LinkdIn to Facebook to personal blogs, none citing a source. Which is strange because that would imply that the two verses are identical when they’re absolutely not. 1.6.5 does not even talk about gravity at all:19

As we can see, there’s no reference to anything cosmological, much less gravity, here. It’s more about a legend where Indra discovered the cows that belonged to the gods but were stolen and hidden in a cave by a class of thieves called _panis_. So where does the cosmological interpretation come from? Turns out, it’s Dayananda Saraswati:20

“Just as human beings grasp and hold objects around them, similarly the Sun, with its strong force, gathers worldly substances through its rays. Afterward, the Sun, by piercing them, destroys them and, with the help of the winds, which carry them to the sky and other regions, continuously moves them up and down, always raising and lowering them in the space or the pole.”

Saraswati reads the verse as an ode to the Sun’s immense power whereby its rays pierce and destroy celestial objects the same way wind destroys the trees. In a departure from Western translations, he reads _guhā_ as space rather than cave.21 While this establishes a cosmological reference, there’s still no sign of gravity. Not in Max Müller’s interpretation, not in Stephanie Jamison’s interpretation, not in Horace Wilson’s interpretation, and not even in Dayananda Saraswati’s interpretation. RV 1.6.5 has nothing to do with gravity. But what about 8.12.30? First of all, it’s not at all identical to 1.6.5, as we already noted earlier:22

This verse extols Indra for placing the blazing Sun in place in the sky, a feat that resulted in all elements of Creation submitting to His authority and dominion. Here again, there’s no term that could even metaphorically be interpreted as “power of attraction” as claimed in the translation popular on social media. “O Indra, by putting forth your mighty rays, which possess the qualities of gravitation and attraction-illumination and motion—keep up the entire universe in order through the Power of your attraction.”

Words like gravitation and attraction are force-fitted without any lexical backing. The actual verse talks about horses and the living world’s subservience to Indra:24

This is the translation Wilson agrees with too, but Dr. Sharma differs. According to his reading, the verse is about “radiations of dynamic forces”:25

“Since the radiations of your dynamic forces expand day by day, the entire worlds of the expansive universe are sustained in order in obedience to your law.”

Whose radiations and whose forces, one might ask. Some interpret it as the Sun, but the entire hymn is addressed to Indra, not Sun. Therefore, even in this interpretation, there’s no reasonable ground to invoke gravity. A more straightforward reading would be that the world is subject to the rule of Indra by whose grace the world prospers and grows every day. Essentially, it’s all down to how we read _harī_. The word has two meanings, among others. In one sense, it’s a reddish-brown horse, the bay horse, especially one that belongs to Indra.26 In another, it refers to the Sun.27 Neither interpretation leads us to gravity.

“The sun moves in its own orbit but holding earth and other heavenly bodies in a manner that they do not collide with each other through force of attraction.”

If this interpretation held, Vedic gravity would be irrefutable. But as per Jamison’s translation, it does not:28

“Savita, the sun, lord of the golden hand of generosity, creator of the sap of life, shines across the regions of heaven and earth. It destroys and eliminates disease and dirt, spreads its light all over, blazes in the heavens and over-reaches the regions of darkness.”

“O Lord, just as this Sun-world, maintaining connection of attraction with many people, illuminates all objects while spreading light and creating harmony in the Earthly realm—may you be naturally endowed with such qualities.”

It’s in this part that he uses the word _ākarṣaṇa_ or attaction, which is erroneously taken to indicate gravity. Not only is this an interpretation rather than translation, but it also offers, in and of itself, no reason whatsoever to be so confidently read as indicative of gravity or any kind of physical force.

But the verse, again, fails to agree. According to Jamison, it has more to do with Indra slaying evil creatures with his mace:32

“Indra, lord of light and power, like the sun, with his actions, i.e., waves of gravitation, holds the earth and manifests its vast expanse. He breaks the cloud formed in the Rohini constellation, striking over its shoulders and releases the showers of rain on earth.”

That’s one interpretation against all others. Is that proof enough? No word in the original Sanskrit verse text translates into gravity or force. Therefore, any interpretation reading those concepts into it is bound to be subjective opinion.

The last piece is RV 10.149.1 which proponents translate as the gravity-induced motion of the Earth around the Sun:

“The Sun has tied Earth and other planets through attraction and moves them around itself as if a trainer moves newly trained horses around itself holding their reins.”

This is the only verse in all of Rigveda that everyone unanimously interprets as a reference to the motion of the Earth around the Sun under influence. Jamison, Wilson, Sharma, and Saraswati, all agree with this interpretation with only mild semantic variations. Some advance as counterpoint, Nirukta 10.32:39

“Savitr has fixed the earth with supports.”

Their argument is that the Earth could not be believed to be fixed in place and in motion around the Sun, both at once. But the argument doesn’t hold because _fixed_ could also mean fixed in a well-defined orbit. If anything, this indicates that our ancients were well-aware that the Earth went around the Sun, rather than the other way around, and that it did so in a non-random orbit. Which in and of itself is a remarkable feat of observational intuition. Moreover, the notion also finds additional corroboration in Atharva Veda 4.11.1:40

     _anadvdndddhdra prthivimuta dydmanadvdndddhdrorvantariksam,
    anadvdndddhdra pradisah sadurviranadvdnvisvarh bhuvanamd vivesa._
    “The cosmic burden bearer of the universe holds and sustains the earth, the heaven and the vast intervening space. He bears all the extensive six quarters of space and indeed it is Anadvan, the burden-bearer, who pervades the entire worlds of the universe in existence, and it is he who holds and sustains them.” (trans. Sharma)

But before we rush to approve the Vedic claim over gravity, we must recognize that Newton isn’t exactly credited with discovering gravity. He was not the first to observe that things that go up, come down. It does not take scientific or mathematical jugglery to establish one of the most ubiquitous observations of life. Humans would have registered the phenomenon for thousands of years even before the first civilization was raised. The fact that celestial bodies including the Sun and the stars are in perpetual motion along strict paths as if dictated by some hidden force would also not go unnoticed by any sufficiently observant society the sustenance of which depended heavily on the night-sky configuration. Both agriculture and navigation demand such observations over years and centuries and their meticulous documentation. Thus, celestial motion is no modern discovery either. Nor do these have to be Vedic monopolies.

What Newton is credited with is not the discovery of gravity or motion but their quantification with mathematical equations. That’s not something anyone did before him, much less the authors of the Vedas. Is it possible they could have? Sure, but is there any proof they did? No.

## Speed of Light

A popular claim credits the authors of the Rigveda with a remarkably accurate calculation of the speed of light more than three thousand years before Rømer. If true, this would be a spectacular feat in the absence of telescopes and sophisticated implements that Rømer and his contemporaries had at their disposal. That’s because unlike gravity, this value is not merely a function of observation and calculation but also precision. Precision that demands more than the naked eye and Bronze Age measuring tools. It’s natural to then wonder how they pulled it off. But before that, we must trace the origin of this claim. And that happens to be not the Rigveda directly but a later commentary by a scholar named Sayana. This commentary contains a snippet that was translated by renowned computer scientist Subhash Kak as a statement of the speed of light in terms of _yojanas_ per half-_nimesha_ :41

     _tathā ca smaryate—‘yojanānāṃ sahastre dve dve śate dve ca yojane. ekena nimiṣārdhena kramamāṇa.’_
    “Thus it is remembered: [O Sun] you who traverse 2,202 yojanas in half a nimeṣa.” (trans. Kak)

Kak published his translation in a paper on _arXiv_ , the world’s largest open repository of non-peer-reviewed papers.42 Sayana lived in the Vijayanagara Empire and is dated to as recently as the fourteenth century, more than 2,500 years after Rigveda. The comment in question pertains to RV 1.50.4, which itself says nothing about the speed of light in any translation:4344

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _taraṇir viśvadarśato jyotiṣkṛd asi sūrya,
    viśvam ā bhāsi rocanam._
    “Transiting, visible to all, a light-maker are you, o Sun.
    You illuminate the whole luminous realm.” (trans. Jamison)
    “O Sun, light of the world, creator of light and Light Itself, it is you alone who light the lights of the universe and reveal the wondrous worlds of beauty and glory. You are the savior, you are the redeemer, taking us across the seas of existence.” (trans. Sharma)

So, we can end the investigation right here with the conclusion that the claim is false and that the authors of the Vedas had no inkling of this constant. However, Sayana, it would appear, did. Which is still no mean feat because the West wouldn’t come up with a calculation for at least another two and a half centuries. That too with far less accuracy. Sayana does seem to have outstripped Rømer, Huygens, and Bradley by a wide, wide margin. Is that the case though? Let’s now return to Kak’s paper.

So, it’s 2,202 yojanas in half a nimesha or wink. Naturally, the accuracy predicates on correctly defining the two units, yojana for distance and nimesha for time. Let’s start with distance—yojana. The unit has been discussed in some detail in the earlier article on Lanka, but let’s briefly go over it again anyway.

* * *

* * *

_Markandeya Purana_ says 15,340 meters or a little over 9.5 miles, as per a 1911 paper.45 _Surya Suddhanta_ , the primary treatise much of Indian astronomy derives from, indirectly gives five miles to a yojana. Now, this work has been dated to the middle of the first millennium AD. Coeval with it is Aryabhata’s work which uses an eight-mile yojana for its calculations.46 That’s a 60% variation in value in near contemporaneous works. Three different works, three different values. If we go further back in time, we come across Kautilya’s _Arthashastra_ which defines yojana as 4.54 miles according to a 1912 paper by John Faithful Fleet published in the Journal of the Royal Asiatic Society.47 But according to Fleet, even in Kautilya’s own time, at least two different yojanas were in vogue—a short yojana of 4.54 miles popular among the general public, and a long yojana of about 9 miles popular in astronomical calculations.48

In sum, we have values ranging from 4.5 miles to 9 miles, depending on function and period. And this is only until about 500 AD. Sayana is still about a thousand years away. There are other values too, but we’ll not explore them. For now, all that matters is that the Rigveda does not define it and those that do, give us 9.5, 9, 8, 5, and 4.5 miles, just to name a few. Coming to nimesha now.

Here too, little consensus exists. Of course, it’s difficult to have a consensus on how long it takes to blink once. The Puranic definition of time starts with _truti_ , which has been calculated to about 473 microseconds in _Bhagvata Purana_.49 From there, it defines nimesha as 900 trutis which comes to about 425.7 milliseconds, a little under half a second.50 At the same time, _Vishnu Purana_ gives us a nimesha of 0.2 seconds.51 We’re already seeing a wild swing withing the Purana corpus alone, but there’s also _Bhavishya Purana_ which gives us 0.018 seconds.52 Outside of the Puranas, we have a nimesha of 0.24 seconds in Kautilya’s Arthashastra53 and 0.43 seconds in the Vedas.54 To bring it all together, we have values ranging from 0.018 seconds to 0.426 seconds within the Puranas and 0.24 seconds from long before the Puranas.

So, the overall swing is as follows: Nimesha between 0.018 seconds (Bhavishya Purana) and 0.43 seconds (Vedas), and yojana between 4.54 miles (Arthashastra) and 9.5 miles (Markandeya).

Plugging these values into Sayana’s verse, we get a range of final values for the speed of light. The speed he gives is 2,202 yojanas per half nimesha, which is 4,404 yojanas/nimesha. For the lower end, we take Arthashastra’s yojana (smallest) and Vedic nimesha (longest). This gives us 4,404 * 4.54 = 19,994.16 miles in one nimesha or 0.43 seconds. Or 46,498 miles per second. For the upper end, we take Markandeya’s yojana (longest) and Bhavishya’s nimesha (shortest). This gives us 4.404 * 9.5 = 41,838 miles in one nimesha or 0.018 seconds. Or 232,4333 miles per second.

The speed of light is 186,282 miles per second, 139,784 miles more than the lower value and only 46 miles less than the upper value. The latter seems to be the closest to the actual, so we can go with that. Which means Markandeya’s yojana and Bhavishya’s nimesha. The question now is, what are the odds Sayana was considering these specific values out of all he had at his disposal and why? This is an excellent case of retroactive curve-fitting. Without a telescope to observe celestial events with any reasonable accuracy, it’s practically impossible to calculate the speed of light. And there is no evidence whatsoever that telescopes existed or were known in the Bronze Age, either in India or anyplace else.

Before we close this discussion, one last bit. Sayana’s statement isn’t even about light. It’s about the Sun. Now, humanity has known about the Sun’s motion, relative to Earth, for thousands of years. Thus, a fourteenth-century scholar estimating its speed across the sky purely from observation is not an improbability.

Kak, however, posits that Sun was just a shorthand Sayana used for sunlight. A synecdoche, if you will. Kak’s reasoning is that interpreting “Sun” literally in this context results in a solar orbit of 2.55 billion miles around the Earth, far from the actual 93 million miles. The “solar orbit” here actually refers to Earth’s orbit around the Sun. But to call that problematic assumes that Sayana could not go wrong in his calculations or assumptions. Remember, he was a poet, not an astronomer. But still, let’s see what Bhagvata Purana says about the speed of Sun, because it does in 5.21.10:55

     _yadā caindryāḥ puryāḥ pracalate pañcadaśa-ghaṭikābhir yāmyāṁ sapāda-koṭi-dvayaṁ yojanānāṁ sārdha-dvādaśa-lakṣāṇi sādhikāni copayāti._
    “When he starts from the city of Indra, he traverses in fifteen Ghatikas (six hours) the distance of two crores and a quarter, twelve lacs and a half and twenty-five thousands of Yojanas and reaches the city of Yama.” (trans. Rau)

This verse tells us that the Sun travels 23,775,000 yojanas in six hours. Since a day is roughly taken to be twelve hours from sunup to sundown, this distance should correspond to a quarter of the entire circumference of the Sun’s orbit around the Earth (actually Earth’s around the Sun). Which gives us a solar orbit of 23,775,000 * 4 = 95,100,000 yojanas. If we take yojana as 7.5 miles, according to Kak’s interpretation of Aryabhata,56 we get 713.25 million miles. Once again, Earth’s orbit around the Sun is only 93 million miles.

## Conclusion

There are many more examples of modern science being projected upon works of remote antiquity, mostly for political and ideological reasons. But with these three big-ticket examples, it must be relatively clearer that these are mere acts of retrofitting and have no basis in scriptural or historical reality.

We don’t need thorough deep dives like this in order to arrive at this conclusion. An excellent reasoning tool called Occam’s razor can help fix many such dilemmas fairly quickly. The idea is to have a common-sense approach to claims that seem outlandish or improbable. The simplest explanation is often the correct explanation. Especially when the claim demands belief in esoteric ideas such as divine intervention, supernatural powers, or magic. This approach can, without complex investigation, help dismiss ideas like prehistoric head transplant, test-tube babies, hundred babies, telepathy, teleportation, _pushpaka vimanas_ , and dozens of similarly mythical and impossible ideas. Ancient texts have an ocean of observational, empirical, and philosophical wisdom to offer. Modern science isn’t part of it.
