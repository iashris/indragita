*How the Fall of Two Empires Birthed Islamic Terrorism*

Some four hundred years ago, there used to be three epicenters of Islamic power—the Safavids in Persia, the Ottomans in the rest of the Middle East, and the Mughals in India, each unilaterally claiming the ultimate custody of the faith. For this story, we’re primarily concerned with the last two, the Ottomans and the Mughals. This remained the state of affairs for a good two hundred years or so before things took a dramatic turn, at the turn of the eighteenth century. Two events drive this story, one involving the Ottomans and the other involving the Mughals. But before we go there, a quick background on the two empires’ history and relationship.

Among the three, the Ottomans were the first to emerge and the Mughals the last. The rise of the Ottomans was the culmination of a tectonic shift in Islamic politics. Islam originated in Arabia and for the first few centuries, remained an Arab-dominated polity. It was at the turn of the tenth century that other ethnicities—the Persians and the Turks—started making their presence noted in the Abbasid power circles. The thirteenth-century Fall of Baghdad served as the final blow and created a vacuum that was quickly filled by what would soon become the Islamic world’s first non-Arab Caliphate, the Ottomans. The Turkic age was now in session. They were everywhere. While one came to dominate the Near East as the Ottoman Empire, another lorded over Central Asia, the Timurids. As the sixteenth century rolled around, the latter gave way to several new powers among them two that form this story’s trinity—the Safavids in Iran and the Mughals in India.

The Mughals and the Ottomans shared a fairly stable relationship. Of respectful skepticism. In other words, it toggled between resentment and goodwill with an underlying sense of distance. The Mughals, for instance, never explicitly recognized the Ottomans as a universal Caliphate. But at the same time, they did not explicitly deny it. The Ottomans, on their part, never recognized the Mughal claim of imperial primacy either. Under the surface of diplomatic bonhomie, there remained a constant state of ideological tension.1 They just feigned indifference. To an extent, like the India and China of today. With that crucial piece of context in place, we should now be good to proceed with the story.

## The Great Turkish War

Toward the end of the seventeenth century, the Ottomans went to war with Europe. We don’t call this a crusade but for all intents and purposes, it bore the character of one—the Caliphate on one side and the entire European Christendom (with the curious exception of France) on the other, led by the Holy Roman Empire. The last time such a battle of faiths was fought on Anatolian soil, it had ended the Eastern Roman Empire. By now, the Ottomans were in control of not just the Near East but also the Balkans and Eastern Mediterranean. Now they were knocking at the doors of Vienna, the seat of the mighty Habsburgs. The latter was no ordinary dynasty, for it held the throne of not just Austria but the Holy Roman Empire itself. This placed the Austrian emperor at the helm of the world’s single most powerful confederacy, and the Muslims were now threatening to change just that.

The Great Turkish War, also known as the War of the Holy League, lasted sixteen years and ended on January 26, 1699, with the Treaty of Karlowitz. Holy League was the name of the Christian coalition. The Caliphate lost territory for the first time in its history marking the beginning of its downfall. Save a few small pockets in the Balkans, all was lost in Europe. Even these small pockets would leave the Ottoman domain in the 1718 Treaty of Passarowitz. For millions of Muslims across the world, this was a major setback. The Ottomans would last another century and a quarter, but make no mistake, its glory days were now in the past. The years ahead would all go by in a constant state of territorial decline with no hopes whatsoever of a revival. The Ottomans weren’t just a political power but also ideological. The Sultan was traditionally the _Khādim al-Ḥaramayn aš-Šarīfayn_ or “Custodian of the Two Holy Mosques.” The dynasty had held the privilege for over two centuries now. This is what gave it its doctrinal clout, besides political. Unable to reconcile with the dynasty’s decline, the Muslim community needed a figurehead to help them make sense of all this and lead them to stability.

There was also another set of Muslims, perhaps smaller in number, who saw in the Ottoman decline a rare opportunity Islam to its lost ideological glory. These were mostly the Arabs. Despite its Arab origins, the religion had been under Persian and Turkish dominance for centuries now. The Ottomans, despite their Caliphate, were still seen as a foreign power in these circles. A foreign power far removed from the fundamentals laid down by the Caliphs of Arabia back in the early years of Islam. This wasn’t just an ethnic discord but also doctrinal. The Ottomans followed and patronized a number of Sufi schools of Islamic thought that were in direct conflict with the Sunni core as laid down by Muhammad. Naturally, a military setback for such a regime was a window of opportunity for anyone looking to “Make Islam Great Again.”

## Death of a Tyrant

Just as the Ottomans were a spiritual anchor for the Muslims at large, the Mughals were one for the Muslims in India. Especially under Aurangzeb but also under his two predecessors. Naturally, his death in 1707 was a major setback to the Muslims on the subcontinent. It’s a crazy coincidence that both Islamic nuclei suffered the same leadership crisis almost at the same time. Not only had Aurangzeb bled his coffers dry in his quest for territorial dominance, his personal austerity notwithstanding, he’d also made all kinds of adversaries both within and without who were only glad to see a window of opportunity in his death. Just as the Great Turkish War marked the beginning of the Ottoman decline, Aurangzeb’s death marked the beginning of the Mughal decline. His successors were too weak to resist incursions from both Muslims like Nadir Shah and Hindus like the Baji Rao.

The Marathas had been in a state of war with the Mughals since long before Aurangzeb’s death, going as far back as the very inception of the Maratha polity under Shivaji. However, the conflict only acquired a more comprehensive character after Shivaji’s death in 1680. The state of war is often referred to as the War of 27 Years. That’s how long the series of battles and skirmishes lasted. Until Aurangzeb died and offered Peshwa Baji Rao a crucial opportunity to finally oust the Mughals from dominance over the subcontinent in 1738.

By now, the Mughal throne was with its eighth occupant since Aurangzeb’s death. That is less than four years per successor. It was a remarkably volatile situation. Islam suffered too. Aurangzeb’s immediate successor lasted less than four months before being killed by his own half-brother who subsequently took the throne as Bahadur Shah. Among the things he did upon coronation was the radical move to insert Ali’s name as the custodian of Islam in the Friday sermons or _khutbah al-Jumuʿa_. To understand why this was radical it’s necessary to understand what khutbah even means. While it’s just a sermon delivered after the call to prayer or adhan—ideally the noon prayer, but could be another as well—every Friday, what makes it crucial is that it’s the part where the ruler’s name is mentioned, unless delivered in a secular democracy like India. Back in the day, it was the caliph’s name. So significant was this mention that skipping it was tantamount to sedition. In sum, the khutbah was the bridge between the political and the doctrinal.

Being mentioned in the Friday sermon was a crucial source of a sovereign’s legitimacy which is why even after the Abbasids declined in political influence, its successor entities continued to rule in the name of the Abbasid caliph of the time. This was, however, a strictly Sunni practice. To the Shiʿas, it was an innovation or _bedʿa_ to mention an earthly ruler instead of their Imam. It should now be clear why the addition of Ali’s name by Bahadur Shah was such a controversial move. The Sunnis saw it as a declaration of the emperor’s conversion to Shiʿism. We don’t know exactly whether he converted, but we do know that the optics created a significant furor. In many places, people even started resenting the Khutbah itself.

By the time of Muhammad Shah, the thirteenth Mughal, the empire had already started to have problems in Gujarat and Deccan. His reign saw the loss of both. Then came the Marathas. Although they failed to take Delhi itself, they did manage to eat into large swathes of Malwa and Bundelkhand, severely sapping the Mughals of their clout. Before the latter could ever recover, in came Nadir Shah’s sack of Delhi. It’s against this backdrop of declining Islamic power in post-Aurangzeb India that a revolution took shape.

## Waliullah

Qutb ud-Din Ahmad was born to a Hanafi theologian named Shah Abdur Rahim who had helped compile Aurangzeb’s Shariʿa-based code, Fatawa-e-Alamgiri. Naturally, he had an upbringing of high piety which came to define the rest of his trajectory. After several years in his father’s seminary, young Ahmad came to Medina in 1730 for higher Islamic learning at the age of twenty-seven. He’d spend a little over a year in the city under the tutelage of several theologians including one Shaikh Abu Tahir Muhammad ibn Ibrahim al-Kurani al-Mada, al-Kurani for short. Al-Kurani himself was an ardent follower of ibn Taymiyya, a thirteenth-century Hanbali hardliner from Damascus.

Upon return, Ahmad assumed the honorific of Waliullah and started preaching as a Naqshbandi initiate in Delhi. He made no secret of his contempt for the Marathas and the Shiʿas and openly proselytized the important contribution of _jihad_ to the rise of Islam. At this point, it must be asserted that he is said to have been categorically against forced conversions or violence.2 Which might seem funny in juxtaposition to his admiration for jihad. And no, not the spiritual kind, but the one that involves brute force and conquers territories. In his seminal work _Ḥujjat Allah al-Baligha_ (Conclusive Argument from God), he says this about Muhammad:3

     _“He established the greatest Caliphate, and with those who accompanied him he waged Jihad on those who opposed them until the command of God was fulfilled despite their unwillingness.”_

Be that as it may, his unremitting advocacy of Shariʿa found instant resonance. Dehlawi also superimposed upon Indian Muslims, an overarching Arab identity. Shortly after the publication of this work, Delhi faced two major invasions, first by the Marathas and then by the Persians. The latter, under Nadir Shah, proved particularly disastrous. The city, it’s said, was reduced to rubble and the carnage was unprecedented. Trying to make sense of the apocalyptic state of affairs, Dehlawi attributed it to the Indian Muslim community’s departure from the Islamic doctrinal core and its adoption of un-Islamic Persian and Indian practices. Over the centuries, Indian Islam had accrued several cultural and doctrinal elements that ran afoul of the core Islamic values as taught by the jurists of the Arab world. Intercessory prayers at holy shrines were one such example. Dehlawi resented these accretions as bedʿa and called for a revival to purge them from praxis. He even started identifying as a man of Arab descent superior to non-Muslim Indians:4

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published
    
    
     _“We are strangerous in this land (of India). Our fathers and grandfathers came to live here from abroad. For us, Arab descent and the Arabic language are causes of pride, these two things bring us nearer to the Lord of the First and the Last, the noblest of Prophets and Apostles…We must give thanks to God for this grace by holding on as much as possible to the customs and traditions of the ancient Arabs, from whom the Prophet came and to whom he addressed himself, and by safeguarding ourselves from the penetration of Persian traditions and Indian habits.”_

While his endorsement of patience and pragmatic nonviolence didn’t resonate, this did. This was the birth of a “Muslim before Indian” sentiment that forms the trope of all Islamist thoughts today. This is probably why despite being proficient in Urdu and Persian, he chose Arabic as the language for his magnum opus, _Ḥujjat_.5 That being said, he was also pragmatic enough to translate the Qurʾan into Persian, a first. Persian was the language of Delhi’s elite and he knew that making the scripture accessible in that language was key to effective propagation.

Over the years, Dehlawi grew immensely influential not just in India but also abroad. He would communicate his teachings via his myriad epistles addressed to scholars, kings, and sultans all over the world. Among them was the Afghan warlord Ahmad Shah Abdali. In a letter to the latter, Dehlawi even solicited military intervention to rescue Indian Muslims from the clutches of the _kufr_ or infidels—the Marathas. In it, he deplored the subservience of Indian Muslims to the rule of the infidels and expressed worries that should the situation continue, Muslims and non-Muslims of the subcontinent would become indistinguishable.6 It’s a rather long letter that opens with a drivel on the pitiable condition of Indian Muslims, then slips into copious flattery of the addressee as “the only king with sufficient manliness, gallantry, and foresight for the task of annihilating polytheism,” before reminding him of his “religious duty to declare a jihad and liberate enslaved Muslims.”7

Interestingly, though, the letter does not mention Abdali’s name anywhere8 which has led many modern Sufi scholars to dismiss it as a call for invasion. Some even question the very existence of it. The letter, however, exists and is part of a compilation by Dehlawi’s own cousin and brother-in-law Shah Muhammad ʿIshiq. That he dabbled in politics despite his Sufi profile is abundantly attested by the fact that this collection exists, for every letter in it is political. At most, one could argue that the epistle in question wasn’t necessarily addressed to Abdali since it’s neither named nor dated. Which would still be a stretch, given it’s clearly addressed to a foreign king, calls for an invasion against the Hindus, and is written _after_ Nadir Shah’s sack of Delhi. Nadir Shah is ruled out, which leaves us with someone who invaded India after him but well within Dehlawi’s lifetime. Abdali fits the profile. Unless the letter was addressed to someone else and ignored. Even then, the fact that he solicited an invasion stands. Ironically, Abdali was part of Nadir Shah’s horde during his 1739 sack of Delhi.

The invasion of India was second on Abdali’s to-do list upon coronation, the first being to switch from Abdali to Durrani. He would mount no fewer than eight invasions into India, all for plunder. When Dehlawi died in 1762, the sixth was underway. The Mughals never really recovered from this loss of influence. Durrani’s invasions did little to restore the Islamic hegemony that Dehlawi died wishing for. On the contrary, less than a decade in Delhi came under _de facto_ Maratha occupation. The Mughal emperor was now a Maratha puppet. Another thirty years and he was a British puppet. The “empire” was now confined to the walls of Shahjahanabad and the “emperor,” a British pensioner with no power beyond his title.

These were the early years of the nineteenth century. Dehlawi’s eldest Shah Abdul Azziz was now at the helm of his father’s seminary. Just as his father resented the Marathas, the son resented the British. Both for the same reason—diminished in Islamic hegemony. And just as the father had invited Abdali to rescue Islam, the son issued a _fatwa_ declaring India a _dal al-harb_ or house of war. This was a call to jihad to free Delhi from the clutches of the kufr.

Like his influential father, Azziz too had an enormous following which only grew larger with the fatwa. Three years after this announcement, a youth from Raebareli named Sayyid Ahmad came under his tutelage to study the late Dehlawi’s puritanical ideals. This lasted about two years after which he returned to his hometown to enlist as a Pindari mercenary. But ten years later, the Pindari leadership ended up making peace with the British which left foot soldiers like Sayyid Ahmad Shahid unemployed. That’s when he made a second trip to Delhi for a second stint in Dehlawi’s seminary. This time, he made quite a name for himself with his committment to jihad and Shariʿa. By his thirties, he had already made a trip to Mecca and further refined his ideology into a thorough blend of spirituality and militancy. Now he was preaching doctrinal sanction for armed jihad and even had members of the Dehlawi family under his tutelage which further amplified his appeal. They had started calling him Syed Ahmad Barelvi after his hometown and seeing him as the rightful inheritor of Waliullah Dehlawi’s legacy.

Barelvi eventually fell in a battle against the Sikhs in Balakot, a major blow to the subcontinent’s Islamic aspirations. Dehlawi’s seminary was still standing but not for long. In the crackdown following the 1857 mutiny, the British ordered its closure. Now the Muslims of India were truly leaderless. Both politically and spiritually. But the jihad must go on. So an alumnus from Bihar, Syed Nazeer Husain, floated his own seminary9 and named it Ahl-i Hadith. While Husain brought Dehlawi’s thoughts to the new movement, his cofounder Siddiq Hasan Khan brought strong Wahhabi thoughts. Khan, the Nawab of Bhopal, was strongly influenced by a man named Ghulam Rasul, also known as ʿAbd al-Haq Benarasi and Najdi Shaikh. Put a pin on this name, he was called Najdi for a reason.

Nine years later, another group inaugurated a madrassah at the old Chattah Wali Masjid of Deoband. They named it Darul ‘Uloom Deoband, a premier institution of hardline Islamic learning. The school’s first rector and one of its founding members was Qasim Nanawtawi, himself a student of Syed Nazeer Husain.

In sum, the failure of 1857 and subsequent closure of Dehlawi’s school spawned at least three big-ticket Islamist movements among countless others. Despite the mutual disagreements among them today, their origins saw abundant ideological overlaps, especially in their objectives of a pan-Islamic hegemony in the Indian subcontinent. Deobandi has often seen itself as an extension of Dehlawi’s Madrasah-i Rahimiyah as corroborated in the poem _Shah Waliullah aur Darul Uloom Deoband_ by Naseem Ahmad Faridi Amrohvi. A more legitimate link, however, comes from the seminary itself which names Waliullah as one of its ideological patriarchs.10 Deoband alumni would later go on to found some of the most violent terror organizations in the world including Taliban, Jaish-e-Mohammed, and nearly a dozen others.

Interestingly, when bin Laden’s al-Qaeda turned its gaze upon India, the Deobandi Ulama was prompt in denouncing them for their extremism. While this could have political motivations, a theory often advanced is the conflict between al-Qaeda’s Wahhabism and Deobandi’s Hanafism. But that would only be partially true because Wahhabism has a much larger footprint on the Deobandi heritage, than one might feel comfortable admitting.

## Wahhab

ʿAbd al-Wahhab was a close contemporary of Waliullah. In fact, it’s possible that both were born in the same year. Wahhab grew up and preached an extremely narrow interpretation of Shariʿa in the central Arabian region of Najd. It’s important to note that the humiliating Ottoman defeat at the hands of the Holy League was only four years before Wahhab’s birth. The Caliphate was on shaky grounds and so was the Muslim world’s faith in its ability to lead. Wahhab made a trip to Madina and spent his twenties studying the Hadiths there under a Naqshbandi and Shafiʿi jurist from India named Muhammad Hayat al-Sindi. Al-Sindi was a fan of of ibn Taymiyya and student of Shaikh Abu Tahir Muhammad ibn Ibrahim al-Kurani al-Mada. Does the name ring a bell? It’s through al-Sindi that young Wahhab got indoctrinated in ibn Taymiyya’s ideals of militant jihad. Having finished his studies,he returned to his hometown Uyayna and started preaching.

Eventually, his intolerant Hanbali precepts came in direct conflict with the Muslims in the region who subscribed to other creeds and didn’t view the Caliphate as unfavorably as him. To the extent that at one point, he and his followers became subject to excommunication and were even barred from performing Haj. When all of Arabia seemed adversarial, one man stretched a hand of amity—the Emir of neighboring Diriyah. His name was Muhammad bin Saud al-Muqrin.

Diriyah quickly became a safe haven for all Wahhabis in Nejd. This safety net only grew stronger with the emir’s territorial successes, for he went on to conquer nearly all of Nejd within just a few years of the pact with Wahhab. With every conquest, he would invite the conquered to join Wahhabism. The creed grew exponentially. All this while, the Islamic world had seen Istanbul as its spiritual capital. The Ottoman Sultan was also the Caliph. This is the _status quo_ Wahhab challenged, even if never directly for the fear og persecution. He held the Ottomans and their lax implementatiomn of Shariʿa in their _vilayets_ squarely responsible for the erosion of Islamic values in the Middle East. A significant part of this resentment could be attributed to his disdain for Sufism as an unacceptable aberration of Islam and the Ottoman royalty’s patronage thereof—almost every Ottoman Sultan was a Sufi; Mahmud II, who ordered the anti-Wahhabi crackdown in 1818, even presented himself as a Sufi leader capable of performing miracles.11

Wahhab died at the ripe old age of eighty-nine in 1792. But his death did little to ameliorate the anti-Ottoman sentiments in his flock. On the contrary, it grew louder. Less than two decades later, the Ottomans were at the gates of Diriyah. The war lasted seven years and resulted in an utter destruction of capital. By 1824, Nejd, along with neighboring Hejaz, had been incorporated into the Ottoman domain. The Saud family was now an Ottoman vassal.

Over time, Wahhabist ideology grew into a major doctrinal force in the Muslim world enforcing the stricted possible interpretation of Shariʿa by both violent and nonviolent means. The Emirate of Diriyah ultimately broke free from Ottoman rule, annexed Hejaz, and grew into a Wahhabi theocracy called the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia. Wahhabism has also been the central doctrine of several terror outfits, the most notorious of them all being al-Qaeda. Wahhabism is also sometimes cited as at least one of the ideologies that drive ISIS.

## Nadir Shah, the Common Factor

So in a space of one turbulent decade at the turn of the sixteenth century, the Islamic world had lost both its ideological nuclei. And when such forces decline, others rush to fill in. That’s what happened here. Two men, both born in the same “decade of decline,” probably in the same year, emerged as spiritual figureheads. Both saw reasons for the decline in the adherents’ general departure from the Islamic core. Muslims had to be shepherded away from decadence and back to “the one true path” of Shariʿa. A new pristine interpretation of The Book was the call of the hour. That’s what the two men did. This was a return to the absolute, incorruptible basics—a way back to the Hadiths. While the two didn’t succeed in reviving their respective caliphates, they did in reviving Islam’s hegemony by injecting the community with a renewed sense of ideological puritanism.

But why are we talking Wahhab and Waliullah in the same vein? It’s not like one’s rise affected the other’s, nor did Istanbul’s decline have anything to do with Delhi’s. So where’s the overlap?

The reason is a common catalyst in a third decline—the decline of the Safavids. This is the “third” we spoke of in the beginning. So we have the Ottomans, the Safavids, and the Mughals (together abbreviated to OSM). Traditionally called the “Gunpowder Empires” for their pioneering use and dependence on the revolutionary new kind of warfare in the Middle Ages. While the Ottomans and the Mughals were Sunni, the Safavids were Shiʿa. They lorded over modern-day Iran, Central Asia, Iraq, Bahrain, and parts of Russia. This empire came to an end less than thirty years after the beginning of the Ottoman-Mughal downfall. And we can pin it all on one individual—Nadir Shah. We’ve already met him before.

Disliked by both Delhi and Istanbul, the man helped himself to the Iranian throne as the first Afsharid monarch after nonviolently deposing Tahmasp II. Now although his dynasty lasted a mere twelve years, it disrupted the Islamic world. For starters, Shah, territorial and despotic while he was, was surprisingly indifferent to the question of religion.12 This shouldn’t come as a surprise to anyone who understands that religious tolerance has been crucial to empire-building since the time of Ancient Rome and Genghis Khan. In fact, it’s even said he switched from Shiʿa to Sunni during his reign in anticipation of adding Sunni Ottoman territories to his realm. While this may or may not be accurate, he certainly made attempts to morph in Shiʿism into a Sunni creed for wider religious acceptance.13

Within a decade of taking the throne, he’d humbled both Mughals (sack of Delhi) and Ottomans (Battle of Kars). It was around this period that Dehlawi and Wahhab shot into theological stardom with their respective maiden works. Wahhab’s Kitab at-Tawhid and Dehlawi’s Ḥujjat Allah al-Baligha, both advocated for and reinforced Islam’s return to its absolute, unremitting fundaments. One became the guiding scripture for Wahhabism, the other for Deobandism that’d emerge a century later.

## A Mêlée of Fanatics

While we have established Nadir Shah as a common force driving the rise of fanaticism in both worlds, we’re yet to know if the two figureheads of jihad ever crossed paths. Considering they were both contemporaries and both spent time studying theology in Medina, it should be a fair possibility.

Turns out, it was. The two men shared a teacher in Shaikh Abu Tahir Muhammad ibn Ibrahim al-Kurani al-Mada, which is how both wound up studying ibn Taymiyya. It’s highly likely they shared a classroom in which case, an exchange of ideas cannot and should not be ruled out. After all, both were motivated by this tearing urge to restore Islam to its absolute core and rid it of every corrupting innovation there was. Only difference was in their approach. Waliullah believed in pragmatic tolerance and patient proselytization to propagate Islam. We’ve already seen that he translated the Qurʾan to Persian despite his strong Arabic affinities, only to reach a wider audience. He was willing to make compromises for the greater objective. He was largely against forced conversions and violence, although this must be read against the backdrop of his letter inviting foreign invasion to rescue Islam from the Kufr.

Wahhab, on the other hand, embarked upon a less nonviolent path14 that led to not only his flock’s confrontation with the Ottomans but also the establishment of a puritanical Islamist emirate. The crossover between the two ideologies didn’t end with Wahhab and Waliullah, it extended into their successors’ political expressions as well. Remember that name we put a pin on? Let’s return to him. Nejdi Shaikh was just an epithet, his name was ʿAbd al-Haq Benarasi. But even that wasn’t his original name. That’d be Ghulam Rasul. He lived in the nineteenth century and in the tradition of other Indian theologians bfore his time, made a trip to Saudi Arabia to study the scripture. However, instead of Medina, the center of Islamic learning at the time, he chose Nejd, the center of Wahhabi thought. This is how he earned that epithet.15

Upon return Nejdi preached Wahhabism in Benaras and initiated, among others, one Wilayat Ali Khan. The latter would also go on to study under Barelvi bringing about one of the first Barelvi–Wahhabi crossovers. He would also emerge as a key figure of Indian Wahhabism and participate in several jihadist campaigns on the subcontinent. But the crossover still doesn’t end there. Remember Siddiq Hasan Khan, the cofounder of Ahl-i Hadith? His education of the Hadith came directly from none other than Shaikh Nejdi. The influence was pronounced and informed much of Ahl-i Hadith’s core precepts.

In short, all three big-ticket schools of Islamist thought on the subcontinent have experienced varying degrees of Wahhabi influence. Notwithstanding the fact that they all go out of their way to disavow and even condemn Wahhabism today.

Thus—and in the most simplistic, reductive terms—one can see violent geopolitical jihad as a series of dominos, the first of which fell with the defeat of Constantinople, on January 26, 1699.
