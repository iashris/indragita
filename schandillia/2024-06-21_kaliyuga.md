Brahma creates, Vishnu preserves, and Mahesh or Shiva destroys. That’s the generally accepted profile of the Hindu pantheon’s supreme trinity. Kalki breaks this template. As the tenth Vishnu incarnate, he departs from the norm and assumes the role of the ultimate destroyer of worlds. Kalki represents regeneration—end of old decay and start of a new, “ideal” world order. But why? Why does Vishnu get the job traditionally meant for Shiva? The clues lie in Kalki’s genesis. The deity makes its debut in Garuda Purana, one of the _Maha Puranas_. The work introduced Kalki as a Vishnu avatar who came into the world riding a pristine white stallion named Devadatta to “chastise the wrongdoers.” In some iterations, he doesn’t stop at just that and goes further to “reboot the world,” so to speak.

Garuda, like much of the Puranic corpus, is a product of Late Antiquity. Also like most other Maha Puranas, it was the work of not one but multiple successive authors. Scholarly consensus places it between the fourth and eleventh centuries. The reference to Kalki can be narrowed down further to around mid-sixth century because one verse introduces him as the prophesied son of Vishnugupta, the last known Gupta ruler whose reign came to an end in 550 AD.

So, it would seem something crazy happened toward the middle of the first millennium that triggered imaginations of a divine intervention of a most violent kind. And for some reason, this had to be Vishnu and not Shiva. What happened? One clue can be found in the decline of the Guptas that coincided with the emergence of Kalki, but there’s a whole lot more. The story of Kalki, and the consequent start of Kaliyuga or Indian Dark Ages, is nothing short of an international thriller with plotlines connecting places like China and Rome. This story has catastrophic wars, an even more catastrophic plague, three volcanoes, and a whole lot of religion. Now, there are two ways to start this story, with Rome or with China. No, the story of Kalki does not begin in India. We’ll start with China—northwestern China to be more precise. Some 2,200 years ago. First some geography. Following people and their actions becomes easier once we comprehend their geography.

## The Ordos Loop

The northern China region is packed with a confusing array of geographical features of all kinds which can make mental navigation a bit of a challenge. We’ll try to keep it simple and only stick to those that matter to our story:

  1. Eastern Steppe

  2. Altai Mountains

  3. Ordos Loop

  4. Gansu Corridor




At the highest level, we have this enormous sheet of grass known as the Eurasian Steppe, or just the Steppes. The technical term for stretches like these is ecoregion. This ecoregion spans roughly five thousand miles from the Danube in the west all the way to eastern Manchuria in the east. While a total of half a dozen subdivisions can be identified within this ecoregion, a broad distinction can be drawn between the eastern and western sections, separated by a wall called the Altai Mountains. To the west of this wall lived the Indo-European peoples, and to the east, the Turkic peoples, or just Turks. For the longest time, neither knew the other existed.

By the way, China in this context refers to China _and_ Mongolia combined. Otherwise in strictly modern context, the bulk of the Eastern Steppe lies in Mongolia and the Altai Mountains mark part of the border between Mongolia and China. To the south of this eastern grassland, we have a vast sandy wasteland, the Gobi Desert. The southern edge of this desert in the east is marked by the Yin Shan range which separates it from a river valley, that of the famous Yellow River or Huang He. This river originates near the Tibetan Plateau and pours into the Yellow Sea after a serpentine 3,300-mile journey. Part of this journey is where the Yin Shan separates it from the Gobi Desert.

The segment of the river that runs parallel to the Yin Shan, is part of a bend—a rectangular, northerly enclosure called the Ordos Loop. Paradoxically, the northern section of this enclosure is an arid desert despite the three-way irrigation offered by China’s second largest river. But the southern section is the exact opposite. It receives additional waters from the Wei River and its many penetrating tributaries. This is where the action is for obvious reasons. The agriculturally bountiful subsection is the cradle of several prehistoric Chinese cultures and still forms one of the most densely populated parts of the country.

The Gobi Desert’s southern fringes are defined by two distinct geographical sections, western and eastern. The latter, as we’ve already noted, is formed by the Yin Shan range. The western, by contrast, is plains. It’s a narrow strip of land that separates the desert in the north from the Qilian Mountains in the south. This range forms the northern fringes of a larger mountainous element called the Tibetan Plateau. The tract separating the desert from the mountain are as crucial to civilization as the Ordos Loop to its east. When the Indo-Europeans and Turks came in contact with each other, it’s through this conduit. This was also the corridor that later steered the Turks out of the Loop and into the wider civilizational buzz of Central Asia. Being situated to the west of the Ordos Loop, the stretch is called the Hexi Corridor, _hexi_ being a local portmanteau of _he_ (river) and _xi_ (west), i.e. “west of the river.” Another name for this feature is Gansu Corridor.

So that’s roughly the geography of our story. If still confused, think of the Eurasian Steppe as a giant green panorama running across the entire width of China and more. Along the eastern border of Mongolia with China and Russia with Kazakhstan is a wall dividing this grassland into western and eastern sections. This wall is the Altai Mountains. Below the Eastern or Mongolian Steppe is the Gobi Desert and below it is the Yin Shan in the east and Gansu or Hexi Corridor in the west. Hexi Corridor separates the desert from the Tibetan Plateau, the Yin Shan separates the desert from the Yellow River. The section of the river that runs along the Yin Shan forms a northerly curve called the Ordos Loop. The northern section of this loop is a desert whereas the southern section is a fertile agricultural region where most Chinese civilizations started. The Gansu Corridor not only separates the desert from the plateau, but it also connects the southern Ordos region with the western grasslands. This is only China. We will discuss Central Asian geography, such as Ili Valley, Tian Shan, and Tarim Basin, as and when we get there, to keep things less confusing. With that out of the way, we can finally start our story, in the southern Ordos Loop.

## The Genghis Khan of Antiquity

This story begins with a legend—the legend of the whistling arrow. Once upon a time, there was a man named Modu. He had an enchanted arrow that whistled as it flew off the bow. Son of a tribal chief named Touman, Modu was keen on replacing his father. The legend attributes this less to mere ambition and more to Touman’s hostile attitude toward his own son. To that end, Modu put together a personal army of ten thousand mounted archers.1 But before deploying them against his father, he needed to fully ascertain their loyalty. So, he called a test. It involved him firing his whistling arrow and then his soldiers blindly shooting in that direction, whatever be at the receiving end.

The first shot was a random wild animal. Only half the men followed the order, demonstrating readiness to kill an innocent creature on command without hesitation. The remaining soldiers, hesitant to commit such an act, failed the test and were promptly executed. The second stage involved Modu’s favorite horse. Once again, the contingent divided, with half adhering to the command and the rest meeting their end. The final and most chilling stage involved Modu’s own wife. And yet again, half obeyed, the rest lost their lives. What remained now was a little more than a thousand soldiers or a mere tenth of the original headcount. Although greatly depleted in number, what remained with Modu was a team the loyalty of which he could blindly count on.2 This, to him, was far more crucial to military success than numbers. With this army, Modu went on to depose his father, have him executed, and finally take his place as the leader of a large tribal confederacy.

While this is mere legend, the overarching chain of events isn’t. The Ordos Loop, especially the southern part, had been inhabited by a number of nomadic tribes originating in, depending on who one asks, Siberia, Manchuria, or Mongolia. Given their barbaric lifestyle, these tribes were a constant threat to the Chinese Qin kingdom south of the Wei River. Around 200 BC, in an attempt to eliminate the threat, the Qin army raided the Loop and drove away its inhabitants,3 who then fled into Mongolia. There, a man named Touman emerged as a powerful leader and successfully brought together all tribes under a common umbrella, the Xiongnu confederacy. Ethnically, these were Turkic peoples although the term would not be coined until centuries later.

Under Modu, after the patricide, the confederacy embarked upon a new expansionist campaign. He had already established himself as a ruthless barbarian with a take-no-prisoners attitude, for it’s not just his father that he’d killed but his entire family. The motive was to leave no descendant alive to ever challenge his inheritance. This only added to his reputation as an unforgiving tyrant with no qualms in shedding blood, not even of his own. Modu was the Genghis Khan of the Iron Age.

The expansionist campaigns brought areas like southern Siberia, eastern Mongolia, and Manchuria into the Xiongnu fold. They also managed to reclaim their Ordos Loop homeland from the Chinese. By the time of Modu’s accession, the Qin had been replaced by Western Han. Under this new dynasty China agreed to make several territorial concessions to the Xiongnu, Ordos Loop being part of them. With the east secured, Modu’s army turned west. Remember Gansu Corridor, the narrow passage between the Gobi Desert and the Tibetan Plateau? That was the only way east for the Xiongnu horde. This string of oases was then under the control of an Indo-European confederacy called the Yuezhi. Modu’s forces overran the corridor, killed the Yuezhi king, and annexed the area to their territory. Now the west was wide open. The Yuezhi king was taken prisoner and executed. Modu’s son, it’s said, had a cup made out of his skull. The remaining Yuezhi fled with their horses and scattered in two major directions. Some went south and settled in the Tibetan Plateau, we call them the “Lesser Yuezhi.” Others took a more northwesterly direction and settled in the Ili Valley. We call them the “Greater Yuezhi.” This is the group that would later enter India as Kushans. We’ll briefly touch upon their flight and eventual advent to India when we discuss the Guptas.

For now, the Xiongnu had established themselves as the apex predators in all of northern China and beyond. By the early second century BC, they had built themselves what is now considered Inner Asia’s first true empire. At its peak, the Xiongnu Empire stretched from the forests of Manchuria in the east to the watershed of Syr Darya in the west and from Lake Baikal in the north to the Ordos plains in the south. From this behemoth would spawn various ruling tribes in the early centuries of the Common Era and one of those is the antagonist of our story. But before we explore them, let’s get a feel of what’s happening in other parts of the world as the Common Era dawns.

## The Golden Age of Rome

Just as the Xiongnu entered a state of political decline, another superpower thousands of miles to the west poised itself for territorial leadership. Rome had been a republic for almost half a millennium when Julius Caesar decided to break the monotony by naming himself dictator. Unfortunately, this experiment ended barely a month later with what is arguably history’s most famous assassination in 44 BC—the Ides of March. Caesar’s death threw Rome into chaos. The senators who stabbed him, hoping to restore the Republic, were wildly off the mark. The public was outraged, and Caesar's popularity fueled a series of civil wars. Octavian, Caesar’s adopted heir, rose to power through these conflicts.

A key moment was the Battle of Actium in 31 BC, where his forces decisively defeated those of Mark Antony and Cleopatra. This victory eliminated Octavian’s biggest rival, added Egypt to the Roman domain, and propelled him to become the first Roman emperor as Augustus Caesar. The Roman Empire was now in session. And so was an era of political stability called _Pax Romana_. This would last two centuries and go down in history as the Golden Age of Rome. This window would experience increased prosperity and commercial activity, a strong central government, energetic literary output, and the establishment of Rome as an invincible transcontinental power, although they still had the Parthians to contend with.

Do note that even though _pax_ translates as “peace,” the period remained far from peaceful. There were enough wars to keep the Roman legionaries busy throughout the Golden Age. We already noted the Parthians but there were also others giving Rome a very hard time. One such conflict was Boudicca’s Rebellion in 60 AD that took a whole year to put down and cost tens of thousands of Roman lives. Another was in a faraway Judea where the first Jewish-Roman War led to the fall of the Second Temple. Out on the frontiers, there were constant skirmishes with various Germanic tribes too, some like the encounter in Teutoburg Wald even resulting in defeat. But these wars notwithstanding, politics remained stable and the Roman core prosperous. Enough to justify the name Pax Romana. Seneca the Younger had coined it, ironically, during the reign of Nero.

Upon the death of Marcus Aurelius, the famous philosopher-king, in 180 AD, the golden age of Roman peace, began to crumble. In fact, his death is held as the endpoint of Pax Romana. His son, Commodus, ascended the throne, possessing far less interest in the burdens of leadership than in personal indulgence. Unlike his father, who had tirelessly campaigned against Germanic threats on the northern borders, Commodus prioritized swift peace treaties and withdrew Roman forces. This lack of military dedication, coupled with poor leadership, rampant corruption, and brutal tendencies, sowed the seeds of instability within the empire. The infrastructure that thrived under Marcus Aurelius began to weaken, marking a turning point towards the decline of the Roman Empire.

Fast forward a hundred and fifty years and we arrive at the Emperor Diocletian. Seeing that the empire had grown too big to keep under a single ruler, he came up with the crazy idea to divide the empire into four semi-autonomous entities, each with its own emperor. We call this arrangement a tetrarchy. The system was conceived to resolve the never-ending civil wars and power struggles Rome had endured ever since Aurelius’ death. But it didn’t work for long. The Tetrarchy began to collapse less than thirty years since inception.

At this point comes Constantine the Great. His reign marks the next turning point in Rome’s history as he arrested the empire’s decline with a series of sociopolitical initiatives. Constantine is best known for a decisive shift in the empire’s religious landscape as he legalized Christianity seven years into his reign with the Edict of Milan. Peace returned to the empire. Speaking of empire, Constantine also ended the Tetrarchy by bringing all Roman lands under a central command, like the good old days of Pax Romana.

Constantine’s biggest claim to fame, other than mainstreaming Christianity in Europe, is something he did in 324 AD, just a year before the monumental Council of Nicaea—the founding of a new capital. Rome, the capital city, was old and in decay. The pre-Christian pagan faith was too strong here and all the action was in the east. Constantine wanted a brand-new capital to make a fresh start and it had to be in the east, for better control over the disturbed eastern frontiers. After much scouting, the Anatolian city of Byzantium was identified as a perfect choice. New infrastructure was commissioned, and the city was redeveloped from the ground up on imperial orders. Once ready, it was renamed Constantinople in the emperor’s honor. The following year, the Christian doctrine was formalized at an ecumenical meet in Nicaea, modern day İznik. In sum, Rome was back on track to become the greatest empire on Earth. It almost did. Why almost? We’ll find out soon, but first a trip to India whose own Golden Age was only kicking in now.

## The Golden Age of India

Remember the Indo-European Yuezhi peoples we spoke of earlier? After the Xiongnu drove them out of the Gansu Corridor, they scattered in several directions in search of new homelands. There were several tribes, five of which made their way into the Ili Valley, today part of the border between China and Kazakhstan. These five tribes—Guìshuāng, Xiūmì, Shuāngmǐ, Xìdùn, and Dūmì—were known collectively as the Greater Yuezhi. Here, they displaced another Indo-European people, the Scythians or Sakas, as they were variously called. But soon got displaced themselves by yet another Indo-European group called Wusun. Their next stop was another river basin further south, the Oxus Valley, where they again displaced the Sakas. The region was then part of the Graeco-Bactrian kingdom. Here, the Guìshuāng successfully worked to end the Greek rule and united all five Yuezhi tribes under a common political banner. Guìshuāng would later corrupt to Kushan. This was 120 BC. The eponymous empire, however, wouldn’t start until 150 years later when the Kushan king Kujula Kadphises crossed the Hindu Kush and established his dynasty’s first Indian domain in Gandhara.

When they arrived in Bactria, it was the Graeco-Bactrian kingdom. Now when they arrived in northwestern India, it was an Indo-Greek kingdom. Luckily for them, the Greek power was already in a state of decline by this point, so the Kushans were able to take much of northern India in under three generations. The most recognized Kushan ruler and Kujula’s great-grandson Kanishka ruled an empire that stretched from Gandhara in the west to Magadha in the east. We won’t discuss the Kushans in any more detail than this because they are not the subject of our story.

By the end of the third century AD, the Kushans had entered a state of decline. Kanishka, in fact, was the high point of their rule in India. This decline became an opportunity for many smaller powers to emerge from the shadows. One such power was the Guptas. Although the dynasty goes as far back as 240 AD, it’s only in 319 AD that we see the emergence of a historically attested character. Chandragupta founded the Gupta Empire when trade with Rome was already on a decline. Recall the end of Pax Romana in 180 AD. As Rome embroiled itself in internal conflicts and civil wars, India grew economically insular. India continued as an economic powerhouse, but the nature of economy changed from foreign trade to agrarian. On the non-agrarian side, a multitude of guilds emerged as trades like ironwork and masonry grew in economic output. With enhanced prosperity came a renaissance of art and literature. It’s for these reasons that the Gupta Era is often called India’s Golden or Classical Age.

The Gupta rule is also noted for a third kind of renaissance, religious. For the longest time since its emergence, Buddhism had dominated India’s religious landscape. Although both Mauryas and Kushans were religiously tolerant, there was a visible bias toward Buddhism in terms of patronage under most rulers. The Guptas were Vaishnavites, they worshipped Vishnu. Of course they patronized all faiths, but Vaishnavism held the centerstage. Some of the earliest Hindu temples emerged under their rule and most of these were to Vishnu. One could argue that the cult of Vishnu was unofficially the State religion. Not that other faiths didn’t thrive.

Besides the mainstreaming of Vishnu, the Gupta era also witnessed a definitive shift in attitude which was reflected in religious iconography. The Kushans had patronized a large pantheon of deities that came from not Indic but also Iranian and Roman sources. These deities found generous placement on the gold coins issued by the regime. The Guptas became the first to “Indianize” many of those representations. Non-Indic deities were adapted to represent Indian deities on new gold _dinars_. The Kushan goddess of wealth Ardoksho, for example, got replaced by India’s Lakshmi. As was the lion-riding Nana who became Simhavahini Lakshmi. The lotus flower also became a central part of Hindu iconography during this period.

Rome resumed its gradual decline after Constantine’s death, India remained ascendent under Samudragupta whose tenure almost coincided with Constantine’s death. And the dynasty was yet to peak. This peak came under Chandragupta II, better known as Vikramaditya, who took the throne in 375 AD. Under his rule, we see the emergence of cave temples like Udayagiri and literary maestros like Kalidasa. This period was the cultural high point in India’s history. His son built the celebrated university of Nalanda where patrons came from as far away as Turkey and Indonesia.

On the other hand, Rome had become a victim of its own success and started becoming too unwieldy to govern. It’s this very problem that Diocletian had attempted to solve by instituting the Tetrarchy. But with Constantine’s reversal, Rome was back to its impractical former self. So, history repeated itself and Rome was partitioned once again. This was done by Emperor Theodosius on his deathbed toward the end of fourth century to prevent a battle of succession between his two sons. This time, instead of four, there were only two divisions—a Western Roman Empire with capital in Rome, and an Eastern Roman Empire with capital in Constantinople.

Back in India, the Guptas continued to maintain their hold over their vast domain which, even though half of the Roman Empire in size, was just as unwieldy if not more. Even as foreign trade declined, India maintained and even enhanced its soft power on the global stage. Samudragupta’s reign saw the first Chinese pilgrim to India. Faxian’s trip lasted two years and left us one of the first foreign accounts of India’s grandeur. The trail blazed by him would lead many more pilgrims to India in the following decades, especially after the institution of the university at Nalanda under Kumaragupta. This establishment is the best illustration of religious tolerance under the Guptas. Despite his own Vaishnavite persuasion, Kumaragupta made generous donations to the largely Buddhist monastery and actively participated in its upkeep. Donations and land grants were made by patrons from as far as Indonesia.

By the time of Kumaragupta, Rome had already suffered a major sack by the Visigoths under Alaric I. This was the beginning of the fall of Rome in the west. The Visigoths were a Germanic tribe, one of many, that inhabited the vast region of central and northern Europe beyond the frontiers of Roman. Roman historians refer to these lands collectively as Germania. For the longest time, these tribes had lived peacefully without disturbing Rome. Now they wanted to live _in_ Rome. They sought territories within Rome, and they sought it so desperately, they were willing to do so militarily. But why? Whence the sudden desperation? It’s as if something was squeezing them tight, pushing them closer to Rome with no place left to go. What could that be?

## The Scourge of God

The last time we spoke of the Xiongnu, they’d already built a large empire that practically spanned the entire width of Inner Asia. History has a policy of cutting back to size anything that grows too big for its shoes. This happened to every large empire ever. Now it was the turn of the Xiongnu. In 48 AD, the empire hit a struggle for succession and split into two—Southern Xiongnu and Northern Xiongnu. The latter was a rump entity confined to a river valley in northern Mongolia and found itself constantly at the receiving end of invasions from the east. The Xiongnu had always been at loggerheads with the Chinese. The whole point of the Great Wall was to keep the nomadic barbarians away from the realm of the sedentary. If you’d recall, the relationship had started to warm up when the Qin dynasty gave way to the Han. But that situation did not last and the Han-Xiongnu animosity became an enduring feature of the region’s political setup.

Seeing the futility of conflict, the Southern Chanyu undertook a policy of pragmatic submission and ended up as a vassal to Han China. The Northern, on the other hand, continued to resist until its decimation in the second century AD. In the wake of this decimation, several surviving Xiongnu tribes scattered away to other parts of Asia and beyond in search of better pastures. One of them, according to Wei Shu, was the Hephthalites. Remember this name, we’ll return to it later. Some including the Hephthalites went south and came to inhabit Transoxiana where the name Xiongnu corrupted to _Xwn_ in Sogdian.4 Others stuck to the steppes and continued west, crossing the Volga and reaching eastern Europe where they acquired names like Armenian _Hon-k’_ and Greek _Ounnoi_.5 Sounds familiar?

The first European mention of these migrants is in the works of Ptolemy who lived in the second century. He recorded the name as _Khounoi_ which later entered Latin works as _Chunni_ where _Ch_ represented a voiceless velar fricative consonant, much like the _ch_ in Scottish _loch_. With time, Chunni lost its velar character, reducing to _Hunni_. Centuries later the same word would enter English as Hun. From this point on, this is what we’ll refer to them as, although there are some experts who dispute the Xiongnu origins of the Huns.

The Huns were in flight, they had lost their homeland to the Han. But they were not powerless. They still had their horses and the storied Turkic ferocity. And they still hoped to rebuild an empire from scratch. Once they crossed the Volga, they found themselves in the interfluve between Volga and Don. The region was inhabited by a community of Iranic nomads called Alans. This would become the Huns’ first European conquest. The Alans who refused to submit were forced to the other side of Don.

At this point, it’s important to get a feel of some relevant geography in order to fully grasp the Huns’ future movements. We’ve already seen the Volga-Don interfluve where the Huns first settled on their westward campaign after crossing the Volga. Don drains into the Black Sea. Further west running almost parallel to it are Dnieper and Dniester, in that order. Today, the Dnieper cuts Ukraine roughly into two halves and the Dniester marks its border with Moldova in its southern reaches. Both drain into the Black Sea. The last major river to drain into this sea on our westward move is the mighty Danube. But while Don, Dnieper, and Dniester are nearly parallel, the Danube follows a more lateral course and connects eastern Europe with the Roman heartland of western Europe. This makes Danube the single most consequential river in Europe’s civilizational history. All these interfluves had traditionally been inhabited by various Germanic peoples collectively called Goths. While there were a large number of Gothic tribes, they’re generally assorted into two top-level categories—eastern and western. Dividing the two is the Dniester River. Now back to the Huns.

After subjugating the Alans, the Huns crossed the Don and opened their first campaign against a Gothic people. These were the Greuthungi or eastern Goths and their biggest draw was their wealth. The Hunnic invasion proved catastrophic for the Greuthungi and even drove their king into suicide. Much of the tribe submitted to its new Hunnic overlord but those that didn’t, fled west. These fleeing Greuthungi crossed the Dnieper and finally reached the banks of Dniester. Across the river was the domain of a tribe of western Goths called Thervingi. The exiles intended to cross the river and seek refuge in the Thervingi country, but the Huns came chasing them and swiftly took control of the region. Now, both eastern and western Goths were in flight, headed to the next riverfront, the Lower Danube.

Across the Danube was Roman country. They were forced to cross the river and seek out a domain for themselves within Roman territory. Rome was hesitant but the bulk of its military resources in the region was deployed against the Sassanians. So, the relatively unremarkable emperor Valens agreed to let the Goths in. This was 370 AD. Within one year of this asylum, however, the Goths were already in revolt against their Roman hosts. Reasons quoted range from famine to abuse. This conflict lasted half a decade and greatly weakened the Roman Empire. By the time Theodosius partitioned Rome into Eastern and Western Empires toward the end of the fourth century, the Huns were in possession of almost all Gothic lands beyond the Roman frontiers. And the Goths were squeezing deeper into Roman lands, leading to sustained conflicts and depletion of Roman resources. It’s during this period that a Visigothic force under Alaric I invaded Rome and sacked it beyond recovery.

But it wasn’t just the Goths, even Huns were a threat just as immediate. The first Hunnic raid into the Roman territory was in the same year the Empire was partitioned. That year, as the Danube froze in winter, a Hunnic army crossed the river and pillaged Thrace. Raids of this kind would continue unabated over the next few decades. By the middle of the fifth century, the enormous Hunnic polity had consolidated under a man named Attila. The Eastern Roman Empire, at this point, was a tributary to the Hunnic Empire. The man was the most ferocious embodiment of his Xiongnu forebears’ territorial instincts and sought to conquer the world. This made him the enemy number one to both Romans and the Goths. The Romans were particularly perturbed because Attila intended to conquer the Western Roman Empire itself, a rather bold plan even by his own standards.

True to his steppe reputation, Atilla was a ruthless military leader whose Blitzkriegs left cities sacked, populations massacred, and entire regions in ruins. He was the most feared conqueror Rome had ever faced. His relentless raids seemed to the Romans as a divine punishment for their moral decline. So much so that they started referring to him as _Flagellum Dei_ , Latin for “Scourge of God.”

Attila ultimately died in 453 AD, but the damage he had inflicted was irreversible. Even though he never got to the Roman capital itself, he had left much of all else—especially Gaul and northern Italy—in utter ruination. Rome, more precisely the Western Roman Empire, fell less than twenty-five years later. This was the end of a 500-year-old entity that was considered pretty much untouchable by allies and adversaries alike.

In faraway India, the disruption of Roman trade that came with the Fall of Rome pushed the Gupta regime into an economic crisis. Part of this crisis was also due to the enormous drain on the imperial treasury due to sustained military campaigns, most of it defensive. The man on the throne when Rome fell was Budhagupta. Skandagupta, the last powerful Gupta dynast, had died in 467 AD and between him and Budhagupta, the throne had gone through three occupants in a space of nine years. In fact, by the time of Skandagupta, the empire had already started unraveling in the face of incessant power struggles and foreign incursions.6 After Rome, it seemed now it was India’s turn. A good question to ask here is, why did Rome’s Fall impact Indian economy if the latter was already so self-sufficient under the Guptas? We know the Gupta economy had shifted dramatically from foreign trade to agriculture. Then what went wrong that toward the later decades of the Gupta Era, gold coinage became significantly debased and even disappeared? We will answer this question after we discuss the Alchons.

## Two Generations of Destruction

To understand what happened to the Guptas in fifth century, we need to look at what happened to the Kushans in the second. After the Xiongnu were driven out of the Gansu Corridor by the Chinese, several tribes sought refuge in different parts of the world. We’ve already discussed how some of them crossed the Volga and brought down Rome. Some others took a more southerly route and ended up first in Transoxiana then Bactria. Toward the middle of the fourth century, a new name starts showing up in Iranian and other chronicles—the Xionites. While their exact geographical Urheimat remains a subject of scholarly debate, many place it in the southern reaches of the Altai Mountains. The Xiongnu did spend some time in this region after their expulsion from the Gansu Corridor, so it’s not unreasonable to assume that the Xionites and the Xiongnu were the same people. In simpler terms, Xiongnu, Xionite, and Hun are all etymological variants of the same entity.

The Xionites themselves have been assorted into two different groups in contemporary accounts—Red and White. White Xionites or White Huns are also identified as Hephthalites. We’ll meet them later. Red Xionites, on the other hand, are the ones that came to Bactria when the region was ruled by the Kushans. In Transoxiana, the Xionites or Huns made themselves a small kingdom and used it as a staging ground for further expansions into Khorasan and beyond. At this point they remained subservient to their more powerful Kushan neighbors who were themselves subservient to the Sassanians of Iran. The Kushans here had both geographically and politically separated from their counterparts in India. Here, the polity was a joint Kushano-Sassanian venture.

But the situation quickly changed as the Huns knocked the Kushans out of this partnership and took over Bactria in collaboration with the Sassanians, likely under a man named Kidara. This was in 350 AD. Remember when Rome was struggling to defend its eastern frontiers against the Goths because it had diverted much of its military manpower to the conflict with the Sassanians? A big reason they struggled so much was because the Sassanians had the Huns on their team. The Kushans over time had become a brand of kingship despite the loss of political authority which is why the Huns of the Oxus continued to strike coins in Kushan format and call their kings _Kushan-shah_.7 This is the beginning of the Kidarite line.

The Kidarite domain included Gandhara on the other side of the Hindu Kush which makes them the first Huns in the Indian subcontinent.8 Finally in possession of a territory rich in commerce and resources, the Kidarites could have continued peacefully forever. But forever is not a word in Turkic lexicons. Soon, a new adversary emerged from within, the Alchons. Most scholars read them as a Kidarite offshoot. Even if they weren’t, they were certainly from the same Red branch as the Kidarites. They succeeded in removing the Kidarites after a mere forty-year run in 370 AD. Another hundred years, and they finally took Gandhara too. But even they couldn’t rest for too long, for there was yet another adversary at the doorsteps.

And that brings us to the pin we dropped a while ago on the Hephthalites. Also known as White Huns, these tribes had been at the Alchon and Kidarite heels ever since their flight from the Altai. By the mid-fifth century, they were in Bactria and by the sixth, there was already a large Hephthalite Empire that stretched from the Aral Sea in the west to the mouth of Tarim in the east and Herat in the south. The Hephthalites have a crucial role to play in our story, but we’re not done with their predecessors in Bactria yet. So back to Alchons.

After losing their Central Asian domain to the Hephthalites, the Alchons moved deeper into India, the only direction they had left to take. By 470 AD, they had taken Gandhara and Punjab from the remaining Kidarites, which made them neighbors with the Guptas of India. Once defeated by Skandagupta a few years back, the Alchons were eager to make a second bid for the Gupta domain. Despite this win, the Guptas were way past their high point and the Huns knew this well. The Alchons were so serious about their Indian ambitions that they even allied with the Hephthals on the other side of the Hindu Kush. The Hephthals being a rich source of warhorses and the Guptas being in rapid economic and military decline offered a perfect cocktail for a second invasion.

At this point, the Alchons in northwestern India were a confederation with not one but four autonomous kings, one each for Gandhara, Swat, Jhelum, and Punjab. The title these kings went by was _Shahi_ , with one going by _Devaraja_ in addition.9 The federation came to an end when the latter went to war with the other three, defeated them, and brought the four groups together under his suzerainty. His name was Devaraja Toramana and he now assumed the title of _Maharajadhiraj_ or “king of kings.”10 Sanskrit texts from the time also speak of him as _Hunaraja_11 or “king of Huns.” Some half a millennium ago, a Yuezhi chief had done the exact same thing in Transoxiana and created the Kushan dominion.

Like the Kidarites and the Sassanians, the Alchons too followed Zoroastrianism, a faith reflected even in Toramana’s rule. Notably, Hunnic coins from this era consistently depict fire altars, a prominent Zoroastrian symbol (a point we will revisit later). However, Toramana’s approach differed from his contemporaries—his reign was marked by a remarkable tolerance for Buddhism, as evidenced by the recent discovery of scrolls and inscriptions detailing the construction of several Buddhist monasteries under his patronage.

The Talagan copper scroll, dating back to 493 AD, reveals that Toramana generously donated to the construction of a Buddhist chaitya.12 Similarly, the Khura Inscription from the same region, i.e. Punjab-Gandhara, credits Toramana, now king of kings for the first time in epigraphic records,13 with funding the building of a vihara. Besides Buddhism, he also patronized Vaishnavism as the famous Boar Inscription of Eran confirms. While the northwest was into Buddhism, the rest of the subcontinent was more into Vedic traditions, mostly Vaishnavism and Shaivism. Remember the Guptas were Vaishnavites. Toramana’s support for Vishnu worship is attested by the famous Boar Inscription of Eran.14

So, long story short, Toramana was fairly tolerant of all faiths. But that’s only half of the story. One must not forget that, at the end of the day, he was a steppe warlord. Barbarity had always been the hallmark of these cultures. While Toramana was tolerant of religions, he wasn’t tolerant of defiance. This trait would be seen in Genghis Khan seven centuries later. While wars and invasions have always involved bloodshed, Toramana stands out with his proclivity for comprehensive civic destruction. The story of his destructive rage goes back to the days of the four kings. As part of his campaign against the other three Shahis, he reduced several cities and settlements in their domains to rubble. The first major city to fall thus was none other than Takshashila. John Marshall calls it “wholesale destruction of Buddhist monasteries and stupas” in the city.15 Toramana’s next big victim was the Kushan-era Buddhist haven of Talkt-i-Bahi which is where the Indo-Parthian king Gondophares is said to have converted to Christianity.16 Toramana not only destroyed the structure beyond recovery, but he even killed most of its inhabitants, most of whom must have been Buddhist monks and theologians.17 While these may paint a crusade-like picture, the destructions were motivated less by religion and more by politics. And yet, this was a mere foreshadowing of what was to come.

Once on top of the Alchon hierarchy, Toramana turned east where a diminishing Gupta hegemony offered a tempting window of opportunity. The campaigns were swift and unremitting. Within just a few quick years, he managed to extend his domain as far east as Mathura. The Guptas were reduced to minor kingships squeezed farther east. Now, Mathura was a possession of prestige and keeping it secure was a pressing imperative. For now, it sat dangerously close to the Guptas who could mount a recovery campaign any day. So Toramana launched a preemptive strike.

From Mathura, the Huns mounted an attack deep into the eastern heartland. The worst affected in this invasion was the city of Koshambi. Koshambi was the to the Guptas what Mathura was to the Huns, a business hub and the administrative seat. Its destruction was absolute and comprehensive.18 Archeological records say the city was completely burned to the ground and never fully recovered.19 A crucial advantage for the Huns, as ever, was their highly mobile cavalry. The Guptas, with a restricted supply of quality warhorses, were at a significant disadvantage. Conversely, the Huns enjoyed a near-limitless source from across the Hindu Kush, courtesy of their Hephthalite kin. Just like Takshashila, Koshambi had stood proud for over a millennium before its ultimate fall. All in all, despite his religious tolerance, Toramana’s reign is one of unmitigated destruction.

What also helped Toramana’s ambitions was, as is already apparent by now, the Huns’ storied lack of ethics. They had no qualms retreating when facing capitulation and no qualms attacking in the dead of the night when the adversary was expected to be the most vulnerable. Their cavalry made little distinction between soldiers and civilians. This meant every attack was an all-in campaign with little left to morals or conventions.

After Toramana came his son Mihirakula. If Toramana was brutal, Mihirakula was the embodiment of Hell. By this point, the Huns in India had largely integrated themselves into the Indic cultural sphere, abandoning most vestiges of their Hunnic heritage, including the Tamga, which had been used since the Kidarite period. Mihirakula, unlike his Zoroastrian father, embraced Shaivism, possibly drawn to Shiva’s apparent destructive proclivities.20 However, in a stark contrast to Toramana’s tolerant religious policy, Mihirakula unleashed a wave of destruction targeting Buddhist institutions across his domain, particularly in Gandhara, which he reoccupied after conquering Kashmir. Monasteries were demolished, statues shattered, and monks killed, pushing Buddhism to the brink of extinction. This brutal campaign followed his father’s earlier destruction of the famed city of Takshashila. On the other hand, Shaivism experienced unprecedented resurgence under him, and the famous Shankaracharya Temple of Srinagar still stands as its most notable testimony.21

Mihirakula’s reign, likely cut short by illness sometime between 530 and 540 CE, marked the end of the Huns’ devastating rule in the subcontinent. Though historical accounts of their brutality may be exaggerated, Mihirakula and his father stand as a period of immense destruction in Indian history. Their conquests plunged the region into economic turmoil, unraveling centuries of prosperity. Flourishing urban centers like Ujjain, Vaishali,22 and Pataliputra23 ended up as ghost towns that took centuries to recover. Culturally, Buddhism suffered a crippling blow, never fully regaining its pre-eminence despite several attempts. Similarly, renowned artistic schools like Mathura and Gandhara faced centuries of obscurity, struggling to recapture their lost brilliance. Mathura, particularly, suffered tremendously due to its association with Buddhism, mirroring the fate of Takshashila.24

All of this in just two generations. The reign of terror between Toramana and his son is seen by many historians as India’s own Dark Ages. But even this was not the end of the story. The world was about to enter its darkest phase since the Bronze Age Collapse and this time, Hell would descend straight from the Heavens.

## The Long Night of Death

As we enter the sixth century, both Rome and India have fallen off their prime. There’s despair and hopelessness across the board and in both cases it’s the same adversary, a mysterious equestrian force from a faraway land, shooting through the cities and leaving a trail of death and destruction in its wake. After the initial wave of destruction, though, things had started to settle down. Both Rome and northern India entered a new fractured order, Rome under its new Germanic overlords and India under its new non-Gupta chieftains. A rump Gupta state too continued in the east but with greatly diminished influence.

And yet, neither Rome, nor India had reasons to celebrate. Four years before Mihirakula’s ultimate end was 536 AD. When the year opened, nobody had the faintest idea that the winter they were experiencing was about to last not months but years. Three volcanoes, the identities of which are yet to be ascertained, had erupted in different parts of the world, at once. Volcanic eruptions are no novelty to our planet but three big ones simultaneously, is no insignificant coincidence. Volcanic eruptions dump an enormous amount of ash and aerosols into the atmosphere. This sudden injection blocks sunlight and triggers what geologists call a “volcanic winter.” This situation can last years, depending on the magnitude of the eruption. When Krakatoa erupted in 1883, it plunged the other side of the world in a volcanic winter for a whole year. Here in 536, we had three such eruptions. A first-hand account from Constantinople speaks of dark, Sunless skies and chilly winds in the month of March, which is otherwise meant to be spring. The same year, Procopius, the Roman historian from Palestina Prima, too wrote of what seemed like a perpetual eclipse:25

> And it came about during this year that a most dread portent took place. For the Sun gave forth its light without brightness, like the Moon, during this whole year, and it seemed exceedingly like the Sun in eclipse, for the beams it shed were not clear nor such as it is accustomed to shed. And from the time when this thing happened men were free neither from war nor pestilence nor any other thing leading to death. And it was the time when Justinian was in the tenth year of his reign.

People expected some relief during summer months but to their disappointment, the temperature fell another 4.5 °F instead of rising. This was still just the beginning. The following year brought yet another eruption hit an already dystopian world. By 540, there were a total of four such events. Temperature that year is estimated to have fallen another five degrees. Cassiodorus, the Roman statesman serving Theodoric the Great in Rome, also wrote extensively about the climate of the time in his letters.26 In one he wrote in the fall of 538, he speaks of bluish Sun and shadowless noons.27 He speaks of Moon without light and summer without heat.28 He speaks of perpetual frost that made apples harden and grapes sour.29 He mentions “alien elements stretched like a hide across the sky.”30

The draught and famine that came in the wake of this climate calamity affected all parts of the world as can be learned from both climate studies and disparate accounts from Chinese, Middle Eastern, and Indian sources. Chinese chronicles even speak of snowfall in August, a climate absurdity of the highest order in normal times. Large-scale food shortages that followed triggered civil wars and riots in every major culture on record. This winter would last as many as thirty years and enter climate history as the Late Antique Little Ice Age or LALIA. A modern climate study concluded that during this period the Northern Hemisphere’s mean annual temperature fell to its lowest in two thousand years.31 A number of similar studies in recent times have examined things like polar ice cores and tree rings to arrive at the exact same conclusion—that a catastrophic eruption or series thereof plunged the Northern Hemisphere into an unprecedented period of extreme cold.3233

Climate disruptions trigger famine and famines trigger epidemics. That’s an established pattern. This one did the same. In 541, the same Procopius who had earlier written about the volcanic winter, wrote about a strange illness that threw its victim into a fatal cocktail of hallucinations, fevers, and swellings in the armpits, groin, and behind the ears. Most victims lapsed into a coma while others continued to suffer. In both cases, the ultimate fate was death. Today we know it was bubonic plague. In the absence of germ theory or modern medicine, the epidemic was seen as divine retribution for worldly evils. Procopius placed the blame squarely on the king in Constantinople at the time, Justinian I:34

> …when the plague came, seizing in its grip the whole civilized world and especially the Roman Empire, and wiping out most of the farmers, and when for this reason the lands, as one might expect, had become deserted, the emperor shewed no mercy to the owners of these lands. For he never relaxed his exaction of the annual tax, not merely as he imposed it upon each separate person, but also exacting the share which fell to his deceased neighbors.

The outbreak started in the rat-infested Egyptian port of Peleusium, today identified with Port Said, and quickly spread to wherever the ships went. By the following year, Constantinople was in its grip where, according to Procopius, it was killing ten thousand a day.

The pandemic would last almost a decade before finally abating in 549 AD. The following year marked the formal end of the road for Vishnugupta, the last Gupta emperor. These are years of extreme, unprecedented turmoil in India. The collapse of foreign trade, the apocalyptic “Ice Age,” and a global epidemic that’s estimated to have killed tens of millions in one fell swoop—it was as deadly a cocktail as there could be. But how did the pandemic and the climate change affect India? We hardly ever associate the Plague of Justinian or the Late Antique Little Ice Age with this side of the world. Should we? Let’s see, the only way to know is through inscriptions from the time. Interestingly, not many exist from the second half of the sixth century. There’s some kind of a void, as if kings just stopped having things to brag about. That’s our first clue, but there’s more—the extremely few that exist from just before this window. One of them is from 544 AD.

Unearthed in central UP’s Barabanki district, the Haraha Stone Inscription was commissioned by a Maukhari king35 when both Ice Age and the Plague were already at their peaks. It’s a sandstone slab and records the renovation of a Shiva temple in twenty-two Sanskrit verses.36 This is perhaps the first epigraphic references to India’s Dark Ages or _Kaliyuga_. The verses, besides paying homage to Maukhari dynastic ancestors, bemoan the breakdown of social, religious, and cultural fabric at the onset of Kaliyuga. The word makes its debut in verse 9:37

> “Qualifying his high bravery with political wisdom, his friendship by honesty, his lofty ambition by his noble descent, his liberality by fit recipients, his manner of life by the injunctions of the Shruti, and his high prosperity by humility, he never tired of the world, though it was immersed in the darkness of the Kaliyuga.”

The term repeats in verse 12 where the mighty and virtuous Maukhari king Ishanavarman is praised for ending Kaliyuga, an era of moral and cultural darkness. Interestingly, his ascension coincides with the end of the greatly weakened Gupta rule. It was just a decade since Mihirakula’s death a welcome milestone in India’s story. Also, it’s the Maukharis that are generally credited with ultimately ending Mihirakula’s reign of terror:38

> “By whom, as by the rising Sun, the world, which was sunk in the darkness of the Kaliyuga, where the paths of virtue were consequently obscured, was again set to work, he being a benefactor of the world, eclipsing the luster of water-lilies in the form of the foes, illuminating the collection of lotus-like faces of his friends, and being possessed of abundant majestic splendor.”

Two verses later we see an undeniable allusion to the climate catastrophe of the prevailing Ice Age. It describes the loss of sunlight and the skies being covered by a screen of dust that made days look like nights:39

> “The victorious one, whose watches can only be determined by means of the water clock, as if it were at night, the world being encompassed in darkness and bewildered as to the beginning, the middle or the end of the day on account of the dust which rises from the earth rent asunder by the agitation caused by the marches of his ocean-like armies on their expedition, screens the luster of the orbit of the Sun, and pervades the quarters.”

The destructive ambience of Kaliyuga is reiterated in verse 15 where the king is said to have kept the world from sinking in an ocean of sin brought about by the Dark Ages:40

> “By whom the earth was forcibly upheld like a broken boat, after fastening it on all sides by hundredfold virtues, when it was sinking below the invisible ocean of the nether regions, being shaken by the storms of Kali.”

Thus, in four verses we see a culmination of all three crises facing India, and the world, in the sixth century. And this brings us back to a question we’d asked long ago when discussing the Gupta fall—Why did the economy shrink despite the shift from Roman trade to domestic agriculture? Shouldn’t the insularity have insulated our economy? Now we know the answer. The climate disaster had triggered crop failures and famines in every part of the world and India was no exception. India was well connected with the rest of the world through the Silk Road network and had a prominent presence in centers like Balkh and Samarkand. India was also a thriving maritime trading partner with Africa and Egypt.

Experts are divided on the exact origins of the plague with some positing Africa while others suggesting Central Asia. One theory even places it in the Himalayan foothills. Regardless of which theory holds true, India had no spatial immunity from its spread. It was connected with every suggested ground zero with vigorous traffic on every route. It’s, therefore, far more reasonable to assume that the Plague of Justinian reached India than it is to assume it didn’t. While this explains Kaliyuga, we’re still left to deal with the Kalki question—why Vishnu and not Shiva? To answer that, let’s first trace the birth of Kalki.

## Kalki and Devadatta

Unsurprisingly, it’s during this period of hopelessness and turmoil that the eschatological character named Kalki made its debut. In a text called Garuda Purana. Here, Kalki is described as a Vishnu incarnate who rides a pristine white stallion named Devadatta and slays evildoers.41 While scholars have dated the work to as far back as fourth century, we can further narrow down its Kalki references to around the middle sixth. That’s because one of the verses references Vishnugupta, a character we’ve already met earlier, as Kalki’s chosen human father.42 In case it’s not clear already, Vishnugupta was the final ruler of the Gupta mainline and was coeval with Mihirakula. His reign ended in 550 AD.

[courtesy Kalki Avatar, The Aniruddha Haldipurkar collection. Accessed 21 July 2024.]

Besides Garuda, Kalki also features in the Bhagvata Purana or Srimad Bhagavatam in a chapter on end times. While the work is unanimously placed between 500 and 1000 AD, a more precise placement in light of its Alvar associations posits 850 AD as the _terminus post quem_.43 But either way, neither reference predates the apocalyptic chain of events discussed in this story. There’s also a dedicated Kalki Purana but that one came much later and is inconsequential to this story. Some may also argue that the debut was long before the Puranas, in Mahābhārata. That isn’t true either. Sure, Kalki does make an appearance in the epic, in Vānaparva’s Markandeya episode,44 but the whole section has now been established as a later addition and not part of the original composition.45

In short, we have a sudden emergence of an almost messianic concept of a destroyer of worlds who comes on horseback and ends all evil. One crucial factor we almost forgot in the decline of India’s foreign trade was the taboo of _Kalapani_ which forbade the Brahmins from any seafaring enterprise. Since Brahmins stayed away from the waters, lest they lost their _varna_ , maritime commerce fell into the hands of Buddhist and Jain merchants who had no Kalapani stricture to worry about. But then came Mihirakula’s persecution of Buddhists and Jains which left much of maritime trade unmanned with few Brahmins rushing to fill in. Yes, Mihirakula persecuted Jains too. One ninth century Jain monk even refers to Mihirakula as Kalkiraja.4647 Further attestations to the monk’s reference comes from Xuanzang who also referred to Mihirakula as Kalkiraja while elaborating on his atrocities in Kashmir.48 Xuanzang, also known as Hiuen Tsang, lived closer to Mihirakula in time than the monk we just met.

Now there’s two ways to interpret Kalki in the Hunnic context here. One is the way of the Puranas, i.e. a horseback-riding sword-wielding savior of dharma, destroyer of evil. The other is as a more direct allusion to Mihirakula as the horseback-riding idiom of violent, destructive change, leaving in its wake a cold, dark reign of famine and pestilence. Either way, in a fleeting half century, a new avatar had neatly made its way into the Hindu pantheon. This is an attestation to the spectacular upheaval wrought by these nomadic invaders from eastern Mongolia.

As for the question, why Vishnu and not Shiva, the clue lies in Mihirakula’s Shaivite persuasion. The Guptas were Vishnu worshippers and were probably seen as a force of resistance against a Shaivite Mihirakula’s tyranny. Note that in Garuda Purana, Kalki is prophesied to be born to a Gupta king. Named Vishnugupta, no less. On a more abstract level, this could be read as the germs of the Shaivite-Vaishnavite friction that would culminate in the first Bhakti movement of the ninth century. Originating in Tamilakam the movement was one of competitive evangelism by these two camps—Shiva worshipping Nayanars and Vishnu worshipping Alvars. The natural disasters in the wake of Mihirakula’s death would have given a strong fillip to the nihilistic, soteriological discourse already stirred by Mihirakula’s actions.

Lest we forget, Devadatta represents the Huns’ equestrian way of life. Like all steppe communities, the Huns too depended heavily on their horses. Even without stirrups or saddles, they ravaged and built empires riding these extremely reliable beasts. While the Guptas had a limited supply, the Huns of India didn’t. They had access to all the finest stallions from across the Hindu Kush courtesy of their Bactrian counterparts, the Hephthalites. Without horses, the Hunnic invasions would have never taken off. Therefore, since the tormentors had horses, so should the tormented. Perhaps that’s why the envisioned messiah was imagined mounted on a pristine white horse.

## A New India Is Born

So, this was the story of Kalki, Kaliyuga, and the catastrophic global context in which they took form. At its core, Kalki is a change agent. A metaphor for regeneration. It brought two titanic empires brought to their knees and paved the way for a new world order, part of which also happens to be Islam. But we’ll save that topic for another day.

In India, this new order meant the rise of new powerful entities to the south of the Vindhyas. Small chiefdoms like the Kadambas and Kalabhras gave way to the emergence of large new kingdoms the likes of which the Peninsula hadn’t seen before.49 The Pandyas, the Chalukyas, the Cheras—these were powers with significant influence that transcended cultural and regional lines, like their counterparts up north had done since before the Common Era. Whereas in the north, small regional entities—Aulikaras, Vardhans, Maukharis, etc.—popped in and out of existence without establishing a meaningful foothold as the landscape continued to fragment. In sum, the fall of the Guptas in the north proved a windfall for aspiring empire-builders in the South. A new India was born.

As for the Huns, they didn’t quite disappear after Mihirakula’s death. Instead, they assimilated. Having patronized and adopted Shaivism and given up his ancestors’ creed, Mihirakula had already kickstarted the process while he lived. And this makes it a good time to revisit the fire altars on Toramana’s coins that we put a pin on before. We know Toramana practiced Zoroastrianism of which fire-worship is a key element. Now while his successors gave up Zoroastrianism for Shaivism, they didn’t give up the fire. Another thing they didn’t give up was their martial ways. So once out of power, they seamlessly placed themselves into the Hindu fold as members of the one varna that could readily accommodate their martial aptitude—Kshatriya. This marks the beginning of the _Agnikula_ lineage.50 It offered the Huns of India not only a legendary bloodline but also legitimacy and acceptance as contributing members of the Hindu society.

In the century following Mihirakula’s death another new name emerged, finding its first mention in Bana’s Harshacharita—Gurjar.51 A second mention appears in a Sanskrit Inscription in Aihole. it’s a Jain monk’s eulogy to the Chalukya king Pulakeshin II.52 The Gurjars were a tribe of Kshatriyas settled in parts of Rajasthan and, in Bana’s story, in constant conflict with the Vardhans. While there’s still debate on their exact origins, some consensus exists on their Hunnic associations. It is now understood that all residual Huns that assumed the Agnikula etiology either became Gurjars or got absorbed into a preexisting Gurjar identity.5354 The very name Hun still lives on as one of the many Kshatriya gotras in Gurjar communities.55

Speaking of fire, a macabre new tradition to emerge under the Huns was that of _Sati_. The practice of widows giving up their lives on their husbands’ pyres simply did not exist before the sixth century. But it’s interestingly reminiscent of a similar practice common among nomadic communities of Inner Asia, the original Hunnic homeland. There, it was common to kill widows, concubines, and even horses, and bury them along with the dead warlord. It’s quite the coincidence that some of the earliest references to such a practice in the subcontinent come from Bana’s Harshacharita56 and the Eran pillar of Goparaja.57 With newer invasions, in the following millennium, the practice would assume a more prominent role in Hindu, especially Rajput, traditions. But it’s during the Huns that it all started. References to such self-immolations in Rigveda is now understood to be a later misinterpretation,58 as is the case with Mahābhārata.59

Scripturally, we’re still in the Dark Ages or Kaliyuga, because Kalki, the prophesied deliverer is yet to arrive. Interestingly, the idea of a future messiah rebooting the world to fix all evils in one clean sweep is shared by all major religions of today.
