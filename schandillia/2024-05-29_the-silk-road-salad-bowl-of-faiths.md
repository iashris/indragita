Before we start this story, it’s important to understand its geography. If you look at a map of Central Asia, several rivers or _daryas_ pop out in view, of which two are of immense import to every story of civilization—Amu and Syr. The Greeks called them Oxus and Jaxartes, respectively. Oxus originates in the Pamir knot and Jaxartes in the Tien Shan. Running more or less parallel, the two rivers irrigate a large expanse in between before finally pouring into the Aral Sea. This irrigated region between the two great waters is what the Greeks called Transoxiana, literally “Land beyond the Oxus.” This watershed is further split into Ferghana (northeast) and Sogdiana (southwest) by the Zarafshan or Sughd River (Polytimetus in Ancient Greek). Sogdiana is home to cities like Bukhara and Samarkand, and Ferghana would go on to gain notoriety as Babur’s homeland. Sughd, by the way, is Sogdiana in Tajik.

Sandwiched between the Amu and the Hindu Kush in the south is Tokharistan, better known as Bactria. This is where stood the famous city of Bactra, also known as Balkh. If Transoxiana is to the east of Oxus, what’s to the west? That’s the enormous territory of Khorasan, today split between Iran, Afghanistan, and Turkmenistan. And finally, to the extreme north of Khorasan is Khwarazm.

To wrap it all up, we have Central Asia that covers it all. Within it lies Transoxiana, surrounded by Khwarazm, Khorasan, and Bactria. Within Transoxiana are Ferghana and Sogdiana (Sughd in Tajik) and within Sogdiana are cities like Bukhara and Samarkand. In Bactria is Balkh. With that picture in clear view, we can get on with our journey, which starts all the way back in the pre-common era when Buddhism had just started crystallizing its presence in the region.

Historically, this had been a Zoroastrian domain since at least 1200 BC if not earlier. Some traditions even name Bactria as Zoroaster’s birthplace, although not much is known for sure. The veracity of any of this notwithstanding, Balkh remained the epicenter of Zoroastrianism for centuries until the second century BC. Between the third century BC and the second century AD, this was the Greco-Bactrian Kingdom. Since the Greeks weren’t particularly intolerant to the local faith, some cultural synthesis was unavoidable. The real upheaval came with the landmark commercial innovation called the Silk Road.

## Trade and Buddhism

Transoxiana had been a mercantile hub since the dawn of civilization. As early as 2000 BC, inhabitants of the Oxus basin had been trading wheat, millets, and ephedra with Western Siberian Hunter Gatherers along the Inner Asian Mountain Corridor. They maintained commercial relations with India, as evident from the Harappan settlement at Shortugai. Once domesticated, horses too joined the fray as a hot commodity. The Chinese were constantly at war with the northern barbarians from Mongolia which is why they even built the Great Wall. To support their war machinery, the Chinese needed horses from the steppe. In exchange, they had silk, rhubarb, and tea to offer. Trade was brisk, but also faced immense risk. There were raiders, bandits, and the remarkably hostile terrain. This is the problem the Silk Road was meant to solve. It was a network of ancient superhighways, each lined with _caravanserais_ or inns to provide both rejuvenation and security to tired merchants. Cities like Samarkand and Balkh became important nodes along this route and made the most of the windfall it brought.

Much of northwestern India was under the Greeks those days. Since one of the Silk Road arteries also ran through the Indian north, India emerged as an enthusiastic participant in the cultural and material exchange in Central Asia. This includes the dominant Indian doctrine of the time—Buddhism. Despite a Zoroastrian dominance, Bactria is understood to have known Buddhism almost since the very inception of the faith. The Pali canon speaks of two brothers, Tapussa and Bhallika, coming under Buddha’s tutelage on a trip to India and then returning home to evangelize.1 Most scholars identify them as traveling merchants from Bactrians.2

By the common era, Buddhism had firmly entrenched itself on the Silk Road as a major mercantile force. This was an interesting mix of business and religion. Monks who were on a mission to propagate their faith, built monasteries along the highway and started serving as caravanserais for traveling merchants. In exchange for hospitality, wealthy merchants patronize these monasteries both monetarily and ideologically. Buddhism offered a tempting escape from the doctrinal strictures of the merchants’ previous faiths. It gave them an attractive new paradigm where instead of one all-seeing, all-powerful entity being in charge of man’s destiny, it was one’s own actions. The idea of karma empowered men to chart their own fate. Wealth made it a tad easier. You could donate generously to monasteries, patronize _viharas_ , commission _stupas_ , and accumulate “karmic points” in exchange. Buddhism made virtues calculable. The goal was not heaven or hell, it was _nirvana_ or enlightenment. To traders and merchants, this was naturally an attractive idea. In fact, Buddha’s very first disciples after his Enlightenment were also merchants, the siblings we spoke of earlier.

Around 120 BC, Bactria came under the rule of a nomadic Indo-European tribe from what’s today China’s Gansu province. Chinese chronicles record it by the name Guishang and it was one of the five that made up the larger confederacy called Yuezhi. A century and a half later the Guishang had crossed the Hindu Kush and started building an empire in India where the name corrupted to Koshano or Kushan. The empire lasted more than three hundred years and proved extremely propitious for Buddhism. The fourth-generation Kushan king Kanishka heavily patronized the religion even though he himself never converted into it. A big part of this was driven by economic interests. A new sect of Buddhism called Mahayana had already taken form by the time of Kushans and with the newfound imperial patronage it started making its way into Bactria and Transoxiana. The Kushans were a polity of many firsts—first Buddha images in human form, first gold coinage in India, first translation of Sanskrit works into a Chinese language. Kanishka’s role as a celebrated patron of Buddhism finds ample recognition in Chinese sources.3

The most notable innovation brought about under Kushan patronage was in the area of Buddhist iconography. Until that point, all depictions of the Buddha were aniconic—a Bodhi tree, a lotus bloom, a pair of footprints, and so on.4 With the Kushans, he finally acquired a human form.5 And Central Asia, along with Mathura, became the epicenter of this new frenzy. By the third century AD, Bactria had started building its two Buddhas in Bamiyan, a work that would later stupefy Xuanzang and stand tall for two extremely hostile millennia. Although these developments happened under the Kushans, the style was unmistakably Greek. The statues took a good two centuries to complete and by the time they were done, Bactria was under a new Turkic race, the Hephthalites or White Huns. By the sixth century, the Silk Road brought in more Turkic peoples from the north into the region. As the politics grew volatile, religion grew stronger. With sovereignty shifting between various Turkic powers with increasing unpredictability, religious institutions offered a refuge of stability and social cohesion. Stability is good for business. Naturally, an increasing number of merchants and scholars started gravitating toward monasteries and other non-political institutions. Over time, the cities’ elites were all part of the region’s theological evolution for the most practical of reasons.

Although there was Buddhist presence in both Bactria and Sogdiana, Chinese sources attribute their Buddhism specifically to Sogdiana. Although they recognize Kanishka as a patron of note, they’re very clear on the earliest Buddhist evangelists in the Han country being Sogdians and not Indians. Many of these pioneered the translation of Buddhist scripture from Sanskrit to the Chinese language which establishes that at least some Sogdians must have spent time in India. Interestingly, though, there was no one official religion in Sogdiana. The oligarchic city-states were too autonomous, and despite the pronounced Buddhist influence, never graduated to theocracy.

## Rendezvous with Zoroastrianism

When Buddhism came to Central Asia, the region was completely under Zoroastrianism, a religion that traces its roots all the way back to the second millennium BC. Bactria, as we’ve already noted is traditionally held as the birthplace of Zoroaster himself. And yet, the creed followed here was not exactly the one followed in Iran. Just as the Christianity of, say, Ethiopia is different from the Christianity of America today, the Zoroastrianism of Sogdiana was different from the Zoroastrianism of Iran then. In Sogdian cities like Samarkand, for instance, it was a polytheistic system where every household had a patron deity of its own. While Iran had just an Ahura Mazda, Sogdiana had a whole pantheon of local and foreign deities. Even the Sogdian diaspora in places like China practiced this eclectic form of Zoroastrianism as a number of elaborate tombs recently unearthed in western China indicate. But why, or rather how? As one would expect, this was the influence of other religions that entered the region in the early centuries of the common era.

One such influence was Manicheism which started sometime in the third century AD. But a major part of it traced a more Indic source. Buddhism arrived and mainstreamed in the region with the opening of the Silk Road. And when it did, much synthesis followed. Earlier, the consensus was on conflict, especially as understood from the writings of Xuanzang. The Chinese traveler came to Samarkand in 630 AD and journaled his experiences in his famous travelogue. He wrote that the people of this city did not worship Buddha but fire. As we all know, fire worship is a key Zoroastrian feature. He further wrote that these worshipers often chased away Buddhists from their temples with pieces of burning wood or firebrands.6 He then successfully lobbied the local ruler to put an end to this practice.

But didn’t we just learn from Chinese sources that Buddhism was well-entrenched in Sogdian cities and that some of the earliest Buddhist evangelists in China were Sogdian merchants? Then how could they be at the receiving end of Zoroastrian oppression as late as the seventh century? The two situations are difficult to reconcile with each other also because we know the region was ruled by the mercantile class which was very pragmatic in its approach to religion. Buddhism had a strong hold on the Silk Road commerce and there were at least two prominent monasteries in Samarkand at the time. Which would make it extremely unwise of the city’s elite to alienate the community on religious grounds. What Xuanzang witnessed was more likely a synthesis of Buddhism and Zoroastrianism, a process that traces its beginnings in the Kushan period. The Kushan pantheon was an eclectic mix of Buddhist, Zoroastrian, Manichaean, and even Shaivite elements. The firebrands Xuanzang saw could be a result of this synthesis, a Zoroastrian ritual to worship Buddha.

## Life in Sogdiana

An excellent illustration of Sogdiana’s interfaith and intercultural symbiosis could be seen in the attitude of the region’s ruling class. When Xuanzang visited Samarkand, for instance, the local ruler was a vassal of the Western Turkic Khaganate, with Tong Yabghu Qaghan or Yabgu Khan as the overlord. But culturally the city was more aligned with its southern Zoroastrian neighbors, the Sasanians. That’s what gave it a distinct Zoroastrian character. And yet, there was no discord or confusion so far as the ruling class was concerned. Yabgu Khan himself held Buddhism in high regard even though he practiced Zoroastrian rituals personally.7 This respect also extended to the “Dharma Master” or Xuanzang. While the traveler’s influence could very well be an exaggeration as his biography credits him personally with effecting the amity between Sogdian Zoroastrians and Sogdian Buddhists, we can nevertheless be certain of the amity.

This cultural intercourse had an uncomplicated reason. During the seventh century, Turkic powers held the sway from the frontiers of China all the way to the Hindu Kush. The Silk Road trade had given them a great deal of wealth and power. This was only possible with liberal religious patronage. The Turkic khagans were in essence nomadic. Their sedentary counterparts, by contrast, were skilled merchants and urban administrators. One offered security, the other offered wealth and stability. Thus, out of pragmatic wisdom, the khagans made it a practice to patronize, even adopt, whatever religion their city-dwelling counterparts followed. This is how they graduated from nomadic warriors to established oligarchic plutocrats. This impartial patronage also led to a rich synthesis of doctrines. Yagbu Khan, for instance, followed Zoroastrianism like the Sogdians with whom he had much of his dealings. At the same time, the Turkic ruler of Huoguo followed Buddhism, in consonance with his immediate Bactrian or Tokharian environs.8

A common thread running through all these societies was prosperity and the emphasis on fun. Amidst all the raids and wars and political intrigues, entertainment remained an integral aspect of the Central Asian life. Xuanzang’s writings too speak of Yabgu Khan’s lavish banquets where grape wine was the dominant beverage. Given his status as Dharma Master, Xuanzang was never served alcohol or meat. Instead, they served him grape juice, sugar, pancakes, and honey. Grapes were a cornerstone of Bactrian and Sogdian culinary identity. This fact would prove consequential later.

## Life in Balkh

After Sogdiana, Xuanzang planned to continue southward into India, but the ruler of Huoguo convinced him to take a detour instead. He ruled over a valley to the east of Balkh in today’s the Afghan province of Kunduz and, like many Turkic rulers in the region, liberally patronized Buddhism. The religion seems to have been more entrenched in Tokharistan than in Sogdiana.

Once in Balkh, the pilgrim came across what he called Nafusengjialan. It was the center of Tokharistan’s Buddhist life, the most wealthy and prestigious establishment in the region. It was a Buddhist monastery, Nafusengjialan being Chinese for its local name _nava vihara_ or “New Monastery,” and it housed sacred relics of the Buddha himself. Remember Tapussa and Bhallika? They’re the ones believed to have brought those relics from India. According to Xuanzang, Balkh those days held the epithet of Little Rajagraha, given to it by both locals as well as its Turkic overlords.9 This name is a shout-out to the original city of Rajagraha—today, Rajgir—in the lower Gangetic plains. The Indian city is where Buddha is said to have first preached immediately after Enlightenment. It was also the first capital of India’s first major kingdom, Magadha. Balkh being called Rajagraha, therefore, is a clear indication of its esteem as a major Buddhist center.

The patron deity of Balkh was a yaksha named Vaiśravaṇa. In China, they call him Píshāméntiān. Originally based on the Hindu character of Kuber, Vaiśravaṇa is noted in scriptures as the “guardian of the north,” one of the Four Heavenly Kings or Tiānwáng. As a guardian deity, Vaiśravaṇa was both feared and revered for his warlike attributes.

Generous mercantile patronage meant it was also a wealthy city. So wealthy it attracted far more than just pilgrims and merchants. Buddha statues here were liberally adorned with jewels and gemstones donated by wealthy patrons. Which made Balk a tempting target for bandits and warlords looking to make a quick fortune.10 This made Vaiśravaṇa’s job even more crucial. One legend as narrated to Xuanzang, for instance, speaks of a khan princeling who planned to rob the monastery.11 In preparation, he even stationed his warriors outside the walls in the night. The raid was planned for the following morning. Fortunately, the plan never materialized because as the khan slept that night, Vaiśravaṇa appeared in his dream and stabbed him in the chest with a long pike.12 As the princeling jolted out of the nightmare, he felt a piercing pain in the chest which turned out to be a fatal heart attack.1314

Long story short, the monastery braved several attempts to loot its treasures over centuries. This was due as much to the rulers as to folklore. The establishment was managed by a dynasty of generational Buddhist priests or _pramukhs_. This family was in charge of not only the administration but also security. Turkic raids were routine and the pramukhs did well to weather them all.

Traveling further through Tokharistan, on his way to India, Xuanzang passed through the Bamiyan valley where he found himself awe-struck by the two enormous Buddhas cut into the mountain.15 These titans were built well after the Kushans, when the region was under the Hephthalites, or White Huns, and other Turkic overlords. These figures, that unfortunately no longer stand, remain the biggest statement of Buddhist expression in the history of the creed.

## From Transoxiana to China

As already noted, China received Buddhism from Sogdiana who got it either from neighboring Bactria or directly from India. Whatever be the case, an eclectic form of Buddhism with copious Zoroastrian cultural influence had reached China by the seventh century. This intercourse, unsurprisingly, traces its roots in the Sogdian city-state of Samarkand where, as we noted earlier, pragmatic considerations brought the two cultures in close proximity to each other without friction or conflict. While Buddhism introduced a new pantheon to the Zoroastrian cult, Zoroastrianism introduced new fire rituals to Buddhism. It’s this synthesis that would later reach China as a new form of Buddhism.

Technically, China’s first interface with Buddhism can be traced all the way back to the second century when Parthian prince An Shigao and Kushan evangelist Lokaksema produced some of the first Chinese translations of the Buddhist scripture. But it’s only in the sixth century that things became more pronounced when a large number of Sogdian merchants migrated to the Chinese heartland. The Buddhism that arrived in China this time was quite distinct from the one that arrived with the Kushans four centuries earlier. This time there was a great deal of Zoroastrian cultural influence. This Zoroastrian influence was, as we’ve discussed before, the result of a larger cultural symbiosis between nomadic khagan warlords and sedentary Silk Road merchants in Central Asia.

The earliest evidence of this symbiosis can be seen in a grave in the city of Xi'an in Western China. It belongs to a Sogdian nobleman named An Jia.16 He originally came from An, or Bukhara, but later emigrated to China in the sixth century migration wave. We know he was Zoroastrian from the fire altars depicted on his grave. It’s shown supported by three camels on the upper part of the gate.17 But what the grave also depicts is two chiefs on horsebacks, one Sogdian and the other Turkic, in two separate scenes. In one scene, they’re shown meeting and in the other, socializing at a banquet, clearly hinting at the symbiosis we’ve been talking about all along.18 An Jia died in 579 AD. Throughout the sixth and seventh centuries, the Sogdians, the Bactrians, and the Turks cooperated and coexisted in the interest of a shared objective—entrepreneurship. This Buddhist-Zoroastrian cooperation was not strictly confined to Central Asia either. The Kushans were known for their patronage of both religions along with Hinduism, even though they ultimately gravitated toward Buddhism.

In its time, Sogdiana was famous for two things—its wine and its dancers. And both are crucial to this story, as we’ll find out later. An Jia, as we’ve learned, was not the only Sogdian in seventh-century China, it was a wave. The wealthy among them, commissioned grand tombs with elaborate engravings that depicted the Sogdian life similar to what we saw on An Jia’s. Nostalgia, perhaps? These engravings and murals offer an excellent insight into the fabled Sogdian nightlife. There was wine, but there were also music, dance, acrobatics, and talent show. Many dancers and acrobats also made their way into China during the great migration of the sixth century.

Tank dynasty history books are replete with mentions of these performers from Samarkand that came in great numbers on horsebacks and camels. The musicians often wore red robes with brocade collars and black scarves, all in silk, to give themselves a distinct appearance. The dancers wore red blouses with green sleeves and green pants and a white sash around the waist.19 Again, all silk. You could also recognize them by the distinct red shoes.20 This is how the Tang chronicles described them. As for the dance, they called it _Húxuăn wu_ or the Sogdian Whirl.21 The same was a reference to the peculiar way the dancer would whirl inside a designated circle. Sources like the _Old Book of Tang_ speak of Sogdians who “whirled like the wind.”22 The performance finds copious depiction on funerary arts, both Sogdian and native Chinese which attests to its wild popularity. Even figurines were produced in the likeness of these exotic performers. Put a pin on this dance, we’ll return to it later.

If China got the whirl from Sogdiana, Sogdiana got a new musical instrument from China. The double-reed wind instrument called _guanzi_. The ancient Chinese musical classification assorts it as a bamboo instrument and the earliest use dates back to centuries before the common era, well into the Zhou dynasty period. Traditionally, the instrument is played during both fun and somber occasions—weddings, funerals, and everything in between. Does this ring a bell? It should, we’ll see toward the end of this article. Meanwhile, guanzi joined cymbals, flutes, drums, and harps as the orchestra around the Sogdian whirl.23 The dance was now a complete entertainment package in and of itself.

## The Caliphate Meets the Oxus

The middle of the first millennium was an eventful period in the story of Central Asia. While merchants from Samarkand and Bukhara took the Sogdian way of life to China, a very different group was bringing a spectacularly different way of life to Central Asia. By the middle of the seventh century, the Arab war machine had already swallowed the Zoroastrian Sasanian country of Khorasan that lay between them and Central Asia. Over the following decades, this conquest expanded further to ultimately include Bactria and Sogdiana. These two regions were reorganized into a new administrative unit called Outer Khorasan. All of this happened within two generations of Muhammad who died in 632 AD. But why?

So the Muslims had always sought, as a part of doctrinal glory, two conquests in particular—al-Hind or India in the east and al-Andalus or Spain in the west. Efforts for both had been afoot since the very beginning of the Caliphate but really picked up in the early years of the eighth century. Jihad isn’t cheap. Wars cost money. And that’s where a third object of desire comes in. China’s silk had enjoyed status as a highly prized commodity all the way since Roman times and one stood to make a lot of money if in possession of its monopoly. Thus, China became an important desirable for the Umayyad Caliphate.

Before we proceed, a quick word on the Muslim world’s India plans. The envisioned conquest had a name we’re all too familiar with today— _Ghazwa-e-Hind_. A controversial subject which is why the need for this special aside. The concept can most confidently be traced to al-Nasaʾi’s _al-Sunan al-Sughra_ , one of six canonical hadith collections together called _al-Sihah al-Sitta_. Hadiths are traditionally graded in four different categories in descending order of authenticity— _sahih_ , _hasan_ , _da‘if_ , and _mawduʻ_. The one we’re discussing is hasan, which makes it less authentic than sahih but “sufficient for use as supporting evidence.” It’s therefore often dismissed by a section of modern academicians as inauthentic. Which seems a little reckless because India has been an object of Arab ambition since the days of Uthman if not earlier.24 Besides, even to this day, the hadith finds unambiguous endorsement from Islamic entities of immense influence and social renown, the latest being the seminary at Deoband.25 Be that as it may, the hadith sure drove the Arab military projects in the east for centuries, so the question of its authenticity frankly is moot. Now back to China and Central Asia.

Those days, the closest Arab domain to the region was Iraq whose governor was a man named al-Hajjaj ibn Yusuf. He reported directly to the Caliph in Damascus. He had one brief from Damascus, to take China, which he in turn delegated to two of his most trusted lieutenants—Qutayba ibn Muslim, the governor of Khorasan, and bin Qasim, governor of Sindh. He set them off with the promise that whoever got there first would get to govern China for the Caliphate. The reward was tempting, the project was difficult.

Naturally, the road to that Holy Grail ran through the Silk Road stations of Transoxiana. Therefore, it made sense to bring this region under Arab dominance first. Khorasan was of interest to the Umayyads only as a conduit to Central Asia. Control of key Silk Road stations in Bactria and Sogdiana meant a windfall that could keep the jihad engines running in Europe, Africa, India, until finally the conquest of China. In sum, attention shifted to Central Asia. And on this, it would seem, Qutayba took the lead. Bin Qasim got busy with India. By 712 AD, the Arabs had taken both India—more accurately, Sindh—and southern Spain. But in addition, Qutayba had also taken some extremely profitable territories along the Amu Darya including Khwarazm and Sogdiana. Now Arabs were the masters of Silk Road.

The next step was to reshape the Central Asian demography to a more amenable form. Once again, Khorasan was a mere steppingstone to Central Asia. That’s why initially, most Arab migrants rather than settling in Persia and Khorasan, continued further east to ultimately settle in Transoxiana and Tokharistan. In fact, Khorasan served, for the most part, as a convenient refuge for Muslim dissidents and exiles.

Of course, Qutayba’s incursion wasn’t readily welcome in Sogdiana. While Zoroastrianism and Buddhism had peacefully coexisted in the region for centuries, this new injection from Arabia was violent and driven not by coexistence but by conquest. In other words, it was different. Much of these early Umayyad incursions was recorded much later by Abbasid historians from Khorasan and Persia, most notably al-Tabari. He wrote extensively about the Arab invasions into Transoxiana and the Sogdian resistance to it. The Sogdians were heavily aided in their resistance by their Turkic overlords and for a good while, presented stiff defiance to the invaders. Eventually, of course, they lost. Sogdiana entered Arab records as al-Sughd and instantly acquired a position of prestige in the Muslim world. At one point, an Arab commander even forbade his troops from chasing the Sogdians who were fleeing from the battle, just to prevent the city’s destruction.26

Qutayba brought the same unhinged barbarism and fundamentalism that’s typically and particularly associated with Arab warlords of the early Caliphal period. It’s said he tried to convert Bukhara three times with no success as people kept reverting to their pre-Islamic ways. The fourth time, he built a large mosque in the city and ordered mandatory attendance to its Friday prayers. This time, obviously under duress, the plan worked and few apostatized. Rebellions were still not infrequent. The Arab response each time was as brutal as the last. For instance, when Qutayba’s forces crushed a rebellion in Paykend, a trading oasis in Bukhara, he ordered every man in town killed and every woman and child enslaved.27 The city was reduced to rubble. A one-eyed merchant is recorded to have offered him 5,000 pieces of silk, worth a million dirhams then, in exchange for his life. Qutayba refused the offer and executed him anyway.28 Many men escaped this fate only because they were away on business in China. Later they paid hefty ransoms to retrieve their wives and children and rebuilt the city. Eventually, Bukhara relented and accepted its fate as an Arab colony.

While Khwarazm and Bukhara came around and ended up as allies, Samarkand resisted much longer. Which is why it’s here that Qutayba’s barbarity was particularly pronounced. This isn’t without reason though. Samarkand was to Central Asia what Rome was to Europe. Naturally, when Arabs decided to gentrify the region, this city became their first choice. Qutayba not only built several mosques but even forced the locals out of their homes to make room for the immigrating Arabs. It was, in every sense of the term, a hostile occupation.

724 AD was a particularly difficult year for the Arabs when the Umayyad troops suffered a major setback against Sogdian rebels in Samarkand. To resolve this situation, the governor of Khorasan Ashras ibn ʿAbd Allah al-Sulami announced tax exemption for converts to Islam, the prerequisite being circumcision and recitation of one _surah_ from the Qurʾan. The idea worked and many Sogdians converted, but facing a sudden loss of revenue, Ashras reneged on the deal triggering a new wave of rebellion. Sogdians even called in Turkic reinforcements this time. Meanwhile, China slowly fell off the Arab bucket list, probably because control of Silk Road was far more profitable for the efforts than control of silk production.

Interestingly, despite losing much of Central Asia to the Arabs, the khagans continued to send envoys to the Tang court in the name of former Turkic rulers anyway. Between 720 and 750 AD, there was a long chain of envoys representing Yabgu Khan, who was dead for over a hundred years now. This was not for any frivolous reason. The idea was to keep China under the impression that the khagans were still in control of affairs. This way, an alliance could easily be appealed for. These envoys even carried gifts for the Chinese emperor with requests for Chinese aid against the Arabs. In 719, a letter purportedly from the “king of Samarkand” spoke of Qutayba’s invasion six years earlier with three hundred mangonels. With the letter came a gift of one horse, one Bactrian camel, and two donkeys. The ask? Military aid, of course. At other times, gifts included the fabled Sogdian wine, the equally fabled Sogdian whirling dancers, and even exotic beasts like cheetahs. All this, however, came to an abrupt end in the middle of the century. And the reason was the emergence of a new power center in the Muslim world—the Abbasids.

## The Abbasid Upheaval

In 750 AD, a little over a century after Muhammad, Abu al-ʿAbbas al-Saffah led a rebellion against the decadent Umayyad regime with the manifesto of restoring Islam to its pristine Rashidun form and the Caliphate to Muhammad’s bloodline. The manifesto worked and the rebellion succeeded. The Abbasid Caliphate was now in session. The new regime also moved capital from Damascus and Harran to Kufa in Iraq. With this, the Muslim world’s epicenter had moved eastward and closer to the fringes of the empire, Transoxiana. In fact, the entire military aspect of the Abbasid Revolution was led not by an Arab but by a Persian general from Transoxiana named Abu Muslim. Caliph al-Mansur later had hm murdered by deceit.

A lot changed with this caliphal switch. The Abbasids proved far warmer to the peoples of Transoxiana than the Umayyads had. Realizing the futility of brute force in the region, they quickly adopted a more diplomatic approach. One big factor here was the large-scale presence of non-Arabs in the Abbasid administration. The regime’s character was distinctly more Persian than Arab and that reflected in its culture. The Persians always shared an affinity with the Turkic peoples of Transoxiana that the Arabs didn’t. The Abbasids followed a culture of assimilation. Many Turkic elites were absorbed into the Abbasid bureaucracy. By the time the capital moved to the newly created Baghdad, the Turkic presence was already strong. On their part, the Abbasids offered them an unprecedented cultural leeway which allowed the people of Transoxiana to maintain their traditional religious and cultural practices even after publicly converting to Islam. This had a curious result both culturally and politically.

Remember how the khagans had warmed up to the Chinese during the Umayyad oppression? Now they were warming up to the Abbasids. Also, even though the Muslims had given up on their China plans, China was now growing interested in Central Asia. Within a year of the new Caliphate’s inauguration, the Tang forces met the Muslims in the Battle of Talas for the control of the Syr Darya. The engagement took place near the Kyrgyz–Kazakh border and is named after the Talas River. The Arabs had an unlikely Tibetan Empire on its side. They won. This brought Central Asia decisively within Islamic fold for the first time. One landmark outcome of this battle was the introduction of paper to the world at large. The technology had been a Chinese monopoly so far but was now in Arab hands thanks to the thousands of Chinese prisoners of war that were brought to Samarkand.

## A New Islam

By this point, the Turkic natives were thoroughly in the Abbasid fold, not just territorially but also militarily and culturally. They were now allies. This was the sharpest departure from how the Umayyads had run their affairs in the region. Instead of adversaries, the Abbasids saw them as partners. Instead of enslaving the conquered, they allowed them to join the Islamic cause and grow. The Turkic elites, on their part, had a long-standing policy of allying with power, which is how as we saw they patronized both Buddhism and Zoroastrianism before Islam. Now that dominant power was Islam and under the Abbasids, it showed less belligerence than before. Therefore, it made little business sense to continue the hostility. This had implications.

The partnership meant many Turkic scholars, warriors, and merchants entered the Abbasid administration as important participants in their society. Baghdad became a major Turkic center. This admixture created a whole new class of elites that would go on to define what came to be known as the “Golden Age of Islam.” We’ll return to this in a bit. First let’s explore Baghdad’s cultural landscape of the time.

Part of this cultural diffusion was the incorporation of forbidden elements such as wine and dance into the Islamic way of life. While they still remain forbidden, the Abbasids are known for their indulgence. Remember the Sogdian whirl? That too entered Islam as a characteristic feature of Sufism. Few remember the Sogdian whirl today, but everybody recognizes whirling dervishes. And now back to the Turkic elites.

Among the Central Asian movers and shakers in the pre-Islamic times was the family of hereditary priests in control of the Buddhist monastery in Tokharistan. Let’s return to the pin we dropped on the nava vihara of Balkh. The administration of this establishment was in the hands of the head priest or pramukh, a hereditary position that had been in the family likely since the days of Kanishka. With the advent of the Abbasids, this family converted. The details of this conversion isn’t known well, but the official Abbasid historian Mas'udi tells us that after the initial destruction of both Balkh and the monastery, which the Muslims called Nawbahar, the pramukh of the time saw merit in marking a presence in the Abbasid capital to protect the interests of his family. So he sent his son to Baghdad. Here, the son likely adopted Islam and acquired a new name, Khalid al Barmak. Barmak is how the Muslims pronounced pramukh.

Khalid quickly grew in influence and had the Arab commander of Khorasan order Balkh’s reconstruction. This was 725 AD. Despite Khalid’s possible conversion, it’s said, Nawbahar remained active after its reconstruction all the way till the Mongols arrived. Even as late as the tenth century, according to Firdowsi’s Shahnameh, it was as important to the locals as Mecca was to the Arabs. When the Mongols arrived two centuries later, Genghis Khan’s own historian Ata-Malik Juvaini quoted Firdowsi on the same.

While we’re not sure if Khalid Barmaki actually became Muslim, we know for sure that his grandson was. By his time, the Barmaki family had grown astronomically influential and close to the Caliph. Fadl ibn Yahya ibn Khalid Barmakid even became the governor of Khorasan and when he did, he built a number of mosques in the region. A big driver of the Abbasid longevity in contrast to its predecessors was paper. Doctrines and imperial fiats could be disseminated with unprecedented efficiency thanks to paper. While many credit the Chinese prisoners of Talas with introducing the technology to the Muslims, some also credits the Barmakids. According to this theory, papermaking was brought to Transoxiana long before the Battle of Talas by Buddhist merchants of Balkh who made frequent trips to Han China. Whatever be the case, the Barmakids sure deserve the credit for bringing it to the Abbasid heartland in Baghdad.

Even before Caliph Harun al-Rashid built his famous House of Wisdom, the Barmakids were advocating for translation of important works from Greek, Chinese, and Sanskrit. In fact, their advocacy was a crucial precursor to the eventual establishment of the House of Wisdom and inauguration of the so-called Golden Age. Many works of Sanskrit literature entered the Arabic corpus thanks in this period. Take Panchatantra, for instance, which came to Baghdad as _Kalīla wa-Dimna_. Even the famous collection of folktales produced during this period, the One Thousand and One Nights, owe much of its content to parables originally penned in Sanskrit among other languages. Unfortunately, the Barmakids did not get to enjoy much of its fruits as the family abruptly fell out of favor at the Caliph’s court and lost its influence under Harun al-Rashid. A scion was even executed. The Baramkeh suburb of Damascus still bears witness to what was once the most powerful family in all of Baghdad second only to the Caliph himself.

But the Barmakids are only one example. The Arab-Turkic intercourse produced many more Islamic elites from the Turkic stock. A most notable example is a man from Bukhara who acquired much doctrinal influence as an early compiler of hadiths in the ninth century, Muhammad al-Bukhari. In the area of pedagogy, we have another example from Khwarazm who introduced the Islamic society to a whole new world of Hindu arithmetic, Muhammad ibn Musa al-Khwarizmi. From a family of Turkic military commanders came a most unlikely religious influencer, philosopher al-Farabi. He studied Alexandrian Christianity in Baghdad and translated the likes of Plato.

By the middle of the tenth century, Turkic slave-soldiers called Mamluks had become a force to reckon with in the Islamic world. By the eleventh century, they’d started to eclipse even the Caliph in authority, which opened a window of opportunity for several local ambitions far from Baghdad. The Caliph in Baghdad being reduced to a nominal figurehead meant several Turkic Amirs and started exploring their own territorial ambitions. Among them was Mahmud of Ghazni who became the first to adopt the epithet of Sultan. This had the curious effect of arresting the brain drain to Baghdad. Now, intellectuals of Transoxiana and around no longer depended on Baghdad’s validation to build influence as patronage from the local Amir or Sultan could work just fine. One such intellectual was Abu Rayhan Muhammad ibn Ahmad al-Biruni, or just al-Biruni for short, who was captured as a slave during Mahmud’s conquest of Khwarazm and sent to India to study Indian sciences.

Ibn Sina—or Avicenna, as the Europeans would later call him—was another scholar who never went to Baghdad and instead, built his entire career within the confines of Central Asia, more specifically Bukhara. A prolific learner, Avicenna learned everything from Euclidean geometry to Indian arithmetic to Aristotelian philosophy to Arabic literature. In fact, he’s said to have read Aristotle’s works forty times and memorized the whole corpus. But what eventually brought him intellectual superstardom was his canon on medicine which, for centuries remained the standard even in Europe.

## Islam after Genghis Khan

The diminished authority of the Caliphate, as we just saw, paved the way for regionalized Turkic polities to sprout all over the Islamic world and even beyond. Among them was India where a Turkic boy captured and sold in the slave market of Nishapur in Iran grew up the ranks to become a Sultan. But something else happened at the same time. That year not one but two ex-slaves captured in war inaugurated their respective innings as empire-builders in two different lands—Qutb ud-Din Aibak in India and Genghis Khan in Mongolia. While Aibak contended with expanding within the Indian subcontinent, Khan was a little more ambitious. He was keen on conquering all he surveyed. By whatever means possible.

As was the case with pre-Islamic Turkic khagans, the Mongols were highly talented equestrian warlords known for their martial skills and nomadic lifestyle. But far more than them, the Mongol horde was a ruthless war machine where compassion held no meaning. The objective was straightforward—absorb whatever submits, destroy whatever resists. They would burn entire cities and slaughter every man, woman, children, even pets, just to punish refusal. The Mongol barbarity made Islam look benign in contrast.

But whereas resistance attracted wholesale destruction and carnage, submission was rewarded with utmost peace and liberty in every way imaginable, including religious. Just like the Turkic khagans before them, the Mongols were least concerned with the religion being practiced in their realm. There was never an effort to convert anyone or even favor a creed. This stemmed from the same sense of pragmatism that had once made the Turkic elites patronize both Zoroastrianism and Buddhism.

Being nomadic, the Mongols had zero experience in governance. Therefore, even in their brutality, there was a method to the madness. It wasn’t indiscriminate. While on the one hand they wouldn’t spare even animals, on the other hand they would absorb skilled administrators and educated elites into their own system as useful _ortoghs_ or partners. This way, several Central Asian Muslims of status ended up in newly conquered Mongol territories as administrators and governors. Despite his barbarity, Genghis Khan thoroughly acknowledged the significance of scholars and men of letters. Such individuals were issued _protection letters_ that they could produce during a Mongol raid to avoid slaughter. This policy wasn’t exclusive to Muslims, Genghis Khan’s own advisor was a Khitan national from China named Yelü Chucai. The Khitan were also a nomadic people like the Mongols, but their ruling elite had pivoted to empire-building and established itself in Northeast China as the famous Liao dynasty. Yelü had entered Genghis Khan’s service after a successful Mongol invasion and would later accompany the Khan to Central Asia.

Yelü’s account of Samarkand during its Mongol invasion tells us that the region was still fertile and abundant in gardens, canals, fountains, and farm produce. Like past historians, he too wrote of Samarkand’s legendary wine culture and windmills. But all this suffered heavy destruction during the invasion which left both Samarkand and Bukhara in utter ruins. But once the conquest was over, the city was rebuilt just as quickly. While Muslims are known for the oppressive jizya extortion from their non-Muslim subjects, the Mongols didn’t follow identity-based taxation. More than that, they even exempted farm produce and wine from taxation. To local merchants of Sogdiana, this was a welcome relief. This fostered trust and loyalty.

About a hundred years later when ibn Battuta of Morocco visited the place on his way to India, he found what he described in his chronicles, “one of the most perfectly built cities in the world.” He wrote of several gardens and waterwheels while also noting a number of old ruins from the Mongol invasion days. By now, the entire region was under what we call the Chagatai Khanate. The Mongol overlords here had long converted to Islam, probably out of the same practicalities that once moved Yabgu Khan into adopting Zoroastrianism.

Such was the Mongol indifference to religious hardlines that they even allowed the locals to offer prayers at the tomb of a Muslim martyr who had died fighting Genghis Khan. Even the Mongols prayed there.

But not everyone came around to this new scheme of things. Some were terrified enough to consider fleeing instead. Among them was a theologian and Sufi mystic from Balkh named Jalal al-Din Muhammad Rumi, Rumi for short. Balkh was then part of a Turkic polity called the Sultanate of Rum, notionally subservient to the Caliphate in Baghdad. Rum, because the regime had taken much of Anatolia from the Byzantines and now considered themselves the heir to the Roman Empire in the east. No wonder when Rumi fled Balkh after the Mongol arrival, his preferred destination was Konya in Anatolia.

Concurrently, another flight out of Balkh was Minhaj-i Siraj Juzjani, a Persian historian from what’s today the Afghan province of Jowzjan. Instead of Anatolia, however, Juzjani went east and settled in Delhi where he’d spend the rest of his days as the Delhi Sultanate’s principal historian. We know his for his seminal work of history, the _Tabaqat-i Nasiri_. In fact, Delhi became home to many such celebrity refugees from Sogdiana and around, among them forebears of Amir Khusrau and Nizamuddin Auliya.

## Central Asian Sufism

We already know how the Mongols partnered with conquered aristocracies as part of diplomatic outreach using protection letters. This was a remarkably effective way to secure loyalties from disparate cultures and religions. That said, the refugees that streamed into various parts of the Islamic and non-Islamic world brought a whole new kind of Islam to the fore. Remember the whirling dervishes that we spoke of earlier? This is how it became a phenomenon. Rumi is often credited with bringing what was essentially a pre-Islamic Sogdian tradition to the Muslim world even though he himself credits others. The performance even acquired a new name in the Islamic world— _Sama_. A whole new Sufi order called Mevlevi had emerged around this tradition in Anatolia by the time Rumi died. That’s why to this day, the dervishes are more closely associated with Turkey than with any other place. With the ancestors of Nizamuddin Auliya, Sufism also entered India.

By this point, the Mongols had already sacked Baghdad and the Abbasids were as much Caliphs as the Mughals would be emperors after Aurangzeb. With Baghdad gone, they had to move base to Cairo where they were mere figureheads of doctrinal authority under the aegis of local Mamluk overlords. The slaves of the past were now the overlords of the Caliph. The era of the Arabs had officially ended. The Sultans of various sovereign kingdoms, however, still needed the investiture from the Caliph to legitimize their rule. In 1343, for instance, Muhammad bin Tughlaq sent an envoy all the way to Cairo just to secure the Caliph’s blessings for his kingship. But beyond that, the Caliph did not hold even doctrinal authority anymore. Which is how a number of Sufi orders originating in Central Asia suddenly started gaining prominence. So much so that they grew even more influential than any Sunni or Shiʿa _ulama_ , especially in India.

The Sogdian whirl or Sama, while initially led by the Mevlevi order, slowly acquired a much wider appeal. Ibn Battuta even noted its presence in far-flunk Granada where he encountered six dervishes, including two from India. In India, Sama became a Sufi mainstay of the Chishti order in India, especially in the Deccan where Burhanuddin Gharib, known for his Sama performances, succeeded Nizamuddin Auliya as the order’s precept. Amir Khusrau wrote many lyrics for Sama performances when under Nizamuddin Auliya’s tutelage. Such was the Sufi influence that Sama remained a dominant artform despite every attempt by the ulamas to abolish it as unislamic.

An interesting effect of Turkic Islam’s prominence in India was the rapid development of Delhi as a world city. The Mamluks of the city missed their ancestral homeland back in Transoxiana and longed to someday go back. They missed its gardens, its streams, its fountains, its grapes, and most importantly, its wine. This would form the emotional leitmotif through the entire Muslim rule in India even beyond the Sultanate. Babur too would die in India wishing to return to Andijan someday. This nostalgia pushed the Mamluk Sultans into developing their Indian capital in the image of Samarkand.

While the Mamluks of India could never reconquer Transoxiana from the Mongols, the Mongols did at one point come dangerously close to India. This was when they invaded and conquered Lahore. Juzjani attributes the ease of this conquest to the betrayal of city’s business class which chose to ally with the invaders instead of resisting. It was the same pragmatism that we’ve seen in play among the Turkic mercantile aristocracy in Central Asia since the days of Kanishka. These traders all had protection letters from the Khan which immunized them from slaughter or disruption. Luckily for the Sultanate, the Mongols did not venture any further east and remained confined to Lahore until the final expulsion from even there in the fourteenth century.

So this is the story of how the Silk Road brought three absolutely disjoint religions together in both violent and nonviolent encounters to create a version of Islam that dominated the Caliphate for well over half a millennium. In fact, Turkic Sufism continued to dominate Islam even after the Abbasids when the Ottomans took over the Caliphate and maintained their affinity to the creed.

Before we end the article, a quick word on something we almost forgot way back—the guanzi. To recap, it’s a wooden double-reed wind instrument from China that became an integral part of Sogdian performances around the sixth century AD and continues to be played during weddings and funerals in the country. So, once again, does it ring a bell? The reason it should, is that we too have something similar that came to our country with the Turkic Muslims. This one too is a wooden double-reed instrument that is almost a mainstay at traditional weddings among certain classes to this day—the _shehnai_. Could guanzi be the ancestor of shehnai?
