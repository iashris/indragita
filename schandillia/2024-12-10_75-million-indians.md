# Did Muslims Claim 75 Million Indians?

*Fact-Checking Hindutva’s Biggest Allegation Against Islamic Rule in India*

- date: 2024-12-10
- audience: only_paid
- url: https://www.schandillia.com/p/75-million-indians
- slug: 75-million-indians

---

In 1973, K. S. Lal published his seminal monograph, _Growth of Muslim Population in Medieval India (1000–1800)_ , presenting a controversial demographic analysis that argued for a significant population decline in India during the medieval period. Lal estimated that India’s population decreased from 200 million in 1000 AD to 125 million in 1500 AD, attributing this substantial reduction of 75 million to the impact of Islamic invasions. This claim prompted critical examination, with prominent historians and ideologues challenging the magnitude of the proposed population loss, suggesting the total population figure was unrealistic. Counterpoints ranged from dismissal of decline to dismissal of scale.

Goes without saying, since the attribution is made to political Islam, the subject is inherently polarizing. Lal himself has been firmly placed among the likes of R. C. Majumdar and Sita Ram Goel as a Rightwing ideologue and even history revisionist. Having said that, the apparition of his bodacious claim continues to haunt ideological and political discourses in the country to this day, more than two decades after his passing. The objective of this article is to establish the veracity, or otherwise, of Lal’s claim purely in the light of historical facts at our disposal. As we always do, we’ll scrutinize arguments from both sides of the aisle for a comprehensive, well-rounded conclusion.

## The Extent of Brutality

Before we engage with Lal’s claims, let’s spend some time articulating the most fundamental element of this discourse, _viz._ Islamic brutality. It’s a given that the further back we go in time, in any part of the world, the easier it gets for hostilities to express as violence. We still have violence but one may argue that a degree of separation exists today between the perpetrator and the victim that didn’t back in the day. Pressing a button to launch a city-obliterating missile does not carry the same moral immediacy as directly swinging the sword that decapitates another living, breathing human. So, past brutalities should hardly scandalize anyone with a keen eye on humanity’s natural moral progression. The question we seek answers to here, strictly in the Indian context, is whether Muslims stood out in either scale or commitment. We’ll do this with some assorted episodes that stand out among countless others. We will also stick to Muslim accounts to the maximum possible extent in order to preclude any possibility of an anti-Muslim bias.

We begin with perhaps one of the most ridiculed rulers to have ever graced the throne in Delhi, Muhammad bin Tughlaq. He reigned for more than two decades in the fourteenth century. While much of his ridicule revolves around his quixotic move to a new capital in Daulatabad which is said to have caused a great deal of economic and social problems, there’s also a darker side to his personality that rarely gets the spotlight. Tughlaq wasn’t just a fool with disastrous whims and silly ideas, but a genocidal maniac whose sword is said to have never rested. We know this from _Muntakhab-ut-Tawarikh_ , a treatise of Islamic history in India by a contemporary of Akbar named ʽAbd al-Qadir Badayuni.

In his account, Badayuni writes about a constant heap of corpses just outside of Tughlaq’s royal pavilion and court.1 Such was the scale that the executioners had grown weary of butchering and sweepers of cleaning up after them.2 A few caveats ought to be kept in mind here. Firstly, this is no primary source, as Badayuni lived more than two centuries after Tughlaq’s reign. That’s too long for a narrative to hold factual integrity, especially when much of it comes from gossip and sensationalism. Secondly, A large number of Tughlaq’s victims were also Muslims who dared to oppose his tyranny and despotism. With these caveats in mind, a case can at the very least be made for Tughlaq’s murderous streak that resulted in a large number of killings even if not as large as recoded by Badayuni.

For a glimpse of Tughlaq’s murderous zeal, we can look to the accounts of ibn Battuta, the famous traveler visited Delhi six years after the catastrophic capital move. In his writings, he recounts the story of two men, a crippled and a blind, who couldn’t make the move and were brought to the Sultan’s notice. The mandate being complete depopulation, staying back was insubordination, a punishable offense. The crippled was thrown off a catapult and the blind man was dragged through the streets so by the time they reached Daulatabad, only one of his limbs remained.3

Since the window of Lal’s claim starts at 1000 AD, we must talk about Muhammad Ghori, also known variously as Muʿizz al-Din, Shihabuddin, and Zangi. Although not the first Muslim invader in the subcontinent, he gets the credit for effecting the first Islamic polity in India, which later went on to be known as the Delhi Sultanate. Qutbuddin Aibak, the inaugural Sultan was once Ghori’s slave. A most chilling account of Ghori’s bloodthirst comes to us from the 23-volume _Tabaqat-i Nasiri_ by the Persian historian Minhaj-i Siraj Juzjani. According to this narrative, in one of his winter campaigns into India, Ghori “sent the refractory race to Hell, and carried on holy war as prescribed by the canons of Islam and set a river of blood of those people flowing.”4 Again, one must be careful not to take expressions like “river of blood” literally because Juzjani, although a contemporary of Ghori, only came to India two decades after the Sultan’s death. Having said that, large-scale carnage remains the key takeaway, although we _will_ discuss a more primary shortly.

Qutbuddin Aibak, the first in the line of Delhi Sultans, although he was crowned in Lahore, was known for immense charity. So much so that they called him _Lakh Bakhsh_ or "the “Giver of Lakhs.” What he was equally well known as, something modern profiles seem to have forgotten, is his bloodthirst. In the same work that we just referenced above, Juzjani attributes to him slaughters running in “hundreds of thousands.”5 Even his contemporary Hasan Nizami panegyrizes him in _Tajul-Ma'asir_ , the first official history of the Sultanate, as the “destroyer of infidels,” a “hero” who was chosen to carry out the “obligations of the religion.”6 What obligations? To wage “holy wars without which the fold of Muhammad’s flock could never be filled.”7 Nizami doesn’t stop there. He further goes on to call Hindus “enemies of religion” and recount how Aibak had “deluged the land of Hind with the blood of their hearts, so that to the very day of resurrection travelers would have to pass over pools of gore in boats.”8 He even speaks of impalements and while he does not give a number, Nizami proudly relates that Aibak “sent the whole world of idolatry to the fire of hell by the well-watered blade of his Hindi sword.”9 The Minar in Delhi, evidently, is a rather insignificant part of his legacy.

The same Nizami also speaks of Ghori. In fact, the latter was still alive when Nizami started work on Tajul, which makes him as primary a source as it gets for both Ghori and Aibak. After Ghori scored victory at the second Tarain encounter, he set out to expand his conquest and part of this campaign was Ajmer. This was shortly after the start of Moinuddin Chishti’s sojourn there. In this campaign, Nizami claims, Ghori’s forces slaughtered a “hundred thousand groveling Hindus,” “destroyed the pillars and foundations of the idol temples,” and established “the precepts of Islam.”10

Again, it’s absolutely possible Nizami was exaggerating the numbers in an attempt to lionize his idols, but the takeaway of an obscenely high kill count is not easy to dismiss.

We have already met Muhammad bin Tughlaq. His successor, Firuz Shah Tughlaq, too came with his own flavor of zealotry. Irish Indologist Vincent Arthur Smith gives us a fairly gruesome insight into his bloodlust. In Bengal, for instance, the Tughlaq scion waged a war of conquest for well over a decade during which he offered a silver tanka for every infidel head, the final tally standing north of 180,000.11 That’s just one campaign in one region. Of course, executions of Hindus for merely practicing their faith in public, holding religious fairs, or building temples abound throughout his own writing.12

Down south, we have the Bahamanis. Here too, we see the same pattern across the board. Ahmed Shah, the ninth of his dynasty, for instance, is noted to have feasted for three days every time his kill count hit twenty thousand during his campaign in the Vijayanagar Empire toward the end of the fifteenth century. Although do note that this account comes to us from the sixteenth century _Tārīkh-i Firishta_ which, although widely celebrated as a remarkable treatise on Islamic history in the subcontinent, is still far from a primary source for the episode in question.13

So far, we’ve only seen examples from between the eleventh and sixteenth centuries, and only partially so. Technically, this is where the observations ought to stop because that’s the _terminus ad quem_ of Lal’s claim. But let’s do it anyway because as we’ll soon see, the Mughals exhibited the same deathly streak in their attitude as that of their predecessors, despite being a different stock. We will examine them only to establish that the only common grounds between them, the Mamluks, and the Bahamani was their religion and their comfort with bloodshed.

Babur’s love for skull towers—something he personally admits in his own autobiography—is all too familiar already, so we won’t dwell on him here.14 Nor does Aurangzeb merit much attention, for the same reason—his fanaticism is hardly a surprise. Let’s talk about Akbar, arguably the best Mughal on record and the only one to be accorded the epithet of “Great.” The biggest highlight of his reign has to be the Siege of Chittor in which, according to his own contemporary Abul Fazal, thirty thousand were slain in the space of one day.15

This tradition of Turkic cruelty did not die with Akbar. His son, better known for his hedonism than his violence, once had a man killed and his companion hamstrung just because they unwittingly disturbed him during a nilgai hunt.16 This comes from his own _Tuzk-e-Jahangiri_. It’s worth noting that it’s under Jahangir that militant Sufism became the most pronounced led by the liked of Shaikh Ahmad Sirhindi.

Shah Jahan, similarly, has a legacy far darker than Taj Mahal, itself a product of wanton deaths and destruction. More than seven million perished17 in the space of two years alone when Mughal forces carried out a scorched-earth campaign in Malwa to punish them for sedition and rebellion. The goriest of pictures is painted by none other than Peter Mundy the British merchant who traveled from Surat to Agra in the thick of this tragedy. In his travelogue, he describes bazaars strewn with the dead and the dying of starvation despite an abundance of food. He says, people had food “almost at their mouths yet died for the want of it, they not having wherewith to buy.”18 At the same time, the bazaars adjoining the emperor’s residence were “plentifully stored with all provisions, being supplied with all things from all parts, far and near, which otherwise, it may be believed, would feel the same calamity with her neighbor towns, for there is little or nothing grows near it for miles.”19 How the Taj was funded can be explained by the economic policies of the time where almost 57% of the peasant’s produce was being appropriated by the State and of the left, another share going to the local _zamindar_.20 Which was in sharp contrast to the prescribed Hindu system of taxation where the standard rate was one-sixth of the produce was 17% going as low as 8% in times or prosperity and as high as 33% in times of emergency.21 In sum, the famine of 1630–32 was as not mere bad weather or poor harvest but calculated State-sponsored carnage.

Mundy also documents several firsthand accounts of towers or _minars_ made of human heads, a tradition dating back to the very origins of the empire, throughout his journey.22 Besides such minars, he also documents corpses hung by the heels in mango groves and planted on stakes, some roasted alive, others beheaded.23 In one of the towns he visited, there were no fewer than two hundred such minars, each with anywhere between thirty and forty heads.24

Much later, Shah Nawaz Khan, historian and courtier of the Nizam of Hyderabad, would reaffirm these minars in his _Ma'athir al-Umara_ , a Persian-language work of Mughal biographies. He pegs the headcount (quite literally) at 200,000 in two rows of minars between Agra and Patna.25 Although Khan barely qualifies as a primary source, his claim, by his own admission, comes from one that does— _Zakhirat-ul Khawanin_ by the seventeenth-century Mughal noble Shaikh Farid Bhakkari,26 also known as Murtaza Khan.

Now that we have a fairly comprehensive idea of the bloodlust that ran across all Muslim rulers of all lineages, Turks and Afghans alike, we should be in a better position to discuss numbers. We’ll start with Lal’s assertions and then take up the counterpoints against him.

## Lal’s Seventy-Five Million

A population decline of 75 million between 1000 AD and 1500 AD—the pre-Mughal era—is at the heart of Lal’s assertion. 1000 AD is when Mahmud of Ghazni launched his campaigns into the subcontinent, and 1500 AD is close to when the Delhi Sultanate fell. Babur arrived in 1526. Lal’s assertion, naturally, rides on two data points—India’s population in 1000 AD, _before_ Mahmud’s first invasion, and India’s population in 1500 AD. As one would suspect, these are no easy numbers to ascertain with a useful degree of accuracy. But some, it would seem, have given it a shot, nevertheless. Lal estimates 200 million for 1000 AD and 125 million for 1500 AD.27 But on what basis? Let’s start with 1000 AD.

Lal’s source of 200 million for 1000 AD is one Jatindra Mohan Datta who made the claim in a 1948 paper in _Modern Review_. In this paper, Datta initially posits 600 million, attributing it to Firishta, then immediately dismisses it as an overestimation by two to three times.28 Which gives us a range of 200-300 million. Before Datta, Vivekananda too had referenced Firishta’s 600 million in one of his interviews in the spring of 1899.29 Lal, on his part, dismisses this figure as exaggeration, primarily because he fails to locate it in Firista’s corpus. But he does see reason in the figure of 200 million as also preferred by Datta.30 Do note that Datta is the _only_ source of Lal’s 200 million for 1000 AD.

On the other hand, economist Colin Clark, best known for formulating the concept of Gross National Product, suggested a much more muted 70 million for 1000 AD.31 Within the same ballpark is 75 million from another economist, Angus Maddison.32 That’s a gap of at least 125 million between Lal’s estimates and those of Maddison and Clark. The only way to reconcile the discrepancy is by analyzing the methodology each used to arrive at their figures. However, neither Maddison nor Clark provides any insight for this specific period. Their works extensively cover later eras, such as the century following Akbar’s death, but offer nothing substantial for 1000 AD. Lal provides little clarity as well, though he at least references Firishta through Datta. But then again, Firishta’s 600 million is nowhere to be found in his writings by Lal’s own admission. So, 1000 AD already rests on tenuous grounds. But that doesn’t mean we have to give up on this data.

While Clark estimates 70 million for 1000 AD, he also accepts 140 million for the second century BC, during Aśoka’s reign, as calculated by economist Dr. Pran Nath.33 This claim, unlike the former, is supported by decent arithmetic. Dr. Nath references Hindu texts to suggest seven million _gramas_ of between fifteen and twenty individuals each, yielding a total population of 105 to 140 million.34 He also uses Buddhist texts, which mention 840 _janapadas_ (not _mahājanapadas_) with approximately 168,000 people each, to arrive at a figure of 141 million.35 Put a pin on this figure, we’ll revisit it shortly and when we do, it’ll become clear how population before the Common Era matters in a conversation about population decline in Muslim India. For now, let’s fast-forward to 1500 AD.

Once again, Lal gives us no direct source or methodology behind his 125 million for 1500 AD, but he does cite the nineteenth-century British administrator William Harrison Moreland for a figure of 100 million at the time of Akbar’s death in 1605.3637 His own assessment—with help from Beni Prasad who estimates 125 thru 130 million38 in a 1921 paper—factoring in elements like rural-urban ratio, army headcount, and numbers from Akbar’s own census as reported in Fazl’s _Aʾin-i-Akbari_ , is between 150 and 165 million.39 He then further revises it down to a final value of 140 million in light of a devastating series of famine that struck the subcontinent in the later decades of the sixteenth century.40 But what about 1500? If we accept 140 million for 1600 and 125 million for 1500, we get an annual growth of barely above 0.1%, which isn’t too far off the global rate of under 0.1% for the period. Thus, 125 million for 1500 should not be difficult to accept if we accept 140 million for 1600.

To further add to this, Lal offers an economic angle to his estimates for 1600 too. He starts with revenue figures at the time of Jahangir’s ascension by the then president of the Dutch factory in Surat, van den Broecke. That figure is 174,500,000 rupees.41 He then triples it to arrive at 523,500,000 rupees as the total agricultural output assuming a 33% taxation.42 The average price of cereals at the time, as gleamed from Aʾin, was about eight dams a maund or about 3.5 maunds a rupee.43 Lal assumes a third of the gross agricultural output as consumption which comes to 174,500,000 rupees or 610,750,000 rupees, or about 110 million heads assuming a consumption of 5.5 maunds per person per year.44 The figure of 5.5 maunds comes from the fact that at the time of his work, India’s annual consumption was roughly 100 million tons with a population of 500 million—200 kilograms or 5.5 maunds per head.45 To the 110 million, Lal adds another 30-35 million from the South and miscellaneous tribal areas outside the pale of taxation to arrive at a figure of 140-45 million.46 These calculations come with a number of issues, but we’ll treat them in a separate section a little later. For now, 140 million it is.

And as we’ve already noted, if it’s possible to grant 140 million for 1600, so is it to grant 125 million for 1500. All it takes is a population growth of 0.1% per annum. To bring it all together, we have some question mark on 1000 AD and data-backed near-unanimity on 1600. With that, let’s see if we can resolve the former, and this is where BC numbers come in.

## Resolving for 1000 AD

We have already established a value of 140 million for the second century BC, as calculated by Dr. Nath. If we accept 70-75 million for 1000 AD, as suggested—suggested, not calculated—by Clark and Maddison, this represents a decline of about 70 million over roughly 1,100 years. A question worth asking here is, what happened during that period to trigger such a lasting population decline? Aśoka’s conquests were bloody, granted. The Kalinga campaign alone is said to have claimed 100,000 lives, although there’s every possibility that the number is more metaphorical than literal. But even then, Kalinga is held as an outlier rather than the norm. The major spate of destruction after that period was the Hunnic invasion in the early sixth century AD.

Other than the Huns, the most significant episodes of violence between the fall of the Mauryas and the advent of Ghori were the invasions by the Greeks, the Sakas, the Kushans, and to some extent, the Sassanians. Of these, only the Hunnic toll is measured in hundreds of thousands, whereas all others stay in tens of thousands. Once established, none of these regimes are recorded to have engaged in wanton violence. Except, of course, the Huns. There must have been famines and pandemics. The period during the middle of the first millennium is recorded to be exceptionally devastating, which is when the concept of Kalki also emerged.

* * *

* * *

But 70 million is too big a number to account for in this manner. Either the population was lower than 140 million in 100-200 BC, or it was more than 70-75 million in 1000 AD. We have the arithmetic for the former, but mere supposition for the latter. Furthermore, according to Maddison, the population remained unchanged between 1 AD and 1000 AD at 75 million.47 Which further narrows down the window of decline to a little over a century between Aśoka and the start of the Common Era. Even if we round it up to 200 years, we’re looking at a decline of 70 million between 200 BC and 1 AD. That’s almost as uncanny as no decline between 1 AD and 1000 AD.

Therefore, although Lal does not provide direct mathematical backing for 200 million in 1000 AD, it is more reasonable to accept this number at face value than the alternative of 70-75 million or lower. There was no military campaign, natural disaster, or famine—nor a series of such events—in the post-Mauryan period significant enough to account for a decline of 70 million in less than 200 years, even including Kalinga. Moreover, there were too many adversities, both natural and man-made, to justify a complete absence of population decline in the first millennium AD.

Having established the numbers for the two ends of the timeline with reason, even if not certainty, it’s worth visiting the opening shots of the decline.

## Mahmud’s Campaigns

Mahmud of Ghazni raided India seventeen times between 1000 and 1027 AD resulting in, by Lal’s estimates, over two million deaths.48 If a single actor was able to precipitate this kind of depopulation in less than three decades, seventy million over five centuries should not sound improbable, especially given the bloodlust transcended regimes and dynasties. But is Lal right in his estimations? Let’s see.

The first campaign was in the year 1000 when several forts and provinces along the western frontier were sacked. Naturally, this would have involved a great deal of killing and enslavement, but Lal offers no figures because none exists in the works of either Firishta or anyone else. The second involved the twin raids on Peshawar and Waihind. For Peshawar, Lal gives 15,000 killed from Mahmud’s secretary, al-Utbi,49 and 5,000 from British historian Henry Elliot.50 Which is strange because Elliot himself mentions 15,000 while translating Utbi’s _Kitab-i-Yamini_51 before settling on 5,000.52 Another translation, the one by Rev. James Reynolds, also agrees with 5,000.53

At any rate, these are just the dead, not the trafficked which would’ve contributed just as much to the depopulation. Here too discrepancies exist in translation. Elliot’s translation gives us 500,00054 whereas that of Reynolds gives 100,000.55 Also note that these are numbers for Peshawar alone. While not available in any primary sources, Lal fairly assumes comparable figures for the Waihind campaign that followed immediately thereafter.

The third raid was on Bhera where although we do not have any figures, we know that everyone who didn’t convert was put to the sword. In Elliot’s translation, “the army of the Sultan kept moving on and committing slaughter and pillage.”56 Next up was Multan where according to Utbi, the invading army indulged in “slaughter, imprisonment, pillage, depopulation, and fire.”57 Lal gives us, with reference to Ghaznavi’s court poet Abul Qasim Hasan Unsuri Balkhi, a death toll of 200,000 in this campaign.58 The following year came the fifth incursion, this time against a local chieftain by the name of Nawasa Shah. The motivation given by Utbi is the latter’s apostasy “toward the pit of plural worship.”59 Ghaznavi is said to have “cut down the harvest of idolatry with the sickle of his sword and spear.”60

In 1008, Ghaznavi returned to Waihind and conducted wholesale massacre, which Utbi characterizes as the “grinding stone of slaughter.”61 The vanquished were slain “wherever they were found in jungles, passes, plains, and hills.”62 Lal references Firishta to quote a figure of 8,000 killed in retreat in just one of the several battles.63 Firishta, however, gives a figure of 20,000 Hindus.64

Ghaznavi’s seventh campaign was to Tarain, variously called Narain (Elliot) and Nardin (Reynolds), in 1010. Here, Utbi doesn’t quantify the massacres but, in Reynolds’ translation, tells us that his army “began with open hand to devastate the lands, to punish the infidels, to overturn the idols, and to make an example of high and low.”65 In Elliot’s translation he speaks of “slaughter in every hill and valley.”66 Besides the fallen, Utbi also mentions 2,000 men trafficked to Ghazna as part of annual tribute from the defeated territory.67 Lal claims this was done until at least for five years which brings the total count to 10,000.68 Reynolds’ translation, however, says “he [the Hindu ruler] should keep two thousand men at his [Mahmood’s] Court,” which sounds like a non-recurring arrangement.69

The following year Ghaznavi returned to Multan and left the city deserted. Two years later, came the ninth expedition which saw, in Utbi’s words, slaves “so plentiful that they became very cheap.”7071 This was in Kashmir and was followed by the notorious Sack of Thanesar. Nobody gives a number for the dead in this campaign, but Utbi speaks of the water in the streams turning undrinkable due to the copious blood of the infidels.7273 Firishta, later gives 20,000 as the number of Hindus enslaved and trafficked to Ghazna.7475 There’s some discrepancy on dates for this campaign—Lal gives 1015, Firishta gives 101176—but that’s immaterial to our study.

The eleventh expedition, to Lohkot in Kashmir, seems relatively inconsequential, for it finds no mention in Utbi’s account. Firishta mentions it but only as a major setback to the invaders. In fact, he suggests two attempts on Kashmir this time, the second resulting in significant casualties on Ghaznavi’s side.77 For his twelfth, Ghaznavi crossed the Yamuna and invaded Mathura. In just one of the several battles in this campaign, at the fort of Kulchand, Utbi pegs the toll at 50,000.7879 In Elliot’s translation, Utbi describes that “the Musulmans paid no regard to the booty till they had satiated themselves with the slaughter of the infidels and worshippers of sun and fire.”80 Such was the bloodlust. A similar picture unfolded at Kannauj which became the invader’s next target in the same expedition. Besides the killings, Lal, referencing the _Tārīkh-i-Alfī_ , records a staggering 750,000 Indians enslaved and trafficked out of the subcontinent.81 No numbers are available for the following three campaigns, although all are described as bloody and devastating, as expected of the Ghaznavi routine by now.

Finally, we come to Ghaznavi’s most celebrated conquest—Somnath. A most detailed contemporary account of this expedition comes from Turkish historian Ibn al-Athir whose _al-Kāmil fit-Tārīkh_ recounts a “dreadful slaughter at the gate of the temple” on day two of the expedition.82 Zakariya al-Qazwini, who references the episode in his thirteenth-century _Āthār al-Bilād_ , writes that more than 50,000 were killed in this raid.83 Having plundered Somnath, the invaders turned their attention to nearby Nahrwara where a fort was captured and everybody in it, put to the sword.84

For the seventeenth and final major campaign, again, we do not have specific numbers, although the theme of large-scale massacre and enslavement has been noted to continue. To bring it all together, Lal adds up 400,000 as the total count of killed and trafficked from just three campaigns and then extrapolates it to two million for all seventeen major and the unaccounted minor raids combined.85 Even if we ignore the final raid in Kashmir where Mahmud suffered humiliation, we have sixteen major campaigns, each described as exceptionally catastrophic. So, the extrapolation checks out.

But Lal may not seem accurate in his arithmetic because the kill count, if we only take the conservative estimates for the numbers readily available, such as 5,000 instead of 15,000 for the second raid and 8,000 instead of 20,000 for the sixth, the total comes to 313,000 killed. Again, these are just for the campaigns that have been quantified. We are not even considering campaigns for which we only have vague statements like “large number put to sword,” “killed in every valley,” or “water turned to blood,” instead of hard numbers. Also, note that we have, for instance, only taken 50,000 for the entire Kannauj campaign whereas the number only pertains to Mahaban, one of the six densely populated sites of massacre in that campaign. And these are only the slain, not enslaved. The latter number closer to 900,000 even by conservative estimates, that is, 100,000 instead of 500,000 for the first Peshawar raid and 2,000 instead of 10,000 for the Tarain raid.

In sum, even if we are to take the conservative side of all available numbers, we get 1,185,000 killed and trafficked from just five campaigns. Even the most conservative of extrapolations should give a number closer to four million than two—3,792,000 to be precise. That’s the depopulation count in just thirty-odd years. We still have more than four centuries ahead of us.

## After Mahmud

After the Ghaznavids came the Ghurids. Led by Muhammad of Ghor or Muhammad Ghori, the incursions lasted about thirty years, the same as the Ghaznavids, and prefaced the first significant Islamic base in the Indian heartland. We have already learned, through Nizami, of the hundred thousand killed in the Ajmer campaign, now we’ll look at more. While the Ghurids are best known for the two big-ticket campaigns of Tarain, there were no fewer than twenty others, six before the first Tarain and fourteen after the second Tarain. And the campaign of Ajmer was just part of the Second Battle of Tarain. The hundred thousand killed, even if exaggeration, was _not_ the total kill count for even one expedition. If extrapolated in the same manner as the estimates for Ghaznavid campaigns, the overall casualty count could once again exceed two million.

The Delhi Sultanate opened with Aibak, a Turkic slave elevated to kingship by his Ghurid masters. Nizami’s work has already familiarized us with the scale of this man’s crusades against the infidels. Lal references Firishta to give a toll of 50,000 for a campaign in Naharwala alone.8687 Between 1200 and 1210, Aibak conducted at least seven similar campaigns in places like Malwa, Ranthambore, Anhilwara, Gwalior, Bundelkhand, and Ajmer. It’s thanks to this feat that the same Juzjani who called him the “giver of lakhs” also called him the “killer of lakhs.” Lal continues the theme with Iltutmish’ 12,000 in Punjab,88 Balban’s hundreds of thousands in Malwa89 and 12,000 in Haryana, and90 Khilji’s 30,000 in Chittor,91 besides other instances where volumes are expressed in metaphors instead of specific numbers. Barani, a Sultanate historian and thinker attributed more kills to Alauddin Khilji than even the pharaohs of Ancient Egypt.92 Accounts of streets lined with stakes and villages turned to heaps of corpses abound in all contemporary records, where exact numbers are not available. The same Battuta who writes of advanced urban centers also writes of flayed victims, deserted villages, famines, and slaves sent out of India.93 In fact, it’s he who first mentions the name Hindu Kush, lit. “Hindu killer,” for the famous mountain range, because of the countless Indian slaves who died of cold in its passes during trafficking.9495

Less than sixty years after Battuta’s departure came Timur’s infamous Sack of Delhi. Although he did not kill indiscriminately, for there is evidence to support the fact that he spared the likes of scholars and merchants, his body count was staggering by even Islamic standards. Firishta estimates upward of 100,000 first taken as slaves, then put to the sword upon Timur’s learning that most were Hindus.96 That’s a hundred thousand killed in a single episode of a single campaign. At least an equal number can be assumed to have died on the battlefield. Although Firishta wrote centuries after Timur, his numbers might even be an understatement, for when he had sacked Isfahan less than ten years earlier, the count was between 100,000 and twice as many.97 For perspective, around fifty pyramids were built each with thousands of severed heads. And this was the brutality when the motivation was economic and political, since Isfahan was being punished for its rebellion against taxation. In Delhi, it was economic and religious, so the brutality can be reasonably expected to top that of Isfahan.

If we move away from Delhi, we could revisit Vijayanagar where we have already familiarized with the Bahamani bloodlust under Ahmad Shah. But the latter wasn’t alone. About half a century before him, his ancestor Muhammad Shah led the first campaign into Vijayanagar, then under Bukka Raya I. In just one episode, the Battle of Mudgal, the Bahamani Sultan is said to have massacred 90,000 Hindus, women and children included.98 As noted, this was a single episode of a campaign that lasted two whole years. But we don’t have to extrapolate because Firishta does give us the total count—a staggering half a million.99 Back north in Kashmir, we have the well-known story of Sikandar Shah Miri who, influenced by the famous Sufi Mir Sayyid Ali Hamadani, had thousands of Brahmins executed for refusing Islam. They were given two choices, convert or die. A total of more than two hundred kilos of _janeu_ is said to have been recovered from the victims and the site of execution is still commemorated as Batta Mazar or “Graveyard of Brahmins.” While the body count of a hundred thousand as estimated from the janeu volume may be gross exaggeration since every source it comes from is tertiary. And in these tertiary sources, the most widely cited reference is Jonaraja who surely speaks of janeu but doesn’t give a number. Nor does he mention massacre. All he writes is that Brahmins were forcefully converted in such large numbers that the janeu recovered from them weight as much as the haul carried by three donkeys.100 Can a massacre be ruled out? Not at all, but is there verifiable evidence that it happened? So far, no. At any rate, Sikandar’s anti-Hindu zealotry is well outside the pale of ambiguity. He was the first to bring Shariʿa to the valley and impose a wide range of humiliating anti-Hindu legislations, a complete list of which can be found in Jonaraja’s volume. The man wasn’t called Butshikan or “Iconoclast” for nothing.

While it’s not possible to catalog the entire list of kills in the Sultanate era with any degree of accuracy, since not all numbers were thus recoded by contemporary observers, it’s abundantly evident that the numbers were staggering. Every primary source from the period has spoken of deserted towns, rivers of blood, and heaps of corpses. If this was the state of affairs for five whole centuries, tens of millions dead and enslaves is not an incredulous notion. To further bolster this gruesome conclusion, we have already gone through the arithmetic for the population levels in the years 1000 and 1500. And it’s in this context that we should now revisit the issues we put a pin on when calculating these values.

## Issues with Lal’s Numbers

Lal established 200 million for 1000 AD primarily on the basis of Datta’s reading of Firishta who he claims to have given 600 million. The problem with this reading, even though he immediately scales it down by two-thirds, is that the figure 600 million does not appear anywhere in Firishta’s work. This is something Lal himself admits. Habib notes this too. Since we cannot source 600 million, the scaled-down value automatically becomes unfounded as well. But Datta isn’t Lal’s only source here. He also references Nath who computes a figure of 141 million for the second century BC with data points from Hindu and Buddhist texts from the period.

While Habib raises concerns about the integrity of Nath’s methodology, he does not provide a reason for his critique. His objections are limited to a footnote questioning Nath’s deduction of 7,350,000 fighting men from eighty-four _deśas_ in the Mahabharata’s India, using 700,000 as the value for eight.101 However, Habib does not explain the basis of his doubts. In contrast, Nath offers comprehensive details supporting each value used in his calculations.102 Furthermore, as previously noted, Nath arrives at the same figure through two additional approaches.

Another objection Habib rightly raises is that the absence of casualty figures for pre-Islamic India does not automatically translate into an absence of casualties themselves. In other words, we know, for instance, that Ghaznavi killed and trafficked millions, but we have no reason to believe this was exceptional or a departure from the prevailing norm. He is right, we have no direct reference to ascertain that large-scale killings did not happen before 1000 AD. But it’s not impossible to triangulate such data from indirect clues, which is what Lal has done, something we have already familiarized with before. If Maddison’s 75 million holds for 1 AD, and Nath’s 140 million holds for 200 BC, it would mean a decline of almost 70 million in a space of two centuries where we did not have foreign invasions or pandemics on an exceptional scale, exceptional being the operative word here. If anything, the population ought to have grown exponentially during the Kushan era, when foreign trade peaked, and under the Guptas, when agriculture and domestic trade flourished. India had become a major player on the Silk Road under the Kushans and a major maritime actor under the early Guptas. And until fairly recently, prosperity has been directly proportional to population. While we do not have exact figures for pre-Islamic India, these historically attested facts make it amply evident that it must have been on the higher side of 140 million in 200 BC.

Habib also questions Lal’s estimates for the year 1600. Lal arrived at a figure of 140 million on the basis of Akbar’s revenue collections as enumerated in Fazl’s Aʾin. For his calculations, Lal considers land revenue collections at one-third of the total produce, a precedent of which he places in the Tughlaq era. The first objection rightly raised by Habib here is Lal’s assumption of collection as assessment.103 The total revenue collected may not, and is not, the total revenue due. Farm distress, bad harvest, corrupt officials—a multitude of factors impact actual collection. Therefore, a population figure calculated on the basis of _collected_ revenue will always be less than one calculated on the basis of _assessed_ revenue. Ergo, the figure for 1600 may not be as low as Lal estimates.

The second objection is to Lal’s assumption of one-third of the produce as the rate of land-tax or _kharaj_. Lal claims this was the average during the Tughlaq era but offers no source to back it up. A rationale for the assumption, however, does exist. In a 1964 paper, R. G. Jauhri informs that the rate of proportional land taxation or _kharaj musqasmath_ has a prescribed range in Islam—between one-fifth and one-third of the total produce.104 But Jauhri also mentions a fixed taxation system or _kharaj wazifat_ that was at times adopted by Firoz Shah Tughlaq to keep things simpler.105 If the taxation was one-fifth, the calculated population figure would be significantly higher than if it were one-third. So, we can see that Lal has proceeded with an assumption that produces the smallest number. But there’s still the problem of fixed taxation. But what was the system during Akbar’s time? Here we have something called a _Dahsala_ system charging one-third of the total produce as per Fazl’s Aʾin.106 So Lal’s rate checks out. But only to an extent because the Dahsala system did not tax every parcel at a uniform quantum. Some farms were taxed every year while others were taxed once every three or four years.107

In his third objection, Habib points out the oversimplification in Lal’s price assessments during Akbar’s time.108 First problem is Lal’s assumption of a fixed average price for all foodgrains without a weighted average of any sort. Although his assumption finds basis in Aʾin’s tables of food prices, the swing is too high to be averaged blindly. For example, rice is anywhere between 90 and 110 dams, whereas wheat is just 12 dams.109 Millets are the cheapest at 6 dams. We’re not even talking fruits and vegetables here that could skew the range even further. A meaningful assessment would warrant careful enumeration of all land parcels under respective cultivation and determining from that the final weightages for each produce category. Of course, in the absence of detailed data from even Fazl’s accounts, that’s an impossibly impractical endeavor. But in the absence of such numbers, merely averaging out all prices to 8 dams for population calculations is a bit arbitrary. Habib seems right in this critique.

Another significant observation is that the prices considered by Lal are only valid for the market, whereas revenue numbers are computed on harvest prices.110 The two values must have been significantly divergent as they are even today.

To conclude, we have successfully rebutted Habib on tax rates, pre-Islamic population, and population levels in 1000 AD. But we’re still left with the two outstanding issues raised by him, _viz_. Lal’s assumption of collected revenue as assessed revenue, and his price averaging. Outside of Habib’s objections, we also have the questions of fixed taxation and tax quantum during Akbar’s time.

Having said that, Moreland’s estimation of 100 million at the time of Akbar’s death still stands uncontested. His calculations do not rely on agricultural produce and thus immune to the problems suffered by Lal’s approach. Instead, Moreland relies on the provisional assumption that the distribution of population density during Akbar’s time was the same as it is today. Distribution of density, that is, not population. Of course, he also provides an exhaustive list of reasons behind this provisional assumption.111

## Conclusion

What we have right now is a rather fuzzy view of India’s demographic evolution over the first five centuries of the second millennium, and understandably so. With help from contemporary records left by both Indian and foreign, both Muslim and Hindu authors, it’s however still possible to triangulate much of consequence. At least enough to paint an acceptable model of how the numbers changed over the centuries. What we then have is a picture where we started off with far more Indians on the eve of the first Ghaznavid invasion than what we ended up with on the eve of the first Mughal invasion. Question is, by how much?

While assigning a precise number is tricky, we know for sure that Lal’s estimation of 200 million Indians in 1000 AD is a product of far more logical and numerical reasoning than the alternative views of 70-75 million. As for 1500 AD, Lal’s 125 million may or may not be even close to accurate, depending on how much trust we invest in his 1600 numbers. For 1600, his estimation of 140 million clearly seems way off the mark, for the number of numerical problems it comes riddled with. But we still don’t have any refutation of Moreland’s 100 million so far. Not from Habib, nor from Maddison or any other figure invested in this conversation. If we take 100 million for 1600 and assume a positive population trend between 1500 and 1600, the number for 1500 dips further below Lal’s estimation of 125 million. Even if we, for the sake of the argument, stick to no change during the century, the population decline between 1000 AD and 1500 AD inflates to 100 million. Lal’s claim is still a modest 75 million in comparison.

Long story short, in the first five centuries of Islamic rule in India, India lost not as many as but _at least_ 75 million people. Further investigation in light of new data points as they become available might change this conclusion, but as things stand, the period stands out as the single most catastrophic in India’s recorded history in terms of human capital.
