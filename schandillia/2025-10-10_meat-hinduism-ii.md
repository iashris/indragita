In a previous article we studied the Vedic and post-Vedic positions on meat as food before the Common Era. We concluded with the understanding that meat was far more ubiquitous in the Indian diet at the time than we like to admit today. That the injunction on meat was really a spectrum with some verses prohibiting it all, others allowing some, and yet others allowing it all. Precisely as it is today.

However, this spectrum had already started a slow shift in favor of higher prohibitions toward the end of the Upanishadic period. Even as Yajnavalkya admits to his love for beef, for instance, he still warns us against it, lest one become the bearer of a grave sin and be reborn as a ‚Äústrange being.‚ÄĚ Was this a Shramanic influence? Did the Buddha‚Äôs emphasis on nonviolence and karma inform the Upanishadic ideals in this regard? That‚Äôs the notion much of mainstream scholarship operates on. It‚Äôs therefore a good idea to ascertain if that really is the case. And if at all that weren‚Äôt the case, the question one cannot deny that Hindus are visibly less meat inclined today than they were back in the day. There has to be a point where something changed drastically and that point is not the Upanishads.

We know that Hinduism underwent its most significant codification in the early centuries AD‚ÄĒa process often described as the _ŇõńĀstrification_ of Vedic philosophy. Although it likely began under the Kushans, it reached its fullest expression under the Guptas. This period witnessed the rise of a new body of literature known as the _DharmaŇõńĀstras_ , the most prominent of which is the _MńĀnava DharmaŇõńĀstra_ , better known as the _ManusmŠĻõti_.

To understand the status of meat during the Common Era, it is essential to examine ManusmŠĻõti‚Äôs view on food. That is our starting point here. For a more comprehensive picture, however, we will also consider how meat was perceived in society under the Kushans, the Ňöakas, the Guptas, and in the post-Gupta India. Although pre‚ÄďCommon Era dietary practices were discussed in a previous article, we will briefly revisit that period to address one crucial omission‚ÄĒthe Mauryas. This will include KauŠĻ≠ilya‚Äôs _ArthaŇõńĀstra_ alongside Buddhist and Jain sources, to finally clarify the question of Shramanic nonviolence in relation to meat consumption.

## Early Common Era

This is the era of the Kushans, the Shakas, and the Satavahanas. Greeks too but their hegemony had already folded by 10 AD, so we won‚Äôt consider them. Of course, short of archaeology, there‚Äôs no direct way to ascertain what people really ate at the time. But a study of secular literature from the time can throw some light. While the Shastras are encumbered with an overarching sacerdotal responsibility, works of a more secular nature are not. The most readily accessible works from this corpus are the medical treatises of Charaka and Sushruta.

Chapter 27 of _Charaka Samhita_ is dedicated to food. And a considerable section thereof discusses meat in unprecedented depth. As many as 55 verses are solely employed in the definition and categorization of various kinds of animals in the context of meat. From 27.56 onward, we are told the effects of consuming each category of meats. In 27.56, for instance, we have a class of creatures the meat of which helps improve strength and libido.15 The previous verse tells us that this class includes, among others, rodents, water animals, amphibians, and certain birds.16 The verses that follow similarly lay out the health benefits of all other classes of meat, and include not just usual suspects like sheep and goat, but also more exotic ideas like peacock, swan, parrot, porcupine, rhino, and many more, a total of no fewer than 32 verses.17 Of special significance is 27.79 where cow meat itself is prescribed for individuals suffering from muscular dystrophy, dry cough, or extreme illness. 18 Clearly, there‚Äôs no taboo whatsoever, and the doctors of the time were freely prescribing meat as medication without the State or clergy coming down on them with penalties. In 27.311, we also learn of _meats_ that are not to be consumed. These include dried or putrid meat and meat from animals that died naturally, of old age, or of illness.19 Animals that are killed by other animals are also prohibited. 27.312‚Äď14 reiterate the prescription of all other meats as a source of not just basic nourishment but also great health.20 In 5.11, we are advised against long-term consumption of cow meat, implying it‚Äôs okay sporadically.21 But even here, the list includes not just cow meat but also pork, fish, buffalo meat, cheese, and even curd.22

Sushruta too agrees and in 46.352 thru 359 of _Sushruta Samhita_ , we find enumerated several varieties of meat preparations including _ullupta_ (minced), _barjita_ (fries), _piŠĻ£ŠĻ≠a_ (made into balls or cakes), _pratńĀpta_ (roasted in ghee over charcoal), _kandŇępacita_ (dipped in mustard oil and aromatic spices, then roasted to a honey hue), _pariŇõuŠĻ£ka_ , and _pradigdha_ ‚ÄĒcollectively referred to as Ňõ _Ňęlya_ (kabobs).23 Of these, the _ŇõŇęlya_ prepared by boiling in oil are described as heat-generating, heavy to digest, and aggravating to _pitta_. In contrast, those fried in clarified butter are considered light, appetizing, pleasing to the palate, and beneficial to the eyes. They are said to cool the system, subdue _pitta_ , and impart both nourishment and gustatory delight.

Thus, the two Samhitas offer not only an extensive taxonomy of meats and their medicinal uses but also a sophisticated understanding of their preparation, digestibility, and humoral effects‚ÄĒreflecting a culture in which meat consumption was both scientifically analyzed and gastronomically refined.

Having said that, there‚Äôs also evidence of absolute abstinence among Buddhist and Brahmin ascetics. From the writings of Syrian Gnostic poet Bardesanes, we learn that an embassy of Indian _gymnosophists_ was received in the Syrian court, then part of the Roman Empire, in the early third century.24 From this embassy, whose members Bardesanes claims to have personally interacted in Mesopotamia, he learns that to these Brahmin ascetics, even touching animal food was impious and unclean.25 Do note that the term used is ‚Äúascetic.‚ÄĚ The gymnosophists were not ordinary Brahmins or householders bound by ritual duties but renunciants who had withdrawn entirely from social and material life. These were hermits devoted to penance and contemplation, having renounced all worldly attachments‚ÄĒincluding clothing itself. The very word itself is a Greek compound of _gymn√≥s_ , meaning ‚Äúnaked,‚ÄĚ and _sophistŠłós_ , meaning ‚Äúphilosopher.‚ÄĚ One who has forsaken even the basic comfort of garments could scarcely be expected to indulge in culinary pleasures such as meat. Whether such abstinence extended to the broader Brahmin or non-Brahmin populace, however, finds no corroboration in the evidence.

Roughly coeval with the Kushans were the Satavahanas who, at their peak, controlled much of present-day Maharashtra, Gujarat, Madhya Pradesh, Telangana, Andhra Pradesh, and Karnataka. Among key archeological activities associated with this dynasty is the ancient site of Chandrakhera, a little over 90 miles southeast of Nagpur. Material findings at this site have offered the most indicative evidence of meat consumption in the Satavahana domain. Case in point being a hoard of over two thousand skeletal fragments comprising the bones of diverse animals, including mollusks.26 When closely examined, at least some of these bones, mostly mammalian, show signs of charring and sharp cuts.27 This most notably includes bovines, more likely zebu if not cow.28 In fact, there are more bovines than even goats.29

In sum, meat was ubiquitous across the subcontinent in the early centuries of the Common Era‚ÄĒfrom Magadha in the east to Maharashtra in the west, and from Kashmir in the north to Karnataka in the south. Yet, alongside this widespread consumption, a discernible moral unease had begun to surface, especially within ascetic communities and with regard to specific meats, most notably that of the cow. Again, could this be informed by the Shramanic traditions of nonviolence and renunciation? We‚Äôll see, but first some light on the most landmark period of India‚Äôs Antiquity, the Guptas.

## The Golden Age

The Gupta Era officially marks the tail end of Indian Antiquity, its fall roughly coinciding with that of Rome. This is the period when Indo-Roman trade hit its peak and Vaishnavism experienced mainstream patronage, although the regime is known to be tolerant and even accepting of all cults including Buddhism and Jainism. For this period, the earliest account of dietary practices comes from the Jaina work _Angavijja_. Further glimpses of food habits appear in the _MŠĻõcchakaŠĻ≠ika_ , and the _BŠĻõhat SaŠĻÉhitńĀ_ , and the works of Kalidasa. The first continuous record, however, comes from Chinese pilgrims like Faxian, Xuanzang, and Yijing. Medical treatises such as the _AŠĻ£ŠĻ≠ńĀŠĻÖga SaŠĻÉgraha_ , _AŠĻ£ŠĻ≠ńĀŠĻÖga HŠĻõdaya_ , and the _Bower Manuscript_ repeat much from earlier texts but add new insights, while the _Puranas_ and _Smritis_ shed light on the era‚Äôs dietary customs, feasts, and etiquette. Long story short, there‚Äôs much we can glean from. Let‚Äôs start with the Chinese gaze.

Faxian or Fa-Hien was an early Buddhist pilgrim who visited India during the reign of Chandragupta II, in the early years of the fifth century. By 412 AD, he was back in China and had already recorded his Indian experiences at length in one of world‚Äôs oldest travelogues, _Foguo Ji_ , better known as ‚ÄúA Record of Buddhist Kingdoms.‚ÄĚ In this work, he mostly speaks of the Middle Country or _Majjhima DeŇõa_. Contrary to what one would guess, this referred to the core area of the Magadha kingdom and nearby regions. and not the Central India of today. It included cities like Mathura, Bodh Gaya, Sarnath, and Rajagriha.

In this Middle Country, Faxian tells us, there were no butcher shops in the cities and the only ones in the business were outcastes known as _chandala_ , who lived outside the city gates.30 This was his experience in Mathura.31 What‚Äôs intriguing, however, is that if no city-dweller partook in meat and only the chandala could slaughter animals and sell their meat, who was buying the meat? Clearly there was some degree of taboo attached to meat-eating, strong enough to relegate its trade to outside the city walls. But just as clearly, not strong enough to ban its consumption entirely. Like we treat alcohol and tobacco today? Or like in some parts we treat meat itself today? Of course, a single source is far from enough to draw lasting conclusions. So, let‚Äôs look at some more.

## Early Medieval India

By the 8th century, the Guptas had long been supplanted by new principalities that had sprouted all over the subcontinent from its remnants. The era of the Indic empires was now over. The literary output of this period gives us a fairly detailed idea of how India ate at the time. A few Puranas were also composed in this period, although the bulk of the Puranic corpus belongs in the Kushan-Gupta era. Part the earliest secular literature from the post-Gupta times is a lesser-known Jain work by the name _SamarńĀiccakahńĀ_. It was composed by Acharya Haribhadrasuri, a Jain mendicant understood to be from Tamil Nadu.

One of the most striking episodes in the SamarńĀiccakahńĀ recounts the fate of a prosperous merchant who, despite dying in meditation‚ÄĒa state ordinarily associated with spiritual attainment‚ÄĒis reborn as a pig.37 In a grim twist of karmic irony, the animal ends up captured and butchered for a feast in his own palace kitchen.38 This was done to replace the meat that the cooks had already prepared earlier but was carried away by a cat.39 A wealthy merchant in the early medieval era is more likely to be a Vaishya than a Shudra. In another episode, a man named Dharana, desperate to save his ailing wife, offers her the flesh of a rabbit that had perished in a forest fire.40 This Dharana too is a merchant, which makes him unlikely to be an outcaste.41

Although many foreigners visited India in this period, we‚Äôll look at one particular visitor from China for our purposes. His name is Yijing or I-Tsing. He spent some fourteen years in the subcontinent, most of which were at the monastery in Nalanda. In his accounts, Yijing gives us some elaboration of the Indian gastronomy as he witnessed. From this we learn that food here was divided into two categories of five each‚ÄĒ _panchabhoganiyam_ and _panchakhadaniyam_.42 The former is items that should be swallowed and the latter, that should be chewed. Of the five in his _bhoganiyam_ list is meat.43 Which is rather strange given how difficult it is to consume without chewing.44 Does this mean meat was commonplace? In and of itself, no. Because there‚Äôs no other mention thereof anyplace else in the work.

Almost half a century before Yijing, India was visited by Hiuen Tsang, also known as Xuanzang. He came during the reign of King Harsha whose dynasty had risen from the remnants of the Gupta Empire. Harsha is remembered for his generosity and for his campaigns against the Huns, which he undertook together with the Maukharis. During his sixteen-year stay in India, Hiuen Tsang traveled widely across the subcontinent, visiting major centers of learning such as Nalanda and documenting his experiences in great detail. Upon his return to China, he composed a monumental account titled _Da Tang Xiyu Ji_ or ‚ÄúThe Great Tang Records on the Western Regions.‚ÄĚ In this work, he described the political landscape, social customs, religious practices, and educational institutions of India with remarkable precision. His observations remain among the most valuable sources for understanding the India of Harsha‚Äôs time.

From this work we learn that items like fish and mutton were commonplace in the Gangetic Plains.45 But we also learn that this diet was mostly confined to Kshatriyas and other non-Brahmin, non-Buddhist communities.46 Also, even where meat was acceptable, animals like oxen, horses, donkeys, pigs, dogs, apes, monkeys, etc. were already taboo.47 We will discuss this work a little more when we explore the Shramana diets, but for now, let‚Äôs move on to some Sanskrit works from the period.

A work from the cusp of ninth and tenth centuries is _Kavyamimamsa_. Although authored by the Rajashekhara, a Maharashtri Prakrit poet, this prescriptive treatise on poetry is in Sanskrit. One of its verses does discuss a specific kind of meat‚ÄĒpork:48

While this example does not prove anything about other kinds of meat, it does confirm the presence of pork in the Hindu diet of the time. Was it widespread? Was it common in all Hindu diets or only that of certain castes? These questions remain unanswered.

For some reason, much of the literature from this era is from Jain writers. We have already seen SamarńĀiccakahńĀ. From the same century we also Somadeva Suri‚Äôs retelling of King Yashodhara‚Äôs story in his _Yashastilaka_. In this story, a ritual involving the sacrifice of a rooster is completed using a model of the bird fashioned out of flour. This way, the gods are propitiated without necessitating an actual slaughter, which is increasingly seen as a cruel practice even in the Puranic interpolations of the time. For example, Bhagavata Purana in 7.15.7 clearly forbids any animal food in funerary rites:49

The work is counted among the five _mahakavyas_ of the Sanskrit canon and held in high esteem. In its sixteenth canto, there‚Äôs a royal feat that involves meat dressed as the bimba fruit.51 In another verse the same feast also features meats of deer,52 goats, and birds.53

The most generous mention of meat comes from the landmark socio-cultural commentary from the twelfth century named _Manasollasa_. The name itself means ‚Äúthe entertainer of the mind,‚ÄĚ which already hints at its endorsement of epicurean liberties. This text was authored by the Chalukyan king Someshvara and therefore must be seen in that context, i.e. from the lens of the royalty rather than the lay practitioner of the faith.

In 3.1430 thru 1435, a pig is roasted on fire and cut into _ŇõuŠĻáŠĻ≠ha_ , pieces of meat as per Monier-Williams,54 which is then roasted again on charcoals and subsequently eaten with rock salt and black pepper.55 In the verses that follow, 1436 thru 1441, the ŇõuŠĻáŠĻ≠has are blended into sweetened curd along with lemon pulp and fumigated to prepare a dish called cakkńĀlikńĀ.56 Further down, in verse 1447, we encounter pieces of meat made into broth and perfumed with aromatic spices.57 The chapter abounds in references to an astonishing variety of meat preparations, including _kavacandńę_ , _puryalńĀ_ , _bhadiŠĻ≠raka_ , _kŠĻõŠĻ£ŠĻáapńĀka_ , _vaŠĻ≠aka_ , _bhŇęŠĻ£ikńĀ_ , _koŇõalńę_ , _pa√ĪcavarŠĻáńę_ , and _maŠĻáŠłćalńęya_. Clearly, meat is relished at royal banquets and remains the pinnacle of epicurean delight.

The only conclusion one can safely draw from this is that while it was increasingly frowned upon and even discouraged strongly in certain quarters, there still existed pockets of non-outcaste communities that enjoyed a meat diet with little apology. These were mostly the Kshatrya royalties. One must also remember that this is a huge subcontinent. And for a landmass of this size and diversity, it‚Äôs not unlikely that even religious injunctions would have varied by the region and socioeconomic status. Hinduism, it would seem, was far from a unitary monolith even back in the day. Now the question one is tempted to ask is, whence this shift away from carnivory?

If our Vedic ancestors had little compunction consuming meat of all kinds, including, by the way, beef, why this growing revulsion in the Common Era? Many scholars pin this on Buddhism and Jainism. Before we conclude this conversation, therefore, it‚Äôs only fair that we throw some light on the Shramanic attitude toward meat diets.

## The Shramana Pragmatism

Let‚Äôs return to the pin we dropped on Hiuen Tsang in the last section. Since he spent much of his time among the Buddhist monks at Nalanda, his observations hold special meaning in the context of the Shramanic diet. We have already learned that meat was consumed, albeit with much reservation among Kshatriyas and other communities of the time. But what about the Shramanas?

One particularly interesting observation concerns the _SarvńĀstivńĀdin_ monks at a _HńęnayńĀna_ monastery. Xuanzang notes that these monks were ‚Äúpure and strict‚ÄĚ in observing the Vinaya rules and that they consumed three kinds of ‚Äúpure meat.‚ÄĚ58 He does not elaborate on what these meats were or what rendered them ‚Äúpure.‚ÄĚ Although the monastery he describes was located not in India but in Yanqi in China,59 the very fact that practicing Buddhist monks partook of meat deemed ‚Äúpure‚ÄĚ is noteworthy. The same is repeated in the context of yet another Hinayana monastery in Qiuci or Kucha, an ancient Buddhist kingdom on the northern edge of the Taklamakan Desert.60 Interestingly, he does not say much about the diet of Buddhist monks in India other than the fact that they, along with Brahmins, abstained from alcohol. One is free to draw their conclusions at this point but for a fuller picture, it‚Äôd help to explore the absolute roots of the tradition.

Going back to works of a more scriptural nature, we have several mentions of meat in the _Jataka_ tales. In 537, titled _MahńĀ-Sutasoma-jńĀtaka_ , we have a king of Kashi who would not eat rice without meat.61 The specific type of meat is not mentioned, but the text makes it clear that it was an essential part of his meal. As the story progresses, the king turns to human flesh and subsequently ends up getting exiled to the forest where he‚Äôs to spend the rest of his days as a man-eating ogre. The story ends with him repenting, breaking free from the curse, and resolving to give up human flesh. Of note is that although he gives up human flesh at the behest of none other than the Buddha, he doesn‚Äôt renounce flesh altogether. Does this mean meat was acceptable to the Buddha?

There are several more meat references scattered throughout the Jatakas. In 408, for instance, we find a meat-market in the city of Mithila.62 In 292, we have fish being prepared in the royal kitchen of Kashi.63 And elsewhere we have meats of deer, wild boar, and several kinds of birds being referenced in various contexts. In 144, the Bodhisattva is born in a Brahmin family and upon coming of age, he chooses to renounce the family life in quest of Enlightenment. In the forest, he attempts to sacrifice an ox to the Fire God but stops for want of salt.64 While at first crack this may seem like an endorsement of beef, a fuller reading shows it to be a moral parable on the futility of ritual sacrifice rather than a sanction thereof.

All these examples confirm, at the very least, that meat was very much a part of the diet in those days even though slaughter and sacrifice of animals was not particularly endorsed by the Buddha. But we still have much to scour. Let‚Äôs bring in the canon this time.

A central component of the Buddhist canon is the _Vinaya Pitaka_ , which outlines the rules, discipline, and conduct governing the monastic community, and is divided into sections such as the _Suttavibhanga_ , _Mahavagga_ , and _Cullavagga_ , which combine procedural codes, ethical guidelines, and illustrative narratives. Together, these divisions maintain the cohesion, moral integrity, and orderly functioning of the early _Sangha_. And meat marks its presence here as well.

In Mahavagga 6.10, the Buddha offers one of the clearest approvals of meat consumption. A monk, suffering from an illness, follows the Buddha‚Äôs advice to visit a slaughterhouse and consume uncooked pork, which restores his health. The Buddha then declares, ‚ÄúI allow, O Bhikkhus, in the case of a disease not human, the use of raw flesh and of blood.‚ÄĚ65 This isn‚Äôt even the only such declaration. In the same chapter or _khandika_ , we have the same reiterated in 6.15.66 In 6.23 is a curious incident involving a monk seeking meat through alms. A sick Bhikkhu requests meat broth from a woman to ease his illness.67 She instructs her servant to procure meat from the market, but the servant returns empty-handed because cattle slaughter is prohibited in the kingdom that day.68 In response, the woman removes a piece of her own flesh and has the servant deliver it to the Bhikkhu.69 Later, the Buddha rebukes the monk‚ÄĒnot for consuming meat, but for failing to inquire about its source.70 This episode reveals two points: cattle slaughter was not universally taboo, hence the occasional and not universal ban, and the Buddha permitted meat consumption, provided it was not human. But how does such allowance of carnivory reconcile with the Buddhist idea of nonviolence anyway? And why does the Buddha seem so ambivalent on the subject?

Another key text of the Pali Canon is the _Sutta NipńĀta_ , a collection of poems and verses imparting ethical teachings, meditation guidance, and reflections on impermanence, compassion, and the path to liberation. In verses 2.2.3 thru 2.2.9, it records Kassapa, a Buddha preceding Gautama, explaining that while killing is sinful, consuming meat is not.71 Also, curiously, he‚Äôs addressed as a Brahmin himself,72 indicating the fluid interchange between Brahminical and Shramanic identities. This section could answer the question we just asked, about the compatibility of meat diets with Buddhist nonviolence. But how does one get meat without killing? Isn‚Äôt that a little hypocritical in some sense?

The picture gets clearer with Vinaya‚Äôs Mahavagga 31.12 thru 14. In these sections, it‚Äôs Siddhartha Gautama, the last Buddha, himself who partakes in the controversial diet. So, the story goes that Sńęha, a Licchavi general, offered meat to the Buddha at his own request, and the Buddha accepted and ate it.73 The _NigaŠĻáŠĻ≠has_ then accused him of consuming flesh from an animal slain for him. In response, the Buddha clarified that meat eating was permissible so long as the animal was not killed for that purpose, allowing it if the eater had neither seen, heard, nor suspected the killing.74 This is the most unambiguous canonical instance establishing that the Buddha not only ate meat but also explicitly sanctioned its consumption under defined ethical conditions, distinguishing intent and knowledge of killing as the determining factors of moral culpability.

Two curious points emerge from this episode. First, it stands in direct contrast to the Brahminical view, which expressly forbade meat from animals _not_ killed for the purpose‚ÄĒa position clearly articulated in Kautilya‚Äôs _Arthashastra_ 2.26.1275 and, as we‚Äôve already seen before, in the Charaka Samhita. Second, even the Buddha‚Äôs own conduct invites debate, for his accusers, the NigaŠĻáŠĻ≠has, were none other than the followers of Mahavira, the Jains.

Speaking of Jains, we find no exceptions whatsoever to their notion of nonviolence anyplace in their canon. Such is the unremitting nature of this stricture that it prohibits meat even when starved or prescribed by a _vaidya_. This is one Shramana group that truly shuns violence in all forms.

## Conclusion

Across two millennia the subcontinent never spoke with one voice. Rigvedic poets relished barbequed steers. Kautilya‚Äôs spies counted carcasses. Gupta courtiers roasted boar while ascetics renounced meat entirely. Manu, ever the lawyer, hedged that meat is evil, meat is meritorious, meat is permitted when the Brahmin requests, and forbidden when he does not. Buddhist monks ate ‚Äúun-suspected‚ÄĚ pork. Jain mendicants starved rather than bruise a worm. Physicians prescribed beef for wasting disease. Playwrights sent butchers‚Äô boys coursing through courtesans‚Äô mansions. Foreign eyes saw cities without butcher shops yet country feasts where goat, deer, rhino, and peacock circled the Phalguna moon.

Yet toward the beginning of the Common Era a clear shift emerged in the Indian attitude toward meat. The change was not sudden but the result of many overlapping influences. The rise of renunciatory ideals, the growing power of non-violent sects, and the relatively stronger Buddhist and absolute Jain injunctions against killing all contributed to a gradual softening of the cultural palate. Vegetarianism took shape less as an external conquest by new faiths and more as an internal Brahminical strategy to out-ascetic the ascetics while keeping ritual control over the occasional consecrated morsel. The cow, long held sacred even when its killing was tolerated, became the unmistakable line no orthodox householder would cross. Her protection turned into a symbol of civic orthodoxy in an age of mingled courts and mixed cuisines. Indians consistently preferred other varieties of meat over beef. Thus, Hindu India reached its modern reputation as a civilization that worships the animal it once ate and that celebrates abstinence while never quite forgetting the taste of the skewer.
