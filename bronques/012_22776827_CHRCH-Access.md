So you have to go to war.

And I suggest that you don't bring a knife to a gunfight.

I personally prefer to bring a tank.

# **MAKE YOUR SCHEDULE: THE NITTY-GRITTY**

If you want to have high output-- You have to thoroughly embrace routine.

Steven King has a great passage in his book "On Writing". He talks about how you have to "meet the devil (creativity) at the same time and place every day. That way it knows where to meet you." Eventually, you'll find him waiting for you there when you arrive.

That's the power of routine.

Find it boring? Some days it will be. Some days it won't be. Welcome to the life of a professional.

Here are the 7 things you need to clarify when you're making a schedule:

## 1\. What time will you wake up?

Get up earlier than everyone else (if you live with people) so you have alone time

## 2\. What time you will start each activity.

## 3\. Where is it happening? (Try to make it "same place / same time" for each activity)

## 4\. How long you will doing this activity. (You need to commit to a block of time to be able to measure progress.)

## 5\. Write EXACTLY what you will be doing and how much of it you will expect to get accomplished? What KPIs (Key Performance Indicators) will you use to measure it? As creatives, we love to keep this loose. "Keeping it loose" is our built-in failure switch. Don't fall for it. _Write down EXACTLY what you will be doing tomorrow and how much of it you expect to get accomplished. Put it in writing WHAT WOULD MAKE TOMORROW A PERFECT DAY._

## **6\. What time will you go to sleep? This is just as important as what time you will wake up. All those trendy success magazines and YouTube videos always talk about the "wake up" times of successful people. But what's just as important (if not more) is bed-time. Most people can wake up early if they're passionate enough. It only takes a few days to re-adjust your basic circadian rhythm. But what's hard is forcing yourself to go to bed when you're still amped. Yet, this is the exact discipline which will allow you to do what you love _longer than anybody else that's alive_. If you don't set, and respect, your "LIGHTS OUT" just as if you were in the army... Well, in the long run... You will run out of steam, just like everyone else that 'tried discipline for a couple of weeks and failed'... Don't be one of these people. Respect this legend behavior and make sleep a non-negotiable.**

  


# **MAKE YOUR SCHEDULE: 'IMPORTANT BUT NOT URGENT' MORNINGS**

In his book 'The 7 Habits Of Highly Effective People', Stephen Covey introduces the idea of "Important but not urgent".

"Important but not urgent" is the category of things you want to do in life that will make you feel like you have a good life. These things are more long-term.

"Important and urgent" is the category of things that stresses people out. There's often an outside force responsible. This is where you become the world's puppet and prioritize other people’s goals by being a slave to their emails, texts, and calls. Everyone does the "Important and urgent". And then you have no energy left for the non-urgent things that will move the needle for their happiness.

Solve that by STARTING YOUR DAY with "Important but not urgent".

And put the hardest things on your schedule at the beginning of the day.

Wake up earlier if you have to.

That way no matter what happens for the rest of the day... You will have invested time into your future.

If you don't have the luxury to put everything in the morning... Do this:

Figure out which activity has the most profound impact on achieving your goals.

Make that first every day.

  


# **MAKE YOUR SCHEDULE: REDUCE CONTEXT SWITCHING**

Make sure your daily schedule has as little "context switching" as possible.

"Context switching" is every time you change to a new activity.

For example: Writing... And then cleaning your room... And then eating... That's two context switches.

Every time you change activities, you'll have to ramp up your energy to get you in the new zone. And every new activity you add in the day takes twice as much energy to ramp into as the last.

So, for profound results... Schedule your day with as little context switching as possible.

If you want to write a song, send some demos out to people, and work on your website. It's better to do large blocks of these on different days than to do small blocks of each for 3 days. Finish the song in one day. Organize the addresses, mail-out letters and package contents and go to UPS all the same day. Start and finish draft one of your website in one day.

What it ends up looking like is these very big blocks of doing ONE thing. That enables you to stay in the zone as long as possible, and will give you results that will surprise even you.

For things that require daily exposure, like practicing an instrument or going to the gym... Schedule these before or after your big block of achievement. And connect them to things you already do. Like after I shower, I will practice for an hour. Make it part of your daily routine.

**IN THE BIG BLOCKS OF TIME YOU'VE DECIDED ON, YOU SHOULDN'T BE DOING ANYTHING ELSE BUT THE THING YOU'VE COMMITTED TO DO. NO EATING. NO PHONE. NO SOCIAL MEDIA. NO EMAILS. NO CONVERSATIONS. NOTHING ELSE. YOU HAVE TO STAY INSIDE THE ONE THING YOU'VE COMMITTED TO DOING FOR THE FULL BLOCK OF TIME NO MATTER WHAT. IF YOU'RE NOT INSPIRED, YOU STAY THERE ANYWAY-- UNTIL THE TIME RUNS OUT. THIS IS THE ART OF DISCIPLINE.**

# **MAKE YOUR SCHEDULE (ADD THIS): WRITING TOMORROW'S 'HEADS DOWN' LIST EVERY DAY AFTER DINNER**

 _Get in the habit of making a HEAD-DOWN LIST for yourself every day._  
  
What's that?  
  
It's a TODO list you execute IN ORDER without thought. A list to perform in "robot mode". A list where you do what must be done without hemming and hawing or wondering if you "feel like it" or not. Just STFU and execute the tasks one after another. Don't think this list is only about boring tasks like cleaning or laundry, though. I write HEAD-DOWN lists that include creative tasks all the time. The idea is to behave like a creativity machine. You will surprise yourself. Remove the ridiculous friction of overthinking everything.  
  
**Key:**  I always have my HEAD DOWN LIST written out the night before. Even if I'm going to a party (cheat day) or I have a family dinner. I write my HEADS DOWN list early. Before I've even hopped into the shower to get ready, that way, when I wake up the following day, it's already there… Waiting for me. I'm ready to hit the ground running without wasting energy thinking of what to do first or next.  
  
**Bonus:**  Having my plan written out before I go out makes me even more present when I'm out there being social. Because nothing's hanging over my head. I can let go and have fun.  
  
**One warning:  **On regular days... Don't try to make your HEAD DOWN LIST right before you go to bed. You'll have low energy and try to rush it. Besides, right before bed is your "wind-down, clear your head" time. Write your list right after dinner instead. And don't forget to include 'making your list' in tomorrow's list, of course!_

# **MAKE YOUR SCHEDULE: DECIDE ON YOUR 'CHEAT DAYS'**

Cheat days are low impact days that happen at the end of your cycle. And they're crucial. Seeing that finish line as you work helps you work HARDER.

**_But understand, you need 9 non-ambiguous "days and nights" of perfect discipline for cheat day to be as effective as it’s supposed to be. If you cheated on your schedule and kept counting up instead of starting over at DAY 01 like you’re supposed to, God momentum won’t happen for you. And it’ll be another one of those things you tried that “didn’t work”.  _**

Cheat days are the days (decided in advance) where you break your discipline. YOU STILL WORK, but the rigid time schedules don't matter.

##  _You can’t gain sustainable momentum if you’re at risk of Cabin Fever._ Cheat days are when you schedule in social time. It's important to see people so you don't burn out. Take this seriously. It will give you a day to look forward to. And a day to schedule people in on YOUR terms. _Refresh your mind. Get some serotonin. See friends. Chill with the fam. Connect with your roommates. Or talk to the lady sitting on the park bench. The other 9 days? Be a total psycho._

Cheat day is the day you're allowed to have junk food.

Cheat day is the day when you can sleep in.

Being "bad" should be actually DIFFICULT by the time it arrives, because you will have developed a routine.

That's why it's important to have this day. Because when you burst the bubble of discipline, and then WILLFULLY and quickly impose it again the next day--_You show your mind who's boss!_

You choose your cheat days far in advance. Typically, by the time your cheat day arrives, you're so addicted to your new routine that you don't even wanna do it.

Do it anyways.

And then the next day...

Whip yourself back into action for the next 10-day journey (or whatever your personal 'extended circadian timeframe' is).

**The only thing YOU CANNOT CHEAT ON is your wind-down routine, bedtime and planning the next day.**

This needs to stay religious so that the next day is FIRE.

Cheat days are to wake up late, have a chocolate bar, and watch some YouTube. NOT to go "full civilian".

  


# **MAKE YOUR SCHEDULE: WEEKENDS DON'T EXIST... THE EXTENDED CIRCADIAN RHYTHM**

Use 10 day planning cycles (or longer) to help you achieve your goal.

Break out of the "7 days in a week" societal pattern.

Your mind has a memory for your patterns beyond just daily sleep habits. I call it the "extended circadian rhythm". Your body knows what day of the week it is and secretes chemicals in you, to prepare for what it thinks is going to happen.

When you're building discipline and momentum you have to disrupt the "normal people" rhythm that you've been following all your life.

Impose a new rhythm to regain your mental power over these old physical processes.

Show your mind who's boss. While everyone slows down on Sunday, you'll still be hitting it hard. This will tell your body that something new is going on now. There's a new rhythm in town.

And when you finally get your "cheat day" on the 10th day (for example)... Everyone else will be going about their business as usual.

Another reminder to yourself that you're "on some other sh-t".

If you can... Do the real baller move and start the 21 days on a Friday morning.

People like to start challenges on Mondays and that's just some "set yourself up for failure" bullshit.

Start on the weekend and Monday will be day 4...!

Pushing your new discipline through the weekend will make you face all those slacker tendencies head-on.

# **PREVIEW... THE CURE FOR INSOMNIA / PAYING THE CONSEQUENCES**

**Go to be at the same time every night.**

**Everyone dies right here: Because they had a "good day", they think they get to go to bed late.**

They figure, if they're doing everything else right then sleep doesn't matter.

Sleep is 🔑.

_Creativity is an alternation between the conscious and subconscious realms._

Nurture BOTH realms. Pride yourself on being disciplined with sleep.

### Not having "sleep discipline" is just planting the seeds for your failure later.

LIGHTS OUT MEANS, LIGHTS OUT.

If you "can't sleep", lie there. As tempting as it is, don't get on your phone. Pay the consequences.

Focus on the thing you’ll be doing tomorrow as you fall asleep.  

Insomnia is funny. You'll usually fall asleep one or two hours before you're supposed to wake up.

**Wake up at the same time every morning.**

This is the part that sucks. Because even if you've only had 30 minutes of sleep... Wake up anyways.

Even though it hurts.

Even though you feel groggy.

Even though you're about to have the worst day of your life.

Pay the consequences.

And tonight... Go to bed at your appointed time. NOT BEFORE, EVEN THOUGH YOU WILL WANT TO.

Pay the consequences.

Keep doing this routine until your mind obeys. (It usually only takes one night. Maximum three.)

With this kind of discipline, it'll be a while before the mind tries that trick on you again. (It'll come back between Day 12-15)

But you know why your insomnia will persist?

Because you haven't accepted the idea of PAYING THE CONSEQUENCES.

You'll get on your phone anyways.

Or you'll "sleep in" anyways. Instead of putting your alarm far away from you and super loud.

Or on your 2 hours of sleep day, you'll crash early instead of waiting for your official bedtime to arrive so that it reset your circadian rhythm.

Yes, you'll try to get away with doing less than what is required because you haven't accepted the idea of PAYING THE CONSEQUENCES.

Well until you do, your results will always be average.

Not just in this. But in your art as well.

# **PREVIEW... WEEK THREE / BEWARE OF 'THE LAW OF 7'**

They say it takes 21 days to create a new habit.

But what "they" DON'T tell you:

Is how hard it will be when you finally get to "Week 3".

The funny thing is it won't look hard.

Quite the opposite if you've done the first 14 days right.

It will FEEL easy breezy. You will have started achieving something that _looks like momentum_.

Yet this is exactly when people start planting their seeds of failure.

![](https://uploads.teachablecdn.com/attachments/4vxeYqJQaCf1IAV1nfuw_giginightowlFINAL.jpg)

Before I tell you how to reverse engineer the powerful magnet that will try to pull you back into lameness this week... Let me explain why this tendency isn't even your fault.

Do you know about the law of 7?

It's a useful concept in numerology that anyone can use to their benefit. Don't worry, you don't have to believe in any of this touchy-feely stuff to use it.

It states that in any cycle divided into 7-- That on the 3rd and 7th spots there will be some kind of hurdle. Just like the spaces between the keys on a piano. The ones without the black keys (yes, they designed musical instruments that way because of these mathematical concepts).

For example that's why "hump day" sucks so much.

And why Sunday is such an energy transition point for, like, everybody.

So when you're undertaking any long project, you have to watch out for "Week Three".

This is the pivotal week where you can track the first seeds of failure even though in the moment you will feel like you're winning.

Here's how it usually plays out:

Week 1...

It's fun. You're finally doing it! Your only struggles are energy and fighting your old habits and "urges". But if you've designed your environment to succeed, it's actually a blast.

Week 2...

You've started getting regular. The new "project" you've started is starting to feel commonplace and familiar. People start to notice you're acting "different". They compliment you on your discipline. You get your first whisps of momentum.

But then Week 3 happens...

You get "cocky".

Remember how you had to beat that chattering mind down into submission during the first 3 days? Remember how it was trying to sabotage you with "insomnia". And how you fought back and won. Afterwards, it's mostly been quiet. If it showed up, you beat it down again like Wack-a-mole.

But what did you think? That it was gonna submit without coming back with new disguises?

In Week 3 your mind will come back at you harder than ever. It will arrive dressed up as your most irresistible weakness:

EGO.

It will make you think you're "more disciplined than all those other losers".

It will make you forget the days where you sucked.

It will make you feel strong so that it may slowly wreak havok.

Your mind dressed up as ego will start convincing your that you’ve been going hard enough.

## So you will rationalize longer breaks... and later mornings... and you tell yourself "Hey, what's a little scrolling on Instagram gonna hurt? I deserve it..." And worst of all you'll start to think you don't need as much sleep. Rookie mistake.

And the permissiveness will only get worse.

A downward spiral that anyone looking-in can easily see. But YOU will be oblivious to. If you have an accountability partner, tell them to look out for WEEK THREE.

Meahwhile... Those around you (that are invested in your failure) will smell blood. Your so called "friends" will encourage you to “take it easy”, “have a little fun”, “spend some time with us”...

All the forces of evil will start working against you.

And not long after that... You'll realize that you're not "into it" any more and you'll slip back to your old ways.

And you'll wonder what happened.

It was going so well.

**_"Week Three" is what happened._**

If you did Week One and Week Two properly... "Week Three" is the bitch you gotta watch out for.

## And today I'm here like the Ghost Of Christmas Future to point out the direction you want to avoid.

Understand that the good feeling you feel once you've reached the end of Week Three is not God Momentum.

It's just a "good moment".

_All momentum is made up of many smaller moments._

God Momentum is made up of hundreds.

Practice your workload before you commit to it so that you know if it's too much or too little. Practice it before you commit to it. Yes, I know... Your ego wants you to jump in right away. Your ego wants you to feel like you're doing something. That way, you have something to brag about. But if you don't have the discipline to practice your workload, you won't have the discipline to keep going. You will run out of gas and fail. Just like every other time. If you want things to be different this time, DO THINGS DIFFERENTLY THIS TIME. Don't try to guess what your workload should be. Practice your workload until it feels right. Then, and only then, start the countdown to the new you.
