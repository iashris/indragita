##    3\. THE MANC VIDEO PLAYBOOK 

![](https://uploads.teachablecdn.com/attachments/a1770bc6b8344a9f9d6913e32c9512ba.png)

An artist needs a completely different playbook for making videos than your average "content-creator".

In the next 5 years, I believe that many of the faces above will be replaced by true artists like yourself.

Use the principles below if you want your art to stand out in this current sea of content. It's how every underdog is winning online. Yes, it takes a little courage at first. But you can sleep easy knowing that these principles will never go out of style. If you're patient, I promise, they will create a dedicated audience for you. No matter what the algorithm is, it will be forced to bend to you, not the other way around.

The premise is simple: Become a unicorn, and your audience will find you. Crazy, but in a homogenized world, this is easier than ever. What's hard is sticking to your guns. 

_Here are the guns to stick to._

  


## 1\. Bring back cat videos.

Remember when YouTube videos used to be mostly cat videos? It was an era where people weren't trying to manipulate the algorithm, or get monetized, or trying to be "cinematic". They were just being themselves.

If you're an artist making videos, you should be taking your cues from THAT era.

Video experts will tell you that "story" is 🔑 to successful videos. And they're not wrong about that. But there's a difference between “a story" and “a story formulas".

You don't have to follow the same 3-act structure everyone else uses. Often, for artists, that's not the kind of stories our videos call for. Sometimes, our stories are messier. More artistic. Like we are.

Real life and art are always more inspiring when things are unpredictable and NOT always neatly wrapped up in a bow.

It's ok if things don't resolve. It's ok if there's no hard lesson at the end. It's ok if you “lose”.

Those content creators’ formulas look more and more like the AI-generated slop coming to replace them.

But successful online videos created by artists go the other way…

They try to include every scrap of humanity they can into what they do. They leave the rough edges intact. They strive to make things that are not devoid of humanity.

They follow where life takes them and capitalize on these moments. Even if it doesn't make sense to the algorithm. Yet.

Artists make mountains out of molehills.

Artists glorify their journey.

Don't call what you're doing a film. Don't call it a movie. Don't even call it a video.

Call what you're doing art.

And then make something deserving of that title.

How?

  


## 2\. Do The Opposite

You know how much I love this quote…

 _"I should do the opposite. If every instinct I have is wrong, then the opposite must be right. So I will do the opposite. I used to sit here and do nothing and regret it for the rest of the day, so from now on I will do the opposite and I will DO something."  — George, on Seinfeld_

Every artist I see creating great videos online is marching 1000% to their own drum.

They are doing the OPPOSITE of what everyone else is doing.

It makes sense too. If you want the audience to SEE you as an artist when you show up online, you need to announce yourself that way. You have to show us that you have a new way of doing things. A new perspective.

And you have to be this person, this artist, right from the beginning.

Before you even put anything out there:

Analyze all the video trends.

Make a list of every Instagram Reels, YouTube or TikTok formula and cliche.

Write them all down.

And now... Go AGAINST them.

Ruthlessly commit to doing the opposite, BUT IN A WAY THAT IS STILL 100% AUTHENTIC TO YOU, TO YOUR PERSONAL EXPERIENCES AND TO YOUR TASTE.

Create your unicorn style.

Avoid anything that looks like old-school Casey Neistat, or Emma Chamberlain, or Mr. Beast, or any of their derivatives. Sure, some of them DO have artistic qualities, but still, their focus is 'content'.

Make your focus ART. ‘Cause that is where the future is taking us. Be there first.

Allow your videos to be slower, longer, and more narrative. Don't play into the "shorter attention span" myth. Maybe some people are getting a shorter attention span, but not those who need ART in their lives. Trust this.

Decide you won't use the same fonts, colors, lower thirds, titles, etc… that anyone else is using.

Decide that you won't use listicles — "top 10 things…" to hook viewers in. If you're an educator, you'll creatively disguise it. Or hook them with a story instead.

Decide that you won't use music services anymore. All your chosen music will be original and produced exclusively for the video. If you can't make music, you'll use a friend's music. Collaborate with an artist.

Decide you will stop using stock footage. And tired memes to punctuate your points.

Decide to leave all YouTube and TikTok cliches and hacks behind. Instead, build a channel focused on connecting with a community that doesn’t care about the thumbnail or title.

Create videos that AI could never make.

Be so over-the-top about being the opposite that it makes every person still doing those cliches feel dated.

This is how you carve your place in the online world deliberately and consciously.

You do the opposite until your taste is what becomes the new normal.

  


## 3\. The Camera You Use Is Your Message

Having a good camera may be hurting you. Not because it's good. But because you think it'll save you.

The art isn't in the camera brand. It's in the idea and the message.

When everyone can be "cinematic", being "cinematic" makes you invisible.

So what's left?

The idea. The message.

Most people are making visuals that ramble on and say nothing. Do the opposite.

Focus on the ideas and the message.

Then, be ok using your phone or your dad's beat-up camera to film it. That way, you're not tempted to make boring video postcards.

What happens when everything looks pretty?  
  
No one notices it anymore. It no longer differentiates you.  
  
And if you want people to notice your videos, YOU MUST DIFFERENTIATE YOUR VISUALS.  


I understand the attraction you have for making pretty videos. But do a little experiment. Look at the artist videos YOU'VE share online in the last month… Then ask yourself: "Did I share these videos because of how they look? Or did I share them because of an idea they were communicating?"  


If you're a videographer or a photographer, then of course... The aesthetic is EVERYTHING. Gear might actually matter for you. (Though, I'm telling you— I've downgraded to "bad gear" often, and still do, all for the sake of message, so…)  


If your art form isn't video, then forget the gear. Convey your artistic vision in the most straightforward way. Bring people into your world with tools that match what you're trying to express.  


So, no matter how "cinematic" your videos are, that's not what will make them matter in today. People want to watch a video that connects to their world. Trying to make it "cinematic" will probably ruin that connection.  


Right now, you're probably using the wrong gear. How do I know? Most people make their camera choices based on what they can afford, and that's the worst way to choose your camera. Or the choose because of what they think "looks cool". Also bad.

_Choose your camera based on what you're trying to say, and who will be watching it (aka "your message")._

**_The camera you use is your message._**

Should you be using a camcorder? Or a RED? Or your phone? Meditate on this. Don't let your ego get the upper hand. Don't be a victim of G.A.S. (Gear Acquisition Syndrome).  


'Cause when the audience says, "That video looks so good." They don't mean the camera you used.  


They mean...  


"It has beautiful framing."  


"It has great lighting."

"It has compelling camera angles."

"I love the way the story is told."

"I like how the sound draws me in."  


And these are all things you can control without spending a penny. The only investment you need is... TIME and CREATIVITY.  


These elements are the "art" of the video.  


Not the camera.  


"I love the look of those Sony camera pixels in your last video…" —said no one ever.  


The gear you use should depend on one thing only: your message.

If you want your videos to be "art" and not "content," use a simple camera. An expensive camera will look horrible if you don't know what you're doing. Simple tools create a charm that communicates authenticity right away. Your creativity shines brighter when people see your limitations.  


Hey, gearhead. Emma Chamberlain STILL uses a camcorder and a Canon rebel for her videos.

She relies on "being interesting" for her videos. Not camera gear.  


  


## 4\. Be Raw

Content creators love to make “travel, spending money, partying, 'living your best life’” videos.

For me, there's no romance in that.

Probably because I'm a New Yorker.

I'm attracted to grit. I love rawness. I don't want everything glossed over. I prefer dirty tiles to marble floors. What inspires me is seeing humans thrive in their struggle.

The artists I respect… They all love to create videos that are so individual and emotional that it would be impossible for AI or even another human to create what they do.

Content creators try to make things look "perfect".

"Perfection erases humanity." — Casey Neistat

If you're trying to make your online videos "perfect", then you're not creating art. More likely you’re trying to follow some content formula.

Art will tell you when it's finished.

(HINT: It's finished as soon as it communicates its message.)

  


## 5\. Create Your Own Wave

If you follow only the first four steps of this playbook, you will surprise yourself. You will wake up soon, and suddenly realize that you have your own formula. Your own style. Your own signature.

Your videos will feel different to people. They will have their own logic.

That's the difference between art and content. Content creators follow the established formulas. But artists… THEY INVENT THEIR OWN LANGUAGE.

Before you upload anything, playfully ask yourself:

_"Can I imagine this video in a museum one day?"_

Start thinking of your videos as performance art, and your channel as a performance art platform.

Tell yourself: "I'm no longer making videos intended for YouTube or TikTok. What I'm really doing is documenting my performance art journey and using those platforms as a temporary museum for my work.”

This simple reframing will help keep you aligned to YOUR aesthetic, YOUR goals, and to YOUR art.

It's how you avoid silly tropes like telling people to "like and subscribe".

Instead… You end up with something that feels fresh.

And ironically, this is exactly what the algorithms love.

  


## 6\. Audio > Visuals

People think the most important aspect of video is the visuals.  


It isn't.  


Sound is.  


"Bad sound" is the way you tell your audience that what they're watching is amateur. No one wants to watch a video with bad sound. It doesn't matter how good the story is. Or how pretty you are. Bad sound is annoying.  


But everyone will watch a video with visuals that suck if everything else is good.  


"Good sound" signals to people that you care about what you're trying to communicate.  


And by "good sound", I don't mean you need some crazy soundtrack and sound effects.  


All I mean is… Make sure we can hear your voice clearly. With no room echo. With no distracting background noises.  


Do you need to buy 1k worth of gear for that? Nope. All you need to do is put your phone closer to your mouth. That will sound better than a 600$ mic that's far away from you.  


Have you seen all those talking head YouTube videos and TikToks with people talking on their phones or holding their little mic? They get it.

  


## 7\. Death By 1000 Cuts

The more you edit. The more you have to edit. Because if you do a million cuts in the first 10 seconds, your viewer will expect that pace to continue. Otherwise, they'll get fidgety and bored.

And then… what's worse is they'll expect that editing style from ALL your future videos. And before long, you'll be spending ages editing video— WHEN VIDEO ISN'T EVEN YOUR MAIN ART FORM!

Stop this insanity.

Another TMS artist started releasing videos a few weeks ago. Her art has always been pure. It's very "live in the moment", "act with authenticity and realness"… But the videos she's been making look more like fashion videos with stylish cut-aways etc… Do they look beautiful? Yes. But are they communicating her message? The jury's out. She's iterating. We're watching.

**_How you edit your video is your message._**

Before you edit… Start by asking, "What am I trying to say? Who am I communicating to? Do I need a million cuts to convey my art to my audience? Or will it feel more natural and authentic with NO CUTS and long takes?" Ask yourself these questions BEFORE you even start shooting. You'll find that editing takes 1/4 of the time, next time.  


The secret to faster editing is to spend more time planning "compelling visuals". And make every decision based on your message.

##    
**8\. Stop improvising. Script. Storyboard.**

Lack of planning your shots is what makes editing such a slog. You'll never have enough footage. You'll never have the right footage.

Put effort into getting these things right ahead of time:

"What am I trying to say?"

"How should the lighting be?"

"What visuals will communicate this emotion?"

"What interesting locations can I use?"

Then, the video will edit itself.

  


## 9\. Never Post A Video Without Context

This rule is especially for artists sharing vertical media.

If you share a random video that looks like B-roll—with no explanation to your audience... Then I have to ask you:

Why are you shadow-banning yourself?

#truthlotion: _Oh, and if you're using the same stock Tiktok music everyone’s using behind your art videos, you've already lost. You've made yourself "content" by default. And now, your video will be judged as such. Good luck with that._

  


## 10\. Your journey is the only thing that people wanna see from you.

A few years ago, I wrote this in a Sermon:  


_"If I was trying to become a successful musician, I wouldn't waste my money on music videos. Nah. Instead, I would produce an ongoing narrative of visual media to document and reinforce the message that's in my music. I WOULD CREATE A WORLD. I wouldn't use social media to promote my music; I'd use social media to document and reinforce the message that's in my music. I wouldn't even share music on there most of the time. I would share the people, places, and ideas behind the music. I would make my world so immersive that by the time you realized that I even make music, you'd already be sucked in to the "movement". Soon, you'd be downloading my tracks, adding them to your playlists, and telling your friends about it."_

And then I discovered a musician doing just that.

Here are the crazy numbers behind his growing success:

He made a music video. It got him 60k views (at the time I discovered it-- it over 100k now).

But then he released a behind-the-scenes (BTS) of that same video 4 months ago, and it got him 528K views (over 800k now).

The first music video is 4 minutes long.

The follow-up BTS is 18 minutes long.

Wait. How is this possible? How did an 18-minute BTS video get 8.8 times more views than a perfectly curated 4-minute video?

"I thought attention spans were getting shorter?"

I dunno. Maybe because everyone and their mother has a music video, which makes most music videos invisible?

I dunno. Maybe because the very premise of a music video is artificial? But when you share your life, struggle, and triumphs with people—that always goes straight to the heart?

See for yourself.

Which video makes YOU feel the most? Which one inspires YOU to make more art?

And don't take my word for it. Just read the comments by fans (and newly converted fans) underneath both videos. (The music video has 114 comments. The BTS video has 668 comments. Seeing a trend, yet?)

Read those comments and let them tell you why the BTS made more impact on them.

And then ask yourself:

"Have I been using my promotional tools in the best way? Have I been communicating something real? Or have I just been phoning it in?"
